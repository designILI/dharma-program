The Year's Shape

Grade 2 is a year of expansion. The children who arrive here have already learned to sit, to chant, to lead, and to make something with their hands. They know Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, and the Manjushri Prayer. They have held a prayer flag and heard the 7-line Guru Rinpoche Prayer as a listening practice. Grade 2 takes everything planted in Grade 1 and asks: How far can this reach?

The year theme is The Widening Circle — Who do we include in our circle of care, and can we keep expanding it? The central question that runs beneath all three units is: How far can our kindness reach? That question is not rhetorical. It is the engine of the year. Students return to it in meditation, in story, in drawing, and in prayer. By the end of Grade 2, they will have extended warmth — deliberately, step by step — to a family member, a friend, an animal, and all beings. That is not a small thing for a seven-year-old.

Bloom's level here is Understand / Apply, with early Analyze beginning to surface. Students are still making, moving, and telling — the making thread remains central — but they are also now connecting ideas across sessions and beginning to ask why. Honour both. A child who says "But why did the Buddha leave his palace?" is doing the work this year is designed to invite.

The three units are designed to move from inward to outward to backward-and-forward. Unit 1 begins inside — with the child's own heart and the people closest to them — and expands outward ring by ring. Unit 2 lifts the gaze to a historical life and asks how transformation happens. Unit 3 returns to the question of help: who helps us, and how does a teacher reach someone who is afraid? Together, the units form a single arc.

The Prayer Set

Every class opens and closes with the full Grade 2 prayer set, in order:

  1. Kyap Dro — from Grade 1
  2. Om Mani Padme Hung — 7× — from Grade 1
  3. Manjushri Prayer — from Grade 1
  4. Sem kye — NEW, introduced in Unit 1, Class 3
  5. Ngowa — NEW, introduced in Unit 1, Class 3

The three prayers carried from Grade 1 should already feel like home. If students arrive and the Kyap Dro is not yet in their bodies — if it still feels effortful — that is useful information. Spend the first class of the year chanting slowly and noticing. For most students in this age range, the familiar prayers will come easily, and the arrival of two new prayers in U1.C3 will feel like a gift rather than a burden.

Introducing the New Prayers

Sem kye is introduced in U1.C3, before the closing prayers. The framing is: "This prayer is a promise — that we want to become wise and kind not only for ourselves, but for everyone." Meaning before memorisation, always. Students hear the teacher chant it once. They are not expected to join in that class. They are simply receiving it.

Ngowa follows immediately in the same closing, with its own framing: "Whatever good came from this class — we don't keep it. We send it out to everyone, everywhere." Connect this explicitly to the prayer flags — the wish going out on the wind. Students who made flags in Grade 1, or who live with flags visible from their windows, will understand this immediately and viscerally. Let that recognition land.

Both prayers are heard before they are expected. By U1.C7, most students will be joining naturally, often without being asked. Do not rush this. Do not ask who knows it yet. Simply chant, and let the group find its way in.

For full pronunciation, meaning, and classroom framing for all five prayers in the Grade 2 set, see PRAYER-REFERENCE.md in the main /curriculum folder.

The Student Leader Protocol

The student leader protocol is inherited directly from Grade 1 and continues unchanged. Each class, one student leads the opening and closing prayers. The rotation follows class order; absent students are skipped and returned to on their next class day. The student is told at the end of the previous class — not the morning of — so they have time to hold it overnight.

The teacher always chants alongside. The leader is never left to chant alone. If a student is reluctant, do not press; note it, skip, and return to them later. Some students who are reluctant in the first rotation find their footing by the second or third. The protocol is not an assessment — it is a practice in itself.

The Making Thread

Three artefacts across the year:

1. The concentric-circles diagram (Unit 1). This is the flagship artefact of Grade 2 and it grows ring by ring across four meditation sessions (C3–C6). Students do not fill in all the rings on the first day. They add one ring per session, returning to the same diagram each time. By the end of Unit 1, the diagram holds — in the student's own hand — a family member, a friend or neighbour, an animal, and all beings. Store these carefully between sessions. They matter.

2. The four-panel comic (Unit 2). Students illustrate the arc of the Buddha's life across four panels. This requires drawing materials — pencils, fine-liners if available, and ideally some colour. Plan what art supplies are accessible before Unit 2 begins. The comic is not about artistic skill; it is about sequence, about understanding that a life has a shape. Students who struggle to draw can use words or symbols.

3. The helper drawing (Unit 3). Built across four sessions, not finished in one class. Keep each student's drawing stored safely — in a folder or portfolio envelope for each child — between Unit 3, Classes 1 and 4. The drawing shows someone who helps the student when they are afraid or stuck. If a student's helper is someone who has died, or someone not currently in their life, receive this without comment and without redirecting toward a "living" helper. The practice of gratitude toward that person is real regardless of where they are.

The three artefacts together form the Grade 2 portfolio. There are no grades and no exams. Assessment is observational: which students engage readily with the guided meditation; which need more time to settle; which show depth in Turn and Talk. Record in My Reflection after each class.

The Concentric Circles Meditation — Four Sessions

Unit 1 contains the heart practice of the year: a four-session guided meditation that grows one ring at a time. Each session begins by briefly recalling all the rings established so far, then adds the new one.

  • C3 — Inner ring: one family member who is struggling (3–4 minutes)
  • C4 — Second ring: a friend or neighbour (4–5 minutes)
  • C5 — Third ring: an animal (5 minutes, wordless, image-based)
  • C6 — Widest ring: all beings (5–6 minutes, vast)

Read the script for C3 aloud to yourself once before that class. The quality of the teacher's voice during the meditation — pace, stillness, no urgency — sets the room. Students who seem distracted or fidgety in C3 often settle into the practice by C5. Do not judge early sessions. The practice is doing something whether or not it looks that way from the outside.

The 7-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer

In Unit 3, students begin learning the 7-line Guru Rinpoche Prayer — meaning first (C3), then sound. They do not memorise it in Grade 2. Memorisation comes in Grade 3. Students first heard this prayer as a listening practice in Grade 1. Grade 2 is the bridge: they now receive its meaning for the first time, and they begin to hear themselves joining in. That is enough.

When teaching the sound in U3.C3, the resident teacher's chanting is the authoritative source. The plain-language explanations in the lesson plan are for meaning-teaching only. See PRAYER-REFERENCE.md.

The Pajapati Thread

In G2.U2.C1, during the mini lesson about the Buddha's birth, one sentence is planted: "His own mother, Queen Maya, died when Siddhartha was just a baby. So he was raised by her sister — his aunt, Pajapati — who loved him as her own. She has her own story, and one day you'll hear it."

That is all. The name is not explained, not returned to, not highlighted as significant. If a student asks about her, the response is: "Her story is coming — it's worth waiting for." Do not elaborate. Do not plant more than what is given here. This thread pays off in Grade 6 and Grade 8, and it needs to arrive there quietly, having been waiting all along.

Practical Notes for the Year

Unit 1, Class 3 is the most important class to prepare carefully. It introduces the guided meditation, Sem kye, and Ngowa all in one session. It is a full class. Read the lesson plan the evening before. Read the meditation script aloud. Know where the pauses are before you are standing in front of the children.

The concentric-circles diagram grows across four sessions. Keep the diagrams stored safely between classes — do not let them go home until the end of Unit 1. Part of what makes the artefact meaningful is that students return to it, again and again, and find it already holding what they gave it last time.

For Unit 2, confirm art supplies before the unit begins. The four-panel comic requires at minimum pencils and paper, but even a single colour — a yellow for the Bodhi tree, a blue for the sky — lifts the work. Ask early, not the morning of.

Unit 3 builds a single drawing across four classes. That means the teacher is responsible for storing sixteen to twenty-five individual drawings safely for several weeks. A labelled portfolio envelope for each student, kept together in a single box or drawer, is the simplest system.

Be attentive in Unit 2 to who a student names as their helper. Receive every answer. Some children will name a grandmother who has passed. Some will name a character from a story. Some will name no one and sit quietly. None of these are problems to solve. They are the practice itself, arriving in the form it needs to arrive in.

The year ends with the helper drawing complete. Students will have, in their hands, three things they made: a map of their expanding care, a story of a life that changed, and an image of someone who helped. That is a good year's work.
