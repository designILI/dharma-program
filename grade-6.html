Year in One Sentence

Grade 6 asks students to look directly at death — as philosophy, as territory, as the ground from which figures of compassion arise — and to discover, in the final unit, that the body itself can carry what the mind has learned.

Central question: What does it mean to face impermanence honestly — and what does the tradition offer us when we do?

This is the first year in the curriculum where students are asked to hold genuine philosophical uncertainty as a pedagogical good. The "Do you believe it?" discussion in G6.U2.C6 is not rhetorical. Students are expected to reason, to disagree with each other, and to reach conclusions the teacher does not predetermine. This is Analyze-level work: examining competing claims, constructing arguments, identifying tensions without resolving them artificially.

Connective Thread

The year moves through four registers, each building on the last.

Unit 1 — Philosophy: Students examine rebirth through three frameworks (anattā, consciousness stream, three non-Buddhist comparisons). They practice Analyze-level thinking: What does this claim mean? What is the evidence? What do I actually think? The philosophical reflection that closes Unit 1 is the first portfolio piece and the first time in the curriculum where genuine intellectual disagreement is invited as a mark of maturity rather than managed away.

Unit 2 — Territory: Students enter the Bardo — the three Bardos, ground luminosity, karmic momentum. This is not abstract. G6.U2.C3 is the exemplar class for the unit and sets its depth standard: it is the class where students encounter the Bardo of Dharmata and ground luminosity, and it should be taught with the full weight of the material rather than reduced. The unit ends with the "Do you believe it?" discussion. Students emerge having been in serious territory. They also encounter Pajapati for the first time (G6.U2.C2) — her grief, her perseverance, the beginning of her story.

Unit 3 — Archetype and History: Tara arrives as a figure who stands at the threshold of everything Unit 2 explored and says: I will not leave you there. The unit holds Tara as archetype and Pajapati as history together — explicitly, in G6.U3.C2, with the statement that gives both their proper weight: "Tara shows what is possible. Pajapati shows that it has actually been done." The Dolma prayer enters in C3 as an arrival, not an introduction — students have earned it. The unit closes with students drawing their personal Tara figure.

Unit 4 — Embodiment: After the weight of the Bardo and the philosophical discussions, students move. Cham Dance brings every figure they have studied across the year — Yama, the Dharma protectors, Padmasambhava — into the body. Students make masks, learn movements, and perform a ceremony. The year closes not with an essay or a discussion but with masked dance and the dedication of merit. This is deliberate: some knowledge needs to be carried in the body, and Grade 6 is where the curriculum first makes that fully explicit.

Bloom's Level: Analyze

Grade 6 is the first year in the curriculum where Analyze is the sustained operating level across all four units.

What this means in practice: students are expected to examine claims rather than receive them, compare frameworks rather than accept the first one offered, construct arguments with reasons, and identify genuine tensions without expecting the teacher to resolve them. The philosophical rebirth discussion (G6.U1) and the "Do you believe it?" Bardo discussion (G6.U2) are both structured to invite real disagreement — not performed debate, but honest intellectual engagement.

Students can evaluate competing claims: "Here is what the Buddhist teaching says. Here is what another tradition says. Here is what I think, and here is why." They can construct arguments: "I believe X because Y, and I think Z is a problem for the position that says otherwise." They can identify tensions: "The teaching says this, but it is in tension with what I know from experience, and I am holding both."

This is the Bloom's level that makes Grades 7 and 8 possible. Grade 7 (Teaching as Practice) requires students to be clear enough in their own understanding that they can transmit it to younger students. Grade 8 (the novel study and the full Pajapati narrative) requires students who can hold complexity without needing resolution. Both of those capacities are built in Grade 6.

  4. Sem kye (Bodhichitta aspiration) — in the set since Grade 4
  5. Ngowa (Dedication of Merit) — in the set since Grade 3

  9. Dusum Sangay — in the set since Grade 5
  10. Dechen Monlam — in the set since Grade 5
  11. Dolma / Tara Prayer — NEW in Grade 6, enters at G6.U3.C3

In G6.U3.C1 and G6.U3.C2, the prayer set runs with the first ten prayers only. The Dolma enters at C3 — after two classes of preparation (Tara's origin story, Pajapati's history) — and remains in the set permanently from that point forward. It is chanted at both opening and closing from G6.U3.C3 onward.

New Prayer Protocol: The Dolma

The Dolma is the Tara Prayer — named for Drolma (སྒྲོལ་མ), the Tibetan name for Tara, meaning "she who liberates."

When it enters: G6.U3.C3 — at both opening and closing.

How it enters: Meaning first, always. The teacher explains the prayer's four structural elements (homage, invocation, petition, aspiration) and the meaning of each key section before the prayer is chanted for the first time. Only after students understand what the prayer says does the teacher lead the first chanting. This sequence — meaning, then sound — is not merely pedagogical preference; it is the principle the curriculum holds across all prayers. Students who chant with understanding are doing something qualitatively different from students who chant by rote.

Resident teacher consultation: Confirm the school's authorised version of the Dolma with the resident teacher before G6.U3.C3 — not after, and not during. Different lineages use slightly different versions; the version taught should be the one the school uses liturgically so that students' practice aligns with the community's.

The eight great fears: The Dolma petition section names the eight great fears from which Tara protects (jigpa gye — འཇིགས་པ་བརྒྱད). These are traditionally represented as external dangers (lions, elephants, fire, snakes, bandits, water, illness, evil spirits) but are simultaneously understood as internal states (pride, ignorance, hatred, envy, wrong views, attachment, doubt, greed). Both registers should be taught. This connects directly to the three poisons from Grade 5's Wheel of Life unit.

Student Leader Protocol

The student leader rotation is inherited unchanged from lower grades. One student leads the full prayer set at each class — opening and closing. The teacher does not correct the student leader mid-set. If the leader loses their place, the class continues or the teacher cues quietly; the role is not interrupted in front of the class.

By Grade 6, the prayer set (up to eleven prayers) is a significant leadership responsibility. If a student is struggling to lead, the teacher may quietly arrange extra practice time before class — not as a correction but as support.

The Making Thread: Four Portfolio Artefacts

Grade 6 produces four portfolio artefacts, distributed across the year:

  1. Philosophical reflection on rebirth (G6.U1 culminating assessment) — a written piece in which the student examines the three rebirth frameworks and states their own reasoned position.
  2. Bardo concept map (G6.U2 culminating assessment) — a visual representation of the three Bardos, their qualities, and their sequence, demonstrating the student's understanding of the territory.
  3. Pajapati journal entry (G6.U3.C2) — a written piece examining Tara as archetype and Pajapati as history: what each one offers that the other does not, and why both are necessary.
  4. "Our Taras" drawing (G6.U3.C4 culminating assessment) — a drawing of the student's personal Tara figure, presented in the share circle with a named connection to the tradition.
  5. Cham mask (G6.U4 culminating assessment) — the mask worn in the final performance, returned home afterward.

Note: the Pajapati journal entry and the "Our Taras" drawing are sometimes referenced together as the Unit 3 portfolio pair; they are produced in the same unit and both belong to the Tara-Pajapati arc.

The Grade 6 portfolio, held together, represents a year of moving from abstraction to embodiment — from philosophical argument to moving in ceremony. Together with the portfolios from Grades 1–5, it shows the arc of a student's Dharma education through the Analyze stage.

The Pajapati Thread

Pajapati Gotami is the most important historical figure in the curriculum. Her name has been held across the program since Grade 2. Grade 6 is the year when the name begins to acquire its full weight — but it is not yet the full story.

Two touchpoints in Grade 6:

G6.U2.C2 — First substantial telling: Students receive Pajapati's grief, her determination, the beginning of her ordination journey. This is a brief but real engagement — enough that the name shifts from symbol to person. The focus is on her grief after her husband's death and her first approach to the Buddha.

G6.U3.C2 — Historical pairing with Tara: This is the more sustained telling. Students receive the full arc: her relationship to the Buddha (she raised him), her widowhood, the three refusals, Ananda's intervention, the ordination, her practice, her enlightenment, her death. This is paired explicitly with the Tara archetype: "Tara shows what is possible. Pajapati shows that it has actually been done."

What is not given in Grade 6: The full narrative of Pajapati's teaching, its implications for the tradition, and the full account of her dying — these are held for the Grade 8 novel study. Do not elaborate beyond what the curriculum provides in the G6.U3.C2 notes, even if students ask. "Her full story is Grade 8. You will understand it better then because you have been carrying her name for so long."

Pedagogical note on female students: G6.U3.C2 is particularly significant for female students. The teacher's post-class reflection should note which students are most moved by the Pajapati material. This is not sentimental data — it is important information for the teachers who will receive these students in Grades 7 and 8.

Thread continuity: Pajapati has now appeared in Grades 2, 6 (twice). She will not receive major curriculum attention in Grade 7, but her name should not be forgotten — the Grade 7 teacher should be aware of what students have received and be able to hold the thread if it surfaces. Grade 8 is the full arrival.

Sensitive Content Notes

The Bardo material: Unit 2 involves direct engagement with death — not as an abstract concept but as a territory with specific features, qualities, and navigational challenges. Some students may have experienced recent bereavement. The teacher should know, before teaching Unit 2, whether any student has lost a family member in the recent past, and should speak with that student privately before the unit begins — not to exclude them, but to acknowledge what they are carrying. The Bardo teaching does not avoid grief; it speaks directly to it. For a student in recent grief, this material can be both difficult and profoundly relevant. The teacher's responsibility is not to manage the student away from difficulty but to ensure the student is not encountering the material alone.

The Pajapati material: The Pajapati story involves a woman being refused three times before being allowed to pursue her spiritual calling. For students who have experienced exclusion, dismissal, or being told they are not enough, this story lands with specific weight. The teacher does not need to manage this; the story is designed to land. What the teacher needs to do is be present for any response that comes — without deflecting, minimising, or redirecting. The story's power is also its sensitivity.

The philosophical rebirth discussion: The rebirth material in Unit 1 may create friction with students whose family religious backgrounds (Christian, Muslim, secular) hold different views about what happens after death. The curriculum does not require students to adopt the Buddhist view. The philosophical discussion explicitly invites students to examine multiple frameworks, including their own. The teacher's task is to hold genuine pluralism — to make space for the student who says "I don't believe in rebirth" as fully as for the student who says "I have always believed this." What is not acceptable is the student who mocks another student's position. The discussion norms should be set clearly at the start of Unit 1.

Practical Notes

Flag B resolved: The Grade 6 Bardo unit was written fresh (not adapted from prior materials). G6.U2.C3 is the exemplar lesson for the unit and sets its depth standard — the teacher should read it before teaching C1 to understand the full arc the unit is building toward.

Cham space and materials: Unit 4 requires cleared floor space for C3 (movements), C4 (mask-making with full workspace), and C5 (performance). The teacher should coordinate with facilities at the start of Unit 4 — ideally before C1 — to ensure the space is available and arrangeable for C3, C4, and C5. Mask materials (card stock, paint, markers, elastic) should be sourced before C4. Simple materials are the correct standard; the meaning carried in the mask matters more than its elaborateness.

Resident teacher consultation for the Dolma: Confirm the authorised version of the Dolma prayer with the resident teacher before G6.U3.C3. This consultation should happen during the teaching of Unit 2 at the latest — not in the class before C3. If the resident teacher wishes to be present for the first chanting of the Dolma, arrange that now.

Performance audience for G6.U4.C5: The decision about whether to invite an external audience (another class, parents, school staff) should be made before Unit 4 C1 — not in the final days. If the audience is invited, they need to know the performance opens with prayer and chanting, that there is no applause cue, and that the closing is a merit dedication, not a curtain call. A brief note to parents or staff before the performance helps set the right expectations.

G6.U2.C3 as exemplar: G6.U2.C3 — the class on the Bardo of Dharmata and ground luminosity — is the depth standard for the unit. If the teacher is uncertain how seriously to take the Bardo material, reading C3 before beginning the unit answers the question. The curriculum does not soften the Bardo for Grade 6 students; they are ready for its full weight.

Grade 6 is complete when the masks go home.
