The Year in One Sentence

Grade 4 asks students to move from receiving ritual to reading it — to understand that every object on a shrine, every gesture in a ceremony, and every sound in a practice is carrying a teaching that can be learned to read.

Central question: How do objects, gestures, and sound encode meaning?

This question is not rhetorical. By the end of the year, students have a concrete, practised answer: they understand what the seven water bowls represent, they have made something with their hands and given it away, and they can explain a bell, a dorje, and a mala to someone who has never seen one. The central question is answered through experience, not through lecture.

Unit 1 was written and taught before this document was compiled. Its outcome — students who can set up and narrate the seven-bowl offering from memory, who carry the concepts of trenpa and puja, and who understand that ritual objects encode teachings — is the foundation on which Units 2 and 3 are built. Everything in this document assumes that Unit 1 has been completed.

The Connective Thread

The three units of Grade 4 represent three different relationships to ritual objects.

In Unit 1, students encounter objects that already exist and already carry meaning. Their work is to understand — to receive the teaching that has been encoded in the seven bowls, to learn what each substance represents, and to set up the offering with precision. The relationship is: student to received object.

In Unit 2, students become makers. They do not receive a torma; they build one, over three sessions of patient, physical work. The relationship changes: student to made object. And then the object is given away, which introduces a third element — the act of release. The torma becomes a teaching about non-attachment precisely because it was made with care. If it were made carelessly, the giving away would mean nothing.

In Unit 3, students become readers. They encounter objects they did not make and do not own — the bell, the dorje, the mala — and they learn to decode the meaning encoded in their form, their sound, and the way they are held. The Field Guide they produce is evidence of this reading capacity.

The arc is: understanding, making, reading. Three different ways of being in relation to a ritual object, each deepening the students' engagement with the year's central question. Name this arc to students at the opening of Unit 3: "In Unit 1 you understood objects. In Unit 2 you made one. Now: we learn to read them."

Bloom's Level Guidance

Grade 4 operates primarily at Understand, Apply, and the beginning of Analyze. This is the appropriate band for nine- and ten-year-olds who are encountering material that is genuinely new and often quite abstract.

What Understand looks like in Grade 4: students can explain what a torma is, what the bell represents, what the Ngowa prayer does. They can describe the symbolic meaning of a ritual object in their own words, not just repeat a definition. Understanding here is not passive — it requires active connection-making, and the teacher should press toward this: "Tell me in your own words. Don't use the words I used."

What Apply looks like in Grade 4: students set up the seven-bowl offering correctly from memory. They work a material into a form using their hands. They count mantra repetitions with a mala. They place objects on a shrine in the correct position. In Grade 4, Apply is often physical — the body is the primary site of application.

What early Analyze looks like in Grade 4: students can hold literal and symbolic readings simultaneously. This is the signature cognitive development of this year. A nine-year-old in Grade 4 can look at a torma and say both "it is a cone made of flour and butter" and "the pointed top represents the mind of awakening" — and hold both truths at once without contradiction. This dual reading is what the tradition calls symbolic literacy, and it is the foundation of all later work in the curriculum. When students demonstrate this capacity — speaking about an object at both levels, fluidly — the teacher should name it: "You just did something important. You read it two ways at once."

The Prayer Set

Grade 4 uses a nine-prayer set at every opening and closing. The Food Offering Prayer appears at the closing only.

Opening and closing: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay

Closing only: Food Offering Prayer (added after the Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer)

The full closing set is: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay

Two prayers are new in Grade 4: the Sevenline extended form and the Dusum Sangay. Both should be introduced at the opening of Unit 1, before they are simply added to the set without explanation.

New prayer protocol: Before adding a new prayer, introduce it by meaning. For the Sevenline extended form: "This is an extended version of the Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer you learned last year — it carries the same invocation, now with additional lines that complete the teaching. We are adding it because you are ready to hold more." For the Dusum Sangay: "This prayer honours the Buddhas of the three times — past, present, and future. We offer to all of them, not only to those we know by name." Meaning first. Then confirm the pronunciation and lead-in with the resident teacher before using these prayers formally in class. Once introduced and confirmed, they are added to the set and remain there for the rest of the year without further explanation.

Student Leader Protocol

Inherited from Grades 1–3, unchanged. One student leads the full prayer set at each opening and closing. The rotation is managed by the teacher; each student should lead approximately once per unit. The teacher sits with the class during prayers — not leading, not correcting in the moment. The student leader holds the space. After class, the teacher can address any errors in pronunciation or pacing quietly and privately.

In G4.U3.C5 — the final class of the year — the teacher leads the opening prayer set (not the student leader). This is an intentional exception: the year's closing deserves the teacher's full presence at the front. The student leader does lead the three repetitions of Om Mani Padme Hung in the Body Moment, and the full closing prayer set. Both roles are honoured.

The Making Thread

Grade 4 produces three artefacts across the year. Together, they form the Grade 4 portfolio.

The first artefact is from Unit 1: a labelled diagram of the seven-bowl offering setup, drawn and annotated by each student. It shows the bowls in order, names the substance in each, and notes what each represents. This is an artefact of understanding — it demonstrates that the student has received the teaching and can hold it in structured form.

The second artefact is from Unit 2: the torma portfolio pages — the design sketch from C1 and the reflective paragraph from C4 ("what I put into this — what it cost me to make it — and what I am about to give away"). The torma itself is not kept; it is offered and released. But the sketch and the paragraph are. This pairing is deliberate: the object goes away, the reflection stays. What the student made and what they learned from making it are two different things, and the portfolio preserves the latter.

The third artefact is from Unit 3: the Field Guide to Ritual Objects. This is the most complex artefact of the year — a multi-entry illustrated reference booklet built over four classes. It is given to students to keep at the end of Unit 3, C5. Unlike the other artefacts, which document past experience, the Field Guide is a forward-looking tool: students can use it in the years ahead, and it should be made with that in mind.

These three artefacts represent three different relationships to knowledge: knowing (the diagram), experiencing (the torma portfolio), and documenting for use (the Field Guide). The progression is intentional. Point it out to students at the end of the year: "You have three artefacts from this year. One tells you what things mean. One tells you what it was like to make something and let it go. One you can use in five years to remember what you learned. They are all different kinds of knowing."

Practical Notes

Before Unit 1 begins: Confirm that the seven-bowl set is available and clean. If the school does not have a formal seven-bowl set, a set of seven matching cups or small bowls will serve as a teaching medium — explain this to students as an example of form and intention. The water offering is introduced in Unit 1, C1; have the bowls ready from day one.

Before Unit 2 begins: Source the making material. The preferred material is tsampa and butter — confirm with the school kitchen whether tsampa is available and whether a small amount of butter can be allocated for the class. If not, prepare salt dough in advance (recipe is in the G4.U2.C1 curriculum guidance). Air-dry clay is the most forgiving substitute and is recommended if dough is logistically difficult. Test the material yourself before C2. Have airtight storage containers ready for the tormas between C2–C4. Confirm with school administration the plan for dissolving or disposing of the tormas after C5 — this is a necessary coordination, not optional.

Before Unit 3 begins: Confirm that a bell and dorje are available. The bell should be in use from at least C1 of Unit 3, and ideally weekly throughout the year — the more familiar the sound, the more the C1 opening moment lands. Confirm mala availability before C3: one mala per student is ideal; one per pair is the minimum. If the school's supply is insufficient, arrange to borrow from the monastery before the class, not during. Confirm the incense/ventilation situation before C2 — do not light incense in a poorly ventilated space; have a scented alternative ready.

The bell throughout the year: The bell is introduced formally in G4.U3.C1, but it should be present and heard from the beginning of the year. The teacher can ring the bell once at the start and end of each class, in addition to the prayer set. This builds familiarity and creates the conditions for the C1 moment — when students hear the bell and are asked "what did you notice?" — to be genuinely reflective rather than merely instructional.

Demonstration Day (Unit 1, C6): This class involves an audience — another class, a group of parents, or school staff — watching students demonstrate the seven-bowl offering. Arrange the audience in advance. The audience should be seated, quiet, and genuinely attentive — not a performance situation, but a witnessing one. Brief the audience beforehand: "You are here to receive what these students have learned. Your attention is the offering you bring."

The year is designed to deepen. What begins in Unit 1 as careful understanding becomes physical practice in Unit 2 and then genuine reading in Unit 3. By the final class, students who engage honestly with the curriculum can look at a shrine and read it, hold a mala and use it, and explain a ritual object to someone who has never seen one. That is the year's achievement — and it is real.
