Grade 8  ·  Unit 3  ·  Class 3

Building

Shaping clay or dough into a stupa form → the movement of understanding from words and diagrams into the hands; knowledge made physical

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleEmbodied cognition
Bloom's levelCreate
LessonG8.U3.C3

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Shaping clay or dough into a stupa form → the movement of understanding from words and diagrams into the hands; knowledge made physical

Cognitive Principle Featured: Embodied cognition — when knowledge is encoded in the hands through sustained making, it is retained differently and more deeply than when held only in language; the act of shaping the dome with full attention to what the dome means changes the relationship between the student and the teaching

Bloom's Level: Create — students produce a three-dimensional object that encodes the Buddhist teaching on the path to enlightenment, working with sustained attention throughout the session

Prior Class Summary: C2 deepened the symbolic analysis of the stupa, connecting each element to specific content from across the eight-year curriculum. Students finalised their designs — type, proportions, curriculum connections annotated. They were told: "Come knowing what you are building."

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the construction sequence for building a stupa model: base first, then dome, then harmika, then spire, then pinnacle Students will UNDERSTAND that the quality of attention brought to the making is inseparable from what is made — building a teaching object with distracted attention is different from building it with the awareness of what each element means Students will BE ABLE TO produce a completed or substantially completed stupa model in the 35-minute building session, proportioned with intention and identifiable in all its major elements Students will FEEL the particular quality of sustained making — the texture of clay or dough, the problem-solving of proportion, the shift from understanding-in-words to understanding-in-hands — and the distinct satisfaction of an object that holds what you know in a form that can be seen and held

Materials Checklist

  • Clay or salt dough — sufficient for one model per student; portions pre-measured and available at each place before class begins
  • Salt dough recipe if needed: 2 cups flour, 1 cup salt, 1 cup water — mix until firm; makes enough for several small models
  • Clay portions: approximately 500g–750g per student for a fist-sized to hand-sized model
  • Shaping tools — butter knives, popsicle sticks, pencils for scoring, or purpose-made clay tools; at minimum one tool per student
  • Water in small containers — for clay work (to keep hands and clay from cracking); not needed for salt dough
  • Armature materials — for students building tall or structurally complex spires: short lengths of wire, small pieces of cardboard, wooden toothpicks or skewers; available at a central station, not forced on students
  • Reference images — stupa photographs and structural diagrams available throughout the session for students who want to check proportions
  • Students' finalised design sketches — at each student's place as a reference guide during building
  • Protective surface covering — newspaper, plastic sheeting, or similar on tables before students arrive
  • Labels or tape and marker — so students can tag their completed models with their name for safe keeping between C3 and C4
  • Drying plan — if using salt dough, models must dry before C4; confirm drying location and time; overnight minimum for a model 10–15cm tall; if using air-dry clay, plan similarly
  • Plan B: If clay and salt dough are both unavailable, students build from cardboard (folded, scored, and glued or taped), foam blocks, or any available material — the medium matters less than the meaning; a well-considered cardboard stupa built with understanding is more valuable than a clay one built by rote; the teacher says simply: "The medium is not the teaching. What you know about what you're building is the teaching."

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 8 prayer set, led by the student leader of the day. Sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Tsog Due pa. Student leader conducts.

Materials are already on the tables. Students notice them before prayer begins. No comment is needed.

After the prayer — brief. No PKA today. The teacher moves directly from prayer to the one instruction that frames the session:

"Last class I said: when you build your stupa, you are not making a model. You are making a teaching object. That is still true. Build it with that quality of attention. Work in silence if you can. If you need to ask something, ask quietly. Your design sketch is in front of you. The reference images are available. Begin."

Mini Lesson — 3 minutes

Practical guidance only — brief, clear.

"Construction sequence: base first, then dome, then harmika, then spire, then pinnacle. Don't try to build everything at once — work in order, from bottom to top, letting each element be stable before you add the next."

"If you're using clay: keep your hands damp. Score the surfaces where two pieces join — press gently and smooth the join. If you're using salt dough: it is stickier than clay; wet joins work similarly. Keep the overall scale in mind — if your base is small, your spire can't be tall or it will fall; if your base is large, the proportions up top need to match."

"The spire should be taller than the dome. The dome should be clearly rounded. The harmika should be distinctly different from the dome — a flat-sided box or platform, not another curve. The pinnacle should be visible at the top."

"As you shape each element: name it to yourself — silently — and its meaning. Not as a memory exercise. As a way of keeping attention present in what your hands are doing."

No vocabulary moment today — the session is for making.

Body Moment: The making itself. Hands in clay. Sustained attention. The teacher participates — circulates continuously but does not hover; allows students to work without constant surveillance, while remaining available for practical questions.

Emotional Hook: Named here for the teacher, not announced to students — the texture of making something that has been studied. The moment when a teaching that has lived in words moves into the hands. The problem-solving of proportion. The particular quality of attention that sustained, purposeful making requires — different from thinking, different from writing, but not less serious.

Activity — 35 minutes

Full building session. Teacher circulates throughout — not with corrections but with questions, encouragement, and practical help.

Circulation questions (ask quietly, one-on-one):

  • "What are you building right now? What does it mean?"
  • "The dome: what is the relationship between its proportion and the spire's? If the dome is large and the spire is short, what does that suggest about the balance of the path?"
  • "What element are you on? What comes next?"
  • "Is the harmika readable — can I tell it apart from the dome by looking?"

Students who finish early: add surface detail — texture, carved or impressed text or mantra (Om Mani Padme Hung, for example), small prayer flags made from paper strips, painted colours if paint is available. The model should be as complete and as considered as time allows.

Students who are struggling with proportion or structure: offer practical help — a piece of cardboard for an armature, a suggestion about scale. The goal is a model that is identifiable and considered, not a technically perfect sculpture.

Silence: The teacher encourages silence throughout the session — not enforces it rigidly, but maintains it as the default. If conversation arises, redirect gently: "Back to the work." Sustained silence during sustained making is a practice, not a restriction.

Debrief — 2 minutes

Brief. Most students will be mid-build or just completing.

"Your stupa is built or nearly built. Next class you present it — you will hold it and explain each element to the group. Between now and then: look at it. Read it. Know what you have made."

"Label it with your name before it leaves your hands."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full Grade 8 prayer set. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer added at close.

Teacher's closing line: "Everything you know is now in your hands. That is where it belongs."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Students keep their stupas safely between C3 and C4 — establish a designated, safe storage location in the classroom or school where models can dry undisturbed. If salt dough: the models need overnight drying minimum; do not rush this. If clay: air-drying clay needs similar time. Do not paint in C3 if drying is needed first — plan painting for the beginning of C4 if necessary, with very brief time allowed, or have students paint between classes. The C4 presentations require each student to hold their stupa and explain each element — they should practise this between classes. Tell them this explicitly at the end of C3: "Practise your explanation. Walk through the stupa from base to pinnacle, naming each element and at least one curriculum connection. Not as a memory exercise — as a transmission." The quality of the C4 session depends on the quality of preparation between C3 and C4. Logistical note: models built by less experienced clay-workers may not be technically refined. This does not matter — what matters is the student's understanding of what each element means and their ability to explain it in C4. A rough but thoughtfully built stupa, presented with genuine knowledge, is more valuable than a polished form that the student cannot explain. Hold this standard clearly in C4.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child built a model stupa from clay or salt dough — working in sustained silence with full attention. Each element of the stupa (base, dome, harmika, spire, pinnacle) encodes a specific Buddhist teaching, and they built it knowing what each part means. Next class they will present it to the group. You can ask them: 'Can you show me your stupa and explain what each part means? Which part was the hardest to build?'"
