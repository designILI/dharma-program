First Draft

A blank page meeting a confirmed topic → a structured first draft of a lesson that has genuine content and a real activity for the younger student

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleScaffolded production
Bloom's levelCreate
LessonG7.U2.C3

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): A blank page meeting a confirmed topic → a structured first draft of a lesson that has genuine content and a real activity for the younger student

Cognitive Principle Featured: Scaffolded production — the five-section lesson structure (Opening, Activation, Main Teaching, Activity, Closing) breaks the daunting whole of "write a lesson" into discrete, manageable units. Students who find the whole overwhelming can begin with one section. The scaffold supports the work without replacing the thinking.

Bloom's Level: Create — the primary creative production of the unit. Students move from planning (C2) to making: a first draft lesson plan in their own voice, for a real audience, using a structure they now understand from the inside.

Prior Class Summary: C2 confirmed each student's teaching topic and introduced four teachability criteria (concrete before abstract, story-friendly, one main idea, teachable depth). Students completed the lesson design worksheet — topic, audience, one main idea, learning objective, concrete anchor, story or analogy. They carry the one-sentence explanation and the instruction: "If you don't have a concrete anchor, find one."

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the five-section lesson structure — Opening, Activation, Main Teaching, Activity, Closing — with approximate timings, and understand the function of each section in the twenty-minute lesson Students will UNDERSTAND the word scaffolding as it applies to lesson design: the lesson plan scaffolds the younger student, the five-section structure scaffolds the lesson plan, and both are temporary supports toward independent understanding Students will BE ABLE TO produce a complete first draft lesson plan using the five-section structure — rough in places, but complete in outline; every section present, with the activity and concrete anchor named and described Students will FEEL the specific energy of a blank page becoming a made thing — and the particular discovery that a first draft, however rough, is more tractable than no draft

Materials Checklist

  • C2 lesson design worksheets (students bring theirs)
  • Fresh paper or dedicated lesson plan pages for the draft, one per student (at least two pages)
  • Pens or pencils, one per student
  • Board with five-section structure written and visible for the full session:
  • Opening (2 min): Settle the room. State the topic.
  • Activation (3 min): One question connecting to what younger students already know.
  • Main Teaching (7 min): One main idea, with concrete anchor and story/analogy.
  • Activity (5 min): Something the younger student makes or does with the teaching.
  • Closing (3 min): One question that helps them take it home.
  • Grade 7 prayer set visible
  • Plan B (for students who stall on the blank page): Have two or three sentence starters ready to give individually: "My lesson opens with..." / "The one main idea I want them to leave with is..." / "The activity I am planning is..." A stalled student almost always needs a starting sentence, not more planning. If the stall persists, the teacher writes the opening two sentences of the student's lesson aloud while dictating from the student — then hands the pen back.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 7 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer at closing only.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 2–3 minutes

"Hold your lesson design worksheet. You have thirty seconds — tell the person beside you what you're planning to teach and the concrete anchor you've chosen. Thirty seconds. Go."

Thirty seconds. Immediate and brief — this is not a conversation, it is an activation. It warms up the material that is about to be written.

"Good. Now write."

This transition is intentionally fast. The lesson's primary function is the drafting session itself; the opening moves students into it without delay.

Mini Lesson — 5 minutes

The five-section structure — and what each section does.

Point to the board. The structure is already written. Walk through it quickly — students know lesson structure from being in lessons; this is naming what they already implicitly know:

Opening (2 min): Settle the younger students. State the topic — not as a lecture, as a promise: "Today we're going to learn about..." This should be brief, calm, and specific. It tells the younger student what to expect.

Activation (3 min): One question connecting to what the younger student already knows. This is not a quiz — it is a bridge. The question links their existing knowledge to the new teaching. Without this, the new idea arrives without a place to land.

Main Teaching (7 min): The one main idea, with the concrete anchor and the story or analogy. This is the core. Seven minutes is not long — which is why "one main idea" is not a suggestion. The concrete anchor comes before the abstract explanation. The story comes before the concept.

Activity (5 min): Something the younger student makes, does, or produces with the teaching. Not listening to more explanation — something they do. Drawing, writing, physical gesture, sorting, creating. The activity is how the teaching becomes theirs rather than the teacher's.

Closing (3 min): One question that helps the younger student take the idea home. Not "what did you learn?" — specific: "If you were going to explain this to your parent tonight, what would you say?" Or: "What is one thing you're going to think about today because of this lesson?"

"Write to this structure. Twenty minutes. Every section present. It does not need to be perfect — it needs to be complete. Rough and complete is much more useful than polished and missing the activity."

Vocabulary Moment:

Write on the board: scaffolding — in education, temporary support that enables a learner to do something they could not yet do alone, with the expectation that the support will eventually be removed as the learner's own capacity grows.

"Your lesson plan scaffolds the younger students — it gives them a structure to hold a new idea. This five-section structure scaffolds your lesson plan — it gives you a structure to build within. Both are scaffolds. Both are temporary. The younger students will eventually understand the idea without needing the lesson. You will eventually design lessons without needing the five-section framework. But right now: the scaffold is what makes the building possible."

Body Moment: Not today — the full session is writing. The activity is the embodied engagement. Note this to students if they expect a body moment: "Today, the work is the body moment. Writing is not abstract — it is physical: holding the pen, finding the word, feeling the resistance of the blank page. That is today's body moment."

Emotional Hook: The blank page meeting the student's genuine knowledge — the specific challenge and energy of making something real that will be tested in a real room with real younger students.

Activity — 30 minutes

Full drafting session.

Students write their first draft lesson plans using the five-section structure. The teacher circulates continuously — not editing, not correcting, but questioning:

  • "What does the younger student do in the activity? Name the specific action."
  • "How will you know if they understood? What will they produce or say that tells you?"
  • "What is the concrete anchor here — what do they touch, see, or draw? Is it in the lesson?"
  • "Your activation question — does a nine-year-old actually have prior knowledge to connect to this? What do they already know about this topic from their own Dharma classes?"
  • "Your main teaching is seven minutes. Have you tried saying it aloud? Does it fit?"
  • "Does your closing question help them take the idea home — or does it just close the class?"

Students are encouraged to write rough and revise within the session — not to polish before the structure is sound. A lesson plan that is beautifully written in the opening and has "TBD" for the activity is not a first draft; it is half a draft.

Students who finish early: "Read your draft from the younger student's point of view. What will confuse them? What will they find most interesting? Is there any moment where you lose them? Mark those places."

Students who are significantly behind at the twenty-minute mark: the teacher sits with them briefly, identifies which section is blocked, and gives a specific prompt to unlock it. Usually: the activity section. "You don't have to know exactly what they'll do yet — just write one option. Even if it changes. The draft needs a placeholder."

Debrief — 3 minutes

"Next class: critique and revision. Bring your draft. The critique is not about whether it's good — it's about whether it will work. Those are different questions. 'Good' means polished and well-written. 'Works' means the younger student understands the one main idea and has done something with it. Come ready to hear that second question asked of your draft."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader.

Teacher's closing line: "The Sang offering carries the prayers outward. Today you built something — rough, first-draft, not finished. Next class someone will look at it honestly and tell you what needs to change. That is the best thing that can happen to a first draft."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: First drafts will vary enormously in quality. This is expected and normal. The most common weaknesses to watch for and note for C4's critique:

  1. No real activity — the activity section describes more explanation, more listening, more questions, but nothing the younger student physically does or makes. Push for a concrete action: "What do they make or do?"
  1. A concrete anchor that is not concrete — e.g., "I will explain the three poisons using an analogy" is not a concrete anchor. A physical rope around the wrist is a concrete anchor. Watch for this.
  1. Multiple main ideas — the main teaching section tries to cover too much. The seven-minute teaching section feels like ten topics crammed into seven minutes. The revision instruction: cut to one.
  1. Activation question requiring knowledge younger students don't have — e.g., asking a Grade 3 student about the Bardo when they haven't studied it yet. The teacher needs to know the younger students' Dharma curriculum well enough to catch this.
  1. Closing question that doesn't travel home — "What did you learn today?" does not help the younger student articulate the idea to a parent or sibling. "What would you tell your parent tonight if they asked what Tsethar means?" does.

Read all first drafts between C3 and C4. Make brief written notes (not on the student's draft, but in the teacher's own log): what's working, what needs attention, whether the student is ready for the critique process. Students who have significant structural problems need specific guidance from the teacher in C4 before peer critique begins — peer critique can't fix structural problems that the student doesn't yet know are problems.
