Three Chairs

Three physical chairs in a room → the three interior positions available to every person who is present when cruelty occurs

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleEmbodied perspective-taking
Bloom's levelEvaluate
LessonG7.U1.C3

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Three physical chairs in a room → the three interior positions available to every person who is present when cruelty occurs

Cognitive Principle Featured: Embodied perspective-taking — the body holds experience that the mind alone cannot access. By physically occupying a position (sitting in a chair, taking a posture, holding a facial expression), students access felt knowledge rather than only conceptual knowledge. The body moment is the entire activity.

Bloom's Level: Evaluate — students assess the experience of all three positions from the inside, and arrive at the bystander question with felt rather than only intellectual understanding: "What stopped you from doing something — and what does practice ask you to do with that?"

Prior Class Summary: C2 examined the four psychological patterns of the person who bullies (fear of exposure, disconnection, learned behaviour, empathy deficits) and mapped them onto the three poisons. The body moment placed students inside shame and the impulse to redirect. They carry that felt knowledge into today's exercise.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the bystander effect — the documented social phenomenon in which the presence of multiple witnesses reduces the likelihood that any individual will intervene — and its mechanism (diffusion of responsibility) Students will UNDERSTAND that the bystander position is not neutral: the decision not to act is itself a decision, and silence functions as participation in what continues Students will BE ABLE TO articulate, from inside the experience of Chair 3, what specifically stopped them from acting — and what would have made acting possible Students will FEEL the specific discomfort of sitting in Chair 2 (the bully chair) and finding something recognisable there; and the particular helplessness and complicity of Chair 3 (the bystander chair) — not as performance but as genuine interior encounter

Materials Checklist

  • Three chairs, arranged in a loose triangle at the front of the room (rearrange before students arrive if possible)
  • Remaining chairs or floor space for observers
  • Board and chalk or marker for vocabulary and the scenario
  • Journals or paper for written reflection (Plan B use)
  • Pens or pencils, one per student
  • Grade 7 prayer set visible
  • Plan B (for the exercise itself): If students are significantly reluctant to physically sit in the chairs — particularly Chair 2 — do not force it. Offer the written alternative: "Write three paragraphs, one from each chair. Use first person, present tense: 'I am sitting in Chair 1. I feel...' Write for three minutes per chair." This is less powerful than the physical exercise but preserves the perspective-taking function. Do not offer Plan B preemptively — present the chairs first. Plan B is a fallback if the room's energy genuinely cannot hold the physical format.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 7 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader.

If the room has already been rearranged, acknowledge it: "You can see the room is different today. The three chairs are the lesson." Food Offering Prayer at closing only.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

"Before we begin: I want to ask about one specific position — not the bully's, not the target's. The bystander's. What does it actually feel like to be a bystander — to be present when something harmful is happening and not know what to do? Not what you think you should feel. What does it actually feel like?"

Take 3–4 responses. Write key words on the board — students typically name things like: frozen, guilty, scared, invisible, wanting it to stop, not wanting to be next.

"Hold onto that list. By the end of today you'll have been inside all three positions — not just this one."

Fallback: If students are quiet or deflect with "I'd just say something" (a common protective response): "What stops someone who wants to say something from actually saying it? What is happening in that moment of hesitation?" This moves toward the felt experience rather than the ideal.

Mini Lesson — 5–8 minutes

Frame the exercise.

"Today's exercise is called Three Chairs. It asks you to fully inhabit three perspectives — not to perform them, not to play a role, but to genuinely try to think and feel from inside each one. This is harder than it sounds. The goal is not to find which chair is 'yours.' The goal is to understand all three from the inside. That is empathy."

Point to each chair as you name it:

Chair 1: The person being bullied.

Chair 2: The person doing the bullying.

Chair 3: The bystander — present, watching, not acting.

"I'll give you a scenario. It is fictional — it is not about anyone in this room. It is specific enough to inhabit and general enough that it belongs to no one here."

Read the scenario: A student — let's call them Student A — has been excluded from the lunch group for three weeks. Not dramatically: they simply find that the table they used to sit at is now always full just before they arrive. When they sit nearby, comments are made about how they eat, what they bring for lunch. The comments are quiet. They are not stopped. Four or five people hear them each time. No one says anything. Student A eats alone.

"This has been happening for three weeks. Today is a day in the middle of it — not the beginning, not a dramatic confrontation. Just a Tuesday."

Vocabulary Moment:

Write on the board: bystander effect — the documented phenomenon in which the presence of multiple witnesses to a harmful situation reduces the likelihood that any individual will intervene.

"This is not a theory — it is a consistently observed pattern. The more people are present, the less likely any single person is to act. The mechanism is called diffusion of responsibility: each person assumes someone else will do something. When everyone assumes this, no one acts. Understanding this effect is not an excuse to use it — it is a map that lets you step outside it."

Body Moment: The Three Chairs exercise itself is entirely embodied — students move between chairs, hold posture and facial expression, and feel the specificity of each position. The preparation ends; the body moment is the activity.

Emotional Hook: The moment in Chair 2 — the discomfort of genuinely trying to inhabit the perspective of the person doing the bullying, and finding something there that is recognisable. Not comfortable. Necessary.

Activity — 25 minutes

The Three Chairs exercise.

Round 1 (8–10 min):

Invite three volunteers — one per chair. Participation is genuinely voluntary; observers are a valid role. The teacher facilitates from beside the chairs, not from the front.

With the volunteer in Chair 1: "You are Student A. It's Tuesday. You're walking toward the lunch area. What are you feeling right now — not what you think you should feel, what are you actually feeling? What do you want? What do you need right now that no one is giving you? What do you wish one of those people at the other tables would do?" Allow 2 minutes. Listen without commenting.

With the volunteer in Chair 2: "You are the person who makes the comments. It's Tuesday. You're at the lunch table. What are you feeling right now, before you say anything? Why does this particular person feel like the right target today? Is there anything you're trying not to think about?" Allow 2 minutes. This is the most important chair to hold with care — the teacher's tone here signals to the whole class that this is exploration, not accusation.

With the volunteer in Chair 3: "You're at the table. You can hear the comments. You see Student A sitting alone. What's happening in your body right now? What are you telling yourself? What specifically is stopping you from doing something? If you imagine standing up and going to sit with Student A — what is the exact feeling that stops that?" Allow 2 minutes.

After all three: "Observers — what did you notice? What shifted for you as you watched?" Take 2–3 responses.

Round 2 (8–10 min):

Rotate — new volunteers take the chairs. The teacher may adjust the scenario slightly: "We're now one week later. The same pattern continues. Nothing has changed. Sit in your chair with that weight."

Same structure — 2 minutes per chair, observer debrief after.

Final question (5 min):

To the group, especially those who have sat in Chair 3: "What specifically stopped you from doing something — not in theory, in the exercise? And: what would have made it possible to act? Was there a moment where you almost moved?"

Take responses. Write key words: fear of becoming the target, not wanting to break the social contract, assuming someone else would act, not knowing what to say.

"That list — that is the real obstacle. Not bad character. Specific fears and social pressures. Those can be understood and worked with. They cannot be worked with if we pretend they aren't there."

Debrief — 5 minutes

"The bystander is not neutral. The decision not to act is a decision. And the bystander effect means that everyone in a group is waiting for someone else to go first — which means no one goes. The practice question is: who is the someone? What does it actually take to be that person? Next class we look at what the options actually are — the real frameworks for bystander response. But today you've been inside all three chairs. Carry that."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader.

Teacher's closing line: "The Sang offering carries our understanding outward. Today we sat inside three kinds of difficulty — target, perpetrator, witness. The understanding we built here is not just ours. We dedicate it."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The Three Chairs exercise is the emotional core of Unit 1. Debrief carefully and do not rush to close — if the exercise generates real feeling (and it often does), the five-minute debrief is not enough to contain it. If necessary, extend the debrief by cutting the second round of chairs to one full round and a shorter final question sequence.

Chair 2 (the bully chair) is the most sensitive. The teacher's tone when facilitating that chair signals everything: if the teacher sounds like they're interrogating a guilty party, no student will genuinely go inside it. The framing must be curious and exploratory, not accusatory. The questions for Chair 2 are deliberately interior: not "why are you being cruel" (accusatory) but "what are you feeling before you say anything — what are you trying not to think about" (interior, specific, psychologically accurate).

If a student shows signs of significant distress — real tears, real shutdown, not performed discomfort — use the school's pastoral process. Do not address it in front of the class. A quiet word and a pathway to support is the right response.

The bystander discussion in C3 feeds directly into C4's three frameworks (direct intervention, lateral support, systemic action). Students need the felt understanding from C3 to use those frameworks with genuine conviction rather than as abstract options. C4 follows directly from the list of specific obstacles generated in the final question of C3 — carry that list forward.

Spiral connection: Grade 6 — Tara as the figure who does not abandon beings in difficulty. The bystander who acts is making a Tara-like move: stepping toward rather than away from suffering. This connection is worth naming in C4's debrief; it gives Buddhist grounding to the frameworks.
