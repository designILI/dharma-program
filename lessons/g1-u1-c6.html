Hoisting Day

Hanging a painted flag in the wind → the completion of a wish that now travels without its maker

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleClosure and Transfer Through Ritual
Bloom's levelApply
LessonG1.U1.C6

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Hanging a painted flag in the wind → the completion of a wish that now travels without its maker

Cognitive Principle Featured: Closure and Transfer Through Ritual — human beings encode learning more deeply when it ends with a meaningful closing event. A ritual is not performance; it is a structured, intentional act that marks a transition. Today's hoisting ceremony marks the transition from "something we made in class" to "something that is now out in the world doing its work." The ritual also triggers the unit's transfer prompt: after today, every time students see a flag moving in the wind, they will retrieve the full arc of what they learned.

Bloom's Level: Apply

Prior Class Summary: In C5, students completed their flags and shared their two-sentence dedications with the class. Each child named a person and a wish. The sharing was received quietly and without evaluation. Flags are dry and ready. Students know that today they go up.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW that their flag is now hung and will continue to carry the mantra into the wind after they leave.

Students will UNDERSTAND that the unit's essential question — how can a wish travel to someone far away? — has now been answered in practice, not just in words.

Students will BE ABLE TO participate in a short dedication ceremony: hang their flag and say or think their wish for their person one more time.

Students will FEEL the satisfaction of completion, the mild solemnity of a small ceremony, and the surprising truth that doing something for someone else feels good.

Materials Checklist

  • All student flags — dry, labelled on the back, stored flat or rolled carefully
  • Teacher's completed flag
  • A length of cord or rope for hanging flags — pre-strung between two fixed points at a height where students can clip or tie their own flag with minimal adult assistance. Ideally outdoors: between a fence post and a tree, along a building wall, or across a courtyard. If outdoors is not possible (weather), string indoors across a window wall so flags will move in any breeze from an open window.
  • Advance prep: The hanging location should be scouted and the cord strung before class. Test that it is taut and at an appropriate height. Tie a knot or mark every 30 cm so flags hang with space between them and do not overlap.
  • Wooden clothespins — 2 per flag (28 total), plus extras
  • Singing bowl
  • One small incense stick or bundle of dried juniper (optional, traditional) — lit briefly at the start of the outdoor ceremony, if the school permits. If not, a small butter lamp placed safely is an alternative. If neither is possible, the ceremony is complete without it.
  • The six syllable cards (one final appearance — propped at the hanging location)
  • A small piece of paper and a pencil or crayon for each student — for the unit reflection (used in debrief)
  • An adult helper if possible — to assist with clipping flags for students who cannot reach

Room setup: Begin indoors in the circle. Move outdoors (or to the hanging location) for the hoisting. Return indoors for debrief. If the hanging location is outdoors, lay flags in order at the starting point so the transition is smooth.

Student leader: Today's leader has special significance — they lead prayers both indoors at opening and again at the hanging site. Brief them on both roles. Assure them: same prayers, just a different location.

Note on tone: Today should feel ceremonial but not solemn to the point of stiffness. Six-year-olds can hold dignity for short stretches — the ceremony should last no more than fifteen minutes. Let there be joy in it.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes (indoors, in the circle)

Students enter. Their flags are not yet in their hands — they are in a stack at the teacher's feet, face-down. Students know they are here; the anticipation is real.

Student leader steps forward.

Kyap Dro (Refuge): Leader recites. the teacher alongside — today, perhaps more slowly than any previous class. Let it breathe.

Om Mani Padme Hung: Seven times. The class has now chanted this mantra across six classes. The sound is genuinely theirs. the teacher can step back today — let the students' voices lead, and simply join.

Manjushri Prayer: Leader recites.

Strike the bowl once.

"Today is Hoisting Day. We've been building toward this since Class 1. The flags are ready. The wind is waiting. Let's go."

Hand each student their flag — their name, confirmed briefly as they receive it. Hold your own flag last.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes (at the hanging location)

Walk as a group to the hanging location — outdoors if possible, single file, flags in hand. This walk is part of the lesson. Students may chatter; let them. the teacher does not rush.

At the hanging site, gather in a loose cluster facing the cord.

"Before we hang — I want to ask you something. Think all the way back to Class 1. What do you remember from the very first day?"

Exact retrieval prompt: "What did we do? And what was our question?"

Strong-response example: A child says, "We chanted and you asked how a wish can travel far away." — "Yes. Does anyone remember what we found out?"

Surface response: "We painted flags." — "That's right — and why? What were the flags for?" Build the answer through the group.

Fallback if very little is recalled: the teacher says: "Let me remind you. We started with a sound. We learned what the sound points toward. We learned how flags carry sounds into the wind. And then we painted our own flags — for a person — and told each other who they were for. That's the whole journey, right there." Brief. Clear. No inflation.

"And today — the flags go up. The Lung-ta takes over."

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes (the ceremony itself)

This is the ceremony. It has five parts, described below. The whole thing takes approximately ten to twelve minutes, including the hanging. Move through it without rushing but without stalling.

Part 1 — Lighting (if using incense or lamp)

If incense or juniper is available: light it briefly and let the smoke drift near the flags. "In our tradition, we purify the space before something important. The smoke says: this matters." Students watch. No further explanation needed.

Part 2 — Final chant

Student leader leads Om Mani Padme Hung one more time — slowly, seven times — at the hanging site, outdoors, with the flags in their hands. This is significant. The mantra that started as a sound students barely knew in C1 is now being chanted by them, together, outdoors, before they hang wishes they painted by hand. Do not rush through it.

After the final "Hung": silence for ten seconds. Wind, birds, whatever is present. Let students hear the world receiving the sound.

Part 3 — Dedication

"Before you hang your flag — one more time: think of your person. Say their name in your mind, or quietly if you like. Your wish for them. Hold that for just a moment."

Ten seconds of quiet. the teacher holds their own flag and does this visibly.

Part 4 — Hanging

"When you're ready — come up, one at a time, and hang your flag."

Students come forward one at a time. Two clothespins each. They clip their flag to the cord. the teacher or the adult helper assists where needed — height, clothespin mechanics — without taking over the act. The child's hands should make the final clip.

After each student hangs their flag, the teacher says: "[Name]'s flag — for [the person they named in C5, if remembered; otherwise simply: for the person you're thinking of]."

Do not rush this. Each flag deserves its moment on the cord. Students who have hung their flag step back and watch as others hang theirs.

After all the flags are hung, the teacher hangs their own.

Part 5 — Looking

Fifteen flags on the cord. In the wind.

"Look."

Nothing more. Let them look. Let the flags move. This is the lesson's peak moment and it needs no words.

After twenty or thirty seconds: "This is how it works. Every time the wind moves them, the Lung-ta is running. Your wish is going."

Body Moment: Spread arms wide, palms out, facing the flags. "Like this: sending." Hold for five seconds. Lower arms.

Vocabulary Moment: "What we just did — dedicating something good and sending it — we call this dedication. That word was in C5 too. Now you've done it. It's in your body."

Emotional Hook: You are creating the felt experience of having done something real — something that continues without you. Watch for the student who watches their flag move and smiles slowly. Watch for the student who goes quiet. Watch for the student who says, without prompting, "It's really moving." The emotional hook in this lesson is completion: the rare childhood experience of having seen something through from beginning to end, and discovering that it is beautiful. Do not describe it to them. Let the flags do it.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes (at the hanging site or walking back)

"Walk back with your partner. Tell them: what was the most surprising thing from this whole unit — from Class 1 to today?"

Students walk and talk. the teacher walks among them, listening without intervening. This is genuine reflection, peer to peer.

When indoors: "Did anyone want to share what their partner said?"

Take two or three responses. Receive them.

Activity — 15–20 minutes (reflection, indoors)

Students sit at tables. Each receives a small piece of paper and a pencil or crayon.

"Three questions. You don't have to answer all of them — just the one that feels most real to you. Draw, write, or use a mix."

Write or say the questions:

  1. Who is your flag for? Draw or write their name.
  2. What did you learn about wishes?
  3. When you see a flag moving in the wind — now that you've done this — what do you think?

Fifteen minutes of quiet work. This is the portfolio piece for the unit. It requires no specific format — a drawing of the person, a few words, a sentence, a picture of a flag in the wind. All are valid evidence of understanding.

the teacher circulates, reads over shoulders, sits briefly with any student who seems stuck:

"Just draw them. Your person — just their face, or something that makes you think of them." This always works.

"Just write the first word that comes to you when you think about wishes."

Scaffold: A student who can't write yet can dictate one sentence to the teacher, who writes it on the paper for them. The student then illustrates it.

Extension: A student who finishes quickly: "Now: draw what you imagine the Lung-ta looks like. Not what you've seen in pictures — what you imagine." This opens imaginative space that requires and encodes all the symbolic vocabulary of the unit.

Collect reflection papers with the flags' back-label names. These go into the class portfolio.

Debrief — 5 minutes

Circle. Students are without materials.

"We started this unit with a question: how can a wish travel to someone far away?"

"We've answered it now — not in words, but in practice. What are the answers you found?"

Let students speak. You will hear: by chanting, by painting it, by hanging it in the wind, by the Lung-ta, by saying the person's name. All correct. All part of the answer.

"I want to leave you with this:"

"Our flags are outside right now. The wind is moving them. You are in here. But the wishes — they're still going."

Pause.

"That's the circle of kindness. We send it out, and it keeps going, even when we're not looking. Even when we've forgotten we sent it."

Let that be the last thing before closing. Do not add more.

Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads for the final time in this unit: Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer.

Strike the bowl once.

"When you go home today — when you pass a prayer flag, on a rooftop or a mountain or at the monastery gate — you'll know something you didn't know six weeks ago. You'll know what's written on it. You'll know what the Lung-ta is carrying. And you'll know that someone painted it for someone they love."

One final bowl strike. The class is dismissed.

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Unit 1 is complete. For Unit 2, carry these students forward: their capacity to chant the full mantra; their understanding of dedication (sending good things to others); their experience of making something carefully for someone else. These are now the foundation. Whatever Unit 2 brings, it builds on ground that is no longer abstract.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child participated in a small ceremony to hang the prayer flag they painted — a flag made for someone they care about, carrying the mantra Om Mani Padme Hung. The flags now hang at school. You can ask them: 'When you see a prayer flag moving in the wind, what does it mean now? Who is your flag for?'"

"When you see a flag moving in the wind this week — whose wish is riding on it?"
