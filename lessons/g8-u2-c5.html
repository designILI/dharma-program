Grade 8  ·  Unit 2  ·  Class 5

Can Anyone Change?

Milarepa's full arc — from mass murder to enlightenment → a personally argued position on whether transformation at the root is possible for anyone

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleBefore-and-after comparison as meta-cognitive evidence
Bloom's levelEvaluate / Create
LessonG8.U2.C5

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Milarepa's full arc — from mass murder to enlightenment → a personally argued position on whether transformation at the root is possible for anyone

Cognitive Principle Featured: Before-and-after comparison as meta-cognitive evidence — holding one's C1 before-position alongside the C5 final response allows students to observe the movement of their own thinking as data; this meta-cognition is itself an instance of the mind examining itself, which is what the unit has been about

Bloom's Level: Evaluate / Create — students write a fully argued final position, synthesising the Twelve Links framework, turning-point analysis, and their own observation and belief; they present it and compare it to their starting position

Prior Class Summary: C4 mapped and analysed the turning points in Milarepa's story — internal readiness + external catalyst = turning point; each turning point interrupts a link in the Twelve Links chain. Students carry their turning-point maps and the claim: the tradition tells this story not as an exception but as a demonstration of what is possible.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the full arc of Milarepa's story — from the earliest episode in C1 to the cave years and enlightenment in C3 — as a complete narrative with identifiable turning points and causal logic Students will UNDERSTAND the distinction between a conviction — a firmly held belief supported by reasons, capable of revision — and a mere supposition held without examination; and that the unit asks for the former Students will BE ABLE TO write a final philosophical response integrating the Twelve Links framework, one specific episode from Milarepa's story, and one connection to their own experience or observation — and to compare it to their C1 starting position with specific attention to what changed Students will FEEL the particular quality of having genuinely revised or confirmed a position through sustained study — not the performance of having learned something but the real experience of one's view having been tested and having held, or moved

Materials Checklist

  • C1 before-positions (held by teacher since C1 — return them at the start of class)
  • C3 mid-unit positions (held by teacher since C3 — return them at the start of class)
  • C4 turning-point maps (returned from last class, or held by students)
  • Paper for the final response — three or more pages per student
  • Pencils or pens
  • Plan B: If time for reading aloud is insufficient after writing, the teacher selects two or three responses to share — one that has changed position substantially, one that has confirmed an initial intuition with argument, and one that holds a position of genuine uncertainty — and reads them aloud on behalf of the students (with their permission), naming what is philosophically interesting about each

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 8 prayer set, led by the student leader of the day. Sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Tsog Due pa. Student leader conducts.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

The teacher returns the C1 before-position and C3 mid-unit position to each student at the start of the PKA.

"Read what you wrote in C1 — your first response to whether someone who has done what Milarepa did can be transformed. Then read your C3 mid-unit position. You have thirty seconds."

Students read. Then:

"One sentence: has your position changed from C1 to now? In what direction — and what moved it?"

Go around the room — brief, one sentence each. No discussion yet. The teacher listens and notes the range.

"Good. You have the beginning of your final response. Now you need the argument."

Mini Lesson — 5 minutes

Frame the final response clearly.

"You have now studied Milarepa's story in full. You have a framework — the Twelve Links — that describes the mechanism of the mind and the conditions under which transformation is possible. You have turning-point analysis. You have your own observation and belief, which have had five classes to develop and be tested."

"Your final response is not a summary. It is not a report on what the tradition says. It is your own argued position on the question: Can anyone change? What does Milarepa's story teach about this, and what do you actually believe?"

"The argument should use at least three things: the Twelve Links framework — where in the chain does transformation begin?; one specific episode from Milarepa's story, not general reference but a specific moment; and one connection to your own experience or observation. If the phenomenological practice in C3 gave you something — use it. If the turning-point analysis from C4 gave you a specific insight — use it. This is your evidence."

"You may argue: transformation is possible, for anyone, given sufficient conditions and willingness. You may argue: transformation is possible in principle but practically unreachable for most people, most of the time. You may argue: the very concept of 'anyone' is the problem — transformation depends so much on specific conditions that 'anyone' is too broad a claim to be useful. All of these are defensible. What matters is that you argue it, not just assert it."

Vocabulary Moment: "The word conviction — a firmly held belief supported by reasons. Not certainty, which is impossible, and not a weak opinion held without examination. A conviction can be revised — it is not dogma. But it is held with one's full being rather than merely supposed. The tradition does not ask for certainty about the nature of mind and the possibility of transformation. It asks for conviction — the quality of believing with enough depth that the belief shapes how you act, how you practise, how you engage with the question of what you might become. Today: argue toward a conviction."

Body Moment: Students hold the C1 before-position in one hand and the C4 turning-point map in the other. "These are the two endpoints of what this unit built. What is the distance between them? Not only in your understanding of Milarepa — in you. What changed in you, not only in your knowledge?" Sixty seconds of silence.

Emotional Hook: The particular quality of having one's position tested and having it hold — or of having it genuinely move — and the recognition that this movement (or this holding) is itself evidence about the nature of mind and the possibility of change.

Activity — 25 minutes

Writing (20 minutes): Students write their final response. The teacher circulates continuously with specific, honest engagement:

  • "What is your claim? One sentence."
  • "Where is the evidence — not general reference to the story, but a specific episode or moment?"
  • "What is the strongest objection to your position? Have you addressed it?"
  • "You wrote in C1 that you thought [X]. Now you're writing [Y]. What produced the change? Say that in the argument."
  • "Is this a conviction — something you actually believe — or a position you think sounds right? They feel different. Which is it?"

Sharing (5 minutes): When most students have a working draft, two or three students read their responses aloud. The class listens. After each reading: one question from the room — a genuine clarification or challenge, not an affirmation — and one response from the writer.

Debrief — 3 minutes

"The unit ends with a question the curriculum has been building toward since Grade 1: what is a human being capable of? Not in a general, inspirational sense — but specifically: can the mechanism of the mind described by the Twelve Links be interrupted at its root? Can avidyā — the fundamental ignorance — actually be addressed? Milarepa's story says yes. It does not say easily. It does not say quickly. It does not say for everyone in a single lifetime, or without enormous cost. But it says: yes, it is possible. This is the tradition's central claim. And it is the claim the rest of Grade 8 continues to test."

"What you argued today is your current honest position. Hold it lightly enough to revise when the evidence warrants."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full Grade 8 prayer set. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer added at close.

Teacher's closing line — one sentence for Unit 2 as a whole: "He began as a murderer and ended as a poet of the nature of mind. The distance between those two things is the teaching."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Collect the final responses after C5, alongside the C1 before-positions. These are the Unit 2 portfolio artefact — and the before-and-after comparison is the most important thing to preserve. When you read them: note what moved, what didn't, and what the quality of the argument is. The comparison is not a measurement of whether students now believe transformation is possible — it is a measurement of whether they can argue a position from evidence, hold a counterconsideration, and produce a reasoned conviction. Students who confirm their C1 position with strong argument have done excellent work. Students who have changed position substantially have also done excellent work. Students who remain genuinely uncertain but have articulated the reasons for their uncertainty have done excellent work. The failure mode is: students who reproduce the tradition's claims without genuine engagement, or who write generically without any connection to their own experience or to specific narrative detail. Unit 3 — The Stupas — begins immediately. This is a complete tonal shift: from story and philosophy to making. After two units of demanding analytical work, the making class arrives as a different mode of engagement — hands, proportion, material, form. Do not frame Unit 3 as lighter or less serious. The stupa encodes in three-dimensional form everything studied in eight years. Building it with genuine understanding is as demanding a task as the Socratic discussion in U1.C5 — different in mode, not in depth. Do NOT introduce the Pajapati thread at the close of Unit 2 or the opening of Unit 3. Hold it for Unit 4. Students have carried her name since Grade 2. The full telling is for Unit 4 only.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child wrote their final argued response to the question: 'Can anyone change?' — drawing on Milarepa's story, the Buddhist teaching on the twelve causes of suffering and transformation, and their own observation and experience as evidence. They also compared their final position to what they wrote in the first class of the unit, to see what moved. You can ask them: 'What is your answer now? Has it changed from the beginning? What convinced you — or didn't?'"
