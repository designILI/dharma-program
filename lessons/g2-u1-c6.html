LessonG2.U1.C6

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Completing the outer ring of the diagram → Holding the aspiration of wellbeing for all beings simultaneously, beyond what can be pictured

Cognitive Principle Featured: Schema completion — over four sessions, students have built a cognitive and emotional map (the diagram) and a meditation structure (settle, recall previous rings, add one new one). Today both reach their limit in the same class. The diagram is physically complete; the meditation pushes beyond what can be individually imagined. This convergence creates a moment of genuine arrival — students feel that they have built something, and that it has reached its natural boundary.

Bloom's Level: Apply / early Analyze. Students apply the full meditation structure (recall all three previous rings, expand to all beings) and complete the diagram. Early analysis is available to students who notice the qualitative difference between the three specific rings (where they could picture individuals) and the fourth ring (where they cannot) — and wonder what kind of wish is still possible when pictures run out.

Prior Class Summary: In C5, students extended the meditation to animals — picturing a specific creature and sending a wordless, image-based wish for its safety and wellbeing. The meditation ended with an open question: "What is bigger than this ring?" That question is answered today.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW that the outermost ring of the diagram holds all beings — every person never met, every creature in the sea, in the sky, underground, in all directions — and that Buddhist teaching calls this the aspiration of Bodhichitta.

Students will UNDERSTAND that when the circle expands beyond what can be pictured, the wish changes form — from a specific image to a vast, felt direction — and that this is not a failure of imagination but its fullest use.

Students will BE ABLE TO complete the outer ring of their diagram and sit through the full 5–6 minute meditation; some will feel something like spaciousness or reaching; some will feel lost or uncertain; both responses are true and worth discussing.

Students will FEEL the odd, genuine spaciousness of trying to hold "all beings" — not a warm settled glow, but a kind of reaching beyond what the mind can contain — which is itself a significant, new experience at this age.

Materials Checklist

  • Concentric circles diagrams from C5 (returned by teacher)
  • Pencils, coloured pencils, and fine-tipped pens or markers — students may want to write "all beings" in different languages, or draw stars, waves, or abstract marks for the outer ring
  • Tibetan singing bowl or bell — Plan B: teacher's audible breath as opening/closing signal
  • Quiet, settled room — same arrangement as C3–C5
  • Optional: a window open so outdoor sounds (birdsong, wind) can enter during the meditation — not required, but if available, name it gently: "The sounds coming in are part of all beings too."

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader leads the full Grade 2 prayer set:

  1. Kyap Dro
  2. Om Mani Padme Hung (7 times)
  3. Manjushri Prayer
  4. Sem kye — most students now joining at least partway
  5. Ngowa — teacher leads at closing (see Closing section)

Before the prayers: "Today we close the circle. The last meditation, the last ring, and your diagram complete. Let's open with everything we know."

Prior Knowledge Activation — 6 minutes

"Last class I left you with a question at the end of the meditation. I didn't answer it. Did anyone think about it this week?"

Pause. Take responses. Even one or two students is enough.

"The question was: what is bigger than the animal ring? What is further out than the birds, the dogs, the insects?"

Take responses: people in other countries, creatures in the ocean, beings in space, beings we've never heard of.

"All of that. Every single thing that is alive and can feel — anywhere. Every person you have never met and never will. Every creature in the sea. Every insect underground right now. Every being in every direction, in every place you cannot picture."

Pause.

"That is the outer ring. And today we put it on the diagram."

Then: "Before we do — let's walk back through everything you've built. Hold up your diagram."

Students hold up their diagrams. "Centre ring: what's in there?" (Family.) "Second ring?" (Friends, neighbours.) "Third ring?" (Animals, creatures.) "And today — the outer ring."

"You've built this over four weeks. Today it becomes complete."

Mini Lesson — 8 minutes

"Here's something I want to tell you before the meditation. When we go to the outer ring today, something different is going to happen inside the meditation."

"In the first three rings, you could picture someone. A face. A specific animal. You could hold them in your mind and send a wish to them — it was aimed. Even if the feeling was quieter in the second ring than the first, you could still find a face."

"Today, you won't be able to picture everyone in the outer ring. There are too many. Billions and billions of beings. You cannot hold their faces in your mind — there is no way to. So the wish has to change."

"Instead of a picture — instead of a face — the wish becomes a direction. A feeling sent outward, in all directions at once, like the flags in the wind. Not aimed at one person, but open to every direction. The way a lamp doesn't only light one wall — it lights the whole room."

"Does a wish like that still do anything? That's a question teachers and monks have thought about for thousands of years. What we know is this: something changes in the person who sends the wish. When you really try to include everyone — when you try not to leave anyone out — something happens to you. You get bigger inside."

Vocabulary Moment: "'All beings' — in Tibetan: semchen tamche, all sentient beings. Every being that can feel. This phrase appears in the mantra, in Sem kye, in Ngowa. You have been saying 'all beings' all year. Today you will feel from the inside what those words point to."

Body Moment: "Sit up straight. Close your eyes for one moment. Breathe in — and as you breathe out, imagine the breath spreading outward from you in all directions at once. Not just forward. Sideways. Behind you. Down through the floor. Up through the ceiling. Out through the walls. One breath, all directions. Do that once." Students do it. "Open your eyes. That is what today's meditation will feel like — just longer."

Emotional Hook: The shift in the room when students genuinely attempt to hold "all beings" — a particular quality of quiet different from the previous meditations. The teacher should not explain or name this in advance; only notice it afterward.

Turn and Talk — 3 minutes

"Turn to the person next to you. Tell them: is there anyone — any person, any creature — that you think is hard to include in the outer ring? Someone you're not sure you want to include?"

Allow two minutes. The teacher circulates and listens.

What to listen for: Students naming people who are unkind, or animals they fear or dislike (spiders, snakes, rats). This is genuine moral territory. Receive it without immediately resolving it. If it comes up in sharing, say: "That's honest. The teaching is that 'all beings' means all — even the ones that feel difficult. We don't have to feel warmth toward them. We just try not to leave them out. There's a difference."

Activity — 18 minutes

Part 1 — Guided Meditation: The Widest Ring (5–6 minutes)

Ring the bell (or audible breath). Students close eyes or look down at their hands.

Teacher reads aloud, slowly. Longer pauses than previous meditations. This can be read verbatim.

"Settle your body. Let everything be still."

Pause 5 seconds.

"One breath in — slow. Let it go all the way out."

Pause 5 seconds.

"Go back to the very beginning. Find your centre ring — the family member, the person you held in C3. Just a glimpse of their face."

Pause 8 seconds.

"Step out one ring. The friend, the classmate, the neighbour. See them for a moment."

Pause 8 seconds.

"Step out again. The animal ring. Picture one creature — any one from last week, or a new one. See it for a moment, comfortable and safe."

Pause 8 seconds.

"Good. Three rings. All of them real, all of them inhabited. Now — step all the way out."

Pause 8 seconds.

"Go so far out you can no longer picture individuals."

Pause 5 seconds.

"Think of every person in this country you have never met. Thousands of them, maybe millions. You cannot see their faces — there are too many. Just know: they are there."

Pause 8 seconds.

"Go further. Every person in every country you will never visit. Every person alive right now — waking up, sleeping, working, crying, laughing — you cannot see them. Just know: they are there. They are real."

Pause 8 seconds.

"Go further still. Every animal in the sea. Every creature underground right now, moving through the soil. Every bird in flight on the other side of the world. Every being in the sky."

Pause 8 seconds.

"All of them — all at once — wanting the same thing: to be okay. To not suffer. To have enough. To feel safe."

Pause 10 seconds.

"Now: send something outward. Not a picture — you can't picture all of this. Send a feeling. A direction. The feeling of: May all of you be well. May all of you be free from suffering. May all of you have what you need. Send it outward in all directions at once."

Pause 12 seconds.

"Stay with this. It might feel strange — like trying to hold the sky with your hands. You can't hold it all. But you can reach."

Pause 12 seconds.

"Stay reaching."

Pause 8 seconds.

"Before you open your eyes — what does this feel like? Different from the family ring? Different from the animal ring? What is new here?"

Pause 8 seconds.

"Take one breath. Let it come all the way back in. And slowly open your eyes."

Ring bell or exhale.

Full thirty seconds of quiet. Do not speak immediately.

"That — that reaching feeling — has a name. We'll name it next class."

Part 2 — Complete the outer ring (10 minutes)

Return diagram sheets. "Open to the outer ring — the last one. This ring holds all beings. You cannot write every name. You cannot draw every face. So draw whatever feels right: stars, waves, dots, lines radiating outward, abstract marks. Write 'all beings' in any language you know. Write semchen tamche if you want to try the Tibetan. Fill this ring with something that feels like 'everywhere and everyone.'"

Students work. Coloured pencils and pens available. The teacher circulates very quietly — this is a creative and contemplative act simultaneously.

Scaffold: Students unsure what to draw may simply write "all beings" in large letters around the ring, or draw a series of small marks — one for each breath they take while working.

Extension: Students who finish may look at their completed diagram as a whole and write one sentence on the back: "The person hardest to include in my circle is ___." They do not need to share this with anyone.

The teacher collects completed diagrams and stores them safely for the C7 presentation.

Debrief — 5 minutes

"Hold up your complete diagram — all four rings. Look at it."

Pause for a moment of looking.

"A few weeks ago, this was a blank piece of paper with some circles drawn on it. Now it's a map of something real — something you actually did, week by week, one ring at a time."

"How did the outer ring feel different from the others?"

Take responses. Receive all of them: "It felt big." "It felt impossible." "My mind kept going back to people I know." "I felt something — I don't know what." All of these are honest reports on a genuine experience.

"Next class is the last class of this unit. You'll show your circles to each other. And we'll name what this practice is called. It has a name — a very old one."

Closing — 3 minutes

The student leader leads:

  1. Kyap Dro
  2. Om Mani Padme Hung (7 times)
  3. Manjushri Prayer
  4. Sem kye — most students now joining meaningfully
  5. Ngowa — teacher leads; students joining where they are ready

After closing: "Every time we say Ngowa at the end — 'may the merit go to all beings' — you now know from the inside what 'all beings' means. It's that reaching feeling. Remember it."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The completed diagrams must be kept safely by the teacher until C7. Students should not take them home yet — the presentation in C7 is the culminating assessment, and students need their diagram in hand for it. Before C7, the teacher should look through the diagrams briefly — not to grade them, but to notice: which students' outer rings feel genuinely expansive, and which feel thin or rushed? These observations inform the C7 presentation conversations, where the teacher can ask targeted questions: "Tell me about the outer ring — what did you put there?" The phrase "it has a name" in the debrief is an intentional tease for C7 — the word Bodhichitta should not be introduced until C7, when students receive it as the name for something they have already experienced. This sequencing matters: the experience before the label, always. Spiral connection: the mantra's purpose ("for all beings") — students have been saying Om Mani Padme Hung since Grade 1, but the "all beings" dimension was only addressed implicitly. This lesson gives that phrase its full meaning for the first time.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child completed their concentric circles diagram — adding the outermost ring, which holds all beings: every person, every creature, everywhere. The meditation today asked them to try to hold 'all beings' in a single wish. You can ask them: What did it feel like? Was there anyone or anything hard to include? What does your outer ring look like?"
