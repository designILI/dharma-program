Reading Our Wheels

The gap between the C1 from-memory sketch and the completed personal Wheel drawing → the arc of learning as something that can be seen, held, and reflected on; and the Wheel as a complete symbolic system now understood from the inside

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleMetacognitive comparison
Bloom's levelAnalyze / Evaluate
LessonG5.U1.C5

Duration: 40 minutes

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The gap between the C1 from-memory sketch and the completed personal Wheel drawing → the arc of learning as something that can be seen, held, and reflected on; and the Wheel as a complete symbolic system now understood from the inside

Cognitive Principle Featured: Metacognitive comparison — when students hold two versions of their own work separated by time and instruction, they can observe the change in their understanding directly rather than being told about it. The before/after comparison externalises learning and makes it visible, converting an abstract process into a concrete object. The three-part written reflection (first imagination → what happened → what I know now) then converts that visible arc into transferable insight.

Bloom's Level: Analyze / Evaluate — students analyze their own understanding by comparing two artefacts; they evaluate what their finished drawing shows and what it still cannot. The three-part reflection asks them to assess not only what they now know but what the process of learning from a symbol reveals about how symbols work.

Prior Class Summary: C4 was the drawing session — students began and substantially developed their personal Wheel of Life drawings, applying the three-class framework (Yama / the hub / the six realms) to produce a complete rendering. They hold the question: "What did drawing reveal that I didn't expect?"

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the three-part reflection format (first imagination → what happened → what I know now) and that it applies to any learning that moves from encounter to understanding.

Students will UNDERSTAND that a great symbol is not a picture that stands for something — it is a complete teaching that holds more than can be said in words; and that their own drawing is now a symbol in this sense, encoding their understanding of the entire system.

Students will BE ABLE TO complete their personal Wheel of Life drawing with all required elements present, write a three-part reflection that moves from the C1 sketch to the finished drawing to a genuine claim about how symbols work, and name one thing their drawing shows clearly and one thing it reveals they still don't fully grasp.

Students will FEEL the satisfaction — tinged with slight awe — of having produced something that contains a complete cosmological teaching, and the honest recognition that understanding is always in-progress: the finished drawing is not the end of learning about the Wheel, but a waypoint.

Materials Checklist

  • Each student's in-progress Wheel drawing from C4 — collected by the teacher at the end of C4, now ready to be returned
  • Each student's C1 from-memory sketch — kept in the labeled folder since C1, now ready to be returned alongside the finished drawing
  • These two artefacts — the C1 sketch and the C4/C5 drawing — must both be ready to hand back at the start of the PKA. The comparison is the heart of this class. If the C1 sketches have been misplaced, the teacher should note in Your Reflection what was lost and how the comparison was approximated (e.g., students recall in writing what they drew in C1, rather than holding the physical sketch).
  • Pencils and coloured pencils or markers — for finishing the drawings
  • Lined paper or journal pages — one per student — for the three-part written reflection
  • Plan B (if paper is unavailable): Students write their reflection on the back of the C1 sketch or on an unused section of their drawing sheet.
  • Chalk or whiteboard markers — for the three-part reflection prompts written on the board before class
  • The Wheel of Life image — still displayed (this is the final class of Unit 1; it can be moved after today)

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 5 prayer set, led by the student leader for this class:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

(Food Offering Prayer at closing only.)

Dechen Monlam — by now, four classes in, the class has been chanting this prayer for the entirety of Unit 1. The teacher listens today rather than leading. Let the student leader carry it, and let whatever the class has learned of the prayer come through on its own. After: one quiet moment before moving to the PKA.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Return both artefacts simultaneously: the in-progress Wheel drawing from C4, and the C1 from-memory sketch. Students receive both in the same moment.

"Hold both. Look at them together."

One full minute of silent looking. Do not speak. Let students look.

After the minute:

"What changed?"

Take two or three responses. Do not evaluate or elaborate — just receive. Students will name obvious things (more elements, more labeling, more structure) and sometimes surprising things (the first sketch caught something emotionally that the finished drawing lost; or the first sketch was more confident even if less accurate). Both observations are valid and worth noting.

"You drew the first sketch after two minutes of looking at the Wheel. You drew the second after three classes of studying it. What is the distance between them — not just in detail, but in understanding?"

One or two more responses, briefly. Then: "That distance is what this unit has done. Today we name it — and we use it."

Fallback (if C1 sketches are unavailable): Ask students to close their eyes and recall what they drew in C1 — what was in the sketch, what wasn't, how it was organised. Write two or three things they remember about their C1 sketch on the back of their finished drawing. Then proceed. The written comparison is less vivid than holding both physical artefacts, but the reflective move is the same.

Mini Lesson — 5–8 minutes

Brief. The teacher writes the three-part reflection structure on the board before class. Students should see it clearly from their seats.

"In Grade 3, after the Tsethar — after you released the fish into the river — we used a reflection format. Some of you may remember it. Three parts:"

Point to the board:

1. What I first imagined / what I saw before (Based on your C1 sketch — what did you think the Wheel was? What did two minutes give you? What were you working with?)

2. What happened (What did you actually encounter over the course of this unit? What surprised you? What was harder or different than you expected? What specific moment or teaching changed your understanding?)

3. What I know now — and what it shows me about how symbols work (What does your finished drawing contain that your first sketch couldn't? What does the process of going from sketch to drawing reveal about what a great symbol does — what it holds, how it teaches?)

"This format works for any learning that moves from encounter to understanding. We used it in Grade 3 for a practice. We're using it now for a symbol. In both cases, the same structure: before, during, after. The 'after' is not the end — it is a waypoint. You are not finished with the Wheel of Life. Unit 2 goes deeper. But today you mark where you are."

Vocabulary Moment:

"The word symbol — revisited, but at a higher level than before. In Grade 4 you learned that objects can encode teachings — a bell holds a teaching about impermanence, about wisdom, about the nature of sound. A symbol, we said, is a picture that stands for something.

"Today: a great symbol is more than that. A great symbol is a complete teaching that holds more than can be said in words. The Wheel of Life cannot be fully translated into sentences — the image does something that language cannot. It shows the whole system at once, in one glance. It presents six realms and the center and the figure outside and the figure holding it — all simultaneously, in relationship to each other.

"Your drawing is now a symbol in this sense. It holds your understanding of the whole system. Someone who knows the Wheel could look at your drawing and read it. That is not a small thing."

Write symbol — a complete teaching held in image on the board.

Body Moment:

"Hold your finished drawing at arm's length. Both arms. Look at it as if you are seeing it for the first time — as a whole, not looking at any single part. What is the first thing your eye goes to?"

Ten seconds of holding and looking.

"That first pull of attention tells you something. The thing your eye goes to first is the thing that is most alive in your understanding — or the thing that is most unresolved. Note it."

Students put the drawing down. The body moment here is this specific quality of looking: held at arm's length, as a whole, without analysis — the way one stands back from a painting in a gallery. This mode of attention — panoramic, receptive, before interpretation — is itself a practice.

Emotional Hook:

The quiet, specific satisfaction of having made something that is actually and not-trivially complete. This is not the satisfaction of having finished a task — it is the satisfaction of having produced something that holds a teaching. Students who feel this are often surprised by it. The teacher does not need to name it or point to it. It is there.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: one thing your drawing shows that you genuinely understand — that you could explain to someone who had never seen the Wheel. And one thing your drawing reveals that you still don't fully grasp — something you drew but couldn't fully explain if asked."

Partners talk for two to three minutes. Teacher circulates and listens.

What to listen for: Students who can articulate the dual reading of the realms (cosmological and psychological) have understood the deepest conceptual layer of Unit 1. Students whose "one thing I don't fully grasp" names the outer rim of twelve nidanas — the chain of dependent origination — are right on track; that layer has been noted but not taught (it belongs to Grades 7–8). Students whose gap is the inner ring of light and dark may need a brief clarifying prompt before the activity begins.

Activity — 20 minutes

Two phases — the teacher manages the transition between them explicitly.

Phase 1 — Finish the drawing (10 minutes):

"First: finish your drawing. Look at the checklist on the board — it is still there from C4. Add anything that is missing. Refine any labels that are unclear. If you have all six realms, the hub, the inner ring, Yama, and the Buddha outside — you are complete. Use this time."

Teacher circulates during Phase 1 with targeted prompts based on the per-student notes made between C4 and C5:

  • To a student missing the inner ring: "The light half and the dark half — can you find it in your drawing? It's the ring between the hub and the six realms. What does it represent? Beings doing virtuous deeds rising, beings doing non-virtuous deeds falling. Add it."
  • To a student whose Buddha is not clearly outside the Wheel: "Is your Buddha inside or outside Yama's grip? In the image — look — there is clear space. Can you show that in your drawing?"
  • To a student who is done and waiting: "Pick one realm. Write inside it — in tiny letters — the ruling klesha of that realm. Not the realm's name. The specific poison that keeps beings there."

Phase 2 — Three-part written reflection (10 minutes):

Teacher announces: "Drawing down. Reflection now. You have ten minutes."

Students write the three-part reflection on their lined paper. The prompts remain visible on the board. Teacher circulates, reading over shoulders and offering occasional questions — not answers:

  • "What specific moment in this unit surprised you? Name that moment in Part 2."
  • "Your Part 3 says the Wheel 'teaches a lot.' What specifically does a symbol like the Wheel do that a list of facts cannot? What does the image hold that words can't?"
  • "In Part 3 — what does this process show you about how your own mind turns? You've been studying the Wheel of Life. What has the Wheel shown you about your own wheel?"

That last question is the deepest available prompt. Offer it to students who have written a strong Part 3 and have time remaining. It is not required.

Debrief — 5 minutes

Transfer question — spoken by the teacher, held by the class:

"The Wheel of Life shows beings turning through six realms, driven by three poisons, held by the figure of impermanence. The Buddha stands outside — the only one who is not inside the Wheel, not held by Yama. He is pointing.

"We have asked this question before, and we are going to ask it again now: what is he pointing at?"

Take responses — do not rush to affirm or correct. Hold the range of answers.

"'Liberation.' 'The moon.' 'A path.' 'The way out.' These are all versions of the same thing. He is pointing to the possibility that the turning stops — that the engine at the center runs down, that the poisons no longer drive, that a being stands outside the Wheel rather than inside it.

"You have now studied what the Wheel is. Unit 2 takes us deeper into each realm — not as a map on the wall, but as a mirror. You will meet yourself in some of these realms in ways that may be uncomfortable. The hungry ghost realm in particular. The question Unit 2 asks is not 'what is that realm?' — you know that. The question is: 'what does it feel like from inside? And what moves a being out?'"

Pause.

"The Wheel is not a picture. It is a teaching. And now you are beginning to read it."

Closing — 3 minutes

Before prayers: Collect finished drawings and reflections — these are the Unit 1 portfolio artefacts. If drawings will be displayed in the classroom or shared with families, let students know.

Full prayer set, student leader:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

Teacher's closing line — Unit 1 closed, Unit 2 named:

"Unit 1 is complete. You have seen the Wheel, you have studied its center, you have surveyed its realms, and you have drawn it from understanding. Unit 2 asks you to enter it — one realm at a time. The Wheel was at the entrance of the temple. Now we go through the door."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author):

Carry forward: The finished drawings and three-part reflections are the portfolio artefact for Unit 1 — the primary evidence of learning for this unit in the Grade 5 portfolio. File them carefully. If drawings are to be displayed, ensure students have consented; some three-part reflections may be personal enough that students prefer them not to be displayed. Offer the choice. Unit 2 begins with the six realms examined at depth, one by one, over six classes. The session on the hungry ghost realm (U2.C2) is among the most psychologically demanding of Grade 5 — it goes deep into the experience of insatiable craving, and for some students this will connect to real experiences of desire, compulsion, or scarcity in their own lives. The teacher should review the Your Reflection notes from C2 and C3 (particularly notes about students who engaged with the "which realm do you visit most?" question with unusual personal intensity) before teaching U2.C2. The goal is insight — not exposure or catharsis. A student who is struggling with the hungry ghost quality in their actual life needs a wise response, not a lesson. Spiral note: the three-part reflection format (first imagination / what happened / what I know now) was introduced in Grade 3, Unit 1, Class 6 — after the Tsethar, when students had released fish and were asked to reflect on the arc of that practice. Its reappearance here should be named explicitly to students ("This format appeared in Grade 3 after the Tsethar. It works for any practice — and engaging with a great symbol is a practice."). Students who remember it from Grade 3 will feel the spiral; students who don't will receive it fresh. Both are fine. This moment of naming the spiral is itself a teaching: you are learning to learn from experience. Before, during, after. You are becoming fluent in this.
