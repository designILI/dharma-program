The Four Sights

An old man, a sick person, a dead body, and a holy seeker — seen through palace gates → the moment seeing becomes impossible to unsee; the beginning of honest knowledge

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleEmotional encoding
Bloom's levelUnderstand
LessonG2.U2.C2

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): An old man, a sick person, a dead body, and a holy seeker — seen through palace gates → the moment seeing becomes impossible to unsee; the beginning of honest knowledge

Cognitive Principle Featured: Emotional encoding — information received in a state of genuine feeling is retained more fully and longer. Each sight is given dedicated time to land before the next is introduced. The sequence builds like a wave: each sight deepens the one before it. The teacher's own steadiness during the difficult sights is the signal that this is safe to hear.

Bloom's Level: Understand

Prior Class Summary: In C1, students met Siddhartha inside his palace — comfortable, protected, separated from the world. They drew the palace and imagined what lay beyond its walls. The class closed with unresolved tension: was the prince actually happy?

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the four sights — an old man, a sick person, a dead body, a holy seeker — and that each one shook Siddhartha in a different way; that Channa was the servant who accompanied him; that the four sights happened outside the palace gates.

Students will UNDERSTAND that seeing suffering clearly and honestly, for the first time, can be the beginning of wisdom — not its opposite.

Students will BE ABLE TO name the four sights and say, in one sentence each, what each one meant to Siddhartha.

Students will FEEL the weight of encountering something real that cannot be undone — the specific seriousness of four sights witnessed without a protective layer between the seeing and the seen.

Materials Checklist

  • Stories of the Buddha / illustrated anchor text (mark the four sights passage)
  • Class timeline (posted from C1 — "Palace, gates closed")
  • Four large sheets of paper, one per sight, OR chart paper divided into four quadrants
  • Markers
  • Plan B: If wall space is limited, use a single chart paper in four sections rather than four separate sheets. The content is identical; only the display format changes.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The rotating student leader leads the full Grade 2 prayer set:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa

Teacher present, unhurried, modelling full attention.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

"Before we go forward — let's go back. What do you remember about Siddhartha from last time? Don't start at the beginning. Start with what's most in your mind right now."

Take three or four responses without correcting or completing. Then:

"What had the king decided? What was his plan for his son?"

"And last class, some of you wondered: was Siddhartha really happy? Today we're going to find out what he saw — and why, after this, he couldn't stay inside anymore."

Fallback: "Turn to your neighbour. Thirty seconds — tell them: who is Siddhartha, and what was his life like?"

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

"One day — Siddhartha was a young man now, married, with a baby son — he asked his servant Channa to take him outside the palace. Maybe he was restless. Maybe something inside him needed to see the world. And Channa agreed."

"They rode out through the gates. And almost immediately — Siddhartha saw something he had never seen before."

[FIRST SIGHT — OLD AGE]

"A very old man. Bent over, his back curved, his skin wrinkled, his hands trembling, walking with a stick. Siddhartha asked Channa: what happened to this person? And Channa said: he got old. That happens to everyone. One day — it will happen to you."

[Pause. Do not move on immediately. Hold the silence for five full seconds.]

"Siddhartha had never known this. He looked at his own young hands and he thought: this will happen to me."

[SECOND SIGHT — SICKNESS]

"They rode on. And they saw a person lying in the road. Sick. Very sick — in pain, unable to move properly, suffering. Siddhartha asked: what happened to him? Channa said: he got ill. Sickness comes to all people — no one is safe from it."

[Pause again. Five seconds.]

"Siddhartha had never been sick. He had never seen anyone suffer like that. And he thought: this can happen to me. This can happen to anyone I love."

[THIRD SIGHT — DEATH]

"Then they saw something that stopped them completely. A group of people carrying a body. Someone had died. Siddhartha asked: what is this? And Channa said: he died. Everyone dies. One day everyone you know will die. One day you will die."

[Pause. This one is longer — ten seconds of silence at minimum. Do not fill it.]

"Let that be what it is. Siddhartha was shaking. His whole life, he had been protected from knowing this — and now he couldn't unknow it."

[FOURTH SIGHT — THE HOLY SEEKER]

"And then — on that same road — a man in simple robes, walking calmly. Not sick, not frightened, not in pain. Something peaceful about him. Siddhartha asked: who is this? Channa said: a holy seeker. Someone who has chosen to leave ordinary life and look for the truth about suffering."

"And something happened inside Siddhartha when he saw that man. The first three sights had opened something — a door — and this fourth sight showed him what might be on the other side."

Vocabulary Moment: "These are called the Four Sights. Not four ideas — four things he actually saw, with his own eyes. And they changed him. Here's a question: why do you think seeing something is different from just being told about it?" [Take one or two brief responses. Don't overextend — keep the narrative momentum.]

Body Moment: "Stand up for a moment. Close your eyes. Imagine you have never seen someone who is old. Your whole life — everyone around you is young and healthy. Now — you see an old person for the very first time. What happens in your body? Where do you feel it?" [Five seconds. Eyes open. Sit down.] "That's a small piece of what Siddhartha felt."

Emotional Hook: Seven-year-olds sometimes think they know what death is because they have heard the word. But there is a difference between the word and the seeing. Siddhartha had been protected from even the word. When that protection lifted, his entire world shifted. This is what the four sights are — not information, but encounter.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Prompt: "Has anything ever surprised you by being different from what you expected — or shown you something you couldn't un-know? It doesn't have to be something sad. Talk to your neighbour."

Structure: Two minutes of partner talk, then two or three responses shared with the class. What to listen for: first encounters — a grandparent's death, a sick animal, discovering something true about the world that changed it. Receive all of these. Do not redirect toward the "right" answer. These are real experiences and they all belong.

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Four-corners activity — adding to the class timeline.

"We're going to add the four sights to our unit timeline. Each sight gets its own space."

Divide the class into four groups. Each group receives one of the four sight cards (numbered 1–4, with the sight named). Each group's task:

  1. Draw what Siddhartha saw
  2. Write or draw what he felt
  3. Write the question that sight put inside him

[Ten to twelve minutes. Teacher circulates — asks: "What did Siddhartha feel when he saw this?" and "What question did this sight ask?"]

Groups share briefly. Teacher adds to the class timeline:

Palace [gates closed] → Old man → Sick person → Death → Holy seeker

Extension: "If you finish early: what do you think Siddhartha did when he got back to the palace that night? What would you do?"

Debrief — 5 minutes

"Here's what I want to sit with: the king built the palace to protect Siddhartha from exactly what he saw today. The plan didn't work. But — was there something the king was right about? Was there something he missed? Was seeing those four sights bad for Siddhartha — or did he need to see them?"

Take three or four responses. Do not close the question. Let the complexity stand.

Closing — 3 minutes

The rotating student leader leads the full Grade 2 prayer set:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa

Teacher sentence, specific to today: "Today Siddhartha saw four things that changed him forever. Next time, we'll find out what he decided to do."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author):

Carry forward: The four sights carry the emotional weight that makes the departure in C3 comprehensible. If students do not feel the weight of what Siddhartha encountered, the departure will seem impulsive rather than inevitable. Give each sight real time — slow down especially at the third sight (death). This is not the place for softening. Seven-year-olds can hold mortality honestly; what they need is not a gentler story but a teacher who is calm while telling it. The teacher's own steadiness is the signal that this is safe to hear. — If a student discloses a personal loss in response to the third sight, receive it briefly and warmly: "Thank you for sharing that. That's real." Continue without turning the class into a grief processing session. Follow up privately if needed. — Spiral connections: Grade 1 U2 (impermanence — leaves and seasons introduced the concept of things changing and ending). Some students may make this connection spontaneously; welcome it. Grade 4 will return to the four sights analytically as part of the Four Noble Truths. — Add to the class timeline today. Keep it posted.
