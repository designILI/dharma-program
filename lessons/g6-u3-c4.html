Our Taras

A drawing of one specific person → the recognition that swift, refusing-to-abandon compassion exists in the student's own life

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principlePersonalisation and transfer
Bloom's levelAnalyze / early Create
LessonG6.U3.C4

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): A drawing of one specific person → the recognition that swift, refusing-to-abandon compassion exists in the student's own life

Cognitive Principle Featured: Personalisation and transfer — when students name an abstract quality (swift compassion) through a specific person they know, the concept becomes durable and usable. The drawing externalises the recognition, making it shareable and held.

Bloom's Level: Analyze / early Create — students connect Tara's defining qualities to a real or imagined figure in their own lives, construct an argument for the connection, and present it to the class.

Prior Class Summary: C3 introduced the Dolma prayer — meaning first, then sound. The prayer joined the standing prayer set. Students copied the key lines with plain-language meanings and practised chanting in pairs. They carry the prayer newly in their bodies and the warmth of a complete unit arriving at its culmination.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the vocabulary of the unit — Drolma, archetype, swift compassion, ordination, vow, threshold — and can use these terms to explain the connection between their personal Tara figure and the tradition. Students will UNDERSTAND that Tara's quality of swift, refusing-to-abandon compassion is not confined to the tradition — it is alive in human lives, including their own. Students will BE ABLE TO draw their personal Tara figure and articulate the connection in the share circle. Students will FEEL the specific warmth of naming someone who has not left them when things were hard — and the recognition that this is a form of gratitude.

Materials Checklist

  • Drawing paper (larger format if possible — A3 or equivalent)
  • Coloring materials: pencils, pens, watercolor or marker if available
  • Portfolio notebooks for vocabulary reference
  • Board space to write the vocabulary synthesis list (Drolma, archetype, swift compassion, ordination, vow, threshold)
  • Plan B: If the share circle proves too exposing for some students — particularly if the figure drawn is a family member during a difficult time — adjust the share format: students can describe the figure without naming them, or can say "this figure is someone I know" without specifying who. The sharing is important; the disclosure is optional.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set including Dolma: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Food Offering Prayer (closing only)

Student leader leads. The Dolma is chanted with particular care today — slower, fuller than in C3. The teacher sets this tone before the prayer set begins: "This is the third time we chant the Dolma together in this class. Each time, it should feel a little more like yours."

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

Teacher: "We've studied Tara — the figure who stands at the threshold and says: I will not leave you there. And we've studied Pajapati — the historical woman who embodied that quality through barefoot walking and refusing to stop. Today: who is your Tara? Not the bodhisattva, not the historical figure — someone in your own life. Who embodies that quality of swift, active, refusing-to-abandon compassion for you?"

Take three or four responses — brief, impressionistic. These are not the final answers; they are the activation. The full response comes in the drawing.

If no responses come: "Think of a time when you were in difficulty — not to revisit it, just to remember it. Who, in that moment, did not leave? Who moved toward you rather than away?"

Mini Lesson — 5 minutes

Brief — this class is primarily making and sharing. The mini lesson frames the assessment.

Teacher: "Your drawing today is of one figure who embodies Tara's quality in your own life. It can be a real person — living or dead. It can be an imagined figure. It can be yourself as you aspire to be. What matters is: this figure does not wait, does not abandon, moves toward difficulty rather than away from it. Draw that. Give them the dignity of a full drawing."

Vocabulary Moment: No new vocabulary today. Synthesis. Write on the board: Drolma · archetype · swift compassion · ordination · vow · threshold. "When you speak in the share circle, use as many of these words as feel true. They are your vocabulary now — use them."

Body Moment: Green Tara posture — one foot slightly extended, one hand in the boon-granting mudra, ready. "Take the Green Tara posture. Be your Tara for ten seconds. Feel what that posture asks of you." Hold. Teacher holds it with the class. Release. "Now draw from what that felt like."

Emotional Hook: The act of drawing someone who has not left — who has moved toward you rather than away — is not neutral. For some students, this figure will be obvious and joyful. For others, naming a person who has been that kind of present may surface something more complex — gratitude mixed with difficulty. The teacher circulates with this awareness. No one is required to explain their drawing before they choose to.

Activity — 25 minutes

Phase 1 — Drawing (15 minutes):

Students draw their Tara figure with care and detail. This is a portfolio artefact — it should reflect the full unit. Teacher circulates: "Tell me about this figure. What do they do when things are hard? When did you see them be this?" The teacher's questions are gentle and specific — they help the student think about what to include in the drawing.

Students may add a title or a phrase from the unit vocabulary. They may draw the figure in a posture reminiscent of Tara, or in an ordinary posture. What the figure does is more important than what they look like.

Phase 2 — Share circle (10 minutes):

Students sit in a circle with their drawings. The teacher models the share format once:

"This is my Tara. Their name is ___. They embody swift compassion by ___. They are like Tara because ___."

Each student in turn holds up their drawing and speaks these three sentences. No one is required to go — but in practice, most will. The teacher receives each sharing fully: brief acknowledgment, no evaluation. "Thank you." Then: the next student.

If the circle runs long, do not cut sharings short — adjust the debrief rather than the sharing. The sharings are the assessment.

Debrief — 3 minutes

Teacher: "You have now drawn the archetype, studied the history, learned the prayer, and named your personal Tara. What does it mean to have a Tara in your life — someone who will not abandon you at the threshold? What does it cost them? What does it give you?"

Do not require answers — let the questions rest. Then: "Next unit: Grade 6 ends with the body. After everything you've read and drawn and discussed — death, the Bardo, Tara, Pajapati — you get to move. Cham Dance. The figures you've been studying all year will be worn, danced, embodied. Come ready to use your whole body."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set — including Dolma. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer at close.

Teacher's closing line: "Unit 3 is complete. Tara is in the prayers. Pajapati is in the portfolio. Your Tara is drawn. The year turns now toward its final form."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The "Our Taras" drawing is the Unit 3 portfolio artefact. Together with the Unit 1 philosophical reflection on rebirth, the Unit 2 Bardo concept map, and the Unit 3 C2 Pajapati journal entry, these are the four Grade 6 portfolio pieces accumulated so far. The Cham mask from Unit 4 will be the fifth. Unit 4: Cham Dance — the year ends with embodied performance, not with words. The thematic thread: Grade 6 has moved from philosophy (Unit 1) to territory (Unit 2) to archetype and person (Unit 3) to embodied enactment (Unit 4). The transition should feel like relief after the weight of the Bardo and the philosophical discussions — students get to move. Arrange physical space before Unit 4 C3 (movement class); alert the facilities team now. Confirm with the resident teacher whether the school wishes to invite an audience to the C5 performance — this decision is best made before C1 of Unit 4. Spiral connections: the bodhisattva vow concept (Grade 5) is alive in the "Our Taras" drawing — the figure drawn embodies the vow. The personal Tara figure connects forward to Grade 7's Medicine Buddha unit, where the question of who heals and protects becomes central again.
