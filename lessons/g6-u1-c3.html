Who Am I?

The direct attempt to locate "the one who is noticing" → the philosophical question of personal identity — what the self actually is, and what that means for what survives death

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleSocratic inquiry as cognitive scaffold
Bloom's levelAnalyze
LessonG6.U1.C3

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The direct attempt to locate "the one who is noticing" → the philosophical question of personal identity — what the self actually is, and what that means for what survives death

Cognitive Principle Featured: Socratic inquiry as cognitive scaffold — the teacher presents three competing frameworks and then genuinely steps back, asking students to evaluate which is most compelling rather than most comfortable. The distinction between "compelling" and "comfortable" does the philosophical work: it asks students to apply evidential reasoning to a personal question rather than retreating to familiarity.

Bloom's Level: Analyze — students compare three frameworks for what the self is, identify the strongest and weakest arguments in each, and construct their own position with supporting reasoning. Genuine philosophical disagreement between students is welcomed and held as a sign that the inquiry is working.

Prior Class Summary: C2 taught anattā — no fixed self, the self as a collection of constantly changing processes. Students connected this to the rebirth teaching: what survives is the momentum of the stream, not a fixed "you." They carry the river analogy and the question "what makes it the same river?" as a tool for thinking about identity across time.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW three frameworks for understanding what the self is: the Western folk view (a continuous experiencing subject), the materialist view (consciousness as entirely brain-dependent), and the Buddhist view (the self as a conventionally useful label for a stream of interdependent processes).

Students will UNDERSTAND that these three views have different implications for the question of what survives death — and that the Buddhist view is not the only philosophically serious position on the table.

Students will BE ABLE TO identify which of the three frameworks they find most compelling, articulate why, and name the strongest objection to their own position.

Students will FEEL the genuine vertigo of trying to locate the observer — the noticing awareness — and finding that looking for it directly is harder than expected. Not frightening: philosophically alive.

Materials Checklist

  • Writing journals, one per student
  • Pens or pencils
  • Board or large paper for listing the three frameworks (students should be able to see them throughout the class)
  • The full Grade 6 prayer cards or sheets (Units 1–2 set — no Dolma yet)
  • Student leader list

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set (Units 1–2 set): Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Note: The Food Offering Prayer appears in closing only. The Dolma (Tara Prayer) is not yet introduced.

Student leader leads. The teacher lets the room hold the quiet at the end of the final prayer before speaking.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "We've been asking: what survives death? But before we can answer that, we have to answer something harder. What am I, right now? Let me ask it directly: who are you? Not your name — I know your name. Not your role, your family, your likes and dislikes. What is the 'you' that has been here all along, underneath all of that? Take sixty seconds — just think. Don't answer yet."

Allow sixty full seconds of genuine silence. This is unusual. Let it be unusual.

Then: "If you want to, share it."

Take 3–4 responses. Receive each fully. Do not affirm or correct. If a student says "I don't know," receive it: "That is a serious answer. The people who have thought hardest about this question often land there first." If a student gives a confident answer — "I am my soul" or "I am my thoughts" — receive it: "Good. Hold that — we're going to test it."

Fallback if students are reluctant: "Write it down first. Finish this sentence: 'The thing that has been here all along, underneath everything, is ___.' Then share if you want."

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Teacher writes three headings on the board and presents each framework. The goal is to present each fairly — not to stack the deck for the Buddhist view.

Framework 1 — The Western folk view:

"This is the view most people hold, often without examining it. There is a continuous, experiencing self — sometimes called a mind, sometimes a soul — that persists through time and is the subject of all your experience. When you say 'I remember my fifth birthday,' there is something that actually remembers — a continuous 'you' that was there then and is here now. On this view, the question of what happens after death is: does this continuous subject go on, or does it end? Different religious traditions answer differently. But the view of the continuous self is common to most of them."

Framework 2 — The materialist view:

"The materialist view says: the self is entirely a product of the brain. There is no 'experience' or 'awareness' separate from the physical processes of the nervous system. When the brain is active, there is consciousness. When the brain stops, consciousness ends — the way a fire ends when the fuel runs out. On this view, there is nothing to survive death, because there was never anything separate from the body to survive it. This is the view of most contemporary neuroscientists."

Framework 3 — The Buddhist view:

"The Buddhist view says: the self is a conventionally useful label for a stream of interdependent processes — body, sensation, perception, mental formation, consciousness. It is real in the way that a river or a flame is real — processually, not substantially. The stream continues; the 'self' as a fixed thing does not. What moves through the Bardo — what moves from one life to the next — is not a passenger but a momentum. A pattern. A tendency."

Teacher then: "All three of these views are held by serious people — philosophers, scientists, meditators, ordinary people doing their best to think clearly. I have presented each one as well as I can. Now I want to know what you think. Not which is most comfortable — which is most compelling? Which is hardest to argue against?"

Body Moment: "Close your eyes. Notice: there is something noticing. There is an awareness that is aware of your breath, of the sounds in the room, of your own thoughts arising and passing. What is that noticing thing? Can you find it — can you locate it? Try." Allow thirty seconds of genuine investigation. Then: "Open your eyes. What did you find? Could you find the thing that was doing the noticing — or only more noticing?"

Vocabulary Moment: "Substrate — what something is made of or rests on. The materialist view says consciousness is substrate-dependent: it depends entirely on the brain as its substrate. Remove the substrate, and consciousness ceases. The Buddhist view says consciousness is processual — and under certain conditions, it can continue without a fixed material substrate, the way a flame can move from candle to candle. This is precisely the disagreement between the second and third frameworks."

Emotional Hook: The moment — specific and recognisable — when looking for the "I" directly reveals that it is harder to locate than expected. Not because it isn't there, but because whatever is doing the looking seems to retreat the moment it is looked for. Teacher names this without elaboration: "If you had that experience just now — the sense that looking for the looker is strangely elusive — that is one of the most important things you can notice in meditation. It is exactly what the anattā teaching is pointing at."

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: which of the three frameworks — folk view, materialist, Buddhist — do you find most compelling? Not most comfortable — most compelling. Why? And what is the strongest objection to the view you chose?"

Structure: each partner states a position and a reason, then names one objection. Teacher circulates — listening for genuine reasoning rather than preference. What to listen for: students who are genuinely wrestling with the frameworks versus students who are choosing based on familiarity. If a student says "the Buddhist one because we're in Buddhist school" — gently challenge: "That's a reason for hearing it sympathetically. Is it a reason for finding it true? What is the strongest argument for it?"

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Journal entry 3. Teacher reads the prompt aloud:

"My current view of what 'I' am — and what I think that means for the question of what survives death."

"This is a genuine philosophical exercise. You do not have to agree with the Buddhist view. You do not have to agree with any of the three. If none of them fully satisfies you, say so — and say what is missing. If you hold a combination, construct it carefully. The standard is: can you give a reason for your position? If yes — write it."

Teacher circulates with philosophical engagement: "What's the strongest argument against the position you just wrote? Write that down too." Not to destabilise — to strengthen. A position that has grappled with its best objection is more philosophically serious than one that hasn't.

Scaffold for students who are stuck: "Start with: 'I find the ___ view most compelling because...' and then name one thing about it that you're not sure about." Partial certainty with named uncertainty is better than false confidence.

Extension for students who finish early: "Write a fourth framework — one that isn't folk view, materialist, or Buddhist. What would it include? What problem does it solve that the other three don't?" This is genuinely difficult and rewards creative philosophical thinking.

Debrief — 5 minutes

Opening question: "Next class we complete the unit's culminating reflection. Before then — hold the question. Not 'what does Buddhism say?' but what do you think? The question is genuinely open, and your thinking matters. What is one thing you are more certain about than you were at the start of the unit — and one thing you are less certain about?"

Take 2–3 responses on each. Do not resolve.

"Good. That combination — more certain about some things, less certain about others — is what genuine philosophical inquiry produces. Come to next class ready to write."

Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full closing prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Teacher's closing line: "You tried to find the thing that was looking — and that is not a small thing to try."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: This is the most philosophically open class in Unit 1 — it should feel like genuine inquiry, not a lecture with a predetermined landing point. If students are genuinely disagreeing with each other or with the Buddhist position, this is the unit working correctly. Disagreement is not a problem to manage; it is the philosophical process functioning as designed. The materialist view deserves a genuinely fair hearing — not a strawman. Many students will find it compelling, and this is intellectually honest given their age and cultural exposure. Receive materialist positions with the same seriousness as Buddhist ones. The goal is not Buddhist conclusion but rigorous process. C4 is the culminating reflection class — students should arrive there having genuinely wrestled with the question over three classes, not having received a doctrinal answer. The "what did you find?" question from the Body Moment is among the most important questions in the unit — students who genuinely tried to locate the looker and couldn't will have had a micro-experience of what the anattā teaching points at. This is worth returning to in C4. Spiral note: the three frameworks map forward to Grade 8, where the Twelve Links of Dependent Origination will be examined as the Buddhist alternative to both folk-soul and strict materialism.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child compared three philosophical frameworks for what the 'self' is — a Western folk view (a continuous experiencing subject), a materialist view (consciousness as entirely brain-dependent), and the Buddhist view (the self as a stream of changing processes rather than a fixed thing). They were asked which they find most compelling, and why. You can ask them: 'Which of the three views did you find most convincing — and what's the strongest argument against it?' or 'Did you try the exercise of looking for the one who is noticing? What did you find?'"
