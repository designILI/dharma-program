What We Honour

LessonG7.U4.C1

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Panglhabsol and the concept of lineage → the vertiginous recognition that what one has received came through people, and those people through others, reaching back through the tradition to its origin

Cognitive Principle Featured: Perspective-taking across time — the lineage map activity asks students to step outside their own position in time and see themselves as a point in a long chain; this spatial/temporal perspective shift is cognitively demanding and emotionally significant at twelve and thirteen

Bloom's Level: Evaluate — students evaluate what it means to honour, identify their own position within a lineage of transmission, and assess what they have received and through whom

Prior Class Summary: C4 of Unit 3 was the sharing circle — students read their healing aspirations aloud and the class dedicated the merit after each reading with the Ngowa. The room held many people through the collective chanting. Students carry the intimacy of that shared practice and the closing line: "That you gave it fully — that you can know."

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW what Panglhabsol is — a Tibetan Buddhist ceremony performed in the Himalayan region honouring the mountain protector Kangchenjunga and the Dharma Protectors, involving offerings, Cham Dance, and the Sang (smoke) offering.

Students will UNDERSTAND that the concept of lineage (brgyud pa) — the unbroken transmission of teaching from teacher to student across generations — is central to Tibetan Buddhist practice, and that Panglhabsol is in part an act of gratitude for that transmission.

Students will BE ABLE TO construct a personal lineage map: their name at the centre, the people who have taught them fanning outward, and (where they can reach) the people who taught those people.

Students will FEEL the vertiginous recognition of lineage — the sense of standing in the middle of something long, something that did not begin with them and will not end with them.

Materials Checklist

  • Student portfolios
  • Writing instruments
  • Large blank paper or an extra page for the lineage map (the map may be too large for a standard portfolio page — provide larger paper if available)
  • Whiteboard for noting the PKA responses
  • Image of Kangchenjunga (the mountain) if available — even a simple photograph carries the weight of the physical presence being honoured
  • Plan B (lineage map paper): If larger paper is not available, students draw the map across two facing portfolio pages, with their name at the fold.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 7 prayer set, student leader.

Prayer sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod

The Rewo Sang Chod is given particular attention today — the student leader is asked to lead it clearly, as it will be the featured practice of the whole unit.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 6 minutes

Teacher: "Think of something in your life that you genuinely honour — not just respect or appreciate, but something whose absence would diminish you. Something that holds you. What is it?"

Take 3–4 responses. Students may name a person, a place, a practice, a piece of music, a relationship, a way of being. All are valid. Write the responses on the board in whatever categories arise.

"Notice: what you named — it usually came through someone or something. It was given to you, or you grew into it because of conditions that were provided by others. In the Tibetan Buddhist tradition, there are ceremonies specifically designed to honour that reality — to formally acknowledge what has been given, what stands behind us, what makes what we are possible. Panglhabsol is one of them."

Mini Lesson — 12 minutes

Panglhabsol — what it is, when it is performed, what it honours.

"Panglhabsol — གངས་ལྷ་བསོལ — is a Tibetan Buddhist ceremony performed annually in the Himalayan region, especially in Sikkim. Its name refers to the appeasing and honouring of the mountain deity: gang (snow mountain), lha (deity), bsol (to appease, to honour)."

"The ceremony is held in honour of the mountain protector Kangchenjunga — in Tibetan, Gangs chen mdzod lnga, the Five Treasures of Great Snow. This mountain — visible from the Himalayan region, one of the highest in the world — is not only a physical landmark. In Tibetan Buddhist understanding, it is an active presence: a Dharma Protector. Not a symbol of protection. A protector — a being with a function in the welfare of the teaching and the community."

"What Panglhabsol involves: offerings of food, incense, and smoke. The Cham Dance — the ceremonial masked dance you encountered in Grade 6. The Sang offering — the burning of juniper as an offering to the protectors and the lineage. Prayers and lineage invocations. The ceremony is not a memorial — it is a living conversation with the forces that hold the tradition."

"The lineage dimension: Panglhabsol is also an act of gratitude — to the teachers, the lineage holders, the figures who kept the Dharma alive and transmitted it across time. Every teacher who has taught this school, every teacher who taught those teachers, every rinpoche, geshe, monk, and laywoman who held the tradition through difficult periods — Panglhabsol honours all of them."

"You have spent this year becoming teachers. In Unit 2, you stood in front of younger students and transmitted the Dharma — something you had received, something you now gave. Panglhabsol is the ceremony that asks: and who gave it to you? And who gave it to them?"

Vocabulary Moment: Lineage — in Tibetan, brgyud pa (བརྒྱུད་པ). The unbroken transmission of teaching from teacher to student, going back to the origin of the teaching. Every practice in this program has a lineage — it came to the students through the teacher, who received it from someone, who received it from someone. The Dharma itself has a lineage: Shakyamuni Buddha → his disciples → the great masters → the founding teachers of Tibetan Buddhism → the lineage holders of each tradition → the rinpoches and teachers of this region → the teachers of this school → this classroom. Students are in that chain.

Body Moment: "Think of three people who have taught you something — not only in Dharma class, but in any domain of your life. Hold all three. Now: go one step back. Who taught them? Who made them capable of teaching you?" Students sit with the expanding chain for thirty seconds. "Now go back one more step. And one more." Brief silence. "You are in the middle of something long. That is what Panglhabsol is acknowledging."

Emotional Hook: The vertiginous recognition of lineage — that what one has received came through specific people, and those people through others, and the chain reaches back into history in a way that exceeds any individual's knowledge or gratitude. This is not anxiety. It is a particular kind of wonder.

Turn and Talk — 4 minutes

"Tell your partner: one person in your personal lineage of teaching — not the famous ones, not the historical figures. The real one. The person who actually transmitted something to you. What did they give you, and do they know they gave it?"

After 3 minutes, take one or two responses from the room. Receive them simply — not expanding, just acknowledging.

Activity — 11 minutes

The lineage map.

Students draw their lineage map on large paper or across two portfolio pages. Structure:

Their name at the centre. Fanning outward in all directions: the names of people who have taught them something — in Dharma, in school, in family, in any domain. Where they can fill it in: the names of the people who taught those people. Arrows or lines indicating the direction of transmission — who gave to whom.

This is not a research exercise. Students do not need to find historical information. They work from what they actually know — which teachers, which relatives, which older students or coaches or elders have given them something. The map will be partial; that is fine and should be said.

Teacher circulates: "Go specific. Not 'my Dharma teachers.' Which one? Who taught you this? Give them a name." The coaching is the same as Unit 3 C3: precision over sentiment.

Debrief — 4 minutes

"In Unit 2 you taught younger students — you were transmitters. In Unit 4, we are honouring the ones who transmitted to us — we are receivers, reflecting on what we received. You are in the middle. Both receiver and transmitter. That is the practitioner's position. That has always been the practitioner's position."

"Your lineage maps are the beginning of the Unit 4 reflection. Hold them. We'll return to them in C3."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set, student leader. Food Offering Prayer included.

Teacher's closing line: "Everyone who is named on your map — they are in this room, in some form. That is what lineage means."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author):

Carry forward: The lineage maps from C1 are the scaffold for C4's culminating reflection ("What I honour, and what I owe"). Students will return to them in C3 when they begin the reflection draft. Some students will have rich, specific maps — parents who are practitioners, grandparents who held the tradition through difficult years, older students who modelled something. Others will struggle to name their teachers by name, which is itself a useful observation: it can be hard to see clearly what has been given when it is very close. Both are valid starting points. Do not correct a sparse map; ask a question that opens it: "Who taught you to [something you have observed the student do well]?"

If the school's actual Panglhabsol ceremony falls during this unit, coordinate with school administration to bring Grade 7 students to participate or observe. This is significantly more powerful than the classroom version — the sight of the Cham Dance, the smell of the Sang offering, the assembled community. Confirm the calendar before Unit 4 begins.

C2 requires materials for the Sang offering — juniper branches (preferred), fragrant incense, or a candle (Plan B). Confirm availability before C2. If juniper or appropriate incense requires ordering or sourcing, do this before the unit begins, not between C1 and C2.

Spiral connections: Grade 6 Unit 4 Cham Dance (the body as vehicle for the Dharma — will be recalled explicitly in C3); Grade 4 Unit 2 torma offerings (making offerings with the hands — connected to the Sang offering's logic); Grade 7 Unit 2 Near-Peer Teaching Project (students as transmitters — now they reflect on being receivers).
