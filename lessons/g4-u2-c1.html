What Is a Torma?

A ritual sculpture made to be given away → non-attachment as an active, caring practice

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleConcrete-before-abstract anchoring
Bloom's levelUnderstand
LessonG4.U2.C1

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): A ritual sculpture made to be given away → non-attachment as an active, caring practice

Cognitive Principle Featured: Concrete-before-abstract anchoring — students encounter the physical form and the act of making before they are asked to think about what relinquishing something means. The image of the torma shape (pointed top, broad base) is used as a body-held anchor for a concept that would otherwise float.

Bloom's Level: Understand — students are meeting a new object and concept. The lesson does not ask them to apply or evaluate yet; it asks them to receive, place in context, and begin to hold the central question emotionally.

Prior Class Summary: C6 of Unit 1 was Demonstration Day — students narrated the seven-bowl offering to an audience from memory, embodying the concept of puja as attentive action. They carry the understanding that ritual objects encode teachings and the metaphor of transmission: what passes from maker or practitioner to receiver.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW what a torma is — its traditional composition (tsampa and butter, or clay as a teaching substitute), its basic form (cone with pointed top and broad base), its purpose as a ritual offering, and the fact that it is not kept. Students will UNDERSTAND that making something with care in order to give it away is a distinct practice — different from making something carelessly, and different from making something to keep. Students will BE ABLE TO sketch a personal torma design and articulate in one sentence what they would find hardest about making something and then releasing it. Students will FEEL the productive tension between investment and release — the honest recognition that the thought of giving away something you made with care is not comfortable, and that this discomfort is exactly where the teaching lives.

Materials Checklist

  • Images of tormas (a range: simple cone to elaborate multi-tiered; printout or screen display)
  • One real torma or demonstration model, OR a simple cone of dough shaped before class to show the basic form
  • Tsampa and butter for handling (small amount, for touch/smell introduction), OR a prepared ball of salt dough (recipe: 2 cups flour, 1 cup salt, 1 cup water — make the night before; it keeps at room temperature)
  • Sketchbooks or plain paper (one per student)
  • Pencils
  • Plan B (if no torma or model is available): Prepare a simple line drawing of the cone form on the board before class — pointed top, broad base, with the symbolic labels ready to add as you teach. Students can still trace the shape in the air.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader for this class leads the full Grade 4 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay (Food Offering Prayer is reserved for the closing.) The teacher sits with the class during the prayers, not leading — the student leader holds the space.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Once prayers are complete and the class has settled, the teacher asks:

"In Unit 1, you learned about seven objects that encode teachings — the water bowls. Each bowl, each substance, had a meaning. Now I want to ask you something different. What if instead of buying an offering, or filling a bowl with water, you made the offering yourself — you shaped it with your own hands? What changes when you make something yourself and then give it away?"

Take 3–4 responses. Do not evaluate or redirect the answers — the teacher is listening for whether students intuitively sense that there is something different about a handmade offering. Common responses: "it means more," "you put effort into it," "it's harder to let go." All of these are entry points, not conclusions. Note them but do not resolve them yet.

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

The teacher introduces the torma. Show images first — the range from simple to elaborate — before explaining anything. Let students look for thirty seconds without commentary.

"This is a torma. In Tibetan: གཏོར་མ. A ritual sculpture — made from tsampa and butter, sometimes coloured with natural pigments, shaped by hand, and offered at a shrine or during a ceremony. Tormas can be this simple" — indicate the basic cone — "or this complex" — indicate an elaborate multi-tiered example. "In this class, we will make the simple kind. But before we touch the material, we need to understand what we are making and why."

Explain the basic facts: what tsampa is (roasted barley flour — the staple of Tibetan food), how it is mixed with butter to hold its shape, how the form is built up by hand. If using salt dough or clay as a substitute, name this directly: "In our classroom we will use salt dough / clay. This is a teaching medium — the form and the intention are what matter, not the specific material. Traditional practitioners understand this distinction."

Explain the types briefly: simple offerings, elaborate shrine pieces, tormas made for specific ceremonies. The students will make a simple form.

Then explain the most important thing: "After a torma is offered — placed on the shrine, dedicated in prayer — it is dissolved in water, buried, or burned. It is not kept. This is not because it has no value. It is because the value was never in the object. The value was in the making and in the giving."

Vocabulary Moment: Write non-attachment on the board.

"In Buddhist teaching, attachment to outcomes or to objects is understood as a source of suffering. We hold things, cling to things, and when they change or go, we suffer. Making a torma is a practice in non-attachment — but it is a very specific kind. You are not meant to make it carelessly. You are not meant to shrug when it is offered. You put real care and real time into making it — and then you release it. The caring and the releasing are both essential. One without the other is not the practice."

Body Moment: The teacher holds a simple torma or a cone of dough in both hands — cupped, presented.

"Look at the shape. Pointed top — this represents the mind of awakening: precise, upward-reaching, directed. Broad base — this represents the ground of practice: stable, wide, able to hold weight." Pause. "Now trace this shape with your hands in the air. Start wide at the bottom — your hands apart — and bring them together, up, to a point above you." Students trace the shape. Do it twice, slowly. "That shape — ground below, awakening above — is what you are going to make."

Emotional Hook: Do not explain this — simply name it and leave it.

"Most of you, at some point in this unit, will feel reluctant to give your torma away. That reluctance — that tug — is the beginning of the teaching. Notice it when it comes."

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: what is something you've made — anything, even something small — that you didn't want to give away, even if you said you were fine with it? Or, if nothing comes to mind: what would be the hardest thing for you to make carefully, over days, and then release completely?"

Students talk in pairs. The teacher circulates and listens — not to correct, but to hear what students reach for. This is data for the teacher about where the emotional centre of gravity is for this group. Common responses involve food they made, drawings, small crafted objects. Let the conversation run its full time.

What to listen for: students who say they would feel fine giving things away (they may be performing non-attachment rather than encountering it), and students who are already genuinely troubled by the idea (they are closest to the real teaching). Both responses are welcome; neither is correct.

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Students sketch their torma design. This is planning, not making. They should draw:

  • The basic shape (cone is required — the pointed top, the broad base)
  • Any decorative elements they want to add (ridges, patterns, a small disc or medallion at the top — keep it simple)
  • A note or label: what will they name this torma?

The teacher circulates and has brief conversations. Keep designs achievable — a student who plans an elaborate multi-tiered construction in the sketch phase will have a difficult making experience. If a design is too complex, the teacher asks: "What is the most important part of this? What would this look like if you kept only the essential?" Simplicity is not a compromise — it is appropriate to the form.

Students who finish the sketch early can write one sentence in their sketchbook: "What I am most looking forward to about making this" and one sentence: "What I am least looking forward to."

Debrief — 5 minutes

"Next class, we begin making. Before you come back, I want you to notice something in your week. When you make something — anything: a drawing, a meal, a bed that you make in the morning — notice what you feel when it is finished. Does the finished thing feel like it belongs to you? Does it feel different from before you made it? Hold that question. You don't need to answer it yet — just notice."

Brief pause. Do not rush to fill the silence.

Closing — 3 minutes

The student leader leads the full closing prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "You are going to make something over the next three classes that you will give away in the fourth. That is unusual. And it is the practice."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Students' torma design sketches go into their portfolios — keep them in class between now and C4 so they are available during the making sessions. The making begins C2: ensure materials are ready before this class, not the morning of C2. If using tsampa and butter (preferred if available from the school kitchen), test the mixture yourself before class — the consistency should be firm enough to hold a shape without crumbling. A ratio of roughly 3:1 tsampa to butter works as a starting point; adjust for humidity. If using salt dough: recipe is 2 cups flour, 1 cup salt, 1 cup water; mix thoroughly, knead for 5 minutes, rest overnight. It keeps for several days wrapped in a damp cloth or sealed container. If using air-dry clay: purchase enough for one fist-sized portion per student; it is the most forgiving medium and is recommended if tsampa is unavailable. In all cases, explain to students why and normalise it: the substitution is not a failure of authenticity — it is itself a teaching about form and intention. Spiral connection: this lesson bridges directly from G4.U1 (the seven-bowl water offering), where students learned that ritual objects encode teachings. The move from receiving and using objects (Unit 1) to making objects (Unit 2) to reading objects (Unit 3) is the connective thread of the whole year. Name this transition explicitly during the PKA.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child was introduced to tormas — ritual sculptures made from flour and butter that are offered at a shrine and then dissolved or burned rather than kept. Over the next three classes, each student will make their own torma to offer in Class 5. You can ask them: 'What is your torma going to look like? What did you sketch?' And, if you want to go deeper: 'What do you think it will feel like to give it away when it's finished?'"
