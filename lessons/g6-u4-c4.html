Making Masks

Blank card stock and paint → a face that will cover one's own and invoke a quality into the performance space

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleMaking as meaning-making
Bloom's levelApply / early Create
LessonG6.U4.C4

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Blank card stock and paint → a face that will cover one's own and invoke a quality into the performance space

Cognitive Principle Featured: Making as meaning-making — the act of constructing an object encodes its significance differently than studying or describing it. When a student makes a Cham mask, the figure's qualities become material; the maker's attention is inseparable from the object produced.

Bloom's Level: Apply / early Create — students apply their knowledge of the figure's appearance and meaning to produce an artefact that will be worn in a real performance.

Prior Class Summary: C3 was the movement class — students learned the Cham walk, one mudra, and one quarter-turn, and practised the combined sequence. They carry the three movements in their bodies and the instruction to practise between classes. They arrive at C4 ready to make the face they will wear.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the requirements of a Cham mask in this context — it must identify the figure, carry the figure's energy, and be wearable for the performance. Students will UNDERSTAND that the mask is not decorative but representational — it makes the figure present again, and wearing it in performance is an act of invocation. Students will BE ABLE TO produce a complete mask of their chosen figure, informed by the meaning profile from C2. Students will FEEL the intimate, slightly strange quality of making a face that will cover one's own — and the seriousness that attaches to objects made with full attention.

Materials Checklist

  • Pre-cut mask forms from card stock or thick paper — one per student (oval shape with eye holes; pre-cut before class to save time during making)
  • Paint (tempera or acrylic) — key colours for the main figures: dark blue/black for Yama and Mahakala, white for Citipati, traditional colours for Guru Rinpoche (red and gold)
  • Fine-tipped markers or black paint pens for detail work
  • Elastic or ribbon for tying — pre-cut, one length per student
  • Hole punch (for attaching elastic)
  • Brushes (various sizes, at least two per student)
  • Water cups and palette paper or trays
  • Students' meaning profiles from C2 (have students retrieve these from portfolios at the start of class)
  • Newspaper or scrap paper to protect the work surface
  • A drying space where masks can lie flat after class
  • Plan B: If card stock mask forms cannot be pre-cut, students work from a flat sheet of card stock and cut their own shape — this takes approximately 5 extra minutes and is acceptable. If paint is unavailable, markers alone produce a strong result. If elastic is unavailable, masks can be held in place during performance (student holds the mask to their face while moving) — this is less ideal for movement but workable. If materials are very limited, students may create a decorated paper headband or crown representing the figure rather than a face-mask — the meaning and invocation remain; the face-covering is the variable.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set including Dolma: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Food Offering Prayer (closing only)

Student leader leads. After prayers: brief movement warm-up — walk + turn + mudra, twice. Standing. "Before we sit and make: let's confirm that the movements are still in the body."

Prior Knowledge Activation — 3 minutes

Teacher: "Before you pick up any materials: find your meaning profile from Class 2 in your portfolio. Read it. Read your own words about who this figure is and what they represent. The mask you make today should look like that figure and carry that meaning. Not decorative — significant."

Students retrieve portfolios and find the C2 meaning profile. One minute to read. Then: materials are distributed.

Mini Lesson — 5 minutes

What a Cham mask in this context needs to do:

"First: identify the figure. Even a simple mask should be recognisable — if someone who knows the tradition sees your mask, they should be able to name the figure. What is the most important visual feature of your figure's face? Start there.

Second: carry the figure's energy. Not cute. Not decorative. The mask should feel like the figure — which means the colours, the expression, and the shapes should reflect the quality you wrote about in your meaning profile.

Third: be wearable. It needs to stay on your face during the performance, not block your vision entirely, and not impede your movement. We will attach the elastic at the end."

Vocabulary Moment: Write on the board: representation — from the Latin, to make present again; to stand in for something. "Your mask represents your figure. When you wear it in the performance, the mask is not a disguise — it is making that figure's quality present in this space. That is the function of representation. It calls something into presence."

Body Moment: Before anyone picks up a brush: the teacher holds up a blank mask form in both hands — palms upward, as an offering gesture. "Hold your blank mask the same way. Both hands, palms up." Students hold their blank mask forms. Brief pause. "This will become something. Put your full attention into what you are about to make. Same quality of attention as the torma in Grade 4 — you are making something that carries meaning. Begin."

Students begin.

Emotional Hook: The experience of creating a face that will cover one's own — intimate, slightly strange, and for many students, unexpectedly absorbing. The quality of absorption itself is the emotional hook; no additional framing is needed.

Activity — 25 minutes

Full mask-making session. Students work from their meaning profiles and preliminary sketches. Teacher circulates with questions — not instructions:

"Does this look like your figure? What is the most important thing about this figure's face — is that visible yet?" "What colour does Mahakala need to be for the audience to recognise him?" "The Citipati are skeletons — what part of the skull is most important?" "What would make this feel more like Yama's gravity?"

No rushing. The teacher does not set micro-deadlines during the making session. Students who work fast are asked: "Is there anything more you want to add? Anything that would make the meaning clearer?"

Students who struggle: "Look at your meaning profile. Start with the one feature that is most important. Add the others after."

Simple materials done with full attention are the standard. An elaborate mask made carelessly produces less than a plain mask made with complete focus.

Elastic is attached in the final five minutes: punch a hole on each side, thread the elastic through, knot it behind. Teacher assists as needed. Masks are set in the drying area — face up, flat — with the student's name on a piece of paper beneath each mask.

Debrief — 3 minutes

Teacher: "You have made a mask. Next class: you wear it. Between now and then — hold it carefully. And practise your movements. The performance is the final class of Grade 6. Come to that class ready to be fully in your body, with your figure's quality in your hands and feet."

Masks stay at school — do not send them home unless the teacher is confident they will return in good condition. State this clearly.

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set including Dolma. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer at close.

Teacher's closing line: "The mask exists now. Next class — it will do what it was made for."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Masks must be kept safely between C4 and C5. If they need to dry overnight, ensure they are stored flat and not stacked. Before C5: prepare the performance space in full — arrange seating for any audience, set up a simple shrine (a table with an image of the Buddha or a relevant figure; a butter lamp or candle if the school permits; the bell and dorje if available). The performance opens with the full prayer set — not with teacher introduction. This means the prayer is the first thing the audience hears. If an external audience is invited (another class, parents, school staff, the resident teacher), confirm their arrival time and that they know the performance opens with prayer and chanting. If masks are not dry in time for C5: allow a few extra minutes at the start of C5 for final drying, or use a fan; do not abbreviate the prayer opening to accommodate the schedule. Note: the C5 performance is the culminating assessment for Unit 4. What is assessed is the mask and the embodiment during the performance, not the performance as a polished product — see C5 notes. Spiral connections: the torma-making from Grade 4 is the most direct spiral connection here — both involve making an object with a specific meaning and with full attention. The theme of the object as an act of invocation (the mask representing the figure) connects to the altar objects and offering practices from Grades 3 and 4.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child made a Cham mask of their chosen figure from the Tibetan sacred dance tradition. They will wear the mask in the final class of Grade 6, when the class performs a short Cham ceremony together. The mask will come home after the performance and is a Grade 6 portfolio artefact. You can ask them: 'Who is on your mask? What does that figure represent? What did it feel like to make a face that you will wear?'"
