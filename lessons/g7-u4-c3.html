Preparing

The full Panglhabsol ceremony and its structure → the recognition that everything studied across seven years lives coherently inside a single traditional ceremony

LessonG7.U4.C3

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The full Panglhabsol ceremony and its structure → the recognition that everything studied across seven years lives coherently inside a single traditional ceremony

Cognitive Principle Featured: Schema integration — when students recognise that the Cham Dance, torma offerings, Sang offering, and lineage prayers all appear together in one real ceremony, the individual memories from previous grades reorganise into a coherent whole; this integration produces understanding of a different order than any single lesson could

Bloom's Level: Evaluate / early Create — students evaluate the full ceremony's meaning and their own position within it, then begin creating the culminating reflection: a personal declaration of lineage and gratitude

Prior Class Summary: C2 performed the simplified Sang offering — students watched the smoke rise and directed their attention with it, sending outward whatever they wanted to offer. They carry the smell of the offering and the closing line: "The smoke has gone. What you sent with it has not."

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the full structure of the Panglhabsol ceremony — its sequence of elements, what each element does, and how the elements connect to the broader tradition.

Students will UNDERSTAND that Panglhabsol integrates practices and elements they have encountered across the curriculum — Cham Dance, torma, Sang offering, lineage prayers — and that seeing them together reveals the coherence of the tradition as a whole.

Students will BE ABLE TO begin the culminating reflection — "What I honour, and what I owe" — with a substantive start on at least two of the four sections, drawing on their lineage map from C1.

Students will FEEL the recognition of integration — that what they have studied over seven years is not a collection of separate topics but a living tradition that holds them all.

Materials Checklist

  • Student portfolios (including lineage maps from C1)
  • Writing instruments
  • Whiteboard for the ceremony sequence outline
  • Extra paper for students whose reflection may overflow their portfolio
  • Plan B (integration activity): If time is short due to an extended Cham walk body moment or rich discussion, the reflection drafting can begin at home and be completed before C4. However, students should be told this clearly at the debrief — not as a surprise before C4. Aim to give every student at least ten minutes of in-class drafting time.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 7 prayer set, student leader.

Prayer sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

Teacher: "Since C2 — has anyone noticed the smell of juniper or incense somewhere in daily life and thought of the Sang offering? Or found themselves thinking about what you sent with the smoke?"

Take 2–3 responses. If students noticed the smell and it called up the memory, receive this: the body holds practice through the senses — that is what happened. If no one noticed, that is also useful: "The practice of noticing the body's memory of practice is itself a practice. We'll keep developing it."

"Today: we look at the full Panglhabsol ceremony — all of its elements together. And then we begin the final reflection."

Mini Lesson — 15 minutes

The full Panglhabsol ceremony — its structure and meaning.

Write the ceremony sequence on the board as the teacher speaks through it:

Opening prayers and invocations → Cham Dance → Torma offerings → Sang offering → Lineage prayers → Closing dedication

"This is the ceremony. Let's walk through it — and as we do, notice what you already know."

Opening prayers and invocations: The ceremony begins with prayers calling upon the Dharma Protectors — inviting their presence, acknowledging their role in protecting the teaching and the community. The community gathers with this intention explicitly stated. "You do this every time you sit down in this class — the prayer set is an invocation. You have done this version of it for seven years."

Cham Dance: "You know this from Grade 6. The masked sacred dance — the body as a vehicle for the dharma. The dancers are not performing for an audience; they are enacting the qualities of the figures they embody. Each mask is a specific being — a protector, a deity, a human-animal guardian. The dance honours those beings by giving them form in the human body."

Pause here.

Body Moment: "Stand up. Take the Cham walk — three deliberate steps, then pause. The way you learned it in Grade 6." Students rise. Three steps — slow, weighted, intentional — then stillness. Hold for a breath. "You last did this in Grade 6. The body remembered. That is what practice does — it stays in the body. The practice is still in you. Sit down."

Students return to their seats. Allow a brief moment of landing before continuing.

Torma offerings: "Grade 4 — making offerings with the hands. The torma is a sculptured offering made from dough and butter — shaped, decorated, and presented to the protectors. The Panglhabsol ceremony includes torma offerings alongside the Sang. The logic of the offering is the same as the Sang: an intentional movement toward the protectors. The material changes; the relational gesture is the same."

Sang offering: "What we did in C2. The smoke travels outward. The offering is carried."

Lineage prayers: "Invocations of the teachers, the lineage holders, the figures who transmitted the Dharma across generations. This is the ceremony naming what your lineage map drew: the chain of transmission from origin to here."

Closing dedication: "The Ngowa. Every class, every practice session, every ceremony ends with the dedication of merit. What was generated here goes outward to all beings. The ceremony closes with the same gesture it opened with — orientation toward beings beyond oneself."

"That is the ceremony. And here is what I want you to see: everything in it, you have encountered. The Cham Dance — Grade 6. The torma — Grade 4. The Sang — last class. The dedication prayer — since Grade 1. The lineage prayers — the chain you drew in C1. You have not been studying separate things. You have been studying parts of a tradition that holds all of them together. Panglhabsol is the ceremony that holds them."

Vocabulary Moment: Liturgy — a fixed form of public worship conducted according to a prescribed order. Panglhabsol is liturgical: it follows a set sequence, and that sequence is not arbitrary. Each element has a function in the whole, and the order matters — invocation before offering, Cham before Sang, dedication last. The fixed form is not rigidity; it is the container that allows the practice to be transmitted across generations without distortion. The form holds the meaning while the meaning moves through practitioners across time.

Emotional Hook: The recognition of integration — that the seven years of this curriculum live inside a single traditional ceremony. Students have been in the tradition all along; Panglhabsol makes this visible.

Turn and Talk — 3 minutes

"Tell your partner: which element of the Panglhabsol ceremony connects most strongly to something you remember from an earlier grade? What is the connection — what did it feel like to encounter it again, in this context?"

Take one brief response from the room. "Thank you. Hold that."

Activity — 10 minutes

Students retrieve their lineage maps from C1 and begin the culminating reflection: "What I honour, and what I owe." Structure on the board:

1. The lineage I am part of — who taught me, and (where I can reach it) who taught them. Name specific people. 2. What this year has taught me about what it means to transmit the Dharma — what I understand now that I did not understand at the beginning of Grade 7. 3. What I am grateful for. Be specific. 4. What I carry forward into Grade 8. Not in general — what specifically.

Teacher circulates with specific coaching:

  • "Section 1: use the lineage map. Name people. Not 'my teachers' — specific names."
  • "Section 4: what specifically will you carry? 'I will carry the Feynman Principle' is more useful than 'I will carry the dharma.' Go concrete."
  • "Section 3: gratitude for what? Gratitude for whom? Name it."

Students should complete at least a first draft of sections 1 and 2 in class. Sections 3 and 4 may be completed before C4 — make this clear in the debrief.

Debrief — 3 minutes

"You began this year studying cruelty — what causes it, what our responsibility is when we see it. You taught younger students. You held healing intention for someone you love. And now you are sitting inside a ceremony that has been performed in the Himalayas for centuries. That arc — from empathy to teaching to healing to honouring — is the arc of practice. It is also the arc of Grade 7."

"Finish sections 3 and 4 before C4. They will be part of what you read aloud. Bring your complete reflection next class."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set, student leader. Food Offering Prayer included.

Teacher's closing line: "The whole tradition lives in one ceremony. You have been inside it all year."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author):

Carry forward: The Cham walk body moment in C3 is a deliberate spiral callback to Grade 6 Unit 4. The student whose Grade 6 body learned that walk now does it again — one year later, in a different context, with a different understanding of what they are doing and why. This is what the curriculum's spiral design produces: a living body memory that is accessed and reactivated across grades. The meaning has deepened; the body's memory is the continuity. Mark it clearly — "You last did this in Grade 6" — so students feel the return.

The ceremony structure on the board should remain visible for the rest of class so students can reference it while writing their reflections. The connection between the Panglhabsol elements and the specific grades where each was introduced (Cham → Grade 6, torma → Grade 4, Sang → this unit) is explicit — name the grades directly. Students who have been in the program since Grade 1 will feel the coherence. Students who joined later will understand the depth of what they have joined.

The culminating reflection ("What I honour, and what I owe") should be complete before C4 — sections 3 and 4 finished at home if not completed in class. C4 is the sharing and declaration; students need a finished piece to read from. The sharing in C4 is ceremonial — not a presentation, not a discussion. A declaration.

Spiral connections: Grade 4 Unit 2 torma offerings (explicit mention in mini lesson); Grade 6 Unit 4 Cham Dance (explicit body moment callback); Ngowa dedication prayer (since Grade 1 — named as the closing of Panglhabsol); Grade 7 Unit 2 three-part reflection format (the four-part structure of the Unit 4 reflection echoes the three-part format from Unit 2 — both are tools for structured honesty).
