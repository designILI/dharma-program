The Second Ring (Friends & Neighbours)

Adding a second ring to the diagram → Discovering that a genuine wish can extend beyond the people we love most

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleSpaced retrieval + progressive deepening
Bloom's levelApply / early Analyze. Students apply last class's practice to a wider circle. Early analysis begins when a student notices the difference in feeling between wishing well for a beloved family member versus wishing well for a classmate they rarely think about
LessonG2.U1.C4

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Adding a second ring to the diagram → Discovering that a genuine wish can extend beyond the people we love most

Cognitive Principle Featured: Spaced retrieval + progressive deepening — the meditation begins by recalling C3's experience (the family member, the wish) before adding a new ring. This is not mere review; reconstruction of the previous meditation activates the emotional memory and gives the expansion something real to push against. Without the inner ring genuinely inhabited, the second ring is only a concept.

Bloom's Level: Apply / early Analyze. Students apply last class's practice to a wider circle. Early analysis begins when a student notices the difference in feeling between wishing well for a beloved family member versus wishing well for a classmate they rarely think about — and wonders why that difference is there.

Prior Class Summary: In C3, students were introduced to Sem kye and Ngowa, learned the structure of the concentric circles diagram, completed the centre ring (family), and experienced the first guided meditation — holding one specific person in mind and sending them a particular wish. That felt experience is the foundation this class builds on.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW what the second ring of the diagram represents — people they see regularly but are not family: friends, classmates, neighbours, familiar community members.

Students will UNDERSTAND that a wish can be directed toward someone we know but don't feel especially close to — and that the wish can be real even if the feeling is quieter.

Students will BE ABLE TO sit through a 4–5 minute guided meditation and add at least one person to the second ring of their diagram; some will find the extension natural; some will find it requires more effort, which is also right.

Students will FEEL the slight unfamiliarity of stretching care toward someone not in their innermost circle — a small, honest effort that is different from the tenderness of C3, and equally worth noticing.

Materials Checklist

  • Concentric circles diagrams from C3 (returned by teacher at start of activity)
  • Index cards from C2 (redistributed as reference, or already in student Dharma folders)
  • Pencils and coloured pencils
  • Himalayan singing bowl or bell — Plan B: teacher's audible breath as opening/closing signal
  • Quiet, settled room — same arrangement as C3

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader leads the full Grade 2 prayer set:

  1. Kyap Dro
  2. Om Mani Padme Hung (7 times)
  3. Manjushri Prayer
  4. Sem kye — teacher leads; students join where ready (no pressure to have memorised)
  5. Ngowa — teacher leads at closing (see Closing section)

Before the prayers: "Last week you sent a wish to someone in your family. That wish went out — it didn't come back. Today we add another ring. But first, we go back to the beginning."

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

"Close your eyes for a moment. Go back to last class. Who did you picture during the meditation — the person in your centre ring? Find their face."

Pause 10 seconds.

"Open your eyes. You don't need to say their name. But did you find them?"

Take a few hands or nods.

"Good. They are still there — in your centre circle, in your diagram, and in your practice. That doesn't go away."

"Now I want to ask you something different: who did you see this week that you normally don't really notice? The person who swept the hall. The student who sits in the corner at lunch. Your neighbour who you walk past every morning. Someone you see all the time but haven't really thought about."

Take two or three responses. Do not prompt or correct — receive whatever is offered.

"Those people — the ones we see but don't fully notice — that's the second ring. That's where we're going today."

Mini Lesson — 8 minutes

"Last week we learned something important: a wish with a real face behind it is stronger than a wish into the air. We started with someone we love — someone whose face is very easy to find. Family."

"Today we go one step outward. Not to strangers — not yet. To the people in the middle: friends, classmates, neighbours. People you see regularly. People who are part of your life, even if you haven't thought of sending them a wish before."

"Here's what might happen: the wish might feel a little quieter. A little more like effort. That's not wrong — it's actually what this practice is for. When the wish is easy, we don't learn much. When it takes a little more effort to really mean it, we're learning to stretch."

"Some of the most important people in someone's circle are the ones they never thought to include until someone asked: 'What about them?'"

Vocabulary Moment: "The word for this kind of expanding care — where you keep adding people to your circle, further and further out — is Bodhichitta. We'll name it properly in our last class. For now, just know: what we are practising is something that has a name. It's real. Teachers and monks have been practising exactly this for thousands of years."

Body Moment: "Hold out both hands, palms up. Look at the circle they make together — the space between your cupped hands. That's your inner ring. Now slowly open your arms a little wider — still palms up, still making a bowl. A little wider. A little wider still. That's the second ring. That's what we're doing today — not pushing love away, just making the bowl bigger."

Emotional Hook: The moment a student realises they are genuinely thinking about someone they usually ignore — and finding, with some surprise, that they actually do want that person to be okay.

Turn and Talk — 3 minutes

"Turn to the person next to you. Tell them: who is one person in your life — not a close family member — that you actually want good things for? Someone outside your usual circle. They can be a classmate, a neighbour, the cook, anyone."

Allow two minutes. The teacher circulates and listens.

What to listen for: Students who name people who are difficult or complicated — the classmate they argue with sometimes, the neighbour who is unfriendly. If this comes up, note it with warmth: "That's a brave choice." Do not force the meditation toward difficult relationships in this lesson, but don't redirect either. Hold it.

Activity — 18 minutes

Part 1 — Guided Meditation: The Second Ring (4–5 minutes)

Ring the bell (or use audible breath). Students settle with eyes closed or looking at their hands.

Teacher reads aloud, slowly. This can be read verbatim.

"Settle your body. Feel the weight of yourself in your seat."

Pause 5 seconds.

"Take one slow breath in. Let it go."

Pause 5 seconds.

"Go back to last week, for just a moment. Find that person from your centre ring — the family member you held in your mind. You don't need to stay with them long. Just find their face."

Pause 8 seconds.

"Good. They are there. They are in your circle."

Pause 5 seconds.

"Now, from that centre — step outward. One ring further."

Pause 5 seconds.

"Think of someone you see often but might not have thought to send a wish to. A friend. A classmate. A neighbour. Maybe someone you like but don't know very well. Maybe someone who sits near you every day but whose face you've never really looked at. Find one person."

Pause 10 seconds.

"Picture their face. Where do you usually see them — in the classroom, on the path, at the gate, in the market? Picture that place. See them there."

Pause 8 seconds.

"Now: what would you want for them? Not a big wish. A quiet one. Something like: 'May you be happy. May things go okay for you today. May you have enough of what you need.'"

Pause 10 seconds.

"You might notice the wish feels a little different from last week's — maybe quieter, maybe less warm. That's fine. The practice is exactly this: seeing if we can mean the wish even when the feeling is smaller. Even a small wish is real."

Pause 10 seconds.

"Stay with it. Keep seeing their face. Keep meaning the wish."

Pause 8 seconds.

"Before you open your eyes — notice: did the wish change anything in you? Even slightly?"

Pause 8 seconds.

"Take one breath. And open your eyes."

Ring bell or exhale.

Thirty seconds of quiet before speaking.

"That is the second ring."

Part 2 — Draw the second ring (10 minutes)

Return the diagram sheets. "Open to your diagram. Look at the second ring — the one right outside the family circle. Write the names, or draw faces or symbols, of the people you thought of today. You can add more than one."

Students work. The teacher circulates quietly. Refer students to their index cards if they need prompting: "Look at your list from the first week. Are any of those people a second-ring person?"

The teacher collects diagrams at the end of class.

Extension: Students who finish may draw a small image in the margin of what the wish felt like — a shape, a weather pattern, a colour. Ask: "If the C3 wish was one colour, what colour is this one? Are they the same?"

Debrief — 5 minutes

"What did you notice? Was the wish different this time?"

Take several responses. Receive all of them: "It was harder." "It felt the same." "I thought of someone I wasn't expecting." "My mind kept wandering." All are valid observations about practice.

"The people in your second ring — they are part of your life. They didn't ask to be thought about today, and they might never know that you sent them a wish. That's okay. The wishing still happens. It still does something."

"Next class, we go further — past people you know entirely. You'll see."

Closing — 3 minutes

The student leader leads:

  1. Kyap Dro
  2. Om Mani Padme Hung (7 times)
  3. Manjushri Prayer
  4. Sem kye
  5. Ngowa — teacher leads

After closing: "Two rings filled. The bowl is getting bigger. Next class — we go past people entirely."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Two things matter most from this class. First: students should have genuinely added at least one person to the second ring of their diagram — not a token name, but someone they actually held in mind during the meditation. If a student's second ring is empty or filled only with best friends who feel more like family, that is worth a quiet conversation: "Is there anyone a little further out you could add?" Second: notice any student who named a difficult person — a classmate they argue with, someone who has been unkind to them. This is important data. Do not push; do note. By C6, some students will be ready to include difficult people in the wider rings naturally. Do not force it earlier. Spiral connection: Grade 1, Unit 2 (Kindness practices, if applicable) — the idea that kindness is not only for those we already love. Also: the Be Kind read-aloud from C2 (kindness rippling outward from one point) — return to that image if students need a bridge: "Remember the book? The kindness didn't stay with one person. It moved."

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child expanded their circle of care outward from family to friends, classmates, and neighbours — using a short guided meditation to find one person and send them a wish. You can ask them: Who did you add to your second ring? Was it easy or hard to mean the wish for them?"
