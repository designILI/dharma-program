What Is It That Continues?

A habit noticed — a recurring way of reacting — and the question of what it would mean for that habit to outlast this life → the third Bardo (becoming) and the teaching on karmic momentum as what carries forward from one life to the next

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleConsequential thinking
Bloom's levelAnalyze
LessonG6.U2.C4

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): A habit noticed — a recurring way of reacting — and the question of what it would mean for that habit to outlast this life → the third Bardo (becoming) and the teaching on karmic momentum as what carries forward from one life to the next

Cognitive Principle Featured: Consequential thinking — the class asks students to follow the implications of the Bardo teaching forward into their own present moment. "If what continues is the momentum of your patterns — if the quality of attention and intention you cultivate now is what will be available in the Bardo — then what are you cultivating?" This is not a rhetorical question designed to produce guilt; it is a genuine analytic exercise in working out the practical consequences of a theoretical position.

Bloom's Level: Analyze — students synthesise the Bardo of becoming with the Unit 1 rebirth teaching (the consciousness stream, the flame analogy), identify how the concept of karmic momentum resolves the apparent contradiction between "no fixed self" and "something that continues," and apply this synthesis to their own observable habits and tendencies.

Prior Class Summary: C3 went into the Bardo of dharmata — the ground luminosity, the peaceful and wrathful appearances, the instruction to move toward light rather than habit. Students did the 90-second awareness practice and touched — however briefly — the quality of bare awareness that the teaching names. They carry the phrase "ground luminosity" and the recognition that meditation is, in some sense, practice for the Bardo.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the third Bardo — the Bardo of becoming (srid pa'i bardo) — as the wandering state in which consciousness is drawn toward rebirth by karmic momentum: the accumulated force of habits, intentions, and unresolved emotional patterns.

Students will UNDERSTAND that what continues through the Bardo is not a "self" in a fixed-soul sense, but the momentum of the stream — the direction it has been shaped to flow — which is itself the product of how one has lived. This resolves the apparent paradox of Unit 1: no fixed self, and yet something continues.

Students will BE ABLE TO complete the third Bardo section of their concept map with structural detail, connect it to the Unit 1 consciousness-stream teaching, and annotate one personal observation about a tendency they notice in themselves.

Students will FEEL the specific quality of the present moment feeling consequential — not as anxiety or guilt, but as a grounded recognition that what one does now is what one is shaping oneself to be.

Materials Checklist

  • Bardo concept maps (returned by teacher at the start of the activity)
  • Pens and coloured pencils
  • Writing journals (optional)
  • The full Grade 6 prayer cards or sheets (Units 1–2 set — no Dolma yet)
  • Student leader list
  • Safe storage for concept maps after class

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set (Units 1–2 set): Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Note: The Food Offering Prayer appears in closing only. The Dolma (Tara Prayer) is not yet introduced.

Student leader leads. After prayers: quiet. Teacher waits.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "We've been through the Bardo of dying and the Bardo of dharmata. What's left?" Take responses: the Bardo of becoming — if liberation doesn't happen, consciousness enters a wandering state and is drawn toward rebirth. Then:

"What gets drawn toward rebirth? Here is the question I want to sit with today: if there is no fixed self — and we established in Unit 1 that there isn't, not in the fixed-soul sense — what is it that moves? What is the thing that the Bardo is pulling toward a new life?"

Let the question sit for a moment. Take two or three initial responses without correcting them. "We'll answer that in the teaching. Hold your answer — we'll see if it changes."

Fallback if students don't recall the previous Bardos well: "Quick map check — in your own words, what happens in each of the first two Bardos? One sentence each." Students can look at their concept maps if needed. This takes two minutes and re-grounds the class in the overall sequence.

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

The third Bardo — becoming:

"In the third Bardo, consciousness wanders. The teaching describes a 'mental body' — experience without a physical form. The being in this Bardo can perceive, can move, but has no physical substance. It drifts. And as it drifts, it is pulled — drawn toward certain conditions, certain situations, certain types of birth — by the force of its accumulated patterns."

"What patterns? The habits of a lifetime. The emotional tendencies that were reinforced again and again. The things that were deeply loved and the things that were deeply feared. The things that were never resolved. These patterns — the accumulated karmic momentum of a life — are like a wind that picks up whatever is loose and carries it. The 'being' in this Bardo does not choose its next life the way one chooses a destination. It is drawn there — the way water finds the lowest point, the way a habit reasserts itself without a decision."

"The teaching is careful about what it is and isn't saying. It is not saying that you are punished for bad actions. It is not cosmic moral accounting — sin equals consequence. It is describing a causal process: the quality of the stream shapes where the stream goes. A stream that has been flowing through arid country arrives at an arid place. A stream that has been flowing through rich country arrives somewhere different."

The resolution of the Unit 1 paradox:

"Here is the answer to the question from Unit 1: no fixed self — and yet something continues. How is that possible? The answer is: what continues is the momentum, not the 'self.' The consciousness stream — the flame that passes from candle to candle — carries the patterns, the habits, the direction of flow. The self, in the fixed-soul sense, does not survive. The patterns do. This is consistent with anattā — and it is also consistent with rebirth. No contradiction."

"Think of the flame analogy from C1. The flame that lights the second candle carries something from the first — not matter, not a fixed thing, but a quality, a direction, a momentum. The second flame burns the way the first flame burned. The habits of the first candle are carried in the quality of the fire."

Karmic momentum — what it is and isn't:

"The term karmic momentum describes this accumulated force. Not fate — the stream is not fixed in its direction forever. A person can change. Genuine practice genuinely changes the quality of the stream. But the changes require real effort, real consistency, real attention — because the existing momentum is always there, always pulling in the direction it has been shaped to go."

Vocabulary Moment: "Karmic momentum — the accumulated force of one's actions, intentions, and habitual patterns. Not punishment. Not fate. A natural consequence of the way the stream has been flowing. Karma (Sanskrit) literally means 'action' — and the teaching is that every action, and especially every intention, shapes the quality of the stream. Karmic momentum is causal, not moral. It describes what happens, not what should happen."

Body Moment: "Think of one habit — a way of reacting, a tendency — that you notice in yourself repeatedly. Something you do or feel or think that keeps coming back. Name it to yourself — quietly, just for you. Don't judge it. Just notice it." Pause for thirty full seconds. "Now: imagine that habit continuing past the end of this life — carried into whatever comes next, as the momentum that shapes the new beginning. Does that change how you feel about it?"

Allow the question to sit. Do not ask students to respond aloud to this particular reflection — it is genuinely personal. Just let it land.

Emotional Hook: The uncomfortable, specific recognition that the tendencies reinforced right now — not in some future more-enlightened version of oneself, but right now — are the tendencies that the Bardo teaching says continue. Not meant to frighten; meant to make the present moment feel real and consequential. Teacher names it: "This is not meant to produce guilt. It is meant to produce the quality of attention that the teaching calls caring. Caring about the quality of the stream."

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: what is one quality or tendency you would most want to carry forward — and one you would most want to leave behind? Not in terms of achievement. In terms of what kind of stream you want to be."

Structure: each partner speaks for about ninety seconds. Teacher circulates.

What to listen for: Students who engage genuinely and specifically ("I'd want to carry my curiosity, and leave behind my tendency to avoid things that are hard"). Students who give abstract or performative answers ("I want to be more compassionate") — gently press: "Can you name a specific moment when you were drawn toward the less-good habit? What was the pull like?"

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Teacher returns concept maps. Students complete the third Bardo section.

The annotation should include:

  1. What the Bardo of becoming is — the wandering state, the mental body, the drawing toward rebirth
  2. What draws consciousness toward rebirth — karmic momentum, habitual patterns, unresolved attachments and aversions
  3. The connection to Unit 1 — how this resolves the "no fixed self, yet something continues" question (write the connection explicitly in their own words)
  4. A personal annotation — one tendency they notice in themselves that they can name as part of their own stream (the teacher makes clear this is for their own map, not for sharing unless they choose to)

Teacher circulates with precise questions: "How does what you wrote in section three connect to what you wrote about anattā in Unit 1?" "What is the word you chose for your personal annotation — and why that word?" "Does the concept of karmic momentum change anything about how you're thinking about this unit?"

Scaffold for students who are uncertain about section three: "Remember the flame analogy — the second candle burns the way the first candle burned. Now: what in the consciousness-stream teaching is like that? Write: 'What continues is not the ___ but the ___.' Fill in those two blanks."

Extension for students who finish early: "Draw a small diagram at the bottom of your concept map — the three Bardos in sequence, with arrows showing the flow from one to the next, and labels at each transition point: what happens at each threshold, and what determines which direction a being moves." This is a high-level synthesis move and rewards careful sequential thinking.

Debrief — 5 minutes

Opening question: "The Bardo teaching says: what you are right now is what continues. Not in a fixed-soul sense — but in the sense that the stream keeps flowing in the direction it has been shaped to flow. What is the most important implication of that? For how you spend your time. For how you practice."

Take 2–3 responses.

"That combination — sobering and empowering — is the right response to the teaching. Sobering because the momentum is real. Empowering because the stream can be shaped. That is what practice is for."

Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full closing prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Teacher collects concept maps.

Teacher's closing line: "The flame carries the quality of how it has been burning. That is both a warning and an invitation."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: By the end of C4, the concept map should have substantial content in all three Bardo sections. In C5, students synthesise and refine — the map moves from a record of what was taught to a genuine conceptual tool that represents the student's own understanding. C5's mini lesson on what makes a concept map complete should be brief, allowing maximum time for student work and the addition of the personal annotation layer. C6's question — "Do you believe it?" — should feel genuinely earned after six classes of careful engagement. It is not asking for doctrinal commitment: it is asking students to evaluate a complex teaching on evidential grounds and argue their position with reasons. The quality of the reasoning matters more than the conclusion. Spiral connection: the karmic momentum teaching connects directly back to Unit 1's consciousness-stream teaching and the flame analogy — this is the place in the curriculum where those earlier concepts pay off. If students don't make the connection spontaneously in the activity, draw it out explicitly in the debrief. The personal annotation section of the concept map is optional to share — the teacher should not read these aloud or reference specific student content without the student's explicit invitation.
