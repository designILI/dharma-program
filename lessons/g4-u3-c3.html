Counting with the Mala

The mala — 108 beads, the counting practice → sustained attention as a trained capacity; the body, speech, and mind engaged simultaneously as a system

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleMulti-channel embodied learning
Bloom's levelUnderstand / Apply
LessonG4.U3.C3

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The mala — 108 beads, the counting practice → sustained attention as a trained capacity; the body, speech, and mind engaged simultaneously as a system

Cognitive Principle Featured: Multi-channel embodied learning — the mala practice engages three distinct channels at once (hand movement, voiced mantra, mental attention), which produces a qualitatively different learning experience from single-channel instruction. Students discover through the practice that the hand gives the mind something to do, which makes it easier — not harder — to be present.

Bloom's Level: Understand / Apply — students receive the meaning of the mala and its practice structure (Understand), then engage in an extended practice session (Apply). The debrief reflects on what happened, which edges toward Analyze.

Prior Class Summary: C2 covered incense and the shrine arrangement — students completed Field Guide entry #2 and can now read a shrine as a structured text representing the Three Jewels. They carry the phrase "a shrine is a text" and the explicit connection between incense smoke and the Grade 1 prayer flags — intention released outward, beyond the sender's control.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the structure of a mala: 108 beads, the guru bead, the tassel; the rule about the guru bead (never cross it — reverse direction); the Sanskrit word japa and its meaning. Students will UNDERSTAND that the mala is not a counting device in the ordinary sense — it is a tool for keeping the mind present, engaging body, speech, and mind simultaneously in what the tradition calls "the three gates." Students will BE ABLE TO hold a mala correctly and use it to count one full round (108 repetitions) or as close as the session allows of Om Mani Padme Hung. Students will FEEL the quality of a class that is mostly silent, with hands moving and voices chanting — the unusual register of a lesson that is experienced in the body more than in the mind.

Materials Checklist

  • Malas — one per student, or one per pair at minimum. Traditional 108-bead malas in wood, bone, or seed (rudraksha, bodhi seed, or sandalwood are all appropriate). If the school does not own enough malas, borrowing from the monastery/temple is strongly preferred over not having them; the physical handling of a mala is irreplaceable in this lesson.
  • A demonstration mala for the teacher — large enough to be seen from several rows away if possible
  • Field Guide booklets (returned from storage)
  • Pencils for partial Field Guide entry #3 completed in class
  • Plan B (if malas are unavailable): Make counting cords in advance — take a length of cord with 21 knots (21 is a traditional sub-count and a manageable number for young students). Students can count repetitions by moving their fingers along the knots. This is not a mala, but it preserves the hand-engagement and the counting structure. Explain: "What we have today is a simplified form of the practice. The principle — hand, voice, mind together — is the same." If even counting cords are unavailable: students count on their fingers (one hand, five fingers, repeat) and note how quickly the counting becomes the centre of attention rather than the mantra. This is itself a teaching — the tool affects the quality of the practice.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader for this class leads the full Grade 4 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay (Food Offering Prayer is reserved for the closing.)

Today, during the Om Mani Padme Hung portion of the opening prayer, the teacher also demonstrates counting with the mala — chanting seven repetitions with the class while visibly drawing the beads, one by one, with the right thumb. Students watch without being asked to do anything yet.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

"You've been chanting Om Mani Padme Hung since Grade 1. You've counted seven repetitions each time — you know the number by feel now, without counting. Here is a question: what if I told you that some practitioners chant it 108 times a day? 1,000 times? 10,000 times during a retreat? How would you keep count?"

Take responses. Students may suggest: marking paper, counting on fingers, using a phone, trusting memory. Receive all suggestions without judging their practicality.

Then: "The answer practitioners arrived at, a very long time ago, is simpler than any of those. One bead per repetition. When you have gone around and back, you know how many you have done. The tool is the mala."

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

"A mala has 108 beads. The number 108 is not arbitrary. It corresponds to the 108 volumes of the Kangyur — the collected teachings of the Buddha — and to the 108 defilements to be overcome on the path of practice. One bead per repetition. When you complete a full round, you have chanted the mantra 108 times."

"There is one special bead: the guru bead. It is larger than the others. It has a tassel or a small pendant attached. The guru bead marks the beginning and end of the mala. Here is the rule: you never cross the guru bead. When you reach it at the end of a round, you reverse direction and begin again. You do not pass over it as if it were any other bead."

"Why? The guru bead represents the teacher — the transmission of teaching from person to person, generation to generation. To cross it would be to place your fingers over the teacher. So instead, you honour it by turning back. This is a small gesture — but it encodes a large teaching about the relationship between student and teacher."

Demonstrate: hold the mala in the right hand, guru bead at the top. "The beads are drawn toward the body — not pushed away — one at a time, with the right thumb pulling each bead." Demonstrate the motion slowly. "The index finger is traditionally not used — it points, and pointing at the teacher is inauspicious. So: thumb, and for support, the middle finger beneath."

"This is the basic technique. When you reach the guru bead: stop, turn the mala, and draw the beads back in the other direction."

Vocabulary Moment: Write japa on the board.

"Japa — from Sanskrit. The practice of repeating a mantra while counting with a mala. Japa is one of the oldest contemplative practices in the world — it predates Buddhism by centuries. You find it in Hindu practice, in Buddhist practice, in Catholic practice with the rosary, in Islamic practice with the tasbih. Different traditions, same principle: the hand counts so that the mind can go deeper."

"The tradition describes this as the three gates working together. Body: the hand moves. Speech: the mantra is spoken or whispered or silently formed. Mind: the attention rests on the meaning or sound of the mantra. All three, together, at the same time. That is different from any other kind of study."

Body Moment: Distribute the malas — one per student (or one per pair). Before any chanting:

"Just hold it. Feel the weight of it. Roll one bead between your fingers. Notice the texture. Now find the guru bead — larger, with the tassel. Hold the mala with the guru bead at the top. Now: try the motion. Not with mantra yet — just the movement. Draw one bead toward you with your right thumb. Feel what that motion is. Now another."

One minute of handling and basic motion, without mantra. Students discover the grip, the resistance of the beads, the rhythm of the pulling.

Emotional Hook: The quality of a class that is mostly silent, with hands moving. Students will feel this register shift as the practice begins — something different is happening, and the body knows it before the mind does.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

This happens after the extended practice session (not before it). Midway through the mala round:

"Keep your hands moving. Tell your partner — without stopping the beads: what is your mind doing right now?"

Students whisper to a partner without stopping the counting. The question is not rhetorical — students actually pay attention to their minds while the hands continue. This is a form of meta-awareness practice: noticing the mind without leaving the practice. Responses are typically: "it keeps wandering," "I'm thinking about lunch," "I'm actually just counting," "it's quiet for a moment and then it jumps again." All of these are true reports. All of them are useful data for the student.

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Extended mala practice — students chant Om Mani Padme Hung while counting with the mala. The goal is one full round (108 repetitions) or as close as time allows.

The teacher chants with the class, leading the pace — a steady, moderate tempo, neither rushing the mantra into meaninglessness nor dragging it into tedium. The teacher's mala is visible — modelling the counting motion throughout.

The room is quiet except for the chanting. The teacher does not instruct during the practice; once it has begun, it continues. If a student loses count: that is fine. Return to the beads, continue from wherever you are. The practice does not require perfect counting; it requires continued engagement.

If students finish one round before the time is up: reverse at the guru bead, begin again. Some students may complete more than one round; this is entirely appropriate.

At the end of the practice period: the teacher brings the chanting to a close gently — "one more, and rest." A moment of complete silence after the final repetition. Let this silence be full, not immediately filled.

Then: the teacher says, quietly: "What happened to time during that practice? What happened to your thinking?"

Brief responses — 2–3 students. Then move to the Field Guide.

Debrief / Field Guide — 5 minutes

Field Guide entry #3 — the mala. Students begin in class and may finish for homework or in C4.

The entry should contain:

  1. An illustration of a mala — the beads, the guru bead, the tassel
  2. The number 108 and what it corresponds to
  3. The rule about the guru bead and what it represents
  4. What japa means
  5. A note from the student's own practice today: one honest sentence about what happened in their mind during the counting.

That last element — the personal practice note — is the most important. It is not graded or shared publicly. It is a record of the student's own encounter with the practice.

Closing — 3 minutes

The student leader leads the full closing prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "You have chanted Om Mani Padme Hung since Grade 1. Today you chanted it 108 times. It is the same mantra. Something is different now."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The mala practice introduced today is one students can sustain independently. At the end of C3 or C4, encourage students to practise with a mala outside of class — even 21 repetitions before sleep is a meaningful practice. If the school has malas that students can borrow for this purpose, facilitate the borrowing. Field Guide entry #3 can be completed in C4 if not finished today; ensure it is not left incomplete by the end of C4. C4 is the completion class for the Field Guide as a whole. Spiral connections: the mala practice connects backward to Grade 1's introduction of Om Mani Padme Hung (where the mantra was first met as a sound, chanted seven times in the prayer set). In Grade 4, that same mantra is now held with counting, intention, and full body engagement — the three gates. The progression from Grade 1 to Grade 4 is the same mantra, deepened. Name this to students: "You have been practising this mantra for four years. Look at what you are doing with it now." The mala practice also connects forward to Grade 7's Panglhabsol preparation, where sustained mantra practice is an explicit preparation element. The seed planted in Grade 4 matters significantly for Grade 7.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child learned to use a mala — prayer beads — to count mantra repetitions. The class chanted Om Mani Padme Hung 108 times while counting the beads. The lesson introduced the Sanskrit word japa (the practice of repeated mantra with counting beads) and the concept of the 'three gates' — body, speech, and mind engaged simultaneously. You can ask them: 'How do you hold the mala? What is the guru bead and what does it represent? What happened in your mind during the chanting?' If there is a mala at home, this is a wonderful opportunity to practise together."
