Making the Crown

Making a leaf crown → gratitude made visible and wearable

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleDual coding
Bloom's levelApply. Students take the understanding from C1 and C2
LessonG1.U2.C3

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Making a leaf crown → gratitude made visible and wearable

Cognitive Principle Featured: Dual coding — when an idea is encoded both verbally (naming a person, saying what they give) and physically (cutting a leaf shape, writing or drawing on it, wearing it on the head), memory and meaning deepen together. The leaf crown is not a craft project that accompanies the lesson; the making is the lesson. Each leaf is a thought made tangible. Wearing it next class will be each child's way of saying: these are the people who have given to me.

Bloom's Level: Apply. Students take the understanding from C1 and C2 — that the tree gave without expecting return, that they have people like this in their lives — and now apply it by translating specific relationships into specific leaves. Each leaf requires: naming a person, identifying what they give, and making that gift visible in some form on the leaf. This is not recall; it is application of gratitude.

Prior Class Summary: In C2, students used role-play to feel the difference between giving with strings attached and giving freely. They named people in their lives who "keep giving without keeping score." They were asked to start thinking about their list. They arrive today ready to make something with what they've been holding.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW that each leaf on their crown represents one person who gives to them, and what that person gives.

Students will UNDERSTAND that gratitude — the feeling of being glad about what someone has given you — can be made visible and shared.

Students will BE ABLE TO make 3–5 leaves, write or draw on each one the name (or image) of a giver and one gift they have given, and assemble these into a wearable crown.

Students will FEEL the quiet, focused pleasure of making something with care — and a growing sense of how many people have given to them, perhaps more than they had noticed before.

Materials Checklist

Per child (prepare one set per student, plus 2 extras for mistakes):

  • 5–7 pre-cut leaf shapes in green card stock or thick green paper, each approximately 12 cm x 7 cm. Leaf shape is simple — slightly pointed oval, like a large mango leaf. If cutting is possible, children may cut their own (see Activity notes). If time or materials are constrained, pre-cut is fine.
  • 1 strip of card stock or stiff paper, approximately 60 cm x 3 cm, for the crown band. Pre-measure and cut to fit a 6-year-old's head (approx. 53–56 cm circumference — add a 4 cm overlap for stapling or taping). If exact sizing is difficult, cut longer and trim to fit during assembly.
  • Crayons or coloured pencils (not markers — markers bleed through thin paper and make the leaf feel messy). 2–3 per child is enough.
  • 1 pencil per child for writing names.
  • Stapler or tape (teacher-managed for assembly at the end).
  • Optional: a single hole-punch and green yarn/ribbon to attach leaves to crown band by tying rather than gluing — more durable and satisfying to assemble.

Room setup: Tables with enough space for each child to spread out 5–6 leaves. Not too close — the making should feel spacious, not cramped. Put materials in the centre of each table in a shared pile; children take what they need. This models sharing without being heavy-handed about it.

Teacher's demo crown: Make one yourself before class. It should be simple and honest — not perfect. Include 4–5 leaves. Write real names on them in your own handwriting. One leaf might be messy or imperfect. Let them see that the making is the point, not the product. Wear it when you demo. If it makes children laugh — good.

Advance prep: Cut all leaf shapes and crown bands before class. Test one full assembly so you know the steps cold. Lay out all materials on tables before children arrive so the room itself signals: we are making something today.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Student leader (rotate) comes forward. Teacher is already wearing the demo crown — casually, not ceremonially. This signals the lesson's tone before a word is spoken.

Kyap Dro (Refuge Prayer) — together, unhurried. Teacher chants with eyes open today — stay present and warm with the children, who will be looking at your crown.

Om Mani Padme Hung — seven rounds.

Manjushri Prayer — together.

Student leader returns to their spot. One child will inevitably mention the crown. Receive it lightly: "You noticed. I made this before class. I'll tell you about it in a minute. But first — let's come in."

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher holds up the demo crown and points to one leaf.

"I made this leaf for my mother. She cooks for me — or she used to. She would make sure I had something warm to eat every single morning. I wrote her name here." (Point to the name.) "And I drew a bowl of soup, because that's what I think of when I think of her."

Point to a second leaf. "This one is for my teacher — not from this school, from when I was small. She taught me how to read. She was patient with me. Very patient."

Retrieval prompt (exact words): "Last class, I asked you to start thinking about your list — the people who give to you, the way the tree gave to the boy. I want to hear one name from someone. Just one. Who's on your list?"

Let three or four children share quickly. Don't dwell on any one name. You're warming the room for the making.

Strong response example: "My uncle — he always brings me things when he comes from town." Receive this: "Your uncle. He thinks about you when he's far away. That's a leaf."

Surface response: If a child says "Everyone" — warm smile, then: "Pick one. The first person who came into your mind just now, when I asked the question."

Fallback if no hands: "Close your eyes for ten seconds. Think about the person who made sure you ate something today. Think about the person who washed your clothes. Think about the person who checks if you're okay when you're sick." Open eyes. "Did someone come to mind?" Almost always: yes.

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Teacher picks up a blank leaf from the table. Hold it up.

"Each leaf is one person. One giver. On your leaf, you'll put two things: their name — or if you don't know how to write it, I'll help, or you can draw their face — and one thing they give you. It can be something you can hold, like food or a gift. Or it can be something you can't hold, like time, or safety, or just — being there."

Demonstrate, slowly, with a fresh leaf:

  1. Write (or draw) the name at the top of the leaf.
  2. In the middle or bottom, draw or write one thing this person gives.
  3. If there's space, add one colour — "because a leaf that has love in it should have some colour."

Show the leaf next to the crown. "When we're done making our leaves, we'll put them on the crown band, like this." Attach the demo leaf to the band. "And next class, you'll wear your crown. You'll share one leaf — just one — with all of us."

Then, quieter: "Here's the thing about this crown. When you wear it, you're carrying the people who love you right on your head. That's not nothing. That's a lot."

Vocabulary Moment: The word gratitude. Use it once, in context: "The feeling you get when you think about someone who has given you something — that warm, full feeling — that feeling has a name. It's called gratitude. Your leaves are going to be full of it." Don't ask children to repeat it. Use it as if it's already theirs.

Body Moment: Before distributing materials, ask everyone to sit up straight, place both hands flat on the table, and take one slow breath in through the nose and out through the mouth. "This is how we begin something we want to make with care." It takes ten seconds and changes the energy in the room immediately. Making begins from a place of quiet attention, not scattered excitement.

Emotional Hook: You are cultivating the pleasure of noticing — the satisfying surprise of realising how many people have been quietly giving to you, often without fanfare. This is not a sad emotion or a guilty one; it is warm and expansive. If a child makes four leaves and then looks up with wide eyes — "I have more people" — that is the crown working exactly as it should.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Before the making begins. This seeds the imagination for the activity.

Prompt (exact words): "Turn to your partner. You have one minute each. Tell them: who are three people on your list? Name them and say one thing each person gives you. Go."

Fixed pairs — same as C1 and C2. Teacher listens, moving around the room. After two minutes:

Reconvene. "Did anyone hear something from their partner that surprised them — a giver they hadn't thought of?" Two or three brief shares.

This Turn and Talk does two things: it activates more names (children prompt each other), and it ensures every child enters the making with at least three concrete people in mind.

What to listen for: Children who only name parents or teachers — gently expand: "What about someone older at school? Someone who helps you at the hostel? A friend who always shares food?" The goal is to expand the children's peripheral vision for givers — the ones we take for granted because they are simply always there.

Activity — 15–20 minutes

The making. This is the heart of the class.

Distribute materials (teacher does this, 2 minutes): Set one crown band and 5–6 blank leaves in front of each child. Crayons and pencils are already on the table.

Instruction (brief — one minute at most):

"You need at least three leaves. Each leaf is one person who gives to you. Name, and one gift. You can make more — up to six. Take your time. If you finish your leaves before time is up, you can decorate the crown band itself — add colours, patterns, whatever feels right. I'll come around and help anyone who needs it with spelling."

Teacher's role during work time: Move slowly through the room. Don't hover. Look at what children are making. Ask quiet questions: "Who's this one for?" and "What do they give you?" These are genuine questions, not assessment. If a child looks up and makes eye contact, smile and nod. This is a room where something real is being made.

The making should be quiet. Not silent — there will be the soft sounds of crayons and whispered conversations between pairs. But quiet and focused. If the room gets noisy and scattered, don't clap or call out. Instead, pick up your own leaf and continue your crown in full view of the class. The visual of the teacher making quietly is more powerful than any instruction.

Assembly (final 5 minutes of activity): With 5 minutes left, teacher says: "Start finishing up. When your leaves are done, bring them to me — or another adult if one is helping — and we'll attach them to your crown band."

Attachment: arrange the leaves along the crown band, overlapping slightly (like scales or feathers), and staple or tape each one. Fit the band to the child's head and fasten the ends. Takes 60–90 seconds per child. If you have a co-teacher or helper, they can run this station while you move through the room; if alone, children can queue briefly at the corner of the room while the rest continue making.

Scaffold for a stuck child: A child who stares at a blank leaf — sit beside them: "Close your eyes. Think about yesterday morning. When you woke up, who was the first person you saw? What did they do?" Almost always: a parent, a caregiver, a hostel auntie. "Write their name. Then draw what they did." A blank leaf is almost always a blank name — they know who they feel grateful for; they just haven't given themselves permission to start.

Extension for an early finisher: "You can make a leaf for yourself — for something you gave to someone else this week. Something you gave without being asked." This connects back to C2 and gives the crown an additional dimension.

Debrief — 5 minutes

Children should be wearing their crowns by now (or close to it). Have them sit in the circle.

Opening question (exact words): "Look around. Everyone is wearing the people who love them on their heads right now. How does that feel?"

Give children room to respond. Expect laughter, warmth, a little shyness. Receive all of it.

Then: "Next class, you're going to choose one leaf — just one — and tell us about that person and what they give you. Start thinking about which one you'll share. It doesn't have to be the most important one. Just one that feels ready."

The "so what" moment — say something like this:

"You know what I notice? Every single one of you had people to put on your leaves. Every single one. Nobody was sitting here with nothing. That's worth noticing — not because someone told us to be grateful, but because it's actually true. We are surrounded by giving. It was happening before we had a word for it."

Don't expand. Let it be enough.

Closing — 3 minutes

Students remain wearing their crowns for the closing. Student leader comes forward — also wearing their crown.

Kyap Dro — together. Wearing the crowns during prayer adds something quiet and lovely — the givers carried into the prayers.

Om Mani Padme Hung — seven rounds.

Manjushri Prayer — together.

Teacher's closing sentence, specific to today:

"Keep your crowns safe tonight. Wear them home if you'd like — or carry them carefully. Next class, they come back with you."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): This is a making class, not a craft class. The distinction matters in how you hold the room. A craft class produces an object; a making class produces an object that carries meaning. Every leaf is a thought made tangible, and the crown is not a decoration — it is a personal devotional object, wearable and visible to others, worn into the closing prayers. The quality of attention in the room during making is part of what the leaves carry. If the room is rushed and noisy, the leaves carry that. If the room is focused and warm, they carry that instead. Hold the latter.

The breath before making (the body moment) is a micro-ritual. Do not skip it. It takes ten seconds and changes the energy in the room faster than any instruction. It signals: we are crossing into something careful.

This class is part of the curriculum-wide thread of making as spiritual act — established first with the prayer flags in G1.U1.C4–C5, continuing here, and deepening through G4.U2 (tormas), G6.U4.C4 (masks), and G8.U3.C3 (stupa building). In each case, the principle is the same: the quality of attention brought to the making shapes what the object becomes. You do not need to name this thread to students — simply hold it yourself and let it govern the atmosphere. This lesson also connects forward to the Gratitude Circle structure: the leaf crowns made here are the material objects that enable G1.U2.C4, which is itself a structural template that recurs in G7.U3.C4 (sharing healing aspirations) and G8.U5.C5 (the Threshold ceremony).

Carry forward into C4: know which children had to be prompted to find givers and which children had more leaves than they expected. The former may need gentle support in C4's Turn and Talk rehearsal. The latter are likely ready to share without hesitation — consider placing one of them early in the Gratitude Circle to set the room's tone. Also: store the crowns flat overnight. A damaged or crushed crown going into C4 breaks continuity for the child who made it.

Watch for the child who looks up mid-making with wide eyes — "I have more people." That is the crown working exactly as intended: it expands the child's peripheral vision for givers, the ones always present but rarely named. Also watch for children who only name parents. Gentle circulation with the question "What about someone at the hostel? Someone who always helps you get ready?" tends to unlock the expansion. Do not insist — offer it once and move on.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child made a leaf crown — each leaf has the name of someone who gives to them, and one gift that person gives. You can ask them: 'Can you show me your crown? Tell me about each leaf.' You may find your name on one of them. Next class, they'll wear their crowns and share one leaf with the group — a small ceremony of gratitude."
