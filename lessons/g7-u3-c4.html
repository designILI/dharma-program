Sharing Our Healing Wishes

Reading the healing aspiration aloud and dedicating the merit → the experience of practising in community — that to hear another person's genuine healing wish is itself a form of receiving the practice

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleSocial encoding through witness
Bloom's levelCreate / Evaluate
LessonG7.U3.C4

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Reading the healing aspiration aloud and dedicating the merit → the experience of practising in community — that to hear another person's genuine healing wish is itself a form of receiving the practice

Cognitive Principle Featured: Social encoding through witness — when a student reads their aspiration to an attentive group and the group responds with the Ngowa dedication, both the speaker and the listeners are changed; the ceremony makes what was private practice into shared transmission

Bloom's Level: Create / Evaluate — the culminating creative act (reading the aspiration) is also an act of evaluation: the student must have judged that the aspiration is specific, genuine, and ready to be offered

Prior Class Summary: C3 was the full aspiration drafting session. Students wrote formal healing wishes for their chosen person — at minimum three paragraphs, following the four-part structure. They have been chanting the mantra daily and have completed or nearly completed their aspirations.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the traditional structure of a Tibetan Buddhist practice session's closing — aspiration, dedication of merit, and the Ngowa as the formal vehicle for that dedication.

Students will UNDERSTAND that reading an aspiration aloud in a community of practitioners is not a performance but a continuation of the practice — and that listening to another person's aspiration with full attention is its own form of practice.

Students will BE ABLE TO read their healing aspiration aloud to the class with presence — holding their chosen person in mind throughout — and receive the dedications offered by the group.

Students will FEEL the intimacy of hearing others' genuine healing wishes — the recognition that everyone in the room is holding someone, and that this room is, for these forty minutes, a community of practitioners.

Materials Checklist

  • Student portfolios with completed healing aspirations
  • Writing instruments (for any student needing final edits at the start of class)
  • Seating arranged in a circle or arc before students arrive, if possible — so readers face the group, not the back of a head
  • Aspirations that the teacher has read between C3 and C4 (see Teacher Notes — teacher reads all aspirations before this class and speaks with any student whose aspiration remains too vague to read aloud)
  • Plan B: If a student has not finished their aspiration, they complete the final elements in the first five minutes while others are settling and preparing. They read when they are ready, even if their turn comes last. No student is excused from reading; the aspiration is the unit's culminating act.

Lesson Body

Opening — 8 minutes

Full Grade 7 prayer set, student leader. The Medicine Buddha mantra is chanted five times together — slowly, deliberately — immediately following the Rewo Sang Chod, before any other activity begins. Five rounds, unhurried.

After the five chantings: one full minute of silence. All students sit in meditation posture, holding their chosen person in mind. No instruction needed — students know why.

Teacher, when the minute ends: "We begin with the practice. Then we share the aspiration."

Prior Knowledge Activation — none

The mantra chanting and the minute of silence are the preparation for this class. There is no separate PKA. The entry into the sharing circle is immediate.

Mini Lesson — 3 minutes

The teacher frames the sharing only. This is brief.

"Reading your aspiration aloud is not a performance. It is a continuation of the practice. When you read, continue to hold the person you wrote it for in your mind. See their face. Let the words be real, not recited."

"We listen to each other's aspirations as practitioners — with full attention, no commentary, no reaction between readings. After each aspiration, we chant the Ngowa together once. That is the dedication: the merit of what was just offered goes outward. Then the next person reads."

Vocabulary Moment: Dedication — the act of giving the merit of one's practice specifically to another being or to all beings. At the end of each reading in this class, the whole class chants the Ngowa together — one dedication for each aspiration offered. This is the traditional closing of a practice session: what we have done here does not stay here. We release it outward.

Body Moment: Before the first reading, teacher: "Sit in practice posture — spine upright, hands in lap, feet flat on the floor. This is the posture of practice. Hold it for the duration of the sharing. If your attention drifts, bring it back to the reader. That return is the practice."

Emotional Hook: The experience of hearing someone else's genuine healing wish — the intimacy of it, and the recognition that everyone in this room is carrying someone. This is named only here in the Teacher Notes; it is not named to students. It is allowed to happen.

Activity — 25 minutes

The sharing circle.

Each student reads their healing aspiration aloud. The order can be volunteered or determined by the student leader before the class begins — either works. What matters is that there is no gap between readings, and that the Ngowa dedication fills the space between each aspiration rather than silence or comment.

Structure for each reading:

  • Student reads their aspiration (all four sections)
  • One breath of shared silence
  • The whole class chants the Ngowa together once — one clean, unhurried chanting
  • Next student reads

The teacher holds the space with full attention throughout. No commentary between readings — not praise, not acknowledgment, only the Ngowa with the group. The teacher's presence is the container, not the narrator.

If a student becomes emotional while reading, the teacher does not intervene verbally. Hold steady. The student continues when they are ready. If they cannot continue, the teacher reads the remaining lines of the aspiration for them — quietly, without explanation.

After all aspirations have been read:

The teacher: "Thank you. We close by directing what is in this room — all of it — outward."

One final joint chanting of the Medicine Buddha mantra, three times together. The teacher guides quietly before it begins: "Hold every person who has been named in this room. Direct the mantra toward all of them. Together."

Three rounds of TAYATHA OM BEKANDZE BEKANDZE MAHA BEKANDZE RADZA SAMUDGATE SOHA.

Then one full minute of silence.

Debrief — 3 minutes

Teacher: "You held one person through this unit — in your mind during class, in the mantra between classes, in the aspiration you just read. That sustained attention across four classes is the practice. Not perfect, not complete — sustained. Whether what you sent reaches them is not something we can measure. That you gave it fully — that you can know."

"Unit 3 is complete. You have been healers. Not because you cured anyone. Because you directed your practice, with full attention, toward a specific person's liberation from suffering. That is what the Medicine Buddha vowed to do. That is what you did."

Closing — 2 minutes

Full prayer set, student leader. Food Offering Prayer included.

Teacher's closing line: one sentence that holds the whole unit: "Four classes, one person, one sustained wish — that is the Medicine Buddha's practice, and it has been yours."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author):

Carry forward: The teacher reads all aspirations between C3 and C4 — not to edit, but to know what is in the room before the sharing begins. If an aspiration is still too vague to be genuinely specific (paragraph 2 does not name an actual suffering the person is carrying), speak with the student before class: "Before you read, I want to ask you — is there a more specific way to name what they're carrying?" This conversation can happen at the door. It is not a correction; it is a final invitation to accuracy.

If a student's aspiration reveals something concerning — a chosen person in serious crisis, or language that suggests the student themselves is in distress — the teacher follows the school's pastoral care protocol after class. The sharing circle is not the moment for this. The teacher holds what they hear and acts after.

Aspirations are the Unit 3 portfolio artefact. Collect them after the sharing. Return them to students before the end of the school year, or keep them in the class portfolio archive as the teacher's school policy directs.

Unit 4 is Panglhabsol. The transition is this: students have been healers (Unit 3), teachers (Unit 2), and investigators of suffering and empathy (Unit 1). In Unit 4 they become honouring practitioners — they honour what has been transmitted to them and the figures who have stood at the threshold of Tibetan Buddhist life. This is a turning toward gratitude. It is a fitting close for a year that began with cruelty and ends with ceremony.

Sensitive content note: this class may carry significant emotional weight. A student whose chosen person is a dying grandparent, a parent with serious illness, or someone in acute suffering will read their aspiration in a room of peers. The teacher's steadiness — not warmth performed, but actual composure — is the most important element of the container. If the teacher is moved, that is also acceptable. What is not acceptable is commentary, explanation, or management of the emotional temperature of the room. Let what is real be real.

Spiral connections: Ngowa dedication prayer (embedded throughout the curriculum since Grade 1 — students know this prayer in their bodies); Unit 1 empathy framework and the position paper (Grade 7 — the arc from analysis to action to aspiration); Unit 2 Teaching Day and the three-part reflection (Grade 7 — the experience of directed care for a specific other person's understanding).
