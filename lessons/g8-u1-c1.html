The Wheel, Revisited

The Wheel of Life image → the philosophical mechanism by which the mind perpetuates its own suffering

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleRetrieval as reframing
Bloom's levelEvaluate
LessonG8.U1.C1

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The Wheel of Life image → the philosophical mechanism by which the mind perpetuates its own suffering

Cognitive Principle Featured: Retrieval as reframing — prior knowledge recalled from memory is not merely reviewed but interrogated at a higher cognitive level, making the familiar strange in a productive way

Bloom's Level: Evaluate — students compare what they understood in Grade 5 with what they can understand now, and begin to analyse the Twelve Links as a causal system

Prior Class Summary: This is the first class of Grade 8. Students have completed seven years of study, including Teaching Day in Grade 7, Medicine Buddha practice, and a formal declaration of gratitude and intention at the close of Grade 7. They arrive carrying the whole curriculum and the question the year poses: "What is the mind, really?"

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the names and one-line definitions of all Twelve Links of Dependent Origination in sequence, and the Sanskrit term Pratītyasamutpāda Students will UNDERSTAND that the Wheel of Life is not merely a cosmological image but a philosophical map of the causal mechanism by which the mind perpetuates suffering — and by which that perpetuation can be interrupted Students will BE ABLE TO draw the Twelve Links as a causal chain with brief annotations, and articulate the difference between their Grade 5 understanding of the Wheel and their current understanding Students will FEEL the particular recognition that comes from encountering a familiar teaching at a depth that makes it genuinely new — the intellectual equivalent of returning home and finding it has always been larger than you knew

Materials Checklist

  • Projected or printed image of the Wheel of Life — large enough for the class to read the details (the rim's twelve links should be visible)
  • Paper and pencils for chain drawings (one per student)
  • Students' own paper or notebooks for annotations
  • Plan B: If projection fails, have 2–3 printed copies of the Wheel image for the class to pass around or share; the lesson proceeds with students drawing from description rather than from the projected image

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 8 prayer set, led by the student leader of the day. Sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Tsog Due pa (NEW — introduced today for the first time).

Before the Tsog Due pa: The teacher pauses and speaks directly: "Before we chant the last prayer — I want to tell you what it is. The Tsog Due pa is the Tsok Offering prayer. It is new this year. The Tsok offering is a feast offering — an accumulation of merit through generosity and dedication. We offer what we have: our practice, our attention, the good of what we have done. It is the most complete form of offering in the tradition. We will chant it together for the first time now." Then chant.

The student leader conducts the full sequence. The teacher participates, does not lead.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

The teacher addresses the class without displaying the Wheel image yet:

"You studied the Wheel of Life in Grade 5. Without looking at anything — what do you remember? How much of it can you reconstruct from memory? Call out what you know."

Students call out whatever comes: the six realms, the three poisons at the centre (the pig, the snake, the bird), the figure outside the Wheel pointing to liberation, the twelve links on the outer rim, Yama holding the Wheel, the hungry ghosts, the hell realms. Take everything offered. Write nothing down — this is oral and fast.

After one to two minutes of retrieval: "Good. Three years later and you remember more than you think. Now: what do you remember about the twelve links specifically? Anyone?" Take whatever comes — even partial, even uncertain. Do not correct yet.

"Hold all of that. Today we go back into this image — but at a completely different depth than Grade 5. What you knew then was accurate. What you are about to understand is the reason the teaching exists."

Strong response if students remember a great deal: "Then let's see if what you remember is what the teaching actually says — or what you remember the teaching saying. Those are not always the same thing."

Fallback if students remember little: "That's exactly right — this is why we return. Three years is a long time, and the Wheel of Life is not simple. Let's rebuild it together, piece by piece, with older eyes."

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Display or reveal the Wheel of Life image now.

"You have seen this image before. You drew sections of it in Grade 5. Today we are not drawing it — we are reading it. These are different activities."

Walk through the image layer by layer. The tone is philosophical examination, not a tour.

The hub: "The three animals at the centre — what are they? The pig, the snake, the bird. In Grade 5 you named them: ignorance, hatred, desire. You called them the three poisons. Today: what is ignorance, philosophically? Not 'not knowing facts.' The Sanskrit term is avidyā — the fundamental misapprehension of the nature of reality itself. Taking the self as fixed and permanent. Taking the world as stable. Taking happiness as something found in external conditions. This is the error that drives everything else in the Wheel. It is not stupidity. It is a structural misreading of what is real. That is what sits at the centre."

The rim: "The outer rim: twelve sections. In Grade 5 you knew 'twelve links' as a label. Now: what are they, in sequence, and why does the sequence matter? Because this is not a list — it is a causal chain. Each one arises from the one before it. Together they describe the precise mechanism by which the mind creates the conditions for its own continued suffering. And — this is the teaching's central claim — if it is a causal chain, then it can be interrupted."

Introduce the Twelve Links in sequence — read each one aloud, give the Sanskrit, and the one-line definition:

  1. Ignorance (avidyā) — fundamental misapprehension of the nature of reality
  2. Mental formations / volitional activity (saṃskāra) — habits and intentions shaped by ignorance
  3. Consciousness (vijñāna) — awareness conditioned by those habits
  4. Name and form (nāmarūpa) — the psychophysical person arising in that consciousness
  5. Six sense bases (ṣaḍāyatana) — the six doors of perception
  6. Contact (sparśa) — the meeting of sense base, object, and consciousness
  7. Feeling tone (vedanā) — the pleasant / unpleasant / neutral quality of each contact
  8. Craving (tṛṣṇā) — the response to feeling tone: grasping the pleasant, pushing away the unpleasant
  9. Clinging (upādāna) — craving hardening into attachment
  10. Becoming (bhava) — the process of actively creating conditions for continued existence
  11. Birth (jāti) — arising into a new existence
  12. Old age and death (jarāmaraṇa) — the inevitable result of birth; and with it, the suffering that sends the chain back to Link 1

Do not slow down to explain each link in depth — that is C2's work. The purpose here is the sequence and the causal logic of it.

Vocabulary Moment: "The Sanskrit term Pratītyasamutpāda — Dependent Origination, or Dependent Arising. Pratītya means 'having met with'; samutpāda means 'arising.' The doctrine: nothing arises independently. Everything that exists does so in dependence on conditions. This is not a metaphysical hypothesis about the universe. It is a precise description of the mechanism by which suffering perpetuates itself — and by which it can be interrupted. That is why this teaching is liberating, not simply philosophical."

Body Moment: "Close your eyes. Find a neutral sensation — the feeling of your hands resting in your lap. Just that. Now: notice whether there is any pleasant or unpleasant quality to it. Not much — it's your hands in your lap. Now notice: does any wish arise? Even the smallest wish for it to be different — warmer, more comfortable, gone? That wish — arising from a feeling tone in response to a simple, neutral sensation — is Links Seven and Eight. You just watched it happen."

Pause. Let this sit. Then: "Eyes open. The Wheel is not abstract. It is this."

Emotional Hook: The recognition — not explained, but allowed — that the mechanism being described is operational right now, in this room, in each person's experience. The teaching is not about an ancient cosmology. It is a live description of what is happening in this moment.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: what is the most important link in the chain — the one that, if interrupted, would stop everything? Why that one? You have two minutes."

Pairs speak. Teacher listens to two or three conversations — gathering positions for the discussion to come in C5. No class debrief of this yet. "Hold your answer. We will come back to this question in four classes."

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Students draw the Twelve Links as a causal chain — not the Wheel's visual, just the sequence. Twelve boxes or circles, connected by arrows, each labelled with the name (English and Sanskrit if the student knows it) and a brief annotation of what it means.

This is the before-map: what they understand before the deep philosophical analysis of C2 and C3. It is not graded. It is a document of their current understanding, which they will carry and add to across the unit.

Teacher circulates. Specific questions:

  • "What's the arrow between Link 7 and Link 8 saying? What causes craving to arise from feeling tone — what's the mechanism?"
  • "Is Link 1 the cause of Link 2, or is it more complicated than that?"
  • "What's the difference between craving (Link 8) and clinging (Link 9)? They sound similar."

Students who finish quickly: annotate the chain with questions — things they don't yet understand about the link's meaning or its relationship to the next. These questions will be answered across C2 and C3.

Debrief — 5 minutes

"The Wheel of Life has been in your life since Grade 5. You've been living inside the sequence it describes for much longer than that — since before this school, since before you could name any of it. The difference between Grade 5 and Grade 8 is not that the teaching changed. It's that you can now see the mechanism. You can read the Wheel as a philosophical argument, not just as a map of realms. That is what the rest of this unit builds."

Final question, not for answering aloud — for holding: "If the chain can be interrupted — at any point — what does that tell you about the nature of the mind that is doing the interrupting?"

Closing — 3 minutes

Full Grade 8 prayer set. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer added at close.

Teacher's closing line, spoken after the final prayer: "You have seen this image before. You will not see it the same way again."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The body moment — Links 7 and 8 in real time — is the pedagogical centre of C1. If students genuinely had the experience of watching a micro-craving arise from a neutral sensation, the philosophical analysis in C2 will land in their bodies, not only in their minds. If they didn't quite arrive there — perhaps the instruction moved too fast, or the room was unsettled — revisit the sensation exercise quietly at the start of C2 before moving into the cluster analysis. Do not skip it. The chain drawings are the unit's primary scaffold: students carry them across all five classes and annotate them as their understanding deepens. Treat them as living documents, not a one-class assignment. Spiral note: the Wheel of Life's three poisons were named in Grade 5 (centre of the Wheel, six realms, the figure pointing to liberation); their psychological operation in relationships was examined in Grade 7 Unit 1 (the bullying unit, where aversion and ignorance drove harm between people). Students who did Grade 7 will immediately feel the connection between aversion (Link 8, pushing away) and ignorance (Link 1, the root misapprehension). Surface this connection explicitly if a student reaches for it: "Yes — exactly. In Grade 7 you saw what those three poisons do in a classroom. Now you are seeing the mechanism underneath what you observed then."
