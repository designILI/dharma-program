Spotting the Moment

Pausing to look up and around the room → training the attention to see opportunities for compassion before they pass

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleMetacognition
Bloom's levelApply
LessonG3.U2.C2

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Pausing to look up and around the room → training the attention to see opportunities for compassion before they pass Cognitive Principle Featured: Metacognition — students are asked not only to act, but to observe their own attention: what did I notice, when did I notice it, and what happened in the gap between noticing and acting? Bloom's Level: Apply — students apply the three-step spotting technique to a real scan of the room; early Analyze as they begin to name why the moment passes before they respond Prior Class Summary: C1 introduced the core metaphor (kindness is a muscle), the vocabulary word deliberate, the fist/open gesture, and the Kind Acts map. Each student committed to one specific kind act before this class.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW three specific habits that help a person spot an opportunity for compassion: slow down, look up, ask whether anyone nearby is struggling Students will UNDERSTAND that the moment is often missed not because someone is unkind, but because attention is pointed elsewhere Students will BE ABLE TO report honestly on what happened with their C1 commitment — success, failure, or the almost-moment Students will FEEL the quiet recognition that they have already missed moments this week — and that this is the ordinary starting point, not a reason for shame

Materials Checklist

  • The Kind Acts map from C1, already on the wall
  • Markers or pencils for each student to add to the map
  • Index cards or sticky notes for documenting their act or attempt since C1
  • Plan B: If any student forgot or did not carry out their commitment, have index cards ready for them to write "what I noticed but didn't do — and what got in the way." An honest near-miss is as valuable a map entry as a completed act. Do not leave blank spaces on the map; document the attempt instead.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full prayer set, led by the student leader for this class. Prayers in order: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer. (Food Offering Prayer at closing only.)

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

The teacher begins directly: "Last class you each made a commitment — one specific kind act you were going to do before today. Let's find out what happened. Raise your hand if you did it."

Pause. Take note of who raises a hand, who doesn't, who is uncertain.

"And raise your hand if you tried and it didn't quite work."

"And raise your hand if you forgot entirely."

Then: "I want to hear from all three groups. What happened? What got in the way?" Take 4–5 responses — go slowly here. This is not a performance review. Students who failed or forgot are not in trouble; their answer is exactly as useful as the success stories. Listen specifically for: timing (the moment passed too fast), embarrassment (didn't want to be seen trying), forgetting (the day got busy), uncertainty (wasn't sure how). Name what you hear: "That's a timing problem. That's an attention problem. That's a courage problem. These are all real things — and they're all what we're training."

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

"Here is the question for today: if most of us missed at least one moment — how do we train ourselves to catch it before it passes?"

The teacher tells a short, specific, first-person story — a moment on the way to or from class when someone needed something and most people walked past. (The teacher can tell this as something they personally observed or imagined — it should feel real, not hypothetical. If retellings of this type of story can be read verbatim, the following structure works: set the scene in three sentences — where, who, what was happening. Then: what most people did. Then: what one person did. The gap between the two is the teaching.) "Why didn't they stop? They weren't cruel. They didn't dislike the person. The moment appeared and was gone before they recognised what it was."

"Here is the enemy of compassion in action: not cruelty — the moment that passes while your attention is somewhere else."

Then: "How do you train yourself to spot the moment? Three things:

  1. Slow down slightly.
  2. Look up from what you're doing.
  3. Ask: is anyone near me struggling right now?

That's it. Very simple. Very specific. Five seconds."

Vocabulary Moment: "We have prayed the Sem kye — the aspiration for bodhicitta, the wish to attain awakening for the benefit of all beings. There is a companion to this: bodhicitta in action. The aspiration is the wish. Bodhicitta in action is when the wish becomes a deed. What we practise in this unit is bodhicitta in action — every time you spot the moment and act, however small."

Body Moment: "Stop. Look up. Look around the room right now — slowly. Take ten seconds. Look at each person. Is there anyone here who looks like they might need something?" Ten seconds of genuine, unhurried looking. Let it be slightly uncomfortable. Then: "That was the practice. It took ten seconds. You can do that anywhere, at any point in the day. You just did it."

Emotional Hook: The almost-moment — the moment you almost helped and then didn't. Most students can name one from the past week without difficulty. This class holds the almost-moment as the starting point for learning, not as evidence of failure. Name this explicitly: "The almost-moment is not a lesser thing than the act. The fact that you noticed — even if you didn't act — means your attention is moving in the right direction. We build from there."

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: a moment you spotted this week — where you noticed someone needed something. What did you notice? And what did you do?"

Both outcomes are equally valid: noticed and acted, or noticed and didn't act. The point is the noticing. Allow 2 minutes for partners to talk, then take 3–4 responses from the class. When a student shares a near-miss, ask: "What do you think you would need to do differently to catch that moment next time?"

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Two parts:

Part 1 — Map entry (5–8 min): Each student adds to the Kind Acts map — documenting what they did, attempted, or noticed since C1, in one sentence or a quick drawing. If a student completed their act: write what happened. If they attempted it but it didn't fully work: write what they tried and what got in the way. If they noticed a moment but didn't act: write the moment. No entry is empty. Teacher circulates to help with wording.

Part 2 — New commitment (8–10 min): Each student commits to their next specific act before C3 — this time, one deliberate step harder than the last. "Push the muscle a little further. If last time you committed to not laughing at your sibling, this time commit to actually saying something kind when they're upset." Help students calibrate: harder doesn't mean impossible. It means a little more intentional effort, a slightly bigger cost.

Students add the new commitment to the map — a second entry, beside or below their first.

Debrief — 5 minutes

"Spotting the moment is the first skill. You cannot act if you don't see the opportunity. And seeing the opportunity — that is already something. It means your attention is pointed in the right direction. Before this unit, some of you were not looking. Now you are looking. That has changed."

One question to leave them with: "Between now and next class: how many times will you slow down, look up, and ask 'is anyone near me struggling?' Let's say three times. Just three. Before next class."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set. Student leader leads. Food Offering Prayer included. Teacher's closing line: "Today you learned that most opportunities for kindness are not missed because of cruelty — they are missed because of where our attention is. May we each carry a little more of our attention outward. May whatever good came from this class go to all beings."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Do not skip the PKA follow-up on C1 commitments — this is the real curriculum of Unit 2. The honest accounting (what happened, what stopped you) is where students develop a vocabulary for the difficulty of compassion in action. By C3, students should be able to name at least two specific things that make acting hard: timing, embarrassment, effort, uncertainty about what is needed. Listen for these across the class and name them when you hear them. Spiral connection: the three-step spotting technique (slow down, look up, ask) will recur implicitly in Grade 5's Wheel of Life work, where students examine what keeps beings in cycles of habitual reaction. The same three steps are the antidote: pause, attend, respond rather than react. You need not name this now, but note which students take the three-step practice seriously — they are building a habit that will support later curriculum.
