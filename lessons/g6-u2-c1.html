The In-Between

The pause between one breath and the next — the gap that is always there → the Tibetan Buddhist teaching of the Bardo as a structured map of the intermediate state between death and rebirth

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleExperiential bridging from the familiar to the unfamiliar
Bloom's levelAnalyze
LessonG6.U2.C1

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The pause between one breath and the next — the gap that is always there → the Tibetan Buddhist teaching of the Bardo as a structured map of the intermediate state between death and rebirth

Cognitive Principle Featured: Experiential bridging from the familiar to the unfamiliar — students are first asked to name an in-between state they have lived through before the cosmological framework is introduced. The breath-gap practice then makes the Bardo concept experientially available at a micro-scale, so the large-scale teaching has a felt reference point rather than being purely abstract.

Bloom's Level: Analyze — students receive the three-Bardo structure as a system and begin mapping it. The question is not "what does each Bardo mean in isolation?" but "how do the three relate to each other — and how does this system describe something about how the mind behaves under pressure?"

Prior Class Summary: C4 of Unit 1 was the culminating reflection on rebirth. Students compared their starting position to their evolved thinking and wrote the Unit 1 philosophical reflection. They carry the question "What continues — and does the answer change how I live?" and the image of the mountains of Tibet as witness to generations of practitioners facing the same territory.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the Tibetan term Bardo (བར་དོ, bar do), its literal meaning ("between two" / "intermediate state"), and the names and basic descriptions of the three Bardos taught in the Bardo Tödöl: the Bardo of dying, the Bardo of dharmata, and the Bardo of becoming.

Students will UNDERSTAND that the Bardo teachings are practical — not only about literal death, but about how the mind behaves in any extreme disorientation — and that the quality of awareness cultivated now is the quality that will be available in the Bardo.

Students will BE ABLE TO begin a Bardo concept map with the three zones named and annotated in their own words, leaving space to develop across the unit.

Students will FEEL the recognition that in-between states are already familiar — that the Bardo teaching names something students already know from their own lives, scaled up beyond ordinary experience.

Materials Checklist

  • Large paper or card (A3 or equivalent), one per student — for the Bardo concept map begun today and developed across C1–C5
  • Plan B: If large paper is unavailable, use regular paper in landscape orientation. Students can tape two sheets together in C3 if the map grows beyond one sheet.
  • Pens and coloured pencils or markers for the concept map (labelling and annotating)
  • Writing journals (optional — for notes alongside the map)
  • The full Grade 6 prayer cards or sheets (Units 1–2 set — no Dolma yet)
  • Student leader list
  • A safe place to store concept maps between classes (teacher holds them)

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set (Units 1–2 set): Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Note: The Food Offering Prayer appears in closing only. The Dolma (Tara Prayer) is not yet introduced — it enters in Unit 3.

Student leader leads. After prayers settle, teacher speaks:

"Unit 1 asked: what continues after death? Unit 2 asks: what happens in the space between? Between one moment and the next. Between one life and the next. The Tibetan Buddhist tradition has a detailed answer to that question. We are going to study it carefully. It is called the Bardo."

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "Before anything about death and rebirth — tell me about a time in your own life when you were between. Between two schools. Between two friendships. Between two ways of being. A time when the old thing had ended and the new thing hadn't started yet. What did that in-between feel like?"

Take 3–4 responses. Receive each fully. Listen for the texture: disorientation, uncertainty, a kind of hovering. Reflect back what you hear: "So there was a sense of not quite knowing where to stand." "The familiar thing was gone but the new thing wasn't yet real." "A kind of waiting that didn't feel like rest."

Then: "That experience — the in-between — is exactly where we're going today. But on a much larger scale. Between lives."

Fallback if students are reluctant: "Has anyone ever changed schools? Or moved to a new place? Or ended a friendship? What did the first few days after feel like — before the new normal arrived?" These are more concrete in-between experiences that most students will be able to name.

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Teacher introduces the Bardo teaching systematically. Write the three names on the board as they are introduced.

The word:

"Bardo — Tibetan: བར་དོ, bar do. Literally: 'between two' or 'intermediate state.' In everyday Tibetan, bardo can refer to any intermediate state — even the gap between sleeping and waking. In the context of the Bardo Tödöl — the text known in the West as the Tibetan Book of the Dead — it refers to the specific states consciousness passes through between death and rebirth."

The three Bardos:

Write and speak each name clearly:

1. The Bardo of dying (Tibetan: chi kha'i bardo) "The process of death itself — as consciousness disengages from the body. This can take hours, or days. The elements of the body dissolve one by one. What remains, as all physical and mental structures fall away, is awareness itself — exposed, unfiltered. The teaching for this Bardo is simple: stay present. Don't panic. Don't grasp for what is dissolving."

2. The Bardo of dharmata (Tibetan: chos nyid bardo) "The bardo of 'the nature of reality' — dharmata means 'the nature of things as they actually are.' After the dying process completes, there is a period of intense luminosity. The ground nature of the mind — its fundamental clarity — is directly exposed. Peaceful and wrathful visions arise. These are not external events; they are projections of the mind's own nature, its own love and fear. If they can be recognised as such — recognised as arising from one's own awareness — liberation is possible."

3. The Bardo of becoming (Tibetan: srid pa'i bardo) "If the first two opportunities are missed — if the ground luminosity is not recognised, if the visions are not recognised as mind — consciousness enters a wandering state. It is drawn toward rebirth by old habits and accumulated tendencies. The 'becoming' Bardo is the process of being drawn back into a new life, shaped by the momentum of what came before."

The key insight — practical application:

"The Bardo teachings are not only for people who are dying. They are practical because the same mind that gets lost in the Bardo is the mind we have right now. How we meet difficulty, disorientation, and uncertainty right now is practice for the Bardo. A mind that has learned to stay present under ordinary pressure has a resource available to it at the moments of extraordinary pressure — including death."

Vocabulary Moment: "Rigpa — Tibetan: རིག་པ. Often translated 'awareness' or 'recognition.' In the Bardo teaching, rigpa is the capacity to recognise one's own nature — to see the ground luminosity as what it is, rather than being swept away by panic or confusion. Practising mindfulness now is practising rigpa. Every moment of clear, stable awareness is a small rehearsal."

Body Moment: "Right now — close your eyes. Let your breath settle. Follow one complete breath. The in-breath rises. The in-breath ends. Before the out-breath begins — there is a moment. A gap. Very small. Very still. Feel it." Students sit in genuine attention to the breath-gap. Allow fifteen seconds of real quiet. Then: "That gap — extended enormously, scaled up beyond anything ordinary — is what the Bardo teaching is attempting to describe. You already know this state. It is not foreign. It is just very, very small in ordinary experience."

Emotional Hook: The recognition that the Bardo teaching names something already familiar — that the in-between is not foreign territory but territory students have already crossed in smaller forms. The teaching makes the familiar strange and the strange familiar. Teacher names it: "The Tibetan Book of the Dead was written for people facing the end of their lives. It turns out to be useful for anyone facing any major transition. That is not an accident — it describes something true about how the mind behaves when the familiar ground is no longer there."

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: what in-between state in your own life was hardest to navigate? What — if anything — helped you find your way through it?"

Structure: each partner speaks for about ninety seconds. Teacher circulates.

What to listen for: Students naming things that helped — stability, someone who stayed present with them, a practice, a routine. These are already, functionally, forms of what the Bardo teaching calls "support for awareness in disorientation." Do not name this yet — let it sit for now.

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Students begin their Bardo concept map.

Distribute large paper. Teacher: "This map is the making artefact for Unit 2. It grows across the next five classes. Today: just the structure and the first layer of annotation."

Instructions:

  1. Draw three distinct zones on the paper — label them with the three Bardo names (the teacher keeps both Tibetan and translated names on the board for reference).
  2. Under each name, write a brief description in your own words — not the teacher's words, not the Tibetan text. What you understand this Bardo to be, as of today.
  3. Leave significant white space in each zone — the map will be annotated and expanded in later classes.

Teacher circulates — checking that students are capturing the essential character of each Bardo (not just copying the names), asking: "Tell me what happens in the second Bardo — in your own words." If a student says "I don't know yet," that is honest: "Write 'I don't know yet — to be developed.' That's better than leaving it blank."

Scaffold for students who are uncertain how to begin: "Draw a horizontal line across the page — or three boxes side by side. Put the dying Bardo on the left, dharmata in the middle, becoming on the right. That's enough structure to start."

Extension for students who finish quickly: "Add a fourth zone outside the three Bardos — label it 'what this teaching says about ordinary life.' What can you already say goes in that zone?"

Debrief — 5 minutes

Opening question: "The Bardo teaching was given for people facing death — literally. But it turns out to be useful for anyone facing any major transition. Why do you think that is? What do the two situations — dying and major life transition — have in common?"

Take 2–3 responses.

"The Bardo teaching has lasted over a thousand years because it describes something true about how the mind behaves under pressure — any extreme pressure, not only death. That is why it is worth studying. Not because death is coming soon — it isn't, for most of us, not yet — but because what it describes about the mind is always relevant."

Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full closing prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Teacher collects concept maps to keep safely for next class.

Teacher's closing line: "You felt the gap between your breaths today. Next class, we go deeper into what that gap becomes."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The Bardo concept map begun today is the unit's making artefact and the portfolio piece for Unit 2. It must be kept safely between classes — students should not take it home until C5. The map grows across all six classes; the architecture of today's map (three zones, space for annotation) must be preserved. This unit should feel more intimate and more serious than Unit 1. Unit 1 was philosophical analysis at a measured distance; Unit 2 is territory — the class is walking into a specific, detailed, ancient map of what happens at and after death. The tone shifts accordingly: more careful, more personal, more experiential. Hold the room with particular attention from this class forward. The breath-gap practice is a recurring touchstone across the Bardo unit — the teacher may return to it briefly in subsequent classes as a grounding device. It is simple, reliable, and available to any student at any moment. Spiral connection: the concept of rigpa (awareness/recognition) is the experiential thread connecting Bardo teaching to meditation practice (introduced Grade 3 and sustained across the curriculum). Students have been practicing awareness of breath for three years. Today they learn the name for the quality that practice is cultivating.
