The Suffering Behind Cruelty

The inner life of the person who bullies → the Buddhist framework for understanding how all harmful action arises from the three poisons

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principlePerspective-taking via felt experience
Bloom's levelEvaluate
LessonG7.U1.C2

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The inner life of the person who bullies → the Buddhist framework for understanding how all harmful action arises from the three poisons

Cognitive Principle Featured: Perspective-taking via felt experience — the body moment places the student inside the emotional starting point of cruelty (shame, the impulse to redirect), making the psychological account not just intellectual but physically remembered.

Bloom's Level: Evaluate — students assess the adequacy of four explanatory frameworks for bullying behaviour (fear of exposure, disconnection, learned behaviour, empathy deficits) against the Buddhist framing, and begin forming a judgment about what understanding cruelty actually requires of them in response.

Prior Class Summary: C1 disrupted the simple story — distinguishing bullying from conflict, naming its psychological roots in fear, powerlessness, and shame. Students wrote Journal Entry 1 and held a ten-second body moment of genuine powerlessness. They carry the recognition that the engine of cruelty is not alien.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the four psychological patterns most commonly associated with bullying behaviour: fear of exposure, disconnection from belonging, learned behaviour, and empathy deficits — and how these map onto the three poisons in Buddhist teaching Students will UNDERSTAND that bystanders are not neutral — their response (or non-response) shapes what gets internalised by everyone who witnesses the situation Students will BE ABLE TO write a second journal entry that honestly addresses the question: does understanding the cause change the responsibility to stop the behaviour? Students will FEEL the specific discomfort of recognising shame's impulse to redirect attention outward — to make someone else the problem — as the engine of most cruelty; and the recognition that this impulse is familiar, not foreign

Materials Checklist

  • Journals from C1 (or fresh paper if journals are not brought)
  • Pens or pencils, one per student
  • Board and chalk or marker for vocabulary and mapping
  • Grade 7 prayer set visible (Rewo Sang Chod second chanting — no re-introduction needed, but reference the meaning briefly)
  • Plan B (for journal entry): If the body moment generates significant emotional activation and students are not ready to write privately, replace the journal entry with a structured pair-conversation: each student tells their partner one thing they understand now that they didn't before, and one thing that still feels unresolved. The teacher circulates and listens. Journal entry moves to the opening of C3.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 7 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader.

Before Rewo Sang Chod: "This is the second time we chant the Sang offering. You know what it means now — the smoke carrying our prayers outward, to all beings. As we chant it today, we are specifically dedicating our understanding in this unit: may it serve beings who are suffering." Then chant. Food Offering Prayer at closing only.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

"Last class we said: bullying is a mistaken strategy the suffering self uses to manage its own pain. Today: what does that strategy actually look like from the inside? What is the person doing the bullying actually experiencing — in their body, in their mind — in the moment they're doing it?"

Take responses. Write key words on the board. Receive without evaluating.

"Most people don't know what to say to that question — because we almost never ask it. We look at the behaviour from the outside and label it. Today we're going to try to look from the inside."

Strong response: If a student offers something accurate (e.g., "they feel powerful," "they might feel nervous," "maybe they enjoy it"), stay with it: "Is that feeling — [whatever they named] — something you've ever felt? Even in a small way? What was that like?"

Fallback: "Think about the last time you saw someone say something cruel to another person. What do you imagine the person saying it actually felt right before they said it? Immediately before — not the plan, just the feeling." This grounds the abstract question in a concrete observable moment.

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Four patterns in the inner life of the person who bullies.

Research and observation consistently identify four overlapping patterns. Write each on the board as it is named:

1. Fear of exposure. There is deep shame about some aspect of the self — something the person is desperate to keep hidden. Cruelty toward others is a deflection strategy: if I make them the focus, no one is looking at me. This is not a calculated strategy in most cases — it happens automatically, as a reflex of shame.

2. Disconnection from belonging. A felt sense of being on the outside of a group one wants to be part of. The attempt to establish belonging through dominance: if I can't belong by being included, I can belong by being powerful. This is a profoundly mistaken strategy — dominance creates fear, not belonging — but it makes sense from the inside.

3. Learned behaviour. Having been bullied, or having witnessed bullying without intervention, and having internalised the message: this is how power works. The child who witnesses cruelty and sees no response — from an adult, from a bystander — may internalise it as simply the truth about how social life operates. This is one reason bystanders matter: what they do or don't do teaches something.

4. Empathy deficits. Not necessarily a permanent condition — often a temporary failure to access the capacity for empathy under pressure, fear, or pain. The person who bullies may, in other circumstances, show genuine empathy. Under the conditions of their own suffering, that capacity collapses.

"Now: these are the psychological descriptions. The Buddhist framing asks: what poisons are these manifestations of?"

Draw the three poisons on the board — aversion, ignorance, desire (students know these from Grade 5, Wheel of Life). Map:

  • Fear of exposure → aversion (to one's own pain, one's own shame — the desperate turning away from it)
  • Disconnection from belonging → desire (grasping after belonging, after security) combined with ignorance (of the interconnectedness of beings — the false sense that dominance creates what one actually needs)
  • Learned behaviour → ignorance (having internalised a false understanding of how power works)
  • Empathy deficits under pressure → ignorance (temporary collapse of the understanding that others feel)

"This framing doesn't excuse anything. It explains. And understanding it accurately is the prerequisite for responding wisely — rather than just reacting."

What practice asks. Not indifference, not vengeance, not even (necessarily) forgiveness first. What practice asks first is understanding: the capacity to look at harmful action and see the suffering behind it, while still refusing to let it continue. These two things — seeing the suffering and refusing to allow the harm — are not in contradiction. They are both required.

Vocabulary Moment:

Write on the board: internalise — to take something from the external world and make it part of one's own inner framework, one's unexamined sense of how things work.

"A child who witnesses cruelty without intervention may internalise the message: 'this is how things work.' Not as a decision — as an absorption. This is one reason bystanders are not neutral. Their response — or their non-response — shapes what gets internalised by everyone watching. What gets internalised becomes the framework through which the next situation is interpreted."

Body Moment:

"I'm going to ask you to recall a moment of shame — not guilt about something you did, but shame. Shame is different from guilt. Guilt says: 'I did something bad.' Shame says: 'I am bad — who I am is the problem.' Think of a moment when you felt that. A specific moment. Hold it for fifteen seconds. Don't narrate it — just be in it."

Fifteen seconds of silence.

"Now: feel the impulse that follows shame. The desire to redirect — to make someone else the focus, to make someone else the problem. You may not have acted on it. But feel whether the impulse was there."

Five more seconds.

"That impulse — that reflex — is the starting point of most cruelty. You've just felt where it begins."

Emotional Hook: The recognition that the engine of cruelty runs on the same fuel as one's own shame and fear. Not as a confession — as an understanding. This is not meant to excuse anything. It is meant to make the response honest rather than performative.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: if someone is bullying because they are suffering — what does that mean for how we respond? Does understanding the cause change the responsibility to stop the behaviour? Or does it change something else?"

Give two full minutes of genuine partner conversation before taking responses.

What to listen for: The distinction between explanation and excuse. Students who conflate them (understanding = letting it go) need gentle challenge: "Does a doctor understand the cause of a disease in order to excuse it — or in order to treat it?" Students who make the distinction cleanly have arrived at the unit's core insight.

Take 2–3 responses to the whole group. Do not resolve the tension — it should remain productive. C3 will deepen it.

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Journal Entry 2: "The suffering behind the cruelty — what I understand about it now, and what it means for how I should respond."

Instructions: "This is not an essay. Write what you actually think. Begin with what you now understand about where cruelty comes from — in your own words, not mine. Then: does understanding it change what you think the right response is? And: is there anything in today's class that you resist — that you're not yet willing to accept? Write that too."

The teacher circulates with quiet specific questions: "What's the hardest part of this to accept?" "What did the body moment tell you?" "Have you written what you actually believe, or what you think you're supposed to believe?"

Students who finish early: "Add a paragraph on the bystander point — what does it mean that the bystander's response shapes what gets internalised? What responsibility does that place on someone who witnesses cruelty?"

Students struggling to begin: "Start with one sentence: 'The thing I now understand about the person who bullies is...' That's your first line."

Debrief — 5 minutes

"Next class is the Three Chairs exercise. You are going to physically and mentally inhabit three perspectives — the person being bullied, the person doing the bullying, and the bystander. To do that well, you need to carry what you know from today: you've already been inside the starting point of cruelty. You felt it in the body moment. That felt knowledge is what makes the Three Chairs exercise real rather than performed. Come ready to go inside all three — not to perform them."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader.

Teacher's closing line: "Buddhism does not ask us to like cruelty or accept it. It asks us to see it accurately. Today we practised that. Next class: we practise sitting inside the experience of all three people in the room where cruelty is happening — including the one who watches."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: This class asks students to sit with a recognition that many will resist — that the engine of cruelty is not alien to them. Some resistance is appropriate and should not be resolved too quickly. The teacher's job is not to make the recognition comfortable; it is to hold the space in which students can encounter it honestly. Do not rush to reassurance.

The body moment (shame and the impulse to redirect) is more charged than C1's body moment (powerlessness). Read the room carefully. If a student shows visible distress, allow them to step out briefly with a quiet word; do not draw the group's attention to it.

C3 requires physical rearrangement and careful facilitation. Read C3 in full before teaching C2. The Three Chairs exercise is emotionally demanding and will surface real feeling — the quality of C2's body moment preparation directly affects the depth of C3.

Spiral connection: Grade 5 — the three poisons (aversion, ignorance, desire) via the Wheel of Life. Students have the language. Use it explicitly: "You know the three poisons from Grade 5 — we studied them through the Wheel of Life. Today we're seeing them operating in a specific, very contemporary context." This connection is worth making verbally; it honours the continuity of the curriculum and activates prior knowledge with genuine relevance.

Spiral connection: Grade 6 — Tara as the figure who does not abandon beings in difficulty. The Buddhist response to cruelty (see the suffering, refuse to let the harm continue) is structurally related to Tara's vow. Worth naming briefly, especially for students who connected with the Tara unit.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child explored the inner life of the person who bullies — the fear, shame, and disconnection that typically drive that behaviour — and how Buddhism frames all harmful action as arising from the three poisons rather than from a bad nature. You can ask them: 'Does understanding why someone bullies change how you think we should respond — and does it change the responsibility to stop it?'"
