Our Diagram

Completing an annotated diagram → the act of articulation as a stage of understanding; putting into words what the hands know

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleGenerative learning
Bloom's levelUnderstand deepening into Analyze
LessonG4.U1.C5

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Completing an annotated diagram → the act of articulation as a stage of understanding; putting into words what the hands know

Cognitive Principle Featured: Generative learning — the act of producing an annotated diagram (rather than copying one) requires students to retrieve, organise, and express what they know. Retrieval and expression each deepen encoding; students who can teach through a diagram understand at a different level than students who can only recognise correct information when shown it.

Bloom's Level: Understand deepening into Analyze — students move from labelling (what is this?) into annotating (what does this teach?). The "what this teaches me about my own mind" annotation is an analytical act: connecting a symbolic meaning to personal inner experience.

Prior Class Summary: C4 was collaborative practice — full sequence, repeated without narration, until the practice began to live in the body. Students carry fluency (or growing fluency) in the physical practice and the felt quality of precision. They arrive today from their hands back to their journals.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW that their completed diagram — seven bowls in correct order with full annotations — serves as a teaching document: something a person who has never made the offering could use to understand what they would be doing and why. Students will UNDERSTAND that articulation is part of learning — that putting what you know into words and images changes how you hold the knowledge; that this is also a form of offering, giving what you know to another. Students will BE ABLE TO complete their annotated diagram with all required elements — name, Sanskrit term, symbolic meaning, and at least one personal annotation per bowl — with sufficient clarity that another person could read it as an introduction to the practice. Students will FEEL the satisfaction of completing something — the particular quality of finishing a piece of work that has accumulated over several classes, that carries real practice in it.

Materials Checklist

  • Student diagram journals — returned at the start of this class (collected after C4 — note: diagram journals were not needed during C4's practice session; they were held by the teacher)
  • Pencils — one per student
  • Coloured pencils or pens — shared sets; not required but support the diagram's legibility
  • The seven Sanskrit terms written on the board for reference: Argham · Padyam · Pushpe · Dhupe · Aloke · Gandhe · Naividya / Shapta
  • Seven offering bowls, set up on the demonstration table — for reference and for the opening retrieval sequence
  • Water vessel — for the brief opening practice sequence
  • Clean cloth
  • Plan B — if the opening practice sequence is not possible (bowls unavailable): Open with the mime sequence only — all seven gestures in order, twice through. The physical grounding is still achieved, though with less fidelity to the actual practice.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 4 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline Prayer (extended form) · Dusum Sangay.

Student leader leads.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Before opening the journals: "Get it in your hands one more time before you put it on paper."

One rapid sequence together at the demonstration table — a representative student (or the teacher) sets up, fills, pauses, and removes while the class watches in silence. Or, if multiple sets are available, everyone does one full sequence at their station.

Then: mime the seven gestures together — one per offering, in order, from memory. No prompting.

"Now your hands know it. Now sit. Now put it on paper."

The transition from body to desk is the curriculum. Do not rush it. The point is that students arrive at their diagrams having just activated physical memory — the knowledge is warm in their hands when they begin to write.

Fallback if time is tight: Skip the full sequence; do the mime only. This takes 60 seconds and still grounds the session in the body before moving to the desk.

Mini Lesson — 5 minutes

"Today you complete your diagram. You've had it open across several classes — names, Sanskrit terms, meanings. Today you finish it and you deepen it."

"Here is the standard: your diagram should be able to teach someone who wasn't here. Not someone who knows the practice — someone who has never seen a seven-bowl offering. They should be able to look at your diagram and understand: what am I doing, in what order, and why does it matter?"

"That is a high standard. It means your annotations need to be complete and clear — not just the name of the bowl, but what it represents and what that representation teaches. Think of your diagram as a letter to someone who needs this practice but doesn't know it yet."

Vocabulary Moment: "The word annotation — a note added to a text or image that explains it. Your annotations are not decoration. They are the teaching itself. A diagram without annotations is a map without labels — you know something is there but not what it means. Your annotations give the diagram its meaning."

Body Moment: One final mime sequence — all seven gestures, in order, together. "Now your hands know it. Now put it on paper."

Emotional Hook: The recognition that this diagram is a record — not just of information learned but of practice done. These hands have poured water into bowls. The diagram carries that.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Before beginning the diagram work: "Tell your partner: what is the one thing about the seven-bowl offering that surprised you most across this unit? What did you not expect to find — in the practice, or in the meanings, or in yourself?"

Give 2 minutes. Take 2–3 responses from the class.

This question serves two purposes: it warms up articulation (students will need to articulate in the diagram work) and it surfaces the genuine learning of the unit — the unexpected things are usually where the deepest understanding lives.

What to listen for: Students who name the precision-as-mindfulness teaching as a surprise ("I didn't expect that the spacing would matter for the mind"), or the idea that the bowls are not about the water ("I thought it was just a ritual, I didn't know they were teaching something"), or something personal ("I didn't expect that pouring slowly would be hard for me"). All of these are evidence of genuine understanding.

Activity — 20–25 minutes

Students work on their annotated diagrams in silence or near-silence. This is focused, individual desk time — quiet enough that the work can be careful.

Required elements for each bowl:

  1. Name (e.g., Drinking water)
  2. Sanskrit term (e.g., Argham)
  3. What it represents (e.g., Cleansing the craving mind / offering the gesture of not-grasping)
  4. At least one personal annotation — what this bowl, or this meaning, teaches the student about their own mind or experience

Write the required elements on the board (or leave the C2 reference visible):

  • Name · Sanskrit term · What it represents · What it teaches me

Optional additions (for students who complete the required elements):

  • A final annotation at the bottom or back of the diagram page — the student's current answer to "Why do we offer?" This revisits the C1 journal entry. Students can look back at what they wrote in C1 and note: what has changed? What do I understand now that I didn't then?
  • A written instruction — one to three sentences telling a reader how to set up the bowls correctly (sequence, spacing, pour, removal).

Teacher circulates throughout. This is not the time for new teaching — it is the time to observe what students have understood, to ask clarifying questions that help students articulate more precisely, and to note where understanding is still incomplete.

Clarifying questions to use in circulation:

  • "Can you say more about what this bowl represents? What is the 'cleansing of craving' in ordinary life — what does craving feel like?"
  • "What did you mean when you wrote 'beauty'? Beauty of what? Offered in what spirit?"
  • "You've written the name and meaning — what does this teach you? What do you notice in yourself when you think about it?"

Brief C5 review for students identified in C4: If two or three students were still losing the sequence or struggling with precision in C4, offer 5 minutes of bowl practice at the start of the activity period before they open their journals. Note: this should be identified before class, not discovered mid-session.

Debrief — 5 minutes

"You have now made the seven-bowl offering with your hands. You have practised the sequence until it began to live there. And now you have explained it with words and images — you have made a diagram that could teach someone who was never in this room."

"One thing remains: Demonstration Day. Next class, you will set up the offering in front of others and narrate it — out loud, to someone who has never done it before. That is the final test of understanding: not whether you can remember it, but whether you can teach it."

"That's the old test. It's been the test for as long as there have been teachers and students. Can you do it? Can you show someone else how? Next class, you find out."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline Prayer (extended form) · Dusum Sangay.

Student leader leads.

Teacher's closing line: "Your diagram is finished. Your hands know the practice. What you have learned is real — you did not just hear about it. Next class: show it."

Collect diagram journals at the end of class. Keep them until Demonstration Day — students may refer to them during C6 if needed, but the goal is narration from knowledge, not from reading.

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Completed diagrams are collected at the end of C5 and held by the teacher until Demonstration Day (C6). Return them at the start of C6 so students can use them as reference if needed — but make clear that the demonstration is not a reading exercise. The diagram is a safety net, not a script.

Before C6: prepare the demonstration space. Set up the shrine or demonstration table in advance. If possible, invite an external audience — another class, a school staff member, the resident teacher, or a senior student. An authentic audience changes the quality of the demonstration in exactly the right direction: it raises the stakes in a healthy way, and it makes the transmission real. Students are not performing for a grade; they are teaching someone who does not know this practice. If no external audience is possible, pairs can demonstrate to each other while the teacher observes — this still works, but loses the quality of genuine transmission.

Spiral connections: The diagram as a teaching document connects forward to units across Grade 4 where students will continue producing annotated artefacts (Unit 2: torma diagram; Unit 4: prayer map). The standard — "this should teach someone who wasn't here" — is a Grade 4 standard that recurs. Establish it firmly here in Unit 1.

The revisiting of the C1 "Why do we offer?" reflection (in the optional extension) is one of the most important moments in the unit if students have time for it. The change between their C1 answer and their C5 answer is the evidence of learning — not just information acquired but perspective shifted. If time permits, invite two or three students to share what changed. If time does not permit, note it for the Demonstration Day debrief in C6.
