Assembling the Portfolio

Selecting the most significant artefacts and drafting the closing declaration → a public vow about what one carries forward as a practitioner

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleGenerative retrieval with articulation
Bloom's levelCreate
LessonG8.U5.C3

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Selecting the most significant artefacts and drafting the closing declaration → a public vow about what one carries forward as a practitioner

Cognitive Principle Featured: Generative retrieval with articulation — the closing declaration is not a summary of what students have learned. It is an articulation of what they have absorbed — the qualities, understandings, and intentions that are genuinely theirs now, as distinct from what was taught to them. The distinction between "what I was taught" and "what I have absorbed" is the most demanding cognitive move of the unit.

Bloom's Level: Create — students produce the first draft of the closing declaration, a three-part structure (what I carry, what changed, what I intend) that will be read aloud as a public vow at the Threshold ceremony

Prior Class Summary: C2 was the full portfolio review — students encountered their own work across eight years, held specific artefacts with their hands, and made notes on what they saw. They carry the arc of the whole curriculum, now visible in their own work.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the three-part structure of the closing declaration — what I carry, what changed, what I intend — and the distinction between each part; what makes a declaration different from a reflection or a summary Students will UNDERSTAND what it means to make a public declaration rather than a private reflection; the concept of a declaration as made in the presence of witnesses and carrying the weight of that witness Students will BE ABLE TO select three to five portfolio artefacts that are most meaningful for presentation at the Threshold; draft a three-part closing declaration that is specific, honest, and directed toward a real intention Students will FEEL the particular gravity of preparing to make something public — the combination of anticipation, vulnerability, and the desire to find words that are actually true

Materials Checklist

  • Student portfolios (from C2 — students should have them)
  • Portfolio review notes from C2 (students should have these)
  • Writing materials — journals or clean paper; this is the primary writing class of Unit 5, so enough time and materials for sustained drafting
  • Closing declaration structure sheet (optional but helpful — a simple printed sheet showing the three-part structure with space for drafting, or written on the board clearly)
  • Timer
  • Plan B: If a student finds the declaration drafting difficult to start (common: this is the most personally demanding writing task of the curriculum), offer a low-stakes entry: "Before you write the declaration, write three sentences that are definitely true about who you are now as a practitioner. Just three true sentences. Then we will look at what you carry from those." This gives the student material to work from rather than facing a blank page with a large task.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 8 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Tsog Due pa · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader rotates.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

Teacher: "From your C2 notes: what surprised you most when you read your own portfolio? Not what you were proud of — what surprised you. Something you had forgotten, something that looked different than you expected, something you saw in the arc that you couldn't see while you were inside it." Take three or four responses. Receive them fully. Then: "Hold that surprise. It will be useful when you write the declaration."

Mini Lesson — 10 minutes

What the closing declaration is

The closing declaration is not a summary of the curriculum. It is not a thank-you note to the school or the teacher. It is not a list of things the student has learned. It is a statement of what the student carries forward as a practitioner — specific, honest, and directed toward the future.

Three parts:

Part 1 — What I carry: The specific qualities, practices, and understandings that are genuinely the student's now. Not what they were taught — what they have absorbed. The distinction matters: being taught something leaves information in the mind. Absorbing something changes how you move through the world. Part 1 names what has been absorbed — and it must be specific. Not "I carry compassion" — what specifically, from what specific class, from what specific practice or making artefact or moment in eight years?

Part 2 — What changed: Something in the student that is genuinely different now than it was at the beginning of the curriculum. Not what they learned — what changed. This part requires honesty and some courage: it means acknowledging that who you were at age six or seven was different from who you are at thirteen or fourteen, and that the curriculum had a hand in the change. What changed, specifically? How do you know?

Part 3 — What I intend: How the student will continue. Not a vague aspiration ("I will be more compassionate") — a specific intention, even a small one. A practice they will keep. A habit of attention they will maintain. One thing they are willing to commit to, in public, at the Threshold ceremony. The Threshold declaration is different from a private journal entry: it is made out loud, in front of the community, and carries the weight of witness.

Vocabulary Moment: "The word declaration — a formal, public statement. When you read your declaration at the Threshold ceremony, you are not sharing a reflection. You are making a declaration — in front of your community, your teachers, your family, yourself, and in some sense the lineage. Declarations are different from intentions: they are made out loud, in the presence of witnesses, and they carry the weight of that witness. The tradition takes declarations seriously. So do you."

Body Moment: Students stand. "This is the posture of someone who is about to make a declaration. Spine upright — not rigid, but present. Eyes forward. Feet on the ground. Hands at your sides or folded in front of you. Feel what this posture asks of the body — the particular quality of standing to say something publicly that you know is true." Hold for thirty seconds. Then sit. "That is the posture you will hold in C5. The words come from this posture."

Emotional Hook: The specific gravity of preparing to make something public — the combination of anticipation, vulnerability, and the desire to find words that are actually true rather than impressive. This is a writing task that the body is involved in, not only the mind.

Activity — 25 minutes (two phases)

Phase 1 — Portfolio selection (8 minutes): Students arrange their portfolio artefacts in order and select three to five pieces that are most meaningful — not most impressive, not most polished. Most meaningful. These are the artefacts they will present at the Threshold ceremony (one to two sentences per artefact, explaining its significance). Students mark their selected pieces and set them aside. Teacher circulates: "Why that one? What does that piece hold that the others don't?"

Phase 2 — Draft the closing declaration (17 minutes): Students write a draft of all three parts. Encourage them to write more than they will read — the draft will be refined between C3 and C4. Teacher circulates with specific, direct prompts:

For Part 1: "Not 'I carry compassion' — what specifically? From which class? From what moment? Can you name the exact practice or artefact or moment when that thing became yours?"

For Part 2: "Not only positive changes. What is something that became more complicated for you over these years? Something you were more certain about at age six than you are now? Honesty in Part 2 matters more than polish."

For Part 3: "One specific intention. What is one practice — even a small one — that you are willing to commit to publicly? Not what you hope for in general — what you will actually do."

Debrief — 2 minutes

Teacher: "Bring your draft to C4. We rehearse the full ceremony — including your declaration — before the real thing. Between now and C4, read your draft once a day. Notice what needs to change."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Tsog Due pa · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Food Offering Prayer. Student leader.

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "What you write today will be read aloud in the Threshold ceremony — in front of the people who love you and the lineage that has held you. Write something true."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Draft declarations should be read by the teacher between C3 and C4. Not for grading — for brief, supportive notes that help each student strengthen their draft. Check specifically: Is Part 1 specific enough (names a practice, class, moment, or artefact — not just a quality like "kindness")? Is Part 2 genuinely honest (includes something that became more complex or uncertain, not only positive growth)? Is Part 3 an actual intention (something the student will do, not just hope for)? Return brief written notes to students at the start of C4, before the rehearsal.

C4 is the full ceremony rehearsal — the prayer set led entirely by students (the teacher sits with the group and does not lead), portfolio presentations (one minute each), declaration readings, Ngowa, and Tsog Due pa. The teacher's role in C4 is observer and refiner. After the rehearsal: specific feedback on timing, clarity, and what to strengthen. Students revise if needed.

C5 must be arranged as a real ceremony before C4 begins. All logistics should be confirmed by the start of C4: room arrangement (chairs in a circle or horseshoe, central surface for portfolio display, shrine or altar at the centre), audience confirmed (parents, teachers, younger students, community members invited), any physical offerings for the Tsog arranged. Do not underestimate this logistics requirement. The Threshold ceremony's quality depends heavily on the care of the preparation.
