Reflecting

Completing the reflection journal by comparing first imagination to lived experience → integration — when something you have done becomes part of how you understand the world

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleMetacognitive Reflection
Bloom's levelApply
LessonG3.U1.C6

Duration: 40 minutes

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Completing the reflection journal by comparing first imagination to lived experience → integration — when something you have done becomes part of how you understand the world

Cognitive Principle Featured: Metacognitive Reflection — the specific structure (first imagination → what actually happened → what I know now) requires students to hold two versions of themselves simultaneously: the self who imagined the release and the self who has done it. This is a cognitively demanding move for eight-year-olds and is only available because the C1 drawings exist as concrete evidence. The before-and-after comparison is not abstract; students can point to it on the page.

Bloom's Level: Apply

Prior Class Summary: C5 was the release. Students released a living creature into appropriate habitat, chanted Om Mani Padme Hung during the release, dedicated the merit with Ngowa, and wrote an immediate reflection at the site. They carry the physical memory of the act — what their hands felt, what they heard, what they saw. The journals hold their site-written response.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the three-part reflection format — first imagination, what happened, what I know now — and be able to name it as a structure they will use again.

Students will UNDERSTAND that integration is not the same as feeling good about something — it is when an experience becomes part of how you see the world, whether or not it felt as expected.

Students will BE ABLE TO complete their Tsethar reflection journal: all three sections filled, the arc of the unit held in one object.

Students will FEEL the particular satisfaction of having done something real — not the feeling of a reward, but something quieter and steadier: the recognition that they acted, that it cost something (time, planning, care), and that the creature is free.

Materials Checklist

  • Students' Tsethar reflection journals (held by teacher since C5 — bring to class)
  • Students' C1 first imagination drawings/writings (held by teacher since C1 — bring to class, one per student)
  • Pencils, optional coloured pencils
  • Glue sticks (if C1 drawings need to be pasted into the journal on a final page)
  • Optional: a quiet piece of music to play during the sustained writing time — not required, teacher's discretion

No Plan B needed — all activities in this class are classroom-based and not logistics-dependent. If C5 was postponed and has now occurred, proceed as written; the physical memory of the release is still present.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 3 prayer set, led by the student leader for today:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer (at closing)

Teacher: "The Seven-Line prayer — let's chant it together. You've chanted it six times now in this unit. Let it settle deeper today."

Chant together. Teacher should listen rather than lead — let what has developed over the six classes be audible.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "Close your eyes. Go back to the release — last class. You're at the site. What's the first image that comes back?"

Give 30 full seconds of silence. Then: "Open your eyes. What's the first image?"

Take 4–5 responses. Receive each one. Note the specificity — "the water was cold and dark," "the fish went straight down and then turned," "I couldn't see it anymore after a few seconds," "it was louder than I thought it would be." These specific images are evidence. Let them fill the room.

Then: "We're going to do something today. We're going back to the very beginning of this unit — to the drawing or writing you made in C1, when I asked you to imagine what a life release would look like. Before you knew. Before you planned. Before you did it."

Distribute the C1 first imagination pieces (held since C1). Students look at them — quietly, without prompting, for 30 seconds.

"What did you imagine then? What did you think it would be like?"

Take 2–3 responses. Let the gap between then and now be felt without forcing it.

Mini Lesson — 5 minutes

Teacher: "Today you're going to finish your journal. Three things to write, in order:

"First: Look at what you drew or wrote in C1. At the bottom of that page, or on a journal page next to it, write: What I imagined Tsethar would be like.

"Second: Turn to the page you wrote at the release site — your immediate response. Read it. Then, below it or on the next page, write: What it actually was like.

"Third: On the final page of your journal, write: What I know now that I didn't know before I did this."

Teacher models the third prompt — one honest example: "In C1, I imagined the release would feel joyful. Dramatic. Like something in a film. What it actually felt like was quieter than that — there was a moment when the creature was in the water and then it was gone, and the water just kept moving. What I know now: acting for a creature's welfare is different from wishing for it. It has a different weight. It stays in your hands longer."

"That is not a perfect answer. It is an honest one. That is all you need to write."

Vocabulary Moment: "The word integration — when something you've done becomes part of how you understand the world. The release happened. It's not just a memory — it's changing how you see things. When you catch yourself, three months from now, moving a worm off a path — and you notice that you're doing it, and you know why — that is integration. The release is in you now."

Body Moment: Teacher: "Before you write — sit for a moment with your hands in your lap. Look at them."

Pause. Then: "These are the hands that held the creature. That held the container. That were at heart level. That's in them now. You don't need to do anything with that — just notice it."

15 seconds of quiet. Then: "Now write."

Emotional Hook: Recognition — the feeling of having done something real, that mattered, that took something from you (time, planning, care, attention). This is not pride. It is closer to steadiness. The sense of having acted in alignment with something you believe. Do not name it as pride; do not name it as happiness. Let the body moment do the work, and let the writing do the rest.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Teacher: "Tell your partner: what is one thing you know now — after this unit — that you didn't know before? Not what you learned in class. What you know from having done it."

Structure: 90 seconds each. What to listen for: the move from in-class knowledge (conceptual: "I know what merit means") to embodied knowledge (enacted: "I know what it feels like to hold something alive and then let it go"). Both are real; the second is the transfer.

After: "Did anyone hear something from their partner that surprised them?"

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Sustained writing time. Students complete all three sections of their journal.

Teacher circulates — not during the first 5 minutes (let students find their way in), then actively during the second half. Crouch down beside individuals: "What did you write for 'what I know now'?" "How does your C1 drawing compare to what actually happened?" "What surprised you most?"

When the third section is complete, students may also add to or complete the cover — any final decoration, their name if not yet prominent, or a word or image that represents the whole unit for them.

When journals are finished: students each choose one sentence from their "what I know now" page. Go around the room — each student reads their one sentence, in sequence, without commentary between them.

This closing round of sentences is the unit's oral culmination. Teacher listens. No evaluation, no "wonderful," no "good." Just: hearing.

Scaffold: For students who are stuck on "what I know now": teacher asks, quietly, "What would you tell a student who had never done a Tsethar, in one sentence, about what it's like?" Sometimes the shift to audience (telling someone else) unlocks what the student can't access in the first person.

Extension: Students who complete all sections and the reading-around may write a final page — a letter to the creature: Dear fish, I hope you are well. Here is what I want you to know from me. This is optional and kept private if the student prefers.

Debrief — 5 minutes

Teacher: "In Grade 2, you sent wishes outward. To your family, to animals, to all beings. In circles, getting wider. You practised Sem kye — 'May all beings be happy. May all beings be free from suffering.'

"In Grade 3 — in this unit — you acted. Not for 'all beings' in the abstract. For one specific creature. In one specific river. On one specific morning. You held it. You released it. You stood there after, with your hands open.

"That specificity — one creature, one river, one morning — that is what makes compassion real. Not the wish. The act. That is what this unit has been about."

Pause. Then: "One last question — and you don't need to answer it aloud. Just hold it: What will you do differently, in your ordinary life, because of what you know now?"

15 seconds of quiet. Then close.

Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full prayer set:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer

Teacher's closing line — the arc of the unit, in one sentence, specific to this day: "You came into this unit with a wish. You leave it with an act. The act is yours now — carry it."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author):

Carry forward: The completed reflection journals are the portfolio artefact for Unit 1. Options for use: share with families at the next parent meeting, keep in individual student portfolios, or display in the classroom briefly before archiving. The journals should not be kept permanently by the teacher — they belong to the students and their families.

The three-part reflection structure introduced today (first imagination → what happened → what I know now) is a format students will use again. Name it explicitly as a named structure: "We have a way of reflecting on a practice now — before, during, after. This works for any practice you'll encounter in the years ahead." In Grade 5, students will use this structure after the Wheel of Life unit — when they reflect on inhabiting each realm imaginatively, they will compare their first imagination to what the practice revealed. In Grade 7, after the Panglhabsol, the same format appears. Each time the teacher can invoke it: "You've done this before. Remember Grade 3?"

Unit 2 begins: Compassion as Action / Everyday — the thread is continuous. The essential question shifts from "What does it mean to act for the welfare of another being?" to "What does it look like when compassion shows up in ordinary, non-ceremonial moments?" The release was a ceremony; Unit 2 is about the non-ceremony version of the same practice. You can name the transition: "In Unit 1, we had a ceremony — planning, preparation, a special day, a special place. Unit 2 asks: what about every other day? What about Tuesday afternoon?"

Spiral backward: This unit completes the arc that began in G1.U4.C2 ("Do bugs feel?") and continued through G2's Bodhichitta circles. Students have moved from philosophical question (G1) to feeling (G2) to act (G3). This is the core developmental arc of the program's early years. The teacher does not need to name this to students — but it is useful to hold it as a teacher.

Spiral forward: The dedication of merit (Ngowa) as enacted here — opening the hands after the act, giving outward — is the physical precursor to Grade 5's Tonglen practice (giving and taking). In Grade 7, the practice of acting despite personal cost (Ahimsa at its most demanding) extends the logic of Tsethar. The thread is long.
