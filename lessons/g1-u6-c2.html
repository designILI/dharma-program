Jataka: The Rabbit Who Overcame Fear

A rabbit hears a loud, mysterious crash and runs in blind panic, triggering a stampede across the entire forest; the bodhisattva (as a wise lion) stops the animals, investigates the sound, and discovers it was only a falling fruit → facing fear and investigating it before running is how panic is stopped and wisdom grows.

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleNarrative structure
Bloom's levelUnderstand / Apply
LessonG1.U6.C2

Note: Standalone rotating session — can be taught at any point in the year.

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): A rabbit hears a loud, mysterious crash and runs in blind panic, triggering a stampede across the entire forest; the bodhisattva (as a wise lion) stops the animals, investigates the sound, and discovers it was only a falling fruit → facing fear and investigating it before running is how panic is stopped and wisdom grows.

Cognitive Principle Featured: Narrative structure — the story follows a cascade of cause and effect that accelerates (one rabbit → thousands of fleeing animals) and then reverses (one courageous act of investigation unwinds the whole catastrophe). Students feel the momentum in both directions, which makes the reversal viscerally satisfying and the lesson emotionally earned.

Bloom's Level: Understand / Apply

Prior Class Summary: "This session may occur at any point in the year. Students carry cumulative year experience. The settle→story→discuss→draw ritual structure is familiar."

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the story of the rabbit who heard a frightening noise and what happened next — all the way to the end. Students will UNDERSTAND that fear can spread from one person to many, and that stopping to ask "what actually happened?" is how panic is broken. Students will BE ABLE TO name a moment in their own life when they felt scared and either ran or stopped to find out what was really there. Students will FEEL the relief of discovering the frightening thing was something ordinary — and carry that feeling as a tool for the next time fear arrives.

Materials Checklist

  • Source: Traditional Jataka tale (Mahavastu / Jatakamala / oral tradition); teacher's oral telling from this plan
  • Drawing supplies: crayons, colored pencils, paper squares for the wisdom wall
  • Optional prop: a piece of fruit (an apple, a mango, a plum) — hold it up when the truth is revealed at the end of the story; the ordinariness of it is the point
  • Wisdom wall: space and tape ready for today's drawings

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader stands at the front. Class settles — feet flat, hands in lap, one slow breath together.

Student leader leads:

  • Kyap Dro (Refuge prayer) — recited together
  • Om Mani Padme Hung — seven times, slow and grounded
  • Manjushri Prayer — recited together

the teacher: "Thank you. One breath — in... and out. Today's Jataka story is about something that happens to every one of us. Being scared. Not just a little scared — very scared, all at once. Let's find out what our teacher from a past life learned about that."

Story Preparation — 2 minutes

"Before I begin: has anyone ever been scared by a sound — a bang, a crash, something in the dark — and then found out it was nothing dangerous at all? Just a door, or a dropped book, or the wind?" (Pause for a few nods or brief responses — don't open it to full discussion yet.) "Hold that feeling — that mix of scared and then relieved. Today's story is about what happens when we run before we find out."

The Story — 8–10 minutes

Tell with varied pace — the panic sections fast and breathless; the investigation section slow and deliberate. The contrast in pacing IS the lesson.

Long ago, in a great forest near a lake, there lived a rabbit. A perfectly ordinary rabbit with brown fur and quick, nervous eyes and ears that caught every sound. He lived among tall trees and ate grass and fruit that fell from the branches above him, and mostly he was content.

One afternoon, the rabbit was resting in the shade of a great Vilva tree — a tree heavy with large, round fruit. It was very quiet. The rabbit's eyes were half closed.

Then — CRASH.

A giant fruit fell from the tree above him and struck the ground with a sound like a thunderclap.

The rabbit did not look up. He did not look around. His eyes flew open, his four legs drove into the ground, and he ran. He had no thought — only: something terrible is coming. Run.

He ran so fast his feet barely touched the ground. He burst through the undergrowth. Other rabbits, seeing him, didn't ask what was wrong — they ran too. "Something terrible! Run!" And they ran.

A family of deer heard the rabbits. Deer do not ask questions when rabbits run — they run too. Then buffalo. Then wild pigs. Then monkeys dropped from their trees and scrambled across the forest floor. The sound of ten thousand feet shook the earth. Animals who didn't even know what they were afraid of fled because the animal next to them was fleeing.

It was a stampede. And it was heading straight toward a cliff above the sea.

Now, in that same forest, there lived a great lion. This lion was the bodhisattva — the being who would one day become the Buddha — and he had a quality that the other animals didn't yet have: he could be afraid and still stop. He heard the thunder of feet. He saw the wave of animals coming toward the cliff. And he thought: something is very wrong. But what?

He ran — but not away. He ran to the front of the stampede. He climbed to the top of a boulder at the cliff's edge, and he ROARED.

Three times he roared. A sound that stopped the world.

The animals at the front skidded to a halt. The wave rippled backward — animals stopping, crashing into each other, stumbling, looking up. The lion stood on the boulder, eyes steady.

"Stop," he said. "What are you running from?"

There was a long, confused silence. The deer looked at the rabbits. The rabbits looked at each other.

"Something terrible," said one rabbit. "A great crash. The world breaking apart."

"Where?" said the lion. "Show me where."

Silence. No one knew. They had all been following the animal ahead of them.

The lion found the rabbit who had started the run. He was shaking. "Show me where you heard it," said the lion.

They walked back — just the two of them — through the now-quiet forest. Back to the Vilva tree. The lion looked up. He looked at the ground. He saw the fruit — large, round, perfectly ordinary — lying in the leaves.

He picked it up. He brought it back to the waiting animals.

This. This is what you ran from.

The forest was quiet for a long moment. Then, slowly, the animals began to breathe again.

The rabbit who had started it all looked at the fruit for a long time. His ears drooped. "I didn't look," he said. "I just ran."

"Now you know," said the lion. "Next time — look first."

The Discussion — 8 minutes

the teacher: "Stay in the story for a moment. Don't move yet."

(Five seconds of silence.)

"What moment in that story surprised you most?"

(Take responses. Listen for: the cascade of animals joining the panic / the lion roaring / the fruit at the end.)

"When the rabbit heard the crash — he didn't look up. Why do you think he didn't look?"

(Guide toward: fear moves faster than thinking. When we're scared, our body runs before our mind can ask questions.)

"Has that ever happened to you? You got scared — really scared — and then found out it wasn't what you thought?"

(Take 2–3 responses. Receive them warmly. Don't rush to the lesson.)

"What made the lion different from all the other animals?"

(Guide toward: he felt the urgency but stopped to ask a question. He investigated instead of running.)

"Here's a hard question: do you think the rabbit was wrong to be scared?"

(Important: the answer is no. Fear is not wrong. The wisdom is about what you do with it.)

Vocabulary Moment: Once the discussion has arrived here naturally — "There's a word for what the lion did — and what the rabbit learned to do at the end. The word is investigation. It means: before deciding something is dangerous, you look. You ask. You find out what's actually there. Investigation is how we turn fear into understanding."

Body Moment: "Let's try something. Close your eyes. I'll make a sound — a small tap on the wall. When you hear it, your first job is to NOT open your eyes and run — your job is to listen carefully and guess what made the sound." (Tap lightly on a desk or wall. Give them a moment.) "Now — what made that sound? A monster? A crashing tree? No — just my hand on a desk. That's investigation. You heard something, you paid attention, you found out. Open your eyes."

Emotional Hook: Don't explain — just ask: "What did the rabbit feel when he saw the fruit? After all that running? What was in his body right then?" (Let students name it: embarrassed, relieved, silly, tired, grateful. Accept everything.) "All of those at once. That feeling is what it's like when fear is solved by truth."

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Assign fixed pairs.

Prompt — read slowly, let them think before talking:

"Tell your partner about a time you were scared of something — a sound, a shadow, something you saw — and you had to decide: run, or find out? What did you do? What happened next?"

One person talks, one listens, then switch.

What to listen for: students naming specific, personal moments of fear — not summarising the story. When a child says "I thought there was someone in my room but it was my coat on the chair" — that is the transfer. That is the session working.

Bring the class back. Invite one or two pairs to share what they talked about.

Adding to the Wisdom Wall — 8–10 minutes

"Now we add to our wisdom wall. Today you're going to draw one moment from the rabbit's story — the moment you felt most in your body while you were listening. It could be the rabbit running, the stampede, the lion on the boulder, or the fruit lying on the ground."

"Choose the moment that stayed with you."

Distribute paper squares and supplies. Students draw in quiet or with soft conversation. the teacher circulates, asking: "What moment did you pick? Why that one?" Listening, not evaluating.

After 6–7 minutes, students bring drawings to the wisdom wall under the label: "The Rabbit Who Overcame Fear — Investigation."

Debrief — 3 minutes

Gather the class at the wisdom wall.

"One last question. If you could ask the rabbit — after all of it — one question, what would you ask?"

(Take 2–3 responses. Examples: "Were you embarrassed?" / "Did you feel better after the lion showed you the fruit?" / "Did you ever run like that again?" Receive each one.)

the teacher — one closing sentence: "The rabbit didn't do anything wrong by being scared. What he learned was to pause, and look, before the fear became a stampede."

Closing — 2 minutes

Student leader returns to the front.

  • Kyap Dro — together
  • Om Mani Padme Hung — seven times
  • Manjushri Prayer — together

the teacher connects the story to the mantra: "Om Mani Padme Hung is also something we can use when fear comes — not to run, but to breathe, and settle, and then look clearly. The mantra gives us the moment to pause that the rabbit didn't have."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author):

Spiral connections: Backward: G1.U4.C2 (Do Bugs Feel? — reasoning from evidence rather than assumption; that class trained students to pause and think before concluding about an insect; this story trains the same cognitive habit at the level of fear: stop and investigate before running); G1.U2 (generosity unit — the cascade of panic in this story is the inverse of generosity: fear is contagious in the same way kindness can be, and the story makes this visible). Forward: G2.U1 (compassion circle — you cannot extend care to what you are running away from; the rabbit's story is about how panic prevents connection); G7.U1 (bystander ethics — the wise lion who stops to investigate rather than join the stampede is exactly the bystander who pauses to assess rather than follow the crowd; name this connection explicitly for older students in retrospect); G8.U2 (confronting what frightens you rather than fleeing is a key arc in Milarepa's story — the same structure of "turning toward" rather than away).

Pedagogical framing: These are standalone rotating sessions — they do not follow each other in sequence and can be taught at any point in the year. Each session uses the same ritual structure (settle → story → discuss → draw → wisdom wall). The pacing instruction in this plan is essential: tell the panic sections fast and breathless, the investigation section slow and deliberate. The contrast in the teacher's own physical pacing is the lesson — students feel the difference between reactive momentum and deliberate inquiry in their own nervous systems before the discussion makes it explicit. The optional fruit prop at the moment of revelation — holding up an ordinary piece of fruit when the truth is discovered — makes the ordinariness visceral and often produces laughter, which is the right emotional release. Note: the bodhisattva here appears as a lion, not a bird — the first time students encounter the bodhisattva in a non-avian animal form. You may note this briefly if students are curious.

What to watch for: The lesson is landing when a student can name the difference between the rabbit's response (run first) and the lion's response (stop and look), and can connect one of those responses to their own experience. The emotional peak of the class is the moment of relief — the frightening thing was just a falling fruit. Watch for the child who says "I've done that" — meaning they've panicked over something that turned out to be nothing. That moment of self-recognition is the hook. Students who can articulate "he stopped everyone by asking what really happened" have grasped the moral at its root.

Thread note: Session 2 of 5 in the G1.U6 Jataka Library. These are standalone rotating sessions, not a fixed sequence — each can be taught in any order, at any point in the year. The wisdom wall accumulates across all five sessions and is reviewed in full at G1.U6.C5.
