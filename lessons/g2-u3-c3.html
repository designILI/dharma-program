The Seven Lines

Learning the meaning of the Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer line by line → a prayer is not just sound; when you understand what you are saying, the words become yours and carry you somewhere real.

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleDual coding
Bloom's levelUnderstand
LessonG2.U3.C3

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Learning the meaning of the Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer line by line → a prayer is not just sound; when you understand what you are saying, the words become yours and carry you somewhere real.

Cognitive Principle Featured: Dual coding — meaning and sound working simultaneously. Students first receive the prayer through language (plain-English line-by-line walk), then through hearing (teacher chants the full prayer in Tibetan while students listen), then through a partial attempt to join. Each mode encodes the material differently. The written line added to the helper drawing adds a third mode — visual and kinetic. Together, these create retention that a single-mode encounter cannot.

Bloom's Level: Understand — Students are asked to grasp the meaning of each line of the prayer, to retain which line is about what, and to begin forming a relationship to the prayer as a whole. Remembering (Grade 1 exposure as a listening practice) is activated at the start; Understand is the new level being built today. Memorisation itself is not yet asked for — that is the explicit Grade 3 task.

Prior Class Summary: In C2, students explored what it means to meet fear with kindness rather than force. They did a body exercise — standing steadily across from a worried partner — and added a setting to their helper drawings. The discussion centred on courage as something you do while still afraid.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the plain-language meaning of each of the seven lines of the Guru Rinpoche Prayer and what each one is describing.

Students will UNDERSTAND that when we say an ancient prayer, we are joining a practice that has been carried for over a thousand years — the prayer connects us to something larger than this room and this moment.

Students will BE ABLE TO choose one line of the prayer that stayed with them and copy it onto their helper drawing as a beginning of a living relationship with the text.

Students will FEEL the specific quality of entering something ancient — the slight vertigo of belonging to a long line of people who have said these same words, across mountains and centuries, before you.

Materials Checklist

  • Students' helper drawings from C2 (stored from last class)
  • Coloured pencils — one set per student or shared on tables
  • Pencils for copying the prayer line
  • The plain-language prayer walkthrough (below) — teacher reads this during the Mini Lesson; no student handout needed
  • The seven lines written clearly on the board before class — both the plain-language version and the Tibetan line the teacher will write for copying. See PRAYER-REFERENCE.md for authorised transliteration.
  • Teacher's own chanting ability — this lesson requires the teacher to chant the full Seven-Line Prayer twice. If the teacher is not yet confident chanting it, practise from PRAYER-REFERENCE.md before class. A recording may be used as a supplement but should not replace the live human voice in the room.

Plan B — if the teacher is not yet confident chanting: Ask the school's resident teacher or a senior student to chant the prayer live in the classroom for students to hear. This is preferable to a recording. Introduce them briefly: "We have a special guest today who is going to chant the prayer for us, so you can hear how it sounds." The meaning-walk still belongs to the classroom teacher. Note in your reflection if a guest chanter was used.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader for today leads the full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa. Teacher settles the room before handing to the student leader. Unhurried. Today's opening has a particular resonance — students are about to begin learning a new prayer. Hold the end of the closing prayers for an extra breath before moving.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

"We've heard about Guru Rinpoche — the teacher who wasn't afraid, who met the frightening spirit with kindness. In Grade 1, your teacher chanted a prayer to Guru Rinpoche — just once, for you to hear. They didn't explain it. They just let you hear it. Do you remember that?"

Some will; many won't. Don't press. "It's fine if you don't remember — the sound may have stayed with you even if the memory hasn't. Today we're going to hear it again. But this time, we're going to understand what it says — every line."

"Before I tell you the meaning — just from the story we heard in class one: what do you think a prayer to Guru Rinpoche would be about? What might it say?"

Take 2–3 responses. Accept everything. Students may say: it would ask him to protect us; it would talk about his power; it would thank him. All of these are present in the prayer. "Let's find out."

Mini Lesson — 14 minutes

The line-by-line meaning walk — this section can be read verbatim:

"I'm going to tell you what each line of this prayer says — in ordinary words. After each one, say it back to me, and we'll take a moment to let it land. Then I'll tell you the next."

"Line one: 'In the northwest land of Oddiyana, in a place of flowers...' — This is telling us where Guru Rinpoche was born. A land called Oddiyana, far to the northwest, full of flowers. Can you picture that — a place with many flowers, in the mountains to the northwest? Say it back: 'Line one is about where he was born.' Good."

"Line two: 'On the heart of a lotus flower, you were born — amazing.' — This is remarkable. Not born from a mother, but found on a lotus — a kind of flower that grows on water. A miraculous birth. Line two is about how he was born — on a lotus. Say it back."

"Line three: 'You have extraordinary powers — we call you Guru Rinpoche.' — This is telling him who he is. Guru means teacher. Rinpoche means precious one. So his name, in words, is Precious Teacher. Line three is his name and what he can do. Say it back."

"Line four: 'You are surrounded by many dakinis and great beings.' — Dakinis are a kind of spiritual being — sometimes called sky-dancers. They travel with him, they accompany him. He is not alone. Line four tells us who is with him. Say it back."

"Line five: 'We will follow your teachings and practise as you did.' — This is us speaking now. This line is a promise. We say: we will try to practise the way you did. We will try to learn. Line five is our commitment. Say it back."

"Line six: 'Please come to us — bless us now.' — This is the invitation. We are calling him. We are asking him to come close and bless us — to send his goodness into our lives. Line six is the invitation. Say it back."

"Line seven: 'Guru Padma Siddhi Hung.' — This is the mantra — the power-syllables. Padma means lotus. Siddhi means accomplishment, the result of practice. Hung is a syllable of calling. Together: we call the Lotus-Born, the accomplished one. Line seven is the sound of calling. Say it back."

"Seven lines. Let me say them very quickly — you tell me what each one is about."

Move quickly through a fast recall: teacher says "Line one?" — students say "Where he was born." Line two — lotus, miraculous. Line three — his name and power. Line four — who is with him. Line five — our promise. Line six — our invitation. Line seven — the sound of calling. This takes about ninety seconds and solidifies the structure before the chanting.

Vocabulary Moment: "The word 'invoke' — to invoke means to call toward you, to invite someone to come. When we say this prayer, we invoke Guru Rinpoche — we call his blessing and his presence. The prayer is not just words we say. It is an invitation we send."

Body Moment: "Close your eyes. Put your hands in your lap. I'm going to chant the whole prayer now — in Tibetan, the way it is traditionally said. Just listen. Don't try to follow along. Let the sound come to you."

Teacher chants the full Seven-Line Prayer, slowly and clearly, once through. Then:

"Open your eyes. I'll chant it one more time. This time — if a syllable comes to you, if you recognise a sound, join in. Just that syllable. If nothing comes, listen. That is also exactly right."

Chant once more. Students join as they can. Do not correct or shape their joining — let whatever comes, come.

Emotional Hook: "This prayer has been said in these mountains for more than a thousand years. When we say it — even the first time, even just one syllable — we are saying the same words that hundreds of thousands of people have said before us. Monks, nuns, farmers, children, teachers. All of them said these seven lines. We are the newest ones. That's not small."

Hold for a moment. Then move gently into the Turn and Talk.

Turn and Talk — 3 minutes

"Tell your partner: which line stayed with you? It doesn't matter if you understood all of it — just which one. Line one about the flowers, line five about the promise, line seven about the sound of calling — whichever one caught something in you."

Give time for both partners. Circulate and listen. You are gathering which line to write on the board — or you may write all seven briefly and let each student choose their own.

Activity — 10 minutes

Return students' helper drawings.

"Today you add one line of the prayer to your drawing — the line that stayed with you, or any line you choose. Write it on your drawing in your own handwriting."

The teacher writes the plain-language version of all seven lines on the board clearly, so students can copy from it. Walk around to help with copying — spelling, spacing, legibility. This is not a test of their writing; it is an act of devotion-by-hand. Students who copy carefully and slowly are not behind. Let the room be quiet while they write.

If a student wants to write two lines, that is fine. If a student cannot decide, ask: "Which one sounds the most like something you want to say?"

Debrief — 3 minutes

"The Seven-Line Prayer has been said in these mountains for over a thousand years. We are just beginning. By next year, in Grade 3, you'll know it by heart."

Not pressure — orientation. The tone is: you are at the beginning of something real, and that beginning is exactly right.

"You now know what you're saying when you hear this prayer. That changes it. It becomes yours in a different way."

Closing — 3 minutes

The student leader leads the full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa. Unhurried.

After the closing prayer set is complete, the teacher adds — without announcement, simply as the next thing: "One more time."

Teacher chants the Seven-Line Prayer once, complete, after the closing. Students listen. This is not a test; it is a gift. The prayer ends the class. No further words needed.

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Students heard the Seven-Line Prayer as a listening practice in Grade 1 — pure sound, no explanation. Today they received its meaning, line by line, and began to join it in sound. In Grade 3, they will memorise it. This is the explicit bridge; say so to students in the debrief (as scripted above). Students who are already joining confidently and attempting to reproduce full phrases are ahead of pace — note who they are; they may be ready to lead a chant in Grade 3. Students who are resistant or confused during the chanting moment — this is normal and not cause for intervention. Some students need more time before their mouth is willing to attempt sacred sound. The prayer will come. Oral transmission from the resident teacher remains the authoritative source for correct pronunciation and melody; see PRAYER-REFERENCE.md. The plain-language walkthrough in this lesson is for meaning-teaching only and should not be used as a substitute for the traditional form. Spiral note: The seven-line prayer joins the standing prayer set as a parallel practice — students now have an expanding repertoire of what prayers do: protect (Kyap Dro), generate compassion (Sem kye), dedicate merit (Ngowa), invoke a specific teacher (Seven-Line Prayer). This implicit taxonomy of prayer types will be made explicit in Grade 4.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child learned the meaning of the Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer — line by line, in plain language — and heard it chanted in Tibetan. They chose one line that spoke to them and wrote it onto their helper drawing. You can ask them: 'What does the Seven-Line Prayer say? Can you tell me what one of the lines means?' They may even try to chant part of it for you."
