Rewriting the Mountains

The mountains of Tibet — ancient, witness to countless lives — → the practical implications of the rebirth teaching for how we live now, and the philosophical reflection that closes the unit

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleMetacognitive comparison
Bloom's levelAnalyze
LessonG6.U1.C4

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The mountains of Tibet — ancient, witness to countless lives — → the practical implications of the rebirth teaching for how we live now, and the philosophical reflection that closes the unit

Cognitive Principle Featured: Metacognitive comparison — students receive their C1 starting-position paragraph alongside their C3 journal entry and are asked to trace what changed and what didn't. This before-and-after comparison makes intellectual growth visible and personal, which is more motivating and more honest than a summative assessment that measures only the endpoint.

Bloom's Level: Analyze — the culminating reflection requires students to (1) characterise their starting view, (2) identify the strongest argument and hardest challenge they encountered, (3) assess whether and why their thinking shifted, and (4) articulate the practical implications of their current view. This is sustained analytical writing, not recall.

Prior Class Summary: C3 was the deep philosophical inquiry — three frameworks for "what I am," the direct attempt to locate the observer, the question of what survives death. Students wrote their current view in journal entry 3 and named what they are more and less certain about. They carry the question: not "what does Buddhism say?" but "what do I think?"

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the word soteriological — relating to liberation or salvation — as the frame for understanding why the rebirth teaching exists within the Buddhist tradition: not as cosmological speculation but as practical instruction for living in a way that leads toward liberation.

Students will UNDERSTAND that whether one holds the Buddhist view of rebirth, a materialist view, or something else, the practical conclusion — that this moment and its quality of action matters enormously — is the same. Both "this is the only life" and "what I do now shapes what comes next" lead to the same instruction: pay attention; act well.

Students will BE ABLE TO write a sustained philosophical reflection that honestly compares their starting view to their current thinking and articulates what the unit has changed, challenged, or confirmed.

Students will FEEL the quality of grounded sobriety that comes from genuinely situating oneself within a long chain of existence — not morbidly, but with the specific weight of seriousness that comes from taking the question seriously.

Materials Checklist

  • Each student's C1 starting-position paragraph — returned by the teacher today (kept since C1)
  • Writing journals, one per student (with journal entries 1, 2, 3 already in them)
  • Pens or pencils, one per student
  • The full Grade 6 prayer cards or sheets (Units 1–2 set — no Dolma yet)
  • Student leader list
  • Portfolio folder or collection envelope for the completed Unit 1 reflections

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set (Units 1–2 set): Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Note: The Food Offering Prayer appears in closing only. The Dolma (Tara Prayer) is not yet introduced.

Student leader leads. After prayers: the teacher distributes the C1 starting-position paragraphs without comment.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "Read what you wrote in the first class. Then open your journal and read entry 3 — what you wrote last class. Read both. What changed? What didn't? Take two minutes — just reading, not writing yet."

Allow two full minutes of reading and quiet comparison. Then:

"If you want to: what changed? Even one sentence."

Take 2–3 responses. Receive each one. Note especially: students who say nothing changed (philosophically valid — examine it), students who report a shift in position (note the nature of the shift), students who say they are more confused now than at the start (this is excellent — name it as progress, not failure: "More precise confusion is better than vague certainty").

Fallback if students are reluctant: "Did you agree with what you wrote four classes ago? Just: yes, no, or partly?" Quick show of hands — this loosens the floor for spoken responses.

Mini Lesson — 5 minutes

Brief. The teacher does not introduce new conceptual content today — the time is needed for the reflection. This mini lesson is framing.

"Whatever view we hold about what happens after death — Buddhist, materialist, or something else — notice something: the practical instruction is the same. If this life is the only one: every moment matters enormously, because there are no second chances. If rebirth is real: every moment matters enormously, because the quality of our actions shapes what comes next. Both conclusions are the same instruction."

Pause. Let that land.

"The mountains of Tibet — they have been here through countless human lives. Through all the generations of practitioners who sat in their shadow, practiced, lived, and died. Monks, laypeople, teachers, students. Each one faced the same question you are facing. Each one, in some way, answered it with their life — with how they spent their time, what they devoted their attention to, how they treated the people around them. The mountains don't remember any of them by name. They just remain."

Brief pause. "What does it feel like to sit in a place where that is true?" This is not rhetorical. It is an invitation to actually feel the depth of time.

Body Moment: Students sit in the meditation posture — eyes closed, spine straight, hands in the lap. Teacher: "You have been in this body for eleven or twelve years. Before that: the Buddhist teaching says there was something. After this life: there will be something. Sit for a moment as if that were true. What changes in your posture? In your breath?" Ninety seconds of genuine, held quiet. Then: "Open your eyes."

Vocabulary Moment: "Soteriological — from the Greek sōtēria, salvation or preservation. A soteriological teaching is one designed to help beings find their way toward liberation — not just to describe reality, but to change how beings move through it. The rebirth teaching is soteriological: it exists inside a larger system designed to help beings find their way out of the Wheel of cyclic existence. It is not meant as cosmological curiosity. It is meant to change how we live."

Emotional Hook: The sense of depth and seriousness that comes from genuinely considering one's place in a long chain of existence — the practitioner sitting now, in this school, in this building, in the shadow of mountains that have seen all of it. Not morbidly — with grounded sobriety. The teacher does not narrate this; the ninety seconds of silence carries it.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: if you took the rebirth teaching seriously — not as belief, but as a working hypothesis, something you tested your life against — what one thing would you change about how you spend your time?"

Structure: each partner answers in about ninety seconds. No counter-argument needed — this is honest personal reflection.

What to listen for: Students who give glib answers ("I'd meditate more") — gently press: "What specifically? When? What would you do less of to make room?" Students who give serious, uncomfortable answers — receive these with particular care. This question can touch real things.

Activity — 15–20 minutes

The Unit 1 culminating reflection. Teacher reads the four-part structure aloud before students begin:

  1. My starting view — what you thought at the beginning of the unit, and why.
  2. What I encountered — the strongest argument you met, and the hardest challenge.
  3. My current view — whether it shifted, how, and why. If it didn't shift, why not.
  4. What this means for how I live — one concrete implication of your current view, honestly stated.

"Three to five paragraphs. You have your C1 paragraph and all three journal entries to draw on. This is not a summary of what Buddhism says — it is your philosophical reflection, in your own reasoning. The standard is honesty and precision, not agreement with any particular view."

Teacher circulates throughout — specific, honest engagement:

  • "What's the strongest argument you're making in paragraph two?"
  • "You wrote that your view shifted. What exactly shifted — the conclusion, or the reasons?"
  • "What would have to be true for your current view to be wrong?"

Do not rush students through this. If the room is genuinely engaged, let the writing run a full fifteen to eighteen minutes.

Scaffold for students who are finding it hard to structure: Point to the four-part structure: "Just go in order. Start with paragraph one — your starting view. What did you write in C1?" This gives a concrete handle.

Extension for students who finish early: "Write a fifth paragraph: what question does this unit leave you with that you most want to carry into Unit 2?" This is a genuine forward bridge and rewards careful reflection.

Debrief — 5 minutes

Opening question: "We are not ending the question today — we are closing this part of it. What is one thing you want to carry out of this unit?"

Take 2–3 responses.

Teacher: "Grade 6 is not the end of this question. Unit 2 takes us directly into the Bardo — the intermediate state between death and rebirth. We will look at that territory directly, with as much care as we've brought to this one. And Unit 3 brings us to Tara — who stands at the threshold of that territory and says: I will not leave you there alone. That is coming. For now: you've done serious work. Collect it."

Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full closing prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Teacher's closing line: "Four classes. One question. The mountains of Tibet have been waiting for your answer for a long time — and now you've written it down."

Collect the completed reflections as students leave. These are the Unit 1 portfolio artefacts.

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The Unit 1 culminating reflections collected today are the portfolio artefact for this unit. File them by student; they will be returned at the end of Grade 6 as part of the full-year portfolio review. Do not grade them — evaluate them for the quality of reasoning and the honesty of engagement. A student who argues clearly for a materialist view is doing excellent philosophical work. A student who parrots Buddhist doctrine without examining it is doing less well, even if the conclusion is "correct." Unit 2 begins immediately: the Bardo. The tone shifts from philosophical analysis to something more direct and more personal — what actually happens at death, according to the Tibetan Buddhist tradition? Students who are still processing the rebirth discussion will continue to do so alongside the new material; the Bardo teachings do not require belief in rebirth to engage with seriously. The Bardo unit is also where the emotional register of the year deepens — grief, disorientation, transition. Hold the room with increasing care from C2.U2 onward. Spiral note: the "mountains of Tibet" image introduced here carries forward into C3.U2 — "The Mountains of Tibet" — where it becomes the central metaphor for witness, continuity, and the practitioner's relationship to a tradition that has mapped this territory across centuries.
