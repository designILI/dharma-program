The Chain of Teachers

Completing the lineage map all the way to the student's own name → the shift from being a receiver of the Dharma to understanding oneself as a transmitter

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleIdentity-based encoding
Bloom's levelAnalyze and Apply
LessonG5.U3.C3

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Completing the lineage map all the way to the student's own name → the shift from being a receiver of the Dharma to understanding oneself as a transmitter

Cognitive Principle Featured: Identity-based encoding — when abstract content (lineage, transmission) is made personally relevant through a concrete act (writing one's own name at the end of a chain that goes back to the Buddha), the encoding shifts from declarative to identity-level. The student is not learning about lineage; they are discovering their place in it.

Bloom's Level: Analyze and Apply — students analyze how oral transmission works — what passes beyond information, and why the relationship between teacher and student is irreplaceable (Analyze) — and apply this understanding by completing the personal lineage map and identifying what they themselves intend to carry forward (Apply).

Prior Class Summary: C2 told the story of Padmasambhava bringing the Dharma to Tibet, the building of Samye, and the translation work of Vairocana. Students carry the word translation and the offering gesture — both hands, at heart level.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the concept of oral transmission (Tibetan: lung, རླུང) and why it is considered qualitatively different from reading a text alone. Students will UNDERSTAND that the chain of teachers from the Buddha to the present is a living chain — that it requires each generation to actively receive and pass on, and that students are already inside this chain. Students will BE ABLE TO complete their lineage maps with their own name at the end and write one sentence about what they intend to carry forward. Students will FEEL the particular quality of responsibility that arises when a person sees themselves not only as the end of a chain but as a potential link in it — a carrier, not merely a recipient.

Materials Checklist

  • Student lineage maps (from C1 and C2 — kept in classroom portfolios)
  • Pencils, fine-line markers, coloured pencils (students may want to highlight their own name at the end)
  • Board or chart paper: school's specific lineage information — teacher prepares this in advance (see Curriculum guidance)
  • A single sheet of blank paper per student (for the extension sentence, or as overflow for the map)

Plan B: If specific lineage information has not yet been gathered, the teacher can write what is known with confidence and mark gaps honestly — "We know the chain comes through this lineage, and that your teachers studied with [name if known]. The connection is real even where we don't have every name." The exercise of writing one's own name at the end of the chain does not depend on having every link filled in. The gesture matters more than the completeness. If a student was absent for C1 or C2 and their map is underdeveloped, pair them with a student who has a complete map so they can reconstruct the structure while still doing their own work.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Student leader opens the full prayer set. Teacher chants with the class.

Full Grade 5 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

(Food Offering Prayer is closing only.)

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

"We've traced the lineage from the Buddha through Padmasambhava to Samye — to the moment the teachings took root in Tibet. Now: how do the teachings get from 8th-century Tibet to this classroom? How does a chain of transmission actually work, in practice? Not in theory — what actually happens between one teacher and one student?"

Take responses. Students may say: the teacher teaches the student, the student learns, the student becomes a teacher. All of this is right. Press slightly: "What does the teacher pass on that isn't in a book? What is transmitted that cannot be found in a text?"

Hold this question. The mini lesson answers it, but the quality of the students' wondering matters.

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Two interwoven threads — the mechanics of transmission and the immediacy of the chain.

Thread 1: How the chain actually works.

"The teachings have been preserved not only in books. Books matter — the translation work Vairocana did was essential. But books alone are not enough. A teaching received from a teacher — received in the presence of someone whose practice is alive — carries something that the same words on a page do not carry. This is not mysticism; it's something most people have experienced. Think of the difference between reading instructions and being shown how to do something by someone who knows it in their hands. The knowing transfers differently."

"In the Tibetan tradition, this is called oral transmission — lung in Tibetan. When a teacher transmits a text or a teaching orally, they are passing something living, not just information. The quality of their understanding, the stability of their practice, the relationship between the words and the life that has been lived with them — all of this comes through the presence of the teacher in a way that cannot be fully captured in writing."

"This is why lineage matters. Not as hierarchy — as living continuity. Each teacher received from their teacher something alive and passed it on alive. If that chain breaks — if someone receives but does not transmit, or transmits carelessly — something is lost that cannot be recovered from books alone."

Thread 2: The chain relevant to this school.

Teacher brings the specific lineage to the class. Name the chain clearly, from Padmasambhava forward to the school's tradition: the key lineage figures, the founders of this school's tradition, the resident teacher, and the teacher in the room.

(The teacher provides the names specific to this school and tradition. The format: "From Padmasambhava, the teachings came through [name] to [name], who is the source of [this school's tradition]. [The school's founding teacher] received from [their teacher]. And I received from [my teacher's name, if the teacher is comfortable sharing it].")

The teacher writes each name on the board or chart paper as they name it.

Vocabulary Moment: Write on the board: lung (རླུང) — oral transmission.

"Lung: the passing of a teaching from teacher to student through the voice and presence of the teacher. A teaching received in lung is considered more powerful than a teaching read alone — not because the words are different, but because of what the relationship between teacher and student carries. This is why we chant together, not in isolation. This is why the resident teacher's chanting is the authoritative source — not a recording, not a transcript. The presence carries something."

Body Moment: Teacher says to the class: "Turn to the person next to you. Teach them one thing you know from this year's curriculum — anything. One thing. Teach it now." One minute.

The room fills with teaching. Then: "Stop. What just happened? You transmitted. You passed something you know to someone else through your voice and your presence. That is lung. That is the chain. You just did it." Brief pause. "That is how it works. That is how it has always worked."

Emotional Hook: The shift from understanding oneself as a receiver to understanding oneself as a transmitter. The chain does not end with the student as its final point — it passes through them toward whoever they will teach, in whatever form that teaching takes.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Prompt: "Tell your partner: who is the most important teacher in your own life — not necessarily in this school? It could be a family member, a friend, an elder, anyone. What did they transmit to you that wasn't just information? What did you receive from them that you couldn't have gotten from a book?"

Structure: each partner speaks for 90 seconds. This question is personal and will produce varied, genuine responses. The teacher listens without intervening.

What to listen for: students who identify something relational — care, modeling, the quality of attention that a person brings — as the substance of transmission. These students have understood the key insight of this class.

Activity — 15 minutes

Students complete their lineage maps — all the way to their own name at the end.

The teacher writes the full chain on the board (prepared in advance). Students copy the chain into their maps, filling in the nodes from C1 and C2 and adding the new links: the school's lineage figures, the resident teacher, the teacher in the room (if the teacher has named themselves), and then — at the bottom — the student's own name.

Students write their name at the end of the chain. In a slightly larger hand. In a slightly more careful script. This should feel like a deliberate act, not an administrative label.

Teacher circulates: "Your name is at the end of a chain that goes back to the Buddha. Look at that. What does it ask of you?"

Do not answer the question for them. Ask it and move on.

Extension: Students who finish the map write one sentence at the bottom of the page, below their name: "One thing I intend to carry forward from this curriculum is ___________." This sentence is their own — not prompted by the teacher beyond the instruction. It can be a practice, a prayer, a quality of mind, a specific understanding. It should be specific.

Debrief — 5 minutes

"The chain ends with your name — for now. At some point, you may become the teacher in someone else's chain. A younger sibling. A child you will teach someday. A friend who asks you about something you know. What would you want to transmit?"

Hold the question without answering it. It is not a prompt for discussion — it is a question to carry.

Then: "Next class: you'll present your map to the class — and explain one link in the chain. The link you find most interesting or most significant. Come ready."

Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader closes the full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "The chain did not end before it reached you. It came this far. Now it rests with you — briefly, for now. Hold it carefully."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The completed lineage map is the portfolio artefact for Unit 3. It must be prepared for presentation in C4 — collect maps after class and return them at the start of C4. Before C4, prepare the room for a gallery format or share circle. C4 is the culminating class of Unit 3, the closing of Grade 5, and the transition into Grade 6. The teacher should be prepared to speak briefly about what Grade 6 holds — Grade 6 is the year of impermanence, grief, and the feminine in Buddhism (Losar and the Wheel of the Year, the Bardo, and Tara). Students who have spent Grade 5 inside the Wheel of Life and the lineage are ready for the Bardo teaching and for Tara. The transfer prompt for this unit — "You are inside this chain. What does that mean for how you practise?" — should be held through C4 without being asked directly; the presentations will embody it. Note regarding the teacher sharing their own teacher's name: this is a powerful model of the chain, and is encouraged where the teacher is personally comfortable. There is no obligation.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child completed their lineage map — tracing the chain of teachers from the Buddha through Padmasambhava to this school, with their own name written at the end. They also explored the concept of oral transmission: what passes from teacher to student through presence and relationship that cannot be captured in a book. You can ask them: 'What is lung? Where is your name on the lineage map? What do you intend to carry forward from Hidden Land Dharma?'"
