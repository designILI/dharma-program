Between Life and Death

The physical and psychological experience of dissolution — things falling apart that have been held together → the dying process in Himalayan Buddhist teaching, and grief as the living person's Bardo

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleDual-thread teaching
Bloom's levelAnalyze
LessonG6.U2.C2

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The physical and psychological experience of dissolution — things falling apart that have been held together → the dying process in Himalayan Buddhist teaching, and grief as the living person's Bardo

Cognitive Principle Featured: Dual-thread teaching — the class holds two subjects in parallel (the dying process and the experience of grief) and asks students to find the structural similarity between them. This is an analytic move: not "grief is like dying" as emotional metaphor, but "grief and dying share a structural feature — dissolution of what has been organised around something that is now gone." The parallel makes both threads more legible.

Bloom's Level: Analyze — students examine the dying process as a described sequence, identify the structural parallels with the lived experience of grief, and connect both threads to the concept of rigpa introduced in C1. The Pajapati thread requires students to hold a historical figure as both a specific person and an instance of a larger pattern.

Prior Class Summary: C1 introduced the three Bardos — dying, dharmata, becoming — and the concept of rigpa (awareness/recognition) as the quality that allows a being not to get lost. Students began their Bardo concept maps. They carry the breath-gap practice as a micro-Bardo experience and the word rigpa as the quality that meditation cultivates.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the Himalayan Buddhist description of the dying process as the sequential dissolution of the four elements (earth, water, fire, air) — each bringing characteristic experiences — and the teaching that awareness becomes more exposed as each element dissolves.

Students will UNDERSTAND that grief is a Bardo-like experience for those who remain — a period of genuine disorientation in which the familiar world has dissolved and a new orientation has not yet formed — and that this is not pathological but a natural response to profound loss.

Students will BE ABLE TO annotate the Bardo of dying section of their concept map with the dissolution sequence and its experiential quality, and add a "grief as Bardo" section with notes on what the two states share.

Students will FEEL the particular weight that comes from hearing Pajapati's name become real — a name held for four years becoming a specific person with a specific grief and a specific act of perseverance.

Materials Checklist

  • Bardo concept maps from C1 (returned by the teacher at the start of the activity)
  • Pens and coloured pencils
  • Writing journals (for notes)
  • The full Grade 6 prayer cards or sheets (Units 1–2 set — no Dolma yet)
  • Student leader list
  • Safe storage for concept maps after class

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set (Units 1–2 set): Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Note: The Food Offering Prayer appears in closing only. The Dolma (Tara Prayer) is not yet introduced.

Student leader leads. Teacher waits for the room to fully settle.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "Last class we mapped the three Bardos. The first one — the Bardo of dying — what do you remember about it?"

Take responses: consciousness disengages from the body; the teaching is stay present, don't panic. Affirm what is correct; add anything essential that is missing.

"Today we go deeper into what that process actually looks like, according to the Himalayan Buddhist tradition. And then we look at something that people who remain alive go through — which has its own Bardo quality. That something is grief."

Pause. Let the word sit.

Fallback if students recall little from C1: Do a quick three-question verbal check: "What does the word Bardo mean? What are the three Bardos? What is rigpa?" Take short answers. This review takes two minutes and ensures the C2 content has a foundation.

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

The teacher holds two threads. Present them in sequence — Thread 1 first, then Thread 2 — before asking students to see the parallel.

Thread 1 — The dying process in detail:

"The Dharma teaching describes the process of dying as the sequential dissolution of the elements — the basic components that make up a living body and mind. There are four: earth, water, fire, and air. As death approaches, each dissolves into the next."

Teacher describes each stage slowly:

Earth dissolves into water: The quality of solidity — the sense of body as firm and grounded — gives way. There is a sense of heaviness, of sinking. The body feels difficult to hold up.

Water dissolves into fire: The quality of flow and cohesion gives way. The body becomes dry, parched. There may be visions of smoke.

Fire dissolves into air: The quality of warmth dissipates. The body cools. Visions of sparks or fireflies may arise.

Air dissolves into consciousness: The quality of movement — breath — stops. What remains is awareness itself, fully exposed. All the ordinary mental structures — personality, memory, habitual thinking — have dissolved with the elements. What is left is naked awareness: unfiltered, without support, without distraction.

"At this moment, the teaching says, there is the greatest opportunity — and the greatest danger. For a practitioner who has spent years cultivating awareness, this exposed awareness is recognisable: it is the same quality they have touched in meditation, now fully revealed. Recognition in that moment equals liberation. For someone who has not cultivated this recognition — who has spent their life distracted, defended, swept along by habit — the exposed awareness may be overwhelming. And then the Bardo of dharmata begins."

"The teaching for those who remain with someone who is dying: reading the Bardo Tödöl aloud is traditional — the words help the dying awareness to orient. What we hold in our mind and heart in the presence of someone dying matters. This is why practitioners are trained to be present at death — not to manage it, but to hold the space."

Thread 2 — Grief as Bardo:

"For those who remain after a death — or after any profound loss — there is a Bardo quality to the experience of grief. The ground shifts. What was familiar is gone. There is a period of disorientation: not knowing how to be, who to be, without the person or the life that is now absent. This is not pathological — it is what profound loss does to the structures of the self."

"Like the Bardo of dying, grief has stages and thresholds. Like the Bardo of dharmata, it involves the arising of things from the inside that feel like they are coming from outside — memories, images, sudden presences. Like the Bardo of becoming, it involves eventually finding a new orientation, a new way of moving, drawn forward by what remains."

Pajapati:

"Pajapati. You have heard that name once before — in Grade 2, when we learned that Siddhartha was raised not by his mother (she died when he was a baby) but by his aunt, who loved him as her own. When Siddhartha left the palace — when he became the wanderer, the seeker — Pajapati was left behind. She had raised him. She had loved him without condition. And now he was gone. What she did with that grief — how she held it, what she made of it — is part of her story. You will hear the rest in Grade 8. For now: remember that grief and the practitioner are not strangers. Grief can be a beginning."

Vocabulary Moment: "Dissolution — the falling apart of what has been held together. In the Bardo of dying, the dissolution of the elements is the dying process. In grief, it is the dissolution of the world as it was organised around a person who is now gone — the routines, the assumptions, the shape of daily life that was built around their presence. Both are bardos. Both require the same basic quality: the capacity to remain present in the disorientation, without grasping for what has dissolved."

Body Moment: "Think of something or someone you have lost. Not necessarily a person — it can be a place, a time, a version of yourself, a friendship that ended. Hold that loss for a moment. Not to make it worse — just to feel its weight." Pause for twenty full seconds of genuine quiet. "That weight is information. It tells you what mattered. Grief is always specific to what was loved."

Emotional Hook: The particular quality of hearing a name that has been held for four years — Pajapati, mentioned in Grade 2, mentioned since — finally become a real person with a real grief and a real act of perseverance. Not a symbol. A woman who loved, who was left behind, and who did not let the loss be the end.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: what is one thing you know about grief — from your own experience or from watching someone you care about? And what is one thing grief teaches that ordinary life doesn't?"

Structure: each partner speaks for about ninety seconds. Teacher circulates.

What to listen for: Students who have direct experience of loss — receive this with care, without making the class into a grief-processing session. Students who are more abstract — gently press: "Can you name a specific moment? Not a general principle — a moment?" The specific is more philosophically useful than the general here.

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Teacher returns concept maps. Students work in two parts:

Part 1 — Annotating the Bardo of dying (10 min):

Add detail to the dying Bardo section of the map. The dissolution sequence: earth, water, fire, air — and what follows. The teaching: stay present, don't panic. Rigpa: the recognition that is available if awareness has been cultivated. Students should write in their own words — not a transcription of the lesson.

Part 2 — "Grief as Bardo" annotation (5–8 min):

Add a section to the concept map — either within the dying Bardo zone or as a connected annotation — labelled "grief as Bardo." Students write: what grief and the dying Bardo have in common structurally (dissolution, disorientation, the absence of the familiar ground), and what support each needs (presence, awareness, someone who does not panic and does not leave).

Teacher circulates with specific questions: "What did you write about what the two states share?" "Can you write one sentence about what Pajapati's story has to do with what you just annotated?"

Scaffold for students who are struggling: "Start with the dissolution sequence — can you draw it as a diagram? Each element dissolving into the next. Then: add one word for what grief feels like at the beginning. What dissolves in grief?"

Extension for students who finish early: "Add a third annotation: what would it mean to practise rigpa — recognition, staying present — in the experience of grief? What would that look like in practice?"

Debrief — 5 minutes

Opening question: "Pajapati loved someone fully, and then he was gone. She held that grief. She kept practising. Her grief was not separate from her practice — it was what made her practice real. What does that say about grief? About what loss can do?"

Take 2–3 responses.

"The Bardo teaching is not only for the dying. It is for everyone who has ever faced a dissolution — of a life, a relationship, a world as it was. The teaching says: stay present. Don't grasp for what has dissolved. And find the quality of awareness that can remain stable in the disorientation. That is what Pajapati did. That is what the tradition asks of all of us."

Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full closing prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Teacher collects concept maps.

Teacher's closing line: "Grief can be a beginning. That is the whole teaching, in five words."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The Pajapati excerpt here is a mid-thread arrival in the curriculum's long story of women practitioners. The name was planted in Grade 2 — students were told it would return. It returns today, briefly, as a historical figure with a specific grief and a specific act of perseverance. Do not elaborate beyond what is given here. If students ask for more: "Her full story is worth waiting for — it comes in Grade 8, and it is worth that wait." Note which students respond to the Pajapati excerpt with particular recognition or emotion — this is worth recording in Your Reflection. The grief discussion in this class may bring up personal loss for some students. Receive this with care, but do not structure the class as a grief-processing session — the pedagogical goal is insight into the structural parallel between dying and grief, not emotional catharsis. If a student shares something that warrants pastoral follow-up, use the school's process. The dissolution-of-elements teaching is presented here in its experiential description, not its full cosmological detail — the five wisdoms and the specific visions of the Bardo of dharmata come in C3. Today's teaching establishes the sequence; C3 fills in the luminous quality of what arises after the sequence completes. Spiral connection: the element-dissolution teaching connects to Grade 4's work on the elements as foundational constituents of the physical world. Students have a prior framework for earth, water, fire, air — this class puts that framework in a new and more personal context.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child studied two things in parallel: the Himalayan Buddhist description of the dying process (as a sequence of dissolution — earth, water, fire, air — until only naked awareness remains), and the concept of grief as a Bardo-like experience for those who remain. We also briefly introduced Pajapati — the aunt who raised the Buddha, whose grief at his leaving became the ground of her perseverance and her practice. You can ask them: 'What dissolves in the dying process, according to the teaching?' or 'What does Pajapati's story have to do with grief?'"
