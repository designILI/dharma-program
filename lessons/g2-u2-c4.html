Under the Tree

Siddhartha sitting under the Bodhi tree until something opened → what it means to stay with a question without turning away until it becomes understanding

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleEmbodied cognition
Bloom's levelUnderstand / Apply
LessonG2.U2.C4

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Siddhartha sitting under the Bodhi tree until something opened → what it means to stay with a question without turning away until it becomes understanding

Cognitive Principle Featured: Embodied cognition — students encode the awakening through their own bodies during the two-minute sit. When the concept of sustained attention is experienced rather than described, it becomes personal knowledge rather than received information. Dual coding: the body experience + the comic panel together anchor the same concept in two different registers.

Bloom's Level: Understand / Apply

Prior Class Summary: In C3, students followed Siddhartha's departure — the late-night ride, the sleeping family he left without waking, the point of no return. The class held two valid things at once: the cost and the necessity. Students carry the weight of what Siddhartha gave up, and the question of what he found.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW that Siddhartha meditated under a fig tree (the Bodhi tree) and made a vow not to rise until he understood; that forces of doubt and temptation arose and he stayed through them; that at dawn he became fully awake — the Buddha; that "Buddha" means "the Awakened One."

Students will UNDERSTAND that Siddhartha's awakening came not from escaping difficulty but from staying with the question — without force and without flight — until it opened.

Students will BE ABLE TO begin a four-panel comic of the Buddha's life and say, in one sentence, what happens in each panel.

Students will FEEL the quality of settled concentration — the specific sense, even in two minutes, that sitting still with something rather than fleeing it can be sufficient.

Materials Checklist

  • Stories of the Buddha / illustrated anchor text (mark the Bodhi tree passage)
  • Class timeline (posted — add final entry today)
  • Four-panel comic template, one per student (A4 paper folded in half, then folded again, opened flat to reveal four panels — or pre-printed template with four equal boxes)
  • Pencils, crayons
  • Timer (two minutes — for the sit)
  • Optional: a leaf or small plant on the teacher's desk to represent the Bodhi tree
  • Plan B: If the class is restless or agitated before the two-minute sit, stand for the Body Moment first (below) before inviting students to sit. If two minutes proves genuinely too long on this particular day, begin with one minute and name that it will grow: "One minute today. In Grade 3, we'll do five." Do not force stillness — only invite it. A child sitting quietly with eyes open is sitting.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The rotating student leader leads the full Grade 2 prayer set:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa

Teacher present, unhurried.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

"We've been following Siddhartha for three classes now. Tell me the story — as much as you can remember. I'll listen."

The teacher does not prompt the beginning — lets students build the story collectively and organically. Occasional light prompts if the narrative stalls: "And then?" / "What happened after the four sights?" / "Who did he leave behind?" Do not correct; receive and quietly affirm.

"Good. You know this story. Today we find out what happened after he rode away."

Fallback: "What are three things you know about Siddhartha so far? Tell a neighbour — thirty seconds."

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

"Siddhartha left the palace. For a long time after that, he searched. He found teachers and learned from them — wise men who taught him how to meditate, how to sit still, how to concentrate. He learned from all of them. But something was still missing. The question was still there."

"He tried going without food — days without eating, to purify his body. He tried extreme practices that nearly destroyed him. He became thin and weak. He nearly died. And still — no answer."

"One day, worn out and searching, he came to a great tree. A fig tree — we call it the Bodhi tree, the tree of awakening. He sat down beneath it. And he made a vow. He said: I will not get up from this spot until I understand. Until I find the answer to why people suffer and how that suffering can end."

"He sat. Days passed. Nights passed. Things arose in his mind — doubt. Fear. The voice that said: this is hopeless, give up, go home, you'll never find it. Other forces seemed to rise up and challenge him: prove that you deserve to be here. He felt all of it. And he did not move. He stayed."

"And then — one morning, as the last stars were fading — something changed. Something opened. He saw clearly. He understood the nature of suffering. He understood how it arises. He understood how it ends. He saw the path through it."

"The sun rose. And Siddhartha was no longer just Siddhartha. He was the Buddha. The Awakened One."

"Tradition says he looked at the morning star and said: 'How wonderful. All beings have this nature. All beings can wake up.'"

[Pause.]

"He had answered the question. Not by escaping it. Not by finding a clever way around it. By sitting still with it — until it opened."

Vocabulary Moment: "Buddha. The word means the Awakened One. Not a god — a person who woke up. Who saw things clearly, fully — the way you might wake up from a deep sleep and suddenly see the room you're in, sharp and real. He woke up to the truth of how things are."

Body Moment — the two-minute sit:

"Now — we're going to try something. We're going to sit like the Buddha under the tree. Not for days. Just two minutes."

"Sit with your back as straight as you comfortably can. Hands resting in your lap. Eyes soft — open or gently closed, whichever feels natural. Just — sit."

"If thoughts come — they will come — that's normal. Just notice them, and come back to sitting."

[Set timer. Two full minutes. The teacher sits too — fully, not watching the class. The teacher's own stillness in this moment is not incidental. It is the lesson.]

[Timer sounds.]

"Good. How was that? What happened in your mind? What did you notice?"

Take three or four responses. Students will have a full range of experiences — restless, calm, surprised, bored, moved. All of these are valid. Receive them all without evaluation.

Emotional Hook: "Something in you knows what it's like to stay with something. Two minutes is almost nothing compared to what Siddhartha did — but it was real. That steadiness is already in you."

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Prompt: "Have you ever kept at something — a question, a puzzle, a feeling — until you understood it or it shifted? What helped you stay? What made it hard to stay?"

Structure: Two minutes with a neighbour. What to listen for: examples from real life — persistence with a maths problem, staying with a friendship difficulty, waiting for something to make sense. All of these map to the same underlying concept. The transfer is implicit; the teacher does not need to make it explicit.

Activity — 15–20 minutes

"Today you start your comic. You'll have one more class to finish it — but today you sketch all four panels."

[Distribute four-panel comic templates.]

"Your comic tells the story of the Buddha's life in four pictures. Here are the four panels:"

[Teacher writes on board:]

  • Panel 1: Siddhartha in the palace — his early life, the gates closed
  • Panel 2: The four sights — something he saw that changed everything
  • Panel 3: The departure — leaving in the night
  • Panel 4: Under the Bodhi tree — the awakening

"You don't have to write words — you can draw only, or mix drawing and words. What matters is that the four panels tell the story in order."

[Twelve to fifteen minutes. Teacher circulates — crouches down to eye level and asks: "What's happening in this panel? Tell me the story." If a student says "I don't know what to draw," ask: "What's one thing you remember from that part of the story?" and let them start from there.]

Extension: "If you finish all four sketches — add thought bubbles or speech bubbles. What is Siddhartha thinking or saying in each moment?"

[Add to class timeline: "Bodhi tree — dawn — the Buddha." This completes the unit timeline. Leave it posted through C5.]

Debrief — 5 minutes

"Look at your four panels. The story starts in a palace with gates closed — and ends under a tree at sunrise. What changed? What was the same?"

Take three or four responses.

"The Buddha didn't find the answer by going somewhere better or by escaping. He found it by staying — with the question, with the tree, with everything that came up in his mind — until something opened. That's the beginning of everything he taught."

Closing — 3 minutes

The rotating student leader leads the full Grade 2 prayer set:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa

Teacher sentence, specific to today: "Today we sat for two minutes like the Buddha under the tree. Next class, you'll finish your comics and tell the story to someone."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author):

Carry forward: The two-minute sit is the body anchor for the entire unit. It works best when the teacher genuinely participates — sitting, not monitoring the class. Students will feel the difference. If the teacher's own stillness is absent, the sit becomes an exercise in compliance rather than an encounter with something real. — Comics should not be collected today — students finish and narrate them in C5. Keep them safe in personal folders or a designated class stack. — The Bodhi tree is a real species: Ficus religiosa. If one grows near the school, name it by pointing out the window. If not, the leaf or plant on the desk does the same anchoring work more quietly. The dual coding (physical object + story) helps students hold the concept. — Spiral connections: the concept of the Buddha as "Awakened One" — specifically not a god, but a person who developed human capacities to their fullest — seeds the Grade 3 unit, which will distinguish between different kinds of spiritual figures (Buddhas, Bodhisattvas, Dharmapalas). The two-minute sit becomes five minutes in Grade 3, ten minutes in Grade 5. This progression is intentional; students are building a practice across years.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child heard the story of the Buddha's awakening under the Bodhi tree — and we practised sitting still together for two minutes, the way the Buddha sat. They also began a four-panel comic of the Buddha's life. You can ask them: what does the word 'Buddha' mean — and what do you think it felt like to sit under that tree for so long?"
