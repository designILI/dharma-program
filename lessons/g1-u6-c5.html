Jataka: A Friend to All

A being known across the land for never turning anyone away — making friends across every boundary of species, caste, wealth, and temperament — finds that this open-heartedness has built a community that returns to protect and sustain the being in their own time of need → open-heartedness is not just kindness; it is the act of building a world you want to live in.

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleNarrative structure
Bloom's levelUnderstand / Apply
LessonG1.U6.C5

Note: Standalone rotating session — can be taught at any point in the year.

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): A being known across the land for never turning anyone away — making friends across every boundary of species, caste, wealth, and temperament — finds that this open-heartedness has built a community that returns to protect and sustain the being in their own time of need → open-heartedness is not just kindness; it is the act of building a world you want to live in.

Cognitive Principle Featured: Narrative structure — unlike stories built on a single crisis, this story accumulates. Each small act of welcome adds to a pattern. The structure mirrors the wisdom: what we do again and again becomes who we are, and that identity eventually shapes everything around us. Students feel the weight of accumulation — friendship as something grown, not just given.

Bloom's Level: Understand / Apply

Prior Class Summary: "This session may occur at any point in the year. Students carry cumulative year experience. The settle→story→discuss→draw ritual structure is familiar."

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the story of the being known as a friend to all, and the community that grew from open-heartedness. Students will UNDERSTAND that open-heartedness means not deciding in advance who deserves your kindness — it means keeping the door open to everyone, including those who seem different or difficult. Students will BE ABLE TO name one person or being they could be more open toward, and what one small step of open-heartedness might look like. Students will FEEL the specific, small surprise of discovering that someone you expected to find difficult is, when genuinely welcomed, not difficult at all — and the quiet that follows that discovery.

Materials Checklist

  • Source: Synthesized from Jataka themes of unlimited friendliness (metta) and open-hearted community (Jataka No. 40 and related tales of the bodhisattva's metta practice); teacher's oral telling from this plan
  • Drawing supplies: crayons, colored pencils, paper squares for the wisdom wall
  • Optional prop: a simple open hand — the teacher's own hand held open, palm up — used as a gesture throughout the story and discussion
  • Wisdom wall: space and tape ready for today's drawings
  • Note: This is the final session of Unit 6. Consider inviting students to look at the full wisdom wall at the end and reflect across all five stories.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader stands at the front. Class settles — feet flat, hands in lap, one breath together.

Student leader leads:

  • Kyap Dro (Refuge prayer) — recited together
  • Om Mani Padme Hung — seven times, warm and unhurried
  • Manjushri Prayer — recited together

the teacher: "Thank you. One breath — in... and out. This is our fifth and final Jataka story for this season. We've heard about a parrot who wouldn't give up, a rabbit who learned to look before running, quail who protected each other, and a fish king whose truth called the rain. Today's story is about something that holds all of those together. It's about a heart that stays open — for everyone."

Story Preparation — 2 minutes

"Before I begin — close your eyes for just a moment. Think of someone who always makes you feel welcome. Someone who seems happy to see you, no matter what. Someone whose presence makes a room feel safer." (Pause.) "You might see their face right now. That feeling — the feeling of being truly welcomed — is what today's story is about. Open your eyes."

The Story — 8–10 minutes

Tell this story with warmth and expansiveness — not urgency or crisis. Let it breathe. The emotional key is accumulation: each friendship added to the last, building a world.

Long ago, there was a being — some say it was a person, some say it was an animal, some say it was a spirit living in a great tree at the crossroads of many forest paths. But everyone in that part of the world knew one thing about this being: no one was ever turned away.

If a bird landed in the tree and needed shelter — welcome. If a lost deer wandered close and needed water — welcome. If a traveler arrived tired and uncertain — welcome. If a creature came who was frightened, or hungry, or grieving, or simply lonely — welcome. If a creature came who was difficult, or prickly, or bad-tempered, or who had once been unkind to others — welcome. No test. No conditions. The door — or the branch, or the shade — was always open.

This was not because the being was unusually powerful, or wealthy, or free from troubles. The being had hard days too. The being had fears, and weariness, and moments of wanting to be alone. But when a creature came to the tree — any creature, from any place — the being would look at them and think: this one needs something. What do I have that I can offer?

Sometimes what was offered was just warmth — presence. A place to sit without judgment.

Word spread, the way things spread in forests — not through announcement, but through the way one creature tells another without quite saying it. I went to the tree. I felt welcome. If you need it, go. And so more came. Creatures who had heard of the open-hearted one came from farther and farther away. The crossroads near the tree became a gathering place. Different species who had never been at ease with each other found that somehow, in the presence of the open-hearted one, they were.

The being did not plan this. The being was simply unable to close.

Years passed. And then: difficulty came.

A long, cruel drought. The paths grew dusty and hard. Fruit grew scarce. The creatures who had gathered near the tree grew thin and worried. They began to drift — some moving on, searching for easier ground.

But many did not leave.

They remembered. They brought what they could. A bird brought seeds. A deer found a hidden spring and showed the others. A traveler who had once been welcomed with nothing but kindness returned now with food and helped carry it to those who could not move. The creatures who had seemed the most difficult — the ones who had arrived prickly and closed and had still been welcomed — some of them turned out to be the most resourceful when things got hard.

The tree at the crossroads became a place of surviving together — not because anyone commanded it, but because an open heart, over many years, had built something. Not a building. Not a rule. A web of connection. A community that held.

When the drought finally broke, the being sat under the tree and looked at what had grown there — at the creatures resting, the paths worn smooth by many feet, the sounds of different voices learning to be near each other — and thought simply:

This is what an open heart makes.

The Discussion — 8 minutes

the teacher: "Sit quietly. Stay in that last image for a moment."

(Ten seconds of silence.)

"What do you notice about this story compared to the others we've heard this year? What's different about it?"

(Take responses. Guide toward: there's no single dramatic crisis. No net to escape, no fire to fight. The story grows slowly.)

"The being never turned anyone away. But — was there anyone who was hard to welcome? Did the story hint at that?"

(Yes — the prickly, difficult, once-unkind creatures were also welcomed. This is important.)

"Why do you think the being welcomed even the difficult ones?"

(Guide toward: open-heartedness doesn't wait until someone has proved they deserve it. It keeps the door open because something might be behind the difficult behaviour that we don't know about.)

"When the drought came — who helped? Was it the creatures who had been perfectly nice? Or was it all kinds?"

(All kinds — including the ones who had seemed most difficult. This is the payoff: open-heartedness creates allies you didn't expect.)

"Here is my question: Do you think you have to be a very special, very holy being to do this? Or is it something ordinary people — ordinary children — can do too?"

(Guide firmly toward: it is ordinary. It is a choice, made in small moments, many times.)

Vocabulary Moment: Arrived through discussion — "The word is open-heartedness. It's two ideas together: open — not closed, not decided in advance — and heartedness, which means acting from the heart, from what you actually feel for the other person. Open-heartedness is choosing to not close before you've even seen who's there."

Body Moment: "Hold out one hand — palm up, open." (Everyone holds a hand out, palm up.) "This is the gesture. Not grabbing. Not holding away. Just open — here, if you need it." (Hold it for five seconds in silence.) "Now close your hand into a fist." (Hold that too.) "Feel the difference." (Open the hand again.) "That's the choice the being in the story kept making. Every time. Lower your hands."

Emotional Hook: Don't explain — just ask: "Think of a time someone made you feel really, truly welcome — when you really needed it. What was that like in your body?" (Let students name it: warm, safe, less alone, happy, like you could breathe. Don't rush through these. Let them sit with the feeling.) "That feeling — someone made that in you. That is what open-heartedness makes."

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Assign fixed pairs.

Prompt — read slowly, give thinking time:

"Think of one person — or one kind of person — you find it hard to be open toward. You don't have to say who they are. Just think of them. Now: what is one very small thing you could do to be a little more open toward them? Just one step. Tell your partner."

One person talks, one listens, then switch.

What to listen for: students identifying real people, real situations, and real small actions — not abstract declarations. When a child says "I could say hi to the new kid even though I don't know them" or "I could not move away when he sits next to me" — that is specific, real, and usable. That is the session working.

Bring the class back. Invite one or two pairs to share what they talked about.

Adding to the Wisdom Wall — 8–10 minutes

"This is our last Jataka story drawing for this unit. Today you'll draw one moment from the story of the open-hearted being. It might be the tree at the crossroads with creatures of all kinds arriving. It might be a single moment of welcome — one creature being received by another. It might be the community during the drought, holding together. Or it might be the being at the end, looking at what their open heart had built."

"Choose the moment that means the most to you."

Distribute paper squares and supplies. Students draw in quiet or soft conversation. the teacher circulates with extra care today — this is the final session, and the wisdom wall is now nearly full.

After 6–7 minutes, students bring drawings to the wisdom wall under the label: "A Friend to All — Open-Heartedness."

If time allows: invite students to step back and look at the whole wisdom wall together — all five stories, all five wisdoms. Ask: "Which story do you feel most connected to? Which animal do you carry with you?"

Debrief — 3 minutes

Gather at the wisdom wall.

"One last question. If you could ask the open-hearted being one question — anything — what would you ask?"

(Take 2–3 responses. Examples: "Did you ever want to close the door?" / "Did it feel lonely sometimes?" / "Did you know it would work out?" Receive each one.)

the teacher — one closing sentence: "An open heart doesn't promise that everything will be easy. It promises something different: that whatever happens, you will not face it alone — because you made it so."

Closing — 2 minutes

Student leader returns to the front.

  • Kyap Dro — together
  • Om Mani Padme Hung — seven times
  • Manjushri Prayer — together

the teacher connects the story to the mantra — and, since this is the final session of Unit 6, connects all five stories: "Om Mani Padme Hung is a prayer of compassion — the wish for all beings to be happy and free. The parrot flew through fire for that. The lion stopped the stampede for that. The quail lifted each other for that. The fish king called the rain for that. And the open-hearted one kept the door wide for that. The mantra holds all five of them."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author):

Spiral connections: Backward: G1.U2 (generosity unit — open-heartedness is the expansion of generosity from material giving to relational openness; the metta modeled here is what generosity becomes when it no longer distinguishes between recipient and non-recipient); G1.U6.C1–C4 (the prior four Jataka sessions implicitly inform this closing story — perseverance in caring (C1), investigation over panic (C2), collective solidarity (C3), inner virtue that changes the world (C4) are all forms of open-heartedness, and this story names the root that held them all). Forward: G2.U1 (compassion circle — this story is the narrative companion to the geometric compassion circle in G2; "a friend to all" is the logic of the expanding rings made personal and story-shaped); G5.U2.C1 (metta practice — the unlimited friendliness embodied here prefigures the formal introduction of metta practice in later grades); G8.U4 (culminating practice — the open-heartedness modeled here returns as the ethical posture of the curriculum's final unit).

Pedagogical framing: These are standalone rotating sessions — they do not follow each other in sequence, but this session works particularly well as the closing session of the Jataka Library, since it gathers and implicitly synthesizes the themes of the prior four stories. Its source is synthesized from multiple Jataka tales of the bodhisattva's metta practice, not drawn from a single canonical text — the teacher should know this and not present it as having one canonical origin. The story's structural key is accumulation: friendship added to friendship, welcome added to welcome, building a world. The teacher's pacing should match this — warm, expansive, unhurried, not crisis-driven. The wisdom wall review at the end of this session, looking back at all five stories together, is the capstone of the Jataka Library. Reserve time for it: do not let it be cut.

What to watch for: The lesson is landing when a student can name not just who they already care for but who they could extend care toward — someone overlooked, someone different, someone difficult. That is the shift from compassion as warmth toward people you already love, to compassion as an open practice toward everyone. The wisdom wall review is structurally important: if students can look at five drawings from five different sessions and name what connects them ("they all kept caring," "they all chose to help even when it was hard"), the Jataka Library has worked as a curriculum thread, not just as five separate stories. If students have only seen one or two of the sessions, the review still grounds this story in a larger arc.
