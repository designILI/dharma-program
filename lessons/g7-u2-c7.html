Reflection

The memory of Teaching Day, held in the mind → the metacognitive understanding that teaching reveals the shape and limit of one's own understanding

LessonG7.U2.C7

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The memory of Teaching Day, held in the mind → the metacognitive understanding that teaching reveals the shape and limit of one's own understanding

Cognitive Principle Featured: Metacognition through structured reflection — by examining what they planned, what happened, and what they now understand, students engage in one of the most powerful forms of learning available: thinking about their own thinking, and using the gap between intention and outcome as the primary source of insight.

Bloom's Level: Evaluate and Create — students evaluate the gap between their intention (what they planned and imagined) and the effect (what actually happened in the room), then produce a written reflection that honestly accounts for both. The reflection is itself a made thing — a document that will be collected as the unit's portfolio artefact alongside the lesson plan.

Prior Class Summary: C6 was Teaching Day — students taught their lessons to a younger class and collected the younger students' verbal responses. They noted two immediate reactions in the corridor: what happened that they didn't plan for, and what worked better than expected. They carry the full experience — the threshold moment, the teaching, the younger students' responses — and the instruction: "Write these down before the memory changes."

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the three-part reflection structure — What I imagined / What happened / What I understand now — and understand that its third section (metacognitive insight) is the one that cannot be fabricated from what was planned Students will UNDERSTAND the word metacognition as a specific skill: thinking about the quality and accuracy of one's own understanding by examining what happened when one tried to transmit it; and that the Feynman Principle is confirmed or challenged by Teaching Day's outcome Students will BE ABLE TO write a complete three-part reflection that is honest about the gap between intention and effect — including, if applicable, honest account of what stumbled and what it taught them Students will FEEL the specific honest encounter with what actually happened versus what was planned — and the particular quality of understanding that only arrives through genuine experience rather than through studying or planning

Materials Checklist

  • Corridor notes from C6 (students bring these — two sentences on what didn't go as planned and what worked better than expected)
  • Journals or fresh paper for the full reflection, one per student (minimum two pages)
  • Pens or pencils, one per student
  • Board with the three-part reflection structure written and visible:
  1. What I imagined — my plan, my expectations, my intentions before Teaching Day
  2. What happened — the honest account: what worked, what didn't, what surprised me, what the younger students actually did
  3. What I understand now — what Teaching Day taught me: about the topic, about teaching, about what I know and don't know
  • Teacher's observational notes from C6 (carried by the teacher — used to support the C7 reflection conversation for individual students)
  • Grade 7 prayer set visible
  • Plan B (for students resistant to honesty about what stumbled): The teacher's observational notes from C6 are the resource. In a quiet, private moment during the writing phase: "I noticed something specific in Teaching Day that I think is worth writing about. Can I share it with you?" Then describe what was observed — specifically, without evaluation. The student can choose whether to include it in the reflection. Do not force it publicly.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 7 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer at closing only.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

"In one sentence — not evaluation, just description — what happened on Teaching Day?"

Go around the room. Every student gives one sentence. The teacher listens without assessment — no "great," no "that's difficult," no ranking. Just receive each one.

These sentences are the raw material. The variety of what students report (smooth, stumbling, surprising, confusing, moving) sets up the reflection's honesty requirement: there is no single "right" kind of Teaching Day experience. The reflection follows from whatever actually happened.

Mini Lesson — 5 minutes

The three-part reflection.

Point to the structure on the board.

"This is the reflection format. You have used versions of this since Grade 3 — it is the curriculum's standard reflection structure. Today you apply it to the most significant thing you've done in seven years of Dharma class."

Part 1 — What I imagined: This is your lesson plan and your expectations. What you thought would happen — in the room, with the younger students, with the concrete anchor, with the activity. What you hoped they would take away. Write this accurately, even if — especially if — it didn't match reality.

Part 2 — What happened: The honest account. What worked. What didn't. What surprised you. What the younger students did — not what you hoped they would do, what they actually did. What they said when you asked "what did you learn?" This section has one requirement: honesty. A reflection that says "it went perfectly" when it didn't is not a reflection. It is a cover story.

Part 3 — What I understand now: This is the hardest section and the most important. What did Teaching Day teach you — about the topic you taught, about the act of teaching, about what you know and what you only thought you knew? This section cannot be written before Teaching Day — it can only come from the experience itself. If you write something here that you could have written in C2, before you taught, it is not a reflection. It is a plan.

"The Feynman Principle: 'if you cannot explain it simply, you do not understand it well enough.' Part 3 is where you find out whether the Principle held — whether Teaching Day confirmed your understanding, challenged it, or revealed a gap you didn't know was there."

Vocabulary Moment:

Write on the board: metacognition — thinking about one's own thinking; examining the quality and accuracy of one's own understanding by looking at what happened when one tried to apply or transmit it.

"Reflecting on Teaching Day is a metacognitive act. You are not just describing what happened — you are examining your own understanding from the outside, using what happened as the evidence. This is one of the most powerful forms of learning available to a human being. Most people do not do it. Most people describe what happened and stop there. You are going further: you are asking what the event taught you about yourself as a knower."

Body Moment:

"Close your eyes. Run through Teaching Day in memory — from the moment we stopped at the door and took one breath, through entering the room, through the first words you said, through the moment the lesson went as you planned and the moment it didn't, through the younger students' responses. Run the whole thing, silently. Two minutes."

Two full minutes of silent memory.

"Open your eyes. Write."

Emotional Hook: The honest encounter with what actually happened versus what was planned — the gap or the alignment, the surprise or the confirmation. The particular quality of understanding that arrives through experience rather than through planning: specific, embodied, irreplaceable.

Activity — 25 minutes

Full reflection writing — three-part format.

Students write. The teacher circulates with specific, genuine questions — not to prompt performance but to unlock honest reflection:

  • "What did the younger students do that you didn't expect? Name something specific."
  • "What would you do differently if you taught this lesson again? Not in the abstract — one specific thing."
  • "What do you understand now, about this topic, that you didn't understand before Teaching Day? Where did the fumble teach you something?"
  • "What did the younger students say when you asked 'what did you learn?' — and does that match what you intended them to learn?"
  • "Is Part 3 something you could have written before Teaching Day? If yes — it's still Part 1. Go deeper."

For students resistant to acknowledging what stumbled: "The Feynman Principle is not a threat. It is useful. What did the stumble teach you? A lesson that went perfectly teaches you that your plan was good. A lesson that stumbled teaches you something about understanding. Which is more interesting?"

For students who had genuinely difficult Teaching Day experiences (a lesson that largely didn't work, a moment of losing the younger students entirely): "Write it honestly. The honest account of a difficult Teaching Day is more valuable as a reflection than a polished account of an easy one. What happened, specifically? And what does it tell you?"

Students who finish early: "Add a final paragraph: if you were going to design and teach another lesson — on a different topic, to the same class — what would you do differently from the beginning, before the first draft? What have you learned about how to design a lesson?"

Debrief — 5 minutes

Two or three students share their reflections — specifically Part 3 ("What I understand now"). The teacher selects students who have written something genuinely honest and specific — not necessarily the strongest lesson, but the strongest reflection.

After each share, no comment from the group. Simply receive it.

Teacher, after all shares: "The teaching did not go into the younger students and stop. It came back to you. That is what the tradition says: when you teach, you learn twice. You gave them something. It returned to you as something different — as understanding of your own understanding. That is the Feynman Principle lived, not just known."

Final sentence: "Unit 2 is complete. You designed a lesson. You taught it. You reflected on the gap between what you planned and what happened. That gap is not failure. It is the curriculum."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader.

Teacher's closing line: "The Sang offering goes outward. In Grade 7, you became transmitters. What you sent outward — to the younger students, in that room — was the teaching. What came back to you, in this room today, is understanding. The smoke carries the prayers. The teaching carries the learning. Unit 2 is complete."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The Teaching Day lesson plan and the C7 reflection together form the Unit 2 portfolio artefact. Collect both. Together they are the most substantial student-generated documents in the Grade 7 portfolio — a complete arc from design to enactment to metacognitive reflection.

Quality markers for strong reflections to note in the teacher's log:

  • Part 2 is specific — it names what actually happened, not an edited version
  • Part 3 is genuinely new — it could not have been written before Teaching Day
  • The younger students' actual responses appear in the reflection (not just what the student hoped they would say)
  • The Feynman Principle appears — implicitly or explicitly — in the student's self-assessment of what they know and what they discovered they didn't know as well as they thought

Quality markers for reflections that need further development:

  • Part 2 is uniformly positive (warning sign — selective memory or reluctance to acknowledge difficulty)
  • Part 3 repeats Part 1 (the reflection hasn't moved beyond what was planned)
  • No mention of the younger students' actual responses
  • The reflection is about the experience emotionally but not analytically

Students whose reflections need development: the teacher may invite them to add one more paragraph before the portfolio is assembled, with a specific prompt: "What did the younger students actually do with your lesson — and what does that tell you about how well the main idea landed?"

Unit 3 connection: Medicine Buddha — the transition from action back to contemplation. Students have been outward-facing for all of Unit 2; Medicine Buddha turns the gaze inward again, but with the earned authority of someone who has transmitted the teaching. The Medicine Buddha practice is for the healing of others — it connects directly to Unit 1's empathy work (seeing the suffering behind cruelty, refusing to abandon beings in difficulty) and to Unit 2's act of transmission (the teaching as a form of care for the younger student). The Grade 7 year's arc — from receiving the teaching to transmitting it, and from self-understanding to responsibility for others — is completed in Unit 2 and deepened in Unit 3.
