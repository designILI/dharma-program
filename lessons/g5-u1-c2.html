The Center

Three animals biting each other's tails in a circle at the hub of the Wheel → the three root mental states (ignorance, desire, aversion) that drive the entire cycle of conditioned suffering

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleEmbodied encoding
Bloom's levelUnderstand / Apply
LessonG5.U1.C2

Duration: 40 minutes

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Three animals biting each other's tails in a circle at the hub of the Wheel → the three root mental states (ignorance, desire, aversion) that drive the entire cycle of conditioned suffering

Cognitive Principle Featured: Embodied encoding — when an abstract concept is paired with a distinct physical gesture, recall becomes kinesthetic as well as verbal. The three-gesture sequence created in this class gives students a body-level vocabulary for the three poisons that can be retrieved faster than words and returned to in any subsequent class without explanation.

Bloom's Level: Understand / Apply — students receive the tradition's account of what the three animals represent, then apply it immediately: they identify these states in their own recent experience ("which of the three do you notice most in yourself?"). The lesson moves from definition to self-recognition within the same period.

Prior Class Summary: C1 was the first encounter with the Wheel of Life — students observed it for two full minutes without interpretation, then received the first orientation (Yama holds the Wheel as impermanence; the layers have different teachings; a small figure outside the Wheel points to liberation). Students drew the Wheel from memory. The question held at the close: "What is at the center? What drives the whole Wheel?"

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the names and symbolic animals of the three root poisons: ignorance (pig), aversion/hatred (snake), and desire/craving (rooster), and that these three appear at the very hub of the Wheel because they are its engine.

Students will UNDERSTAND that the three poisons are not exotic or unusual states — they are ordinary, recurring mental conditions that drive one another in a self-sustaining cycle; and that the Buddhist tradition identifies ignorance as the root from which the other two grow.

Students will BE ABLE TO perform the three-gesture body vocabulary (hands over eyes / reaching forward / pushing away), label the three animals correctly in their developing Wheel drawing, and name one of the three poisons that they recognise in their own recent experience.

Students will FEEL the quiet recognition — which often arrives without drama — that these three states are not ancient or foreign concepts but the texture of yesterday morning, last week, and right now.

Materials Checklist

  • Large, clear image of the Wheel of Life — displayed throughout Unit 1 (same as C1; should already be up)
  • Each student's in-progress Wheel drawing from C1 — returned at the start of the Activity
  • Plan B (if C1 drawings are not available): Distribute fresh blank paper. Students begin a new working drawing from scratch; the C1 from-memory sketch remains in the folder for C5.
  • Pencils (one per student)
  • Chalk or whiteboard markers — the teacher will draw the three-poison cycle on the board during the Mini Lesson
  • Optional: a close-up print or zoomed projection of only the hub (the three animals) for the PKA and Mini Lesson, in addition to the full Wheel displayed

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 5 prayer set, led by the student leader for this class:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

(Food Offering Prayer at closing only.)

Dechen Monlam — second time for the class. Some students will already be finding the sounds and rhythm. The teacher joins in without leading; let the student leader carry it. Afterward, one brief word from the teacher — not every class, just here at C2 while the prayer is still new: "Notice how the Dechen Monlam — our wish for all beings to be happy — sits alongside what we are about to study. We study suffering so that our wish is not empty. It is grounded."

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

"Last class I asked you to hold a question: what is at the center of the Wheel? What drives the whole thing? What came to you between then and now?"

Take three or four responses. Accept everything — "desire," "death," "karma," "the animals," "something bad." Students who noticed the three animals in C1 may already be close.

Then: "Let's go look." Direct students' attention to the hub of the Wheel — point to it in the displayed image, or bring up the close-up view.

"What do you see there — at the very center?"

Students: three animals. Biting each other's tails. Moving in a circle.

"What animals are they?"

Let students identify them. They may need to look closely — pig (or boar), snake, rooster (or bird). Students may disagree on which is which; let the looking be active. Confirm when you have a clear consensus.

"Why do you think these three animals are at the very center of the Wheel? Not at the edge — not in one of the sections — but right at the hub, the axle, the thing everything else turns around. Why these three?"

Take responses. Accept all — then: "Here is what the tradition says."

Fallback (if students are quiet): Ask one specific question: "If the Wheel is a machine — what is the engine of a machine? What makes it go?" This reframe almost always generates responses.

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

The teacher moves through the three poisons with care — not rushing past any of the three, but not lingering so long that the cycle relationship gets lost.

The Pig — Ignorance (moha):

"The pig or boar represents ignorance — in Sanskrit, moha; in Tibetan, གཏི་མུག, timuk. But this is not stupidity — not the ignorance of not knowing a fact. This is a deeper not-knowing: fundamental confusion about the nature of reality. Not seeing clearly how things actually are. We don't see how impermanent everything is. We don't see how our actions create our experience. We move through life as if asleep — reacting, grasping, pushing away — without ever quite seeing what is actually happening.

"This is the root poison. It is placed first because without it, the other two could not take hold."

The Snake — Aversion/Hatred (dvesha):

"The snake represents aversion — in Sanskrit, dvesha; in Tibetan, ཞེ་སྡང, zhé dang. The recoiling from what we don't want. The striking at what we find threatening, irritating, painful. The snake moves fast — aversion can arise in a fraction of a second. Someone says something and the snake is already moving."

The Rooster — Desire/Craving (raga):

"The rooster represents desire — in Sanskrit, raga; in Tibetan, འདོད་ཆགས, dö chak. The reaching toward what we want. The pecking — again and again — at what attracts us. Craving is not just wanting something big; it is the constant low-level pull toward what is pleasant, comfortable, familiar, pleasurable."

The cycle — draw it on the board while speaking:

"Now: here is why they bite each other's tails. They drive each other."

Draw three arrows forming a triangle:

"Ignorance — not seeing clearly — leads to desire. Because when we don't see how things really are, we believe that getting what we want will make us happy. Permanently. So we crave.

"Craving leads to aversion. Because when we don't get what we want — or when we get it and then lose it — we push away. We resist. We resent. The snake strikes.

"And aversion creates more ignorance. Because when we are in the grip of hatred or resistance, we see even less clearly. The anger clouds everything. The confusion deepens.

"And from that deeper confusion — more craving. The wheel turns."

Step back from the board and let students look at what has been drawn.

"The three animals are at the center because this is the engine. Everything else in the Wheel — every realm, every form of suffering — is a consequence of these three, driving each other, endlessly."

Vocabulary Moment:

"The word klesha — Sanskrit. Tibetan: ཉོན་མོངས, nyönmong — often translated as 'afflictive emotion' or 'mental poison.' A klesha is a mental state that disturbs and distorts — it makes us see things wrongly, feel things disproportionately, and act harmfully. The three animals are the root kleshas — the ones all other afflictive states grow from. Anger, jealousy, pride, laziness — these are all expressions of one or more of these three roots.

"When you hear the word klesha — ཉོན་མོངས — think: a mental state that clouds the mind the way mud clouds water."

Write klesha / nyönmong / ཉོན་མོངས on the board.

Body Moment:

"Stand up. I'm going to teach you three gestures — one for each poison. Use them when I name the poison."

Teacher demonstrates each, then students practice:

  1. Ignorance: Both hands placed over the eyes. "Not seeing clearly. The pig. Ignorance."
  2. Desire: Both hands reaching forward, fingers open, grasping. "Reaching for what we want. The rooster. Desire."
  3. Aversion: Both hands pushing firmly outward, palms forward. "Pushing away what we don't want. The snake. Aversion."

Teacher calls each poison by name — students make the gesture. Repeat the sequence three times, increasing speed slightly:

"Ignorance. Desire. Aversion. Ignorance. Desire. Aversion."

Then, once: call the animal instead of the poison:

"Pig. Rooster. Snake."

Students must make the matching gesture. This tests whether the association has been made — it almost always produces laughter and accelerated attention.

Sit back down.

"Notice how each gesture felt different in your body. Reaching is different from pushing is different from covering your eyes. You've felt all three of these in your body today already — in some form. You felt them yesterday. These are not distant concepts."

Emotional Hook:

The recognition that arrives — usually quietly, sometimes with a small laugh of surprise — is this: these are not exotic Buddhist categories, they are what happened this morning. The teacher does not need to name this directly. The body moment and the turn and talk will surface it. What the teacher holds internally: this lesson is asking students to see themselves with unusual clarity, without judgment. That is a delicate thing. It deserves stillness.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: which of the three — ignorance, desire, or aversion — do you notice most often in yourself? Not as a confession, not as something you're ashamed of — as an observation. Like a scientist observing the weather. Be specific if you can: not just 'desire,' but 'desire when I want to keep playing when I should stop,' or 'aversion when someone criticises me.' Specific is more honest than general."

Partners talk for two to three minutes. Teacher circulates and listens. This is a moment of genuine self-disclosure — do not push for whole-group sharing unless students volunteer.

What to listen for: Students who can name a specific, concrete instance of the poison they notice most. Students who recognize the cycle in themselves ("when I don't get what I want, I get angry — and then I don't see anything clearly"). Students who are reluctant to answer may be deflecting or may simply be sitting with something real — both are valid. Do not require sharing.

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Return each student's in-progress Wheel drawing from C1 (the working drawing, not the from-memory sketch, which stays in the folder).

"Today's task: add the hub to your Wheel drawing. Draw the three animals in the center — the pig, the snake, the rooster — biting each other's tails in a circle. Label each one with its name and its poison."

Students work from the displayed image, which shows the hub clearly. Teacher circulates with specific prompts:

  • "Which direction does the snake face in the image? Draw it that way."
  • "The pig's body — does it face left or right?"
  • "How are they connected to each other? Are they touching? What does the connection look like?"
  • "Underneath each animal, write its name and its poison in small letters."

Students should also label the three poisons in the cycle diagram style (like what was drawn on the board) — a small note in the margin of their drawing, or alongside the hub.

Extension (for students who move quickly): Once the hub is drawn and labeled, students write a two-sentence caption for the hub — in their own words, not the tradition's — that explains what drives the Wheel. The constraint: no Buddhist vocabulary. What is happening in the center, in plain language?

Debrief — 5 minutes

"The Buddhist tradition spent two thousand years mapping the sources of human suffering. It asked: where does suffering come from? And it did not say: from bad luck, from bad people, from unfair circumstances. It said: from these three, inside. What do you think about that claim?"

Take three or four responses. Some students will push back — "but sometimes bad things just happen to you." This is good. The teacher can hold the tension without resolving it: "That's a real question. It doesn't mean your suffering is your fault. It means that how we respond — the craving, the aversion, the confusion — shapes what happens next. We'll keep returning to this."

End with: "You now know what is at the center of the Wheel. Next class, we move outward — to the sections of the Wheel, the places beings turn through. There are six of them. You've met the engine. Now we'll see where it drives beings."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set, student leader:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

Teacher's closing line:

"You now know the names of the three things that drive the Wheel. And you know them in your body, not just your mind. That matters. The pig, the snake, the rooster — they were already in you before you knew their names."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author):

Carry forward: The three-gesture body vocabulary (hands over eyes / reaching forward / pushing away) is a retrievable encoding that costs nothing to repeat. Use it as a warmup retrieval at the opening of C3 and C4 — simply name the poison or animal, students make the gesture; no explanation needed. This also functions as a quick comprehension check across Unit 2 when specific realms are being linked to their ruling kleshas. The turn and talk in this class is intentionally personal — "which do you notice most in yourself?" — and is designed to stay in pairs, not move to whole-group sharing. Do not require whole-group sharing of personal observations unless students volunteer. Note in Your Reflection which students engaged readily with the self-observation task and which deflected. Neither is a problem, but students who find the self-observation very uncomfortable may need particular care when Unit 2 examines the hungry ghost realm (U2.C2 is among the most psychologically demanding sessions in Grade 5). Spiral connection: this lesson is the first time in the Dharma Program that students are explicitly invited to see themselves as instances of the thing being studied — not to observe a concept from the outside but to locate it in their own experience. This mode of learning (self as data) was introduced gently in Grade 3's Tsethar unit (what did it feel like to release something?) and Grade 4's water bowl work (what does it feel like to give with no condition?). Grade 5 intensifies it. Students are ready. The teacher's role in this intensification is steadiness — not pushing, not rescuing, but holding the space with warmth so that genuine recognition can occur.
