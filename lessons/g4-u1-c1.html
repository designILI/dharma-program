Grade 4  ·  Unit 1  ·  Class 1

Why We Offer

The open palm → the mind that does not grasp

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleActivating prior experience before introducing new frames
Bloom's levelUnderstand
LessonG4.U1.C1

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The open palm → the mind that does not grasp

Cognitive Principle Featured: Activating prior experience before introducing new frames — students have already made offerings (Saga Dawa, Tsethar); today those experiences are named and examined, so that new conceptual vocabulary lands on something felt rather than something abstract.

Bloom's Level: Understand — students are not yet doing the practice; they are examining the act of offering itself, asking what it is and why human beings do it. The thinking required is interpretive: why do humans give things away?

Prior Class Summary: This is the first class of Grade 4. Students carry Grade 3's full year: a real Tsethar practice (compassionate release of life), Kind Acts practice sustained across the year, and the Saga Dawa observance including water bowl and butter lamp offerings. They arrive with embodied experience of making offerings; today that experience receives a name and a structure.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW that the act of offering is a universal human practice found across traditions and cultures, and that in Buddhist practice it is specifically called puja. Students will UNDERSTAND that a Buddhist offering is not a transaction but a training of the mind — the generosity of the gesture changes the one who makes it, and through Ngowa the merit is dedicated outward to all beings. Students will BE ABLE TO articulate, in their own words, why they think human beings make offerings — drawing on personal experience as well as the class discussion. Students will FEEL the physical and affective difference between an open hand and a closed fist — a small, honest encounter with the grasping mind and the offering mind, in their own bodies.

Materials Checklist

  • Student journals or small booklets — one per student (these will serve as the diagram journal carried across C1–C5; if pre-made booklets are not ready, folded A3 sheets stapled work well)
  • Pencils — one per student
  • A short list of offering examples written on the board before class (optional visual anchor): wedding garlands · harvest gifts · food for guests · butter lamps · flowers at a shrine
  • Plan B — if journals are not yet ready: Students write the C1 reflection on a single sheet, which the teacher collects, labels, and places inside a folder for each student. These sheets become the first pages of the journal once it is assembled.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 4 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline Prayer (extended form) · Dusum Sangay.

C1 only — Introduce the two new prayers before the full set is led by the student leader.

Say to the class: "Grade 4 adds two prayers to our set. We will chant them slowly today so you can hear them and understand what they mean. They will deepen across the year — you don't need to memorise them today. Just listen."

Sevenline Prayer (extended form): Before chanting, say: "The Sevenline Prayer calls on Guru Rinpoche — the great teacher who brought the Dharma to Tibet. We have chanted the seven-line prayer before. This form is longer — it carries more of the teaching. As we chant it, notice: it is an invitation, not a demand. We are not commanding Guru Rinpoche to appear. We are opening a door." Chant once through, slowly.

Dusum Sangay (བདུས་གསུམ་སངས་རྒྱས): Before chanting, say: "This prayer's name means 'Buddha of the Three Times' — past, present, and future. It is a prayer to Guru Rinpoche understood as the embodiment of all awakened beings across all of time. Grade 4 is the first year we chant this prayer, because this year you are old enough to hold a big idea: that time has three parts, and that awakened awareness exists in all three. We will talk more about this through the year." Chant once through, slowly.

Student leader then leads the full set, including the two new prayers.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Say to the class: "In Grade 3, you made offerings during Saga Dawa — water bowls, butter lamps, preparations. You also did a Tsethar. Both of those were acts of giving. Today's question is not about what you offered — it's about why. Why do humans give things? Not because they have to — but when they want to. What is happening when a person makes an offering?"

Pause. Give the question space.

Take 3–4 responses. Listen for: the idea of respect, the idea of gratitude, the idea of wanting something in return (this is honest — do not correct it yet; it will be addressed in the Mini Lesson), the idea of habit or duty.

Strong response to listen for: A student who says something like "it feels like you're giving part of yourself" or "it changes something inside you." Name this: "That's an important observation. Hold that."

Fallback if responses are thin: "Think of a time you gave something away — not because you were told to, but because you wanted to. What made you want to? What happened after?" This makes it personal before it is conceptual.

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

"Offering is one of the oldest things humans do. Long before writing, before cities, before most of what we call civilisation — people were making offerings. They offered food to the land before planting. They offered water to the river. They offered flowers to the dead. At weddings: gifts. At funerals: flowers. When an important guest arrives: food, a comfortable seat, water to drink. Something in us recognises that significance requires giving something away."

"What is different about a Buddhist offering? Two things."

"First: it is not a transaction. When we make an offering in this practice, we are not buying a favour. We are not trading water bowls for good luck. The Three Jewels — the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha — do not need our water. So why offer? Because the offering changes us. The gesture of giving without holding on — that is a training. Every time we open our hands and offer without wanting something back, we loosen the habit of grasping. And the habit of grasping is one of the roots of suffering."

"Second: the offering does not stop with the shrine. That is what the Ngowa prayer — the dedication — is for. When we chant Ngowa, we are sending whatever goodness was created by the practice outward to all beings. The offering becomes infinite — not because of magic, but because the intention reaches everywhere."

Vocabulary Moment: "The Sanskrit word puja — you may hear this word used at the monastery or during ceremonies. It means worship, reverence, or honouring. A puja is not prayer in the Western sense — it is an act of respectful attention, given form through gesture and object. When we make the seven-bowl offering, we are doing a form of puja. You are learning to read the language of puja — what the objects mean, what the gestures do, what the attention is for."

Body Moment: "Put both hands out in front of you — palms up, open. Feel what that gesture is. Open. Not grabbing. Not holding. That is the gesture of offering." Pause. Let the class sit with it for a moment.

"Now close your hands. Make a fist." Pause. "Feel that. That is the grasping mind — not a bad thing, just something to notice."

"Now open your hands again." Pause.

"The difference between the closed hand and the open hand is the difference between the grasping mind and the offering mind. Every time we make an offering — every time we open our hands — we are practising that shift. That's what the bowls are for."

Emotional Hook: "Offerings have been made on this land — in these mountains — for a very long time. Longer than any of us can picture. People have walked up to shrines in rain and cold and sun, set out bowls, poured water, lit butter lamps. They didn't ask whether it mattered. They kept the practice. We are not inventing something new. We are entering something old."

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: what is something you have given away — not because you had to, but because you wanted to — that cost you something? It doesn't have to be an object. It could be time, or help, or saying sorry. What did it feel like after?"

Give pairs 2 minutes to talk. Then: "Did anyone find — and you don't have to share this — that giving something away made you feel lighter? That's what we're going to be studying this unit. That feeling has a shape to it. The seven-bowl offering gives it a structure."

What to listen for: Students who describe giving something away and feeling relief, or pride, or connection — these are the experiential anchors for the teaching about offerings changing the giver.

Activity — 15–20 minutes

"Open your journal to the first page. At the top, write today's date and this title: Why do humans offer?"

"Write your answer. Not the 'right' answer — your answer. You have been thinking about this for the last twenty minutes. What do you actually think is happening when a human being makes an offering? Why do we do it?"

"Write at least three sentences. You can write more. This is not a test — it is your thinking. You will come back to this page at the end of Unit 1, and you will be able to see how your thinking has changed."

Teacher circulates. Do not correct or redirect — this is a genuine thinking exercise, not a comprehension check. If a student is stuck, ask: "What did you give away last year at Saga Dawa? Why did you make that offering?" Return the student to their own experience.

Extension: Students who finish early can add a second question to their journal page: "What does it feel like, in your body, to make an offering? Try to describe the physical feeling, not just the idea."

Debrief — 5 minutes

"By the end of this unit, you will be able to set up a correct seven-bowl water offering — the right order, the right spacing, the right way to pour and remove the water. And you will be able to explain what each bowl represents and why. But here is the more important thing: you will understand why the practice exists. What it is doing to the mind of the person who makes it."

"That's what we're really studying this year — the language of ritual. Not just what things are, but what they do. The seven bowls are not just bowls. They are a teaching, encoded in objects. Next class, we find out what the teaching is."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline Prayer (extended form) · Dusum Sangay.

Student leader leads.

Teacher's closing line: "You have made offerings before without knowing exactly why. Next class, you will know — and the knowing will change how the practice feels."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The C1 written reflection — "Why do humans offer?" — is the opening page of each student's diagram journal. This journal is a small booklet or portfolio carried across C1–C5 and returned to students after Demonstration Day (C6). Keep it safely between classes. Do not grade the C1 reflection — its value is as a before-and-after record: students will revisit it in C5 and again on Demonstration Day, and the change in their answer is one of the clearest indicators of genuine learning in this unit.

The open-palms body moment is worth returning to across the unit as a quick settling gesture at the start of class — it encodes the spirit of the practice without words. By C6, you may not need to name it; students will already know what it means.

Spiral connections: Students made water bowl offerings in G3.U3 (Saga Dawa preparation classes). They used the two-handed offering gesture — both hands at heart level — in G3.U3.C3. The Tsethar practice also required genuine giving without holding on (releasing the animal). Grade 4 gives all of that experience a name — puja, the offering mind, the open hand. This class is the conceptual naming of what they have already done in the body.

For the two new prayers: if the resident teacher is available to chant Dusum Sangay or the extended Sevenline Prayer with the class today, invite them. If not, chant slowly and emphasise meaning over pronunciation on this first encounter. Pronunciation will improve over the year; understanding should come first.
