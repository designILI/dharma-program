Born Again?

A flame passing from one candle to another → the Buddhist teaching on rebirth as the transmission of a consciousness stream, not a fixed soul

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principlePrior knowledge activation as anchor
Bloom's levelAnalyze
LessonG6.U1.C1

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): A flame passing from one candle to another → the Buddhist teaching on rebirth as the transmission of a consciousness stream, not a fixed soul

Cognitive Principle Featured: Prior knowledge activation as anchor — students are asked to surface their existing view before any new content is introduced, so the new material has something to land against rather than landing in a vacuum. The starting-position paragraph locks that anchor in writing for later comparison.

Bloom's Level: Analyze — students are not asked to accept or reject the teaching, but to examine it alongside other positions and consider what implications it carries. The philosophical question "what would change if this were true?" is an analytic move, not a recall task.

Prior Class Summary: This is the first class of Grade 6. Students carry the full structure of the Wheel of Life from Grade 5 — six realms, three poisons, three antidotes, the figure outside pointing to liberation, and their own name placed at the end of a lineage chain stretching back to the Buddha. They were told at the end of Grade 5 that Grade 6 would enter the territory of death, impermanence, and the Bardo. They arrive with some unease and genuine curiosity.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the Buddhist view of rebirth as distinct from the Hindu concept of ātman — what continues is not a fixed, unchanging soul but a consciousness stream, a momentum of causes and effects, habits, and tendencies.

Students will UNDERSTAND that the rebirth teaching is not empirically provable or disprovable in ordinary terms, and that the Buddhist tradition has always held it as such — what the tradition asks instead is: what would change about how you live if you took it seriously?

Students will BE ABLE TO articulate their own starting view of what happens after death and set it alongside the Buddhist position without collapsing the difference between them.

Students will FEEL the philosophical seriousness of standing at the edge of a question that does not resolve easily — a productive, grounded sense of genuine inquiry rather than anxiety or false certainty.

Materials Checklist

  • Writing journals or loose paper, one per student (for the starting-position paragraph)
  • Pens or pencils
  • Two candles and a lighter (for the teacher's demonstration — see Body Moment)
  • Plan B: If candles cannot be used in the classroom (fire safety), describe the flame analogy in words and invite students to cup their hands and perform the passing gesture without a physical flame. The gesture carries the teaching without the prop.
  • The full Grade 6 prayer cards or sheets (Units 1–2 set — no Dolma yet), one per student or posted visibly
  • Student leader list (to track rotation)

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set (Units 1–2 set): Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Note: The Dolma (Tara Prayer) is not yet introduced — it enters in Unit 3. The Food Offering Prayer appears in closing only.

Student leader leads the prayers. After prayers settle, the teacher waits a breath, then speaks:

"Grade 6 is a year that goes somewhere most school curricula don't go. We are going to spend time thinking carefully about death, about what comes after, and about what that means for how we live right now. This is serious territory — and it is the territory that Buddhist practice has been mapping for two and a half thousand years. Let's begin."

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "Before I say anything: what do you already think happens after death? Not what you've been taught — what do you actually think? Take thirty seconds, just for yourself."

Allow thirty seconds of genuine silence. Then:

"If you want to, share it."

Take 3–4 voluntary responses. Receive each one fully — no correction, no affirmation, no "that's interesting." Simply: "Thank you." If a student says "I don't know," receive that too: "That's a real answer. Uncertainty is a live position."

After 3–4 responses: "Thank you. All of those are live positions — positions that serious people hold, that religious and philosophical traditions have held for thousands of years. Today we add the Buddhist view to the conversation. Not to replace what you said — to sit beside it."

Fallback if students are reluctant to share: "Write it down first — just for yourself. No one will read it unless you want them to." Then take responses from those who volunteered something on paper.

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Teacher establishes the four key points of the Buddhist rebirth teaching, pausing between each:

1. Not the same as a soul. "The Buddhist teaching on rebirth is sometimes confused with the idea of a soul — a fixed, permanent self that travels from body to body unchanged. Buddhism does not teach that. What Buddhism says continues is more like a stream — a momentum of causes and effects, habits, tendencies, unresolved patterns. Imagine not a passenger who gets out of one car and into another, but the direction the car was heading — that direction continues."

2. The flame analogy. Teacher lights one candle, lets it settle, then touches the wick of a second unlit candle to the flame of the first, lighting it.

"One candle lights another. Is it the same flame? Not exactly — the flame on the second candle is not the flame from the first candle, transported. But neither is it completely unrelated. Something was transmitted. A quality passed. This is the closest analogy the tradition gives for what happens to consciousness as it moves from one life to the next."

Body Moment: Extinguish both candles. "Cup your hands. Imagine a small flame in them." Students cup their hands. "Now — slowly, without any dramatic gesture — move your hands apart as if you're passing the flame to the person beside you." Students pass the imaginary flame around the room, hand to hand. Slowly. No one rushes. Teacher: "Notice: no flame actually traveled. And yet something moved. That's the analogy. Hold that."

3. It is consistent with the Wheel. "Everything we studied in Grade 5 — the six realms, the three poisons, the three antidotes — describes what shapes this momentum. A life dominated by hatred tends toward the hell realm. A life of generosity tends upward. The Wheel is not just a picture of where consciousness lives — it is a map of where the stream is headed, based on how it has been flowing."

4. It is not provable in ordinary terms. "The Buddhist tradition has never claimed that rebirth can be empirically demonstrated — tested in a laboratory, measured with instruments. It asks a different kind of question: what would change about how you live if you took this teaching seriously? That is the philosopher's question, and it is the question we will be asking all unit."

Vocabulary Moment: "Vijñāna-santāna — Sanskrit for 'consciousness stream.' Vijñāna means consciousness or awareness; santāna means continuum, stream. Not a solid thing — a process. Like a river: always moving, always recognisable as the same river, but never the same water twice. The Buddhist teaching says this stream is what continues — not a fixed self."

Emotional Hook: The philosophical vertigo of taking seriously the possibility that this life is not the first one. Teacher names it directly, without dramatising: "There is a particular feeling that comes from genuinely considering the possibility that you have been alive before — that the choices you make in this life are connected to choices made before it, and choices that will be made after. That feeling — the weight of it, the strangeness of it — is appropriate. Let it be there."

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Turn to your partner. Here is the question: if rebirth were real — if this is not your first life — what do you think you might have been doing before this one? And what might that mean for what you're here to do now?"

Structure: each partner speaks for about ninety seconds without interruption. Then switch.

What to listen for (teacher circulates): Are students engaging the implication, or deflecting into humor? Humor is fine — it is a natural response to philosophical vertigo. Gently redirect: "That's one answer. What if it were actually true?"

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Students write their starting-position paragraph — a genuine philosophical entry, not a summary of the lesson. The prompt:

"What I currently think about what happens after death — and why I think it."

Teacher says: "This is your before-marker. We will return to it at the end of the unit. Write honestly — not what sounds right, not what you think I want to hear. What you actually think, and your best current reason for thinking it. Three to five sentences is enough. More is welcome."

Teacher circulates — not reading over shoulders, but available: "What's the hardest part of writing this? What do you find yourself wanting to say but aren't sure how?" For students who finish early: "Can you write one sentence about what would have to be true for you to change your mind?"

Scaffold for students who are stuck: "Start with: 'I think that when someone dies...' and finish that sentence. Then write why."

Extension for students who want to go further: "Write a second paragraph: what position do you find hardest to argue against — and why?"

Debrief — 5 minutes

"We are not here to decide whether rebirth is true. We are here to think carefully about what it would mean — and whether thinking about it changes anything about how we live. That is the philosopher's question. It is the question that the Buddhist tradition has been sitting with for two and a half thousand years. You are part of that conversation now."

Opening question: "What is the strongest reason you can think of against the rebirth teaching — the objection that would be hardest to answer?"

Take 2–3 responses. Do not resolve them. "Good. Hold that. It will be important."

Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full closing prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Teacher's closing line: "You have just done something that most school curricula don't ask of students your age — you have looked honestly at one of the oldest questions human beings have ever asked, and you have written down where you stand. That takes courage. We'll come back to what you wrote."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The starting-position paragraph written today is the before-marker for Unit 1 — store it safely and return it to students in C4. Do not let students keep it; they will revise their thinking and the comparison matters. This unit requires a particular tone throughout: philosophical inquiry, not religious instruction. Students are not asked to believe the rebirth teaching. They are asked to examine it and consider its implications. This distinction should be named clearly in C1 and held across all four classes — any drift toward "this is what we believe" should be corrected gently but explicitly. Some students will be strongly influenced by their family's religious background (Christian, agnostic, Hindu, or their own version of Tibetan Buddhist). All positions are received without contradiction or affirmation. Spiral note: the Wheel's six realms (Grade 5, Unit 2) are the destination-space within which rebirth operates. Students already know the map — this unit asks them to consider the journey between lives, and what determines where a being lands. Do not re-teach the Wheel; refer to it as shared ground.
