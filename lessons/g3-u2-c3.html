Our Kind Acts

Writing one sentence about what a kind act cost → examining the difference between genuinely compassionate action and performance

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleCognitive dissonance as learning
Bloom's levelApply / early Analyze
LessonG3.U2.C3

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Writing one sentence about what a kind act cost → examining the difference between genuinely compassionate action and performance

Cognitive Principle Featured: Cognitive dissonance as learning — students encounter the uncomfortable possibility that a kind act they are proud of may have been partly motivated by wanting recognition. The discomfort, held honestly, deepens the practice.

Bloom's Level: Apply / early Analyze — students apply the concepts of genuine kindness and cost to their own recent acts; Analyze begins as they examine their motivation honestly

Prior Class Summary: C2 trained the skill of spotting the moment — slowing down, looking up, asking whether anyone nearby is struggling. Students committed to a harder act than last time and practised the three-step attention habit.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW that a kind act done without an audience is equally real as one that is witnessed, and equally valid Students will UNDERSTAND that genuine compassion in action does not depend on reciprocity — on being thanked, recognised, or noticed Students will BE ABLE TO name what their most recent kind act cost them, and reflect honestly on whether they hoped for something in return Students will FEEL the mild, specific discomfort of looking honestly at their own motivation — without shame, but without looking away

Materials Checklist

  • The Kind Acts map from C1 and C2, already on the wall
  • Markers or pencils for each student to add to the map
  • Index cards or sticky notes (for documenting the "cost" entry)
  • Plan B: If a student did not carry out the harder act from C2, do not leave them without a map entry. Invite them to document the attempt or the near-miss: "Write about what you noticed you could do — and what stopped you. That is also data about what kindness costs." Their card goes on the map alongside everyone else's.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full prayer set, led by the student leader for this class. Prayers in order: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer. (Food Offering Prayer at closing only.)

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

The teacher begins: "You committed to a harder act this time. What happened?"

Take 4–5 responses. After each one, ask: "Harder how? What exactly made it harder than the first one?" Listen for: the effort was greater; it required courage (saying something when it was easier to be silent); it cost time or comfort; there was no immediate reward. Name the kind of hardness when you hear it: "That sounds like a courage cost. That sounds like a time cost. That sounds like a comfort cost."

If a student says it wasn't actually harder — probe gently: "What would have made it harder? What would the next step up be?"

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

"Today we go a little deeper. Two questions."

Question 1: What makes a kind act genuinely kind?

"A kind act is not performed for the other person's appreciation — or at least, it shouldn't need to be. It is done because the person in front of you matters, regardless of whether they say thank you, regardless of whether anyone sees you do it. Think of the Giving Tree from Grade 1 — the tree didn't ask for thanks. It gave because giving was what it did."

Ask the class: "If you help someone and they don't say thank you — was it still a kind act?" (Yes.) "If no one sees you do it — did it still happen?" (Yes.) "If you do something kind and the person doesn't even realise you helped — was it real?" (Yes.) "The act went into the world. It happened. That is enough."

Question 2: What does it cost?

"A kind act that costs nothing — that required no effort, no choice, no giving-up of something — is easy to do and easy to undo. The acts that matter are the ones that cost something: time, energy, comfort, status. The Tsethar cost six classes of time and preparation. A Tuesday kind act costs something smaller — but it still costs. What did yours cost?"

Vocabulary Moment: "The word reciprocity — the expectation of getting something back. I give you something; I expect you to give me something in return. True compassion in action has no reciprocity expectation. You act because the being in front of you matters — not because you will receive appreciation, attention, or a favour later. This is hard. It goes against how we are wired. But it is the practice."

Body Moment: "Think of something kind you did this week. Now check — inside yourself, honestly: did you do it hoping for something back? Appreciation? Someone noticing? A thank-you? This is not a judgment question. It is an honest check." Allow ten seconds of genuine quiet. "Now: did it matter anyway? Was it kind even if you wanted recognition? Can both things be true?" Hold the question without resolving it. "Both things can be true. We are learning to act even when the wanting-something-back is there. We don't have to be perfect. We have to be honest and keep going."

Emotional Hook: Most eight-year-olds have a strong instinct for reciprocity — they notice when thanks is not given, when effort is not seen, when generosity is not returned. This lesson does not shame that instinct. It names it, holds it honestly, and gently presses against it. The discomfort of acting without recognition — of doing something real that no one sees — is the emotional terrain of this class.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: what was the hardest kind act you've done this unit — across all three classes now. What made it hard: the effort it took, or the fact that no one noticed?"

Allow 2 minutes. Then take 3–4 responses. Listen for students who are genuinely grappling with the recognition question versus students who give the answer they think is expected. Both responses are useful — the performing student is doing the same thing the lesson is about.

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Two parts:

Part 1 — Map entry (8–10 min): Each student adds their most recent act to the Kind Acts map — one sentence describing what they did. Then: a second sentence about what it cost. "What did this act cost you? Time? Effort? The comfort of staying silent? Something else?" Teacher circulates: "What exactly did you give up or give differently in order to do this? Was it worth it?"

Part 2 — Cost reflection (5–8 min): Students write one sentence — on their card or directly on the map — answering: "Was it worth it? And did you want something back?" This does not need to be a settled answer. It can be honest uncertainty: "I think it was worth it, but I also wanted them to say thank you and they didn't." Both parts belong on the map.

Debrief — 5 minutes

"A kind act that nobody sees — that still happened. The fish we released during the Tsethar does not know who released it. The person you helped this week may not remember by tomorrow. But the act went into the world. It is real. And that is what Ngowa is — we dedicate the merit and send it outward. We don't keep it. We give it away, and the giving is the whole point."

One question to leave them with: "Before next class — one more act. And this time: try to do one that no one will know about but you."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set. Student leader leads. Food Offering Prayer included. Teacher's closing line: "Today you looked honestly at your own motivation — what you hoped for, what you gave. That honesty is the practice. May whatever good came from this class go outward, to all beings, seen and unseen."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The question of reciprocity and recognition is one of the most important in the whole curriculum. It will return formally in Grade 5 (Wheel of Life — the hungry ghost realm is partly constituted by needing endless return from the world) and again in Grade 8 (the ethical dimension of Bodhichitta and the six paramitas). Note which students are genuinely engaging with the question of motivation and which are performing the engagement — both observations are worth recording for the teacher's own reflection. Do not single out the performing students; their performance is itself a form of the lesson's subject.

Spiral connection: The Giving Tree reference connects to Grade 1 Unit 2 (Generosity). If students do not make the connection, make it for them briefly: "In Grade 1, you worked with the story of a tree that gave without asking for anything back. You're living that story now." The concept of Ngowa (dedication of merit, already in the prayer set) is the liturgical companion to this lesson — the prayer and the practice are the same teaching. Name this: "When we say Ngowa, we're doing what this lesson is about. We send the goodness out. We don't hold it."
