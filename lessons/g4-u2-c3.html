Shaping

Refining the torma's form and adding detail → the difference between imposing a plan on material and listening to what material can do

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleMetacognitive awareness during physical practice
Bloom's levelApply with early Analyze
LessonG4.U2.C3

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Refining the torma's form and adding detail → the difference between imposing a plan on material and listening to what material can do Cognitive Principle Featured: Metacognitive awareness during physical practice — students are asked to observe their own process while it is happening. The "put it down and look" moment creates a deliberate interruption of habitual effort: looking at one's own work as if for the first time trains the same reflective capacity that meditation develops. Bloom's Level: Apply with early Analyze — students continue to apply skill to material, but the lesson also asks them to step back and evaluate: what is the torma doing, what does it want to become, what is the quality of their attention right now? The Turn and Talk introduces peer observation as a form of analysis. Prior Class Summary: C2 was the first making session — students worked the material for the first time, built the basic cone form, and encountered the gap between what they planned and what their hands could achieve. They carry the concept of zopa (patience as active continuation) and the experience of working an unfamiliar material. Their unfinished tormas have been stored and are returned today exactly as they were left.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the word craftsmanship — what it means and how it is developed (through repeated practice and genuine care, not through talent). Students will UNDERSTAND that there are two modes of working with material: imposing a plan, and listening and collaborating; and that the best ritual objects often come from the second. Students will BE ABLE TO refine their torma's basic form and, where structurally appropriate, add at least one decorative element from their original sketch. Students will FEEL the shift that happens when a form begins to become particular — when the generic lump of material stops being anonymous and starts being theirs.

Materials Checklist

  • Stored tormas from C2 (returned to each student)
  • Making material — small additional portion per student available in reserve, in case material was lost, cracked, or dried between sessions
  • Work surface protection (wax paper or cloth squares, as in C2)
  • Small bowls of water (one per table) for smoothing, if using salt dough or clay
  • Blunt wooden shaping tool or skewer per student (optional but useful for fine detail work)
  • Students' design sketches from C1, for reference
  • Sketchbooks or portfolio paper — for the optional one-sentence portfolio note at end of session
  • Plan B (if a student's torma has cracked or broken beyond repair between C2 and C3): Have reserve material ready. Do not make this a large event — simply provide new material and say: "Start from where you are. The patience it took to begin is still there." The structural loss is not a loss of the learning.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader for this class leads the full Grade 4 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay (Food Offering Prayer is reserved for the closing.)

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Tormas are distributed — returned exactly as stored, one per student. Students place them on their work surfaces.

"You left this unfinished last class. You are returning to it now. Before you touch it: what have you been thinking about it, if anything? Has it occupied your mind at all?"

Brief, honest responses. Some students will have thought about their torma during the week — about what they wanted to change, what they planned to add. Some will not have thought about it at all. Both are accurate responses, and neither is more correct. Do not suggest that thinking about it is the expected outcome.

Then: "Today we refine — not start over. Work with what you made. The imperfections are part of the object now. Your job is not to correct the past. Your job is to continue from where it is."

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Keep this brief. Two to three minutes of focused talk, then move to the Body Moment and into the making.

"There are two ways of working with material. In the first, you have a plan — a clear image of what the finished thing should look like — and you impose that plan on the material. You force it into the shape you decided. In the second, you work differently. You notice what the material wants to do. You notice its resistance, where it yields easily, where it holds. And you work with that, adjusting your plan as you go. The best ritual objects — and the most satisfying things to make — often come from the second way. Not from abandoning your plan, but from holding it lightly enough that the material can also contribute."

"This is not mysticism. It is craftsmanship. The skilled maker is not the one with the strongest idea, but the one who can hold an idea and stay responsive at the same time."

Vocabulary Moment: Write craftsmanship on the board.

"Craftsmanship is the quality that comes from making something with full attention and genuine skill. It is not the same as talent. Talent might give you a head start. Craftsmanship is developed — through repetition, through care, through staying with the work when it is difficult. The torma you make in this class, over three sessions, is a crafted object. That is something."

Body Moment: "Put down your torma for a moment. Don't touch it. Look at it as if you are seeing it for the first time — as if someone else made it and you are encountering it now. What do you notice? What is it already asking to become? What does it want?"

Thirty seconds of looking. The teacher does the same — looking at the demonstration torma without touching it, modelling genuine looking. This pause is not decorative; it is the cognitive reset that makes the next phase of making different from simply continuing where one stopped.

After thirty seconds: "Now: continue."

Emotional Hook: The moment in this session when the form becomes particular — when the material stops being generic and the student begins to recognise it as theirs. This cannot be planned for; it arrives somewhere during the making period. The teacher watches for it and, when it is visible in a student's body language (a slight leaning back, a pause, a different quality of looking), can name it quietly: "Something just shifted for you — I noticed."

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

This happens after approximately ten minutes of making — mid-session, when students have been working long enough to have something to show.

"Set down your tools. Pick up your torma with both hands and turn to your partner. Show it to them — not critique, just look. Your job: tell them one thing you notice about their torma that they might not see themselves. Not whether it's good or bad — just what you notice."

Students pair up. Two minutes of observation and quiet comment. This is peer observation, not evaluation — the teacher should model this distinction by circulating and making their own observations in the same register: "I notice the base here is very even," not "this part is better than that part."

What to listen for: observations that are concrete and specific ("the top is leaning left," "the base is wider on one side") are ideal. Abstract evaluations ("it's nice," "I like it") are less useful — if these arise, the teacher can ask: "Can you say what specifically you like? What detail?"

Activity — 15–20 minutes

The full making period. Students refine the form and add decorative detail where structurally appropriate.

The teacher circulates with specific, positive observations — noting what is working, not cataloguing what is not. Some formulations that are useful:

  • "I notice you've brought the base into a more even circle — that took attention."
  • "The ridge you've added here echoes the shape of the whole — that's good design, even if you didn't plan it."
  • "You're working very carefully with this section — what is happening under your hands?"

For students who achieve the basic form and all planned decoration early: set the torma aside and write one sentence for the portfolio — a description of the torma in the student's own words. Not "what I did" but "what it is": describe the object as if to someone who cannot see it. This is harder than it sounds and produces thoughtful writing.

At the end of the making period: store tormas again, in the same careful way as C2. Announce clearly: "Next class is our last making session. Come ready to finish." This is not a threat — it is a preparation. The student needs to know that C4 is the end of the making, so that they arrive with intention.

Debrief — 5 minutes

"As the form becomes clearer — as it becomes more yours — does that change how you feel about offering it? You are making something that will be given away. And it is becoming more itself. What does that feel like?"

Take 2–3 responses. Do not resolve the question. This is the question the unit is built around, and it should remain open through C4 and into C5. Students who feel genuine reluctance are not failing — they are closest to the heart of the teaching. Students who feel ready to give it away may be practising a kind of equanimity, or may not yet have felt fully attached to their work. Both conditions are worth noting.

Closing — 3 minutes

The student leader leads the full closing prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "The form is yours now — it has your particular marks in it. Next class, we finish it. Come ready."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: C4 is the finishing session — students should be able to complete their tormas in one period. Assess at the end of C3 where each student's form is: does it stand? Is the basic cone structurally sound? Decoration can be incomplete going into C4, but the structural form should be there. If several students are significantly behind on structure, prioritise structure in C4 before any decoration. Warn students clearly at the end of C3 — "next class is our last making session" — so they arrive with that understanding. If material has dried between C2 and C3 for some students (common with salt dough in dry climates), have reserve material ready. For clay: provide a damp cloth to wrap unfinished work during the session if the clay is drying too quickly under warm hands. C4 note: confirm before C4 that you have arranged for the dissolution or disposal of the tormas after C5 — this needs school administration awareness. If dissolution in water: have a clean vessel ready. If burning: requires outdoor space and adult supervision. The students do not need to witness this — it can happen after the class and be reported to them in the next unit. What matters is that the teacher knows what will happen and can speak about it truthfully in C5. Spiral connection: the "listening to material" theme introduced in this lesson connects back to G3's work with the body as a vehicle for teaching (where students learned that the body knows before the mind does) and forward to G5's Wheel of Life (where students will encounter complex symbolic form and be asked to "read" rather than immediately interpret).

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child continued making their torma — the ritual sculpture they will offer on the shrine in Class 5. Today's session focused on refining the form and beginning to add detail. The class discussed craftsmanship — how skill is built through attention and care, not talent. You can ask them: 'What did your torma look like today? What did you add? Does it feel like it's becoming yours?'"
