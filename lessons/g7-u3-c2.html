More Than the Body

The Medicine Buddha mantra and the three poisons → the Buddhist teaching that illness and suffering arise from the same root, and that the mantra is a vehicle for directed healing attention

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleEmbodied encoding
Bloom's levelUnderstand / Evaluate
LessonG7.U3.C2

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The Medicine Buddha mantra and the three poisons → the Buddhist teaching that illness and suffering arise from the same root, and that the mantra is a vehicle for directed healing attention

Cognitive Principle Featured: Embodied encoding — the mantra is learned not as text but through the body (listening, joining, carrying); understanding follows the physical experience of chanting it, not the other way around

Bloom's Level: Understand / Evaluate — students deepen their understanding of the Buddhist framework for illness and evaluate the claim that healing the "root cause" (the three poisons) is the Medicine Buddha's real work

Prior Class Summary: C1 introduced Sangye Menla — his appearance, his twelve vows, and the meaning of genuine healing intention. Students chose one person to hold through the unit and wrote their C1 portfolio entry. They carry the thirty-second body moment: the felt quality of genuinely wishing for a specific person to be well.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the Medicine Buddha mantra — TAYATHA OM BEKANDZE BEKANDZE MAHA BEKANDZE RADZA SAMUDGATE SOHA — its syllables and its meaning, including the significance of bekandze (eliminating pain/illness, including the pain of the three poisons).

Students will UNDERSTAND that the Buddhist framework locates the root of all suffering — including physical illness — in the three poisons (ignorance, desire, aversion), and that the Medicine Buddha's healing therefore addresses both the physical and the root dimensions of suffering.

Students will BE ABLE TO chant the Medicine Buddha mantra from memory with reasonable accuracy and direct it toward their chosen person with genuine sustained attention.

Students will FEEL the physical resonance of the mantra in their body — the specific quality of a healing sound directed toward someone specific — as distinct from reciting words.

Materials Checklist

  • Student portfolios
  • Writing instruments
  • Whiteboard for writing the mantra text and meaning
  • Teacher's own voice (the mantra is introduced through the teacher's chanting — no recording needed or preferred; the live voice is the teaching)
  • Plan B: If the teacher is uncertain of the correct pronunciation, practise beforehand using a reliable recording of the mantra. The syllables are: TAY-a-THA · OM · be-KAN-dze · be-KAN-dze · ma-HA be-KAN-dze · RAD-za · sa-MUD-ga-te · SO-HA. Accuracy matters more than speed — a slow, clear pronunciation is preferable to a fluent but inexact one.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 7 prayer set, student leader.

Prayer sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod

Prior Knowledge Activation — 6 minutes

Teacher: "Last class I asked what healing looks like. Today: what causes illness, in the Buddhist understanding? Not physically — what causes suffering at its root?"

Take responses. Students have encountered the three poisons in Grade 5. If they do not surface immediately, the teacher can guide: "What is at the centre of the Wheel of Life? What are the three figures in the hub?" Guide toward: the pig (ignorance), the snake (aversion/hatred), and the bird (desire/attachment).

Once surfaced: "Right. The three poisons. Today we look at how the Medicine Buddha's healing addresses not just the symptoms — the illness, the pain — but those three roots. And we add the mantra."

Mini Lesson — 13 minutes

Why the Medicine Buddha's healing is more than the body.

"The Buddhist teaching is not that physical illness is imaginary or that it can be cured by willpower. Physical illness has physical causes — diet, environment, injury, aging. All real. But the Buddha taught something more: that underneath all suffering — including physical illness — is the same root. The three poisons."

"Ignorance: not knowing the interdependent nature of reality. We think we are a fixed, separate self — a thing that must be protected, fed, and preserved. That misperception generates grasping (desire) and pushing away (aversion). And that misalignment with reality — that constant struggling against the way things actually are — is the source of suffering in every domain, including the suffering of a body that is ill and a mind that cannot find peace."

"The Medicine Buddha's twelve vows address both levels. He vows to heal the physical — the hunger, the sensory impairments, the illness. And he vows to help beings encounter the teaching that addresses the root. That is why his practice is 'more than the body.'"

The mantra.

Write on the board: TAYATHA OM BEKANDZE BEKANDZE MAHA BEKANDZE RADZA SAMUDGATE SOHA

"The Medicine Buddha mantra. Let's take it apart:

Bekandze — eliminating pain and illness. Specifically, the pain of illness — but also the pain caused by the three poisons. Bekandze (again) — the repetition intensifies the invocation. Maha Bekandze — greatly eliminating pain. Maha means great. Radza Samudgate — arising like the king of medicine. Soha — so be it. May this be so. Tayatha — thus it is / in this way. Om — the sacred syllable of body, speech, and mind.

So the whole mantra is something like: 'Thus — Om — eliminating illness, eliminating illness, greatly eliminating illness — arising like the king of medicine — so be it.' It is an invocation of the healing quality, addressed to the Medicine Buddha, on behalf of a specific being."

"The mantra is not a magic formula. It is a vehicle for sustained, directed attention. The attention is the practice. The mantra gives the attention a form — a sound that carries it."

Vocabulary Moment: Psychosomatic — from Greek psyche (mind) + soma (body). The relationship between mental and physical states. The Buddhist teaching that suffering arises from mental states (the three poisons) does not contradict modern medicine — in fact, modern medicine increasingly recognises the psychosomatic dimension of illness: how states of mind affect the body's immune function, pain experience, and recovery. The Medicine Buddha's practice addresses both dimensions, which is why it has been used for centuries for healing of the sick, the dying, and those in mental and emotional suffering.

Body Moment: The teacher chants the mantra slowly and clearly, once through, while students listen with eyes closed, hands in lap. No pressure to join. Just listen.

Second chanting: "Join any syllable you recognise." Students begin to find the sound.

Third chanting: teacher's voice gradually fades. Students carry it.

After the third chanting, a moment of silence. Then: "Where did you feel it in your body? Not where did you think it — where did you feel it?"

Take two or three responses. The physical location varies — chest, throat, top of the head, behind the eyes. All are right answers.

Emotional Hook: The mantra landing in the body — the physical experience of a healing sound directed toward someone specific. This is not description; it is the event of the class.

Turn and Talk — 3 minutes

"Tell your partner: as you chanted the mantra, who were you holding in your mind? What did it feel like to direct that sound toward that person?"

Teacher listens to two or three exchanges. Does not evaluate or respond — just receives.

Activity — 10 minutes

Step 1 (5 minutes): Students copy the mantra into their portfolios with its meaning — syllable-by-syllable breakdown as written on the board. Teacher encourages: "If it helps, write the phonetic pronunciation under each syllable. This is in your portfolio permanently — it's your mantra reference for the rest of the unit."

Step 2 (5 minutes): A second full chanting together, this time with eyes closed and the chosen person held in mind throughout. Teacher guides quietly before it begins: "See their face. Hold their name. Direct the mantra toward them — not as a performance of directing, but as a genuine wish. You are sending this toward them." The teacher chants with the class, not louder than the class — holding the group, not leading from above.

After: brief silence. Then students may open their eyes.

Teacher circulates between the writing and the chanting — watching, not correcting. The presence is what matters.

Debrief — 4 minutes

"The mantra is now in your body. Between today and our next class, chant it at least once a day for the person you're holding. It does not need to be loud. It can be silent — mouthing the syllables, hearing them internally. The practice is the intention. The mantra is the form. Every time you chant it for that person, you are practising."

"The three poisons — ignorance, desire, aversion — are what the Medicine Buddha vowed to address at their root. Your practice cannot eliminate the three poisons in your chosen person. But the sincere wish that they be free from suffering — held in the form of the mantra — is itself a real thing. It changes the practitioner. The tradition also says it reaches outward. Whether or not we can verify that, the practice is worth making."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set, student leader. Food Offering Prayer included.

Teacher's closing line: "The mantra is yours now. Carry it for the person you chose."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author):

Carry forward: Between C2 and C3, students are asked to practise the mantra daily for their chosen person. This is the first explicit between-class practice instruction in the curriculum at this level — not just "think about it" but "chant it, once, for this person." Note in C3's Prior Knowledge Activation whether students practised — not to evaluate or grade, but because the question surfaces the reality of the room. Students who practised will have something to report; students who forgot will remember that they forgot. Both are useful starting points for C3.

The three poisons (Grade 5 — the centre of the Wheel of Life) are the spiral connection here. Students who remember the Wheel clearly will make the connection to the Medicine Buddha's healing immediately. If there are students who joined the program in Grade 6 or 7 and do not carry that prior knowledge, the PKA is designed to surface it without putting those students at a disadvantage — the teacher can reconstruct the three poisons from the group's collective memory in the PKA phase.

The psychosomatic vocabulary moment is a deliberate bridge between the Buddhist framework and the scientific register. Grade 7 students are old enough to hold both. The point is not to make Buddhism seem more legitimate by aligning it with science — it is to show that the insight is not isolated, that different frameworks can arrive at the same recognition from different directions.

Spiral connections: Grade 5 Wheel of Life and the three poisons (PKA spine); Grade 4 Unit 1 metta practice as first formal introduction to loving-kindness; Grade 7 Unit 1 empathy framework (healing intention as empathy directed and sustained).
