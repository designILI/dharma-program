Reclaiming the Untold

The near-loss of Pajapati's full story from the tradition — and the act of recovering it → what it means to reclaim what was not fully told, and why that act is itself a form of practice

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleSynthesis through original production
Bloom's levelCreate
LessonG8.U4.C7

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The near-loss of Pajapati's full story from the tradition — and the act of recovering it → what it means to reclaim what was not fully told, and why that act is itself a form of practice

Cognitive Principle Featured: Synthesis through original production — the final piece is not a summary of what students have learned. It is the creation of something new from the materials of the unit. At the Evaluate/Create level, students are asked to make something that could only exist because they have spent six classes inside this story. The form of production (poem, letter, written response, visual) is chosen by the student because different forms say different things, and students know themselves well enough now to know which form serves their response.

Bloom's Level: Create — students produce an original final piece in response to the essential question of the unit; they synthesise across the full curriculum (Tara, Bardo, Twelve Links, Milarepa, the stupa) in the service of their response

Prior Class Summary: C6 followed Pajapati's monastic life, her teaching, and her enlightenment — the tradition's formal recognition of her as an arahant, dying in the Buddha's presence with her achievement acknowledged. Students completed their fifth close-reading paragraph and carry the full narrative arc and the debrief line: "She practised until the chain was gone."

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW that Pajapati's full story was not centrally canonised — that it was told around the edges of the tradition and had to be recovered by scholars and practitioners; that the bhikkhuni lineage she founded was historically suppressed and was revived in the 20th century; that the act of recovery is itself a form of practice Students will UNDERSTAND what "canonise" means and the social and political dimensions of what gets included in a tradition's central texts; why the partial loss of Pajapati's story is information about the tradition, not a reason to reject it; what it means to "reclaim the untold" Students will BE ABLE TO produce an original final piece that responds to the unit's essential question and includes at least one explicit connection to an earlier part of the curriculum; they can reflect on the before-and-after arc visible in their own writing from C1 to C7 Students will FEEL the specific intimacy of having followed a life from birth to death — the particular grief of knowing that life was almost lost — and the particular satisfaction of having held it, in full, for seven classes

Materials Checklist

  • Pre-reading responses from C1 — returned to students at the start of the activity. The teacher has held these since C1.
  • Students' five close-reading paragraphs (C2–C6) — students have been keeping these; confirm they have them.
  • Writing materials: journals, loose paper, coloured pencils or paint if available (for students who choose a visual form)
  • The question board from C1 — visible and present for the final time; questions should be reviewed together
  • Space for 2–3 students to share their final pieces in the debrief (plan for this — invite, do not require)
  • Timer
  • Plan B for activity: If a student is genuinely stuck on form, offer three concrete options in writing: (1) A paragraph-length written response to the essential question, drawing on evidence from the unit. (2) A short poem (eight lines minimum) addressed to Pajapati directly or about her story. (3) A visual piece with a written title and three written lines explaining the connection to the curriculum. Every student should produce something in this class.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 8 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Tsog Due pa · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader rotates.

Teacher, after the prayer set: "We chant the Dolma — Tara's prayer — before the final class of Pajapati's story. Because Tara and Pajapati are the same quality, in two different forms. The archetype and the history. Both present."

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

Teacher returns the pre-reading responses from C1. "Read what you wrote in the first class of this unit. Read it quietly." Students read — allow two full minutes of silence. Then: "Look at the question board. Which questions have we answered across these seven classes? Which ones remain?" Take responses — mark the answered questions (a checkmark, a line through them — whatever the teacher has used across the unit to track). Then: "What questions remain? Those are yours to carry. The curriculum cannot answer everything. Some things you will find in practice."

Mini Lesson — 10 minutes

Reading excerpt: Teacher reads aloud the final passage — approximately 8 minutes. This passage covers the meta-story: not what happened in Pajapati's life, but what happened to her story afterward.

  • After her death, the bhikkhuni lineage grew. For centuries, women's monasticism flourished in parts of the Buddhist world. But in the Theravada tradition (which preserved the Pali canon), the bhikkhuni lineage was interrupted — in some regions, it effectively died out. There were periods of centuries in which no fully ordained women's monastics existed in the Theravada tradition. The lineage that Pajapati founded, that required her three askings and hundreds of miles of barefoot walking, was suppressed and lost.
  • In the 20th and 21st centuries, feminist Buddhist scholars, practitioners, and activists began the work of recovery and revival. The Theravada bhikkhuni lineage has been revived. The scholarship on Mahapajapati Gotami has deepened. Her story — once peripheral, once fragments — has been reconstructed and returned to the tradition.
  • And: her name appears in the Pali canon among the great disciples. She is there. She was always there. The tradition preserved enough to allow the recovery. That, too, is part of the story.

After the reading: "The tradition does not fully tell Pajapati's story. We have been working from fragments, from peripheral texts, from scholarship, from imagination working responsibly with what the tradition preserved. That act of reconstruction — of reclaiming what was not centrally told — is itself a form of practice. We have been practising it for seven classes."

Vocabulary Moment: "The word canonise — to formally include in the official body of a tradition. The canon is what the tradition decides is central. What gets canonised and what does not is never a purely spiritual decision — it is always also a social and political one: who has the authority to decide what is central, and whose experiences are treated as universal. Pajapati's story was not fully canonised. That is information about the tradition — not a reason to reject the tradition, but a reason to look carefully at what it includes and what it leaves out, and to do the work of recovery when something essential has been left at the edges."

Body Moment: Students hold all five of their close-reading paragraphs in their hands — the stack from C2 through C6. "Hold the stack. Feel its weight — not its literal weight, but what it represents: seven weeks of following one life, paragraph by paragraph. Read the stack from beginning to end, quickly — just the first sentence of each paragraph is enough." Allow ninety seconds. "What is the arc? From the first paragraph to the last — what changed in her, from the beginning of the reading to the end? And what changed in you?"

Emotional Hook: The particular experience of having followed a life from beginning to end — the intimacy of having been present for all of it. And the grief, arriving now, of knowing it was almost lost. And underneath both: the recognition that the curriculum has been preparing students for this story for six years, and they are ready to honour it.

Activity — 20 minutes

Final piece. Students choose their form:

  • A written response to the unit's essential question: What does it mean to choose liberation — when the world has not chosen it for you?
  • A poem — addressed to Pajapati directly, or about her, or about what her story means for the tradition and for the student
  • A letter to Pajapati — telling her what her story has given you
  • A visual piece (drawing, mandala, representation) with a written title and written commentary that makes the curriculum connection explicit
  • Any other form the student proposes — teacher approves if it genuinely serves the response

Requirement for all forms: At least one explicit connection to something from earlier in the curriculum. It can be Tara, the Bardo, the Twelve Links, the Medicine Buddha, the stupa, Milarepa, the wheel of life — any thread. The connection should be genuine, not decorative.

Teacher circulates. Key prompts:

  • "What form did you choose? Why that one — what does this form allow you to say that another form wouldn't?"
  • "What is the connection to the earlier curriculum? Can you be more specific? Which class? Which concept?"
  • "What surprised you about the story — and can that surprise be in your piece?"
  • "What do you want the reader (or viewer) to feel when they encounter your piece? Are you making choices toward that feeling?"

Debrief — 5 minutes

Share: invite 2–3 students to read or present their final pieces. After each: silence for five seconds before the next. Then the teacher places the pre-reading responses beside the final pieces — students hold both. "That is what seven classes did. That is what six years of carrying the name made possible. That is what eight years of this curriculum has been building."

One final look at the question board. Any remaining unanswered questions: "These travel with you."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Tsog Due pa · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Food Offering Prayer. Student leader.

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "Mahapajapati Gotami — great leader of a great gathering — walked back a third time. She practised until the chain was gone. And we have, at last, told her story in full."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The final pieces and pre-reading responses from C1 are the Unit 4 portfolio artefact — the before-and-after record of seven classes inside this story. Collect both carefully. They will be returned to students in U5.C2 as part of the full portfolio review. Do not lose them in the interval.

This is the peak of the curriculum. The teacher who reaches G8.U4.C7 has taken students through the full Pajapati arc — from the name first dropped in Grade 2, through two Grade 6 touchpoints, through seven classes of narrative. What happens in this class is the payoff for six years of design. Allow it to be what it is. Do not rush the debrief or compress the sharing. The final pieces students produce in C7 are often the most artistically and intellectually accomplished work in the portfolio — because they are made at the moment when all the context is simultaneously present.

Note in your reflection after C7: which students were most moved? Which produced the most surprising final pieces? Which students made connections you did not anticipate? These observations matter: they are evidence of what the curriculum design did and did not produce in this specific cohort. They should inform notes passed to the next teacher or cohort if the curriculum continues.

Unit 5 is the closing movement — the completion of the prayer repertoire, the full portfolio review, the drafting and rehearsal of the closing declaration, and the Threshold ceremony. The Pajapati thread is complete. What remains is the curriculum itself: the recognition, in five classes, of what eight years produced.

Spiral connections present in C7 (all of them, simultaneously): This class is the curriculum's synthesis point. Every thread that has been running is present: Tara (archetype to history, fully connected in C5 and returned here in the opening and closing); the Bardo (grief as ground of practice, first named in Grade 6 and fully applied in C3); the Twelve Links (the chain interrupted, first studied in U1 and applied to Pajapati's arahantship in C6); the stupa (the curriculum encoded in form — Pajapati's story as the curriculum's own story of what the form is for); Milarepa (the other great practitioner of Grade 8, the other example of enlightenment emerging from devastating ground). The final piece is the student's synthesis of all of it.
