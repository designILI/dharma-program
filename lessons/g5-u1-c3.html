The Turning Rim

The six coloured sections of the Wheel's main body → six modes of conditioned existence, understood both as cosmological realms and as recurring psychological states

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleDual coding
Bloom's levelUnderstand / Apply
LessonG5.U1.C3

Duration: 40 minutes

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The six coloured sections of the Wheel's main body → six modes of conditioned existence, understood both as cosmological realms and as recurring psychological states

Cognitive Principle Featured: Dual coding — pairing a name and description with a distinct physical gesture (one per realm) creates two independent retrieval pathways. When a student later encounters the word "hungry ghost realm," the gesture fires before the definition, activating embodied memory faster than verbal recall alone. The panoramic structure of this class (all six realms in one pass) is deliberate: establishing the complete map before any deep-dive prevents students from over-fixating on single realms while missing the system.

Bloom's Level: Understand / Apply — students receive all six realms as a structured overview, then apply the dual reading (literal + psychological) immediately in the turn and talk ("which realm do you visit most often as a state of mind?"). The lesson holds both readings simultaneously without collapsing one into the other.

Prior Class Summary: C2 taught the hub — the three root poisons and their animals (pig/ignorance, snake/aversion, rooster/desire). Students added the hub to their working Wheel drawing, identified which poison they notice most in themselves, and carry the three-gesture body vocabulary. The question held at the end of C2: "Where does the engine drive beings? What are the six sections of the Wheel?"

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the names and characteristic experiences of all six realms: Gods (Lha), Demi-gods (Lhamin/Asura), Humans (Mí), Animals (Dü), Hungry Ghosts (Yidag), and Hell beings (Nyalwa), and can locate them approximately on the Wheel's image.

Students will UNDERSTAND that the six realms are held in two readings simultaneously — as cosmological states of existence in the Buddhist tradition, and as psychological qualities of mind that any being (including a human in this classroom, today) can inhabit momentarily — and that neither reading cancels the other.

Students will BE ABLE TO perform one gesture per realm, label all six realms on their developing Wheel drawing, and name which realm they visit most often as a state of mind — with at least one concrete, specific example.

Students will FEEL the slightly uncomfortable recognition of seeing their own familiar mental states organised into a cosmological map — the sensation of having your inner weather named by a tradition that predates you by two thousand years.

Materials Checklist

  • Large, clear image of the Wheel of Life — displayed throughout Unit 1 (already up from C1)
  • Each student's in-progress Wheel drawing (with hub completed from C2)
  • Plan B (if drawings are not available for any reason): Distribute fresh paper. Students will add the six realms in the correct approximate positions from the displayed image.
  • Pencils (one per student)
  • Chalk or whiteboard markers — the teacher may wish to draw a simple spoke-diagram of the six realms on the board during the Mini Lesson to accompany the image
  • Optional: a large printed or projected version that clearly shows all six realm sections (coloured differently if possible)

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 5 prayer set, led by the student leader for this class:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

(Food Offering Prayer at closing only.)

Warmup retrieval — three-poison gestures (before handing to student leader for the remaining prayers):

Teacher: "Before we open — the three poisons. Show me."

Teacher names each, students gesture:

"Ignorance." (Hands over eyes.) "Desire." (Hands reaching forward.) "Aversion." (Hands pushing away.)

Then the animals:

"Pig. Rooster. Snake."

Students make the matching gesture for each animal. This takes under sixty seconds and retrieves C2's vocabulary before C3 builds on it. Student leader then leads the prayer set.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

"You know what drives the Wheel — the three poisons at the center. Now: where does the Wheel take beings? The engine is running. The Wheel is turning. What is it turning through?"

Direct students to the Wheel image. Point to the six sections — without yet naming them.

"How many sections do you see in the main body of the Wheel?"

Students: six.

"Six realms. Six modes of existence. Six places beings turn through, again and again, driven by the three poisons. If you were going to guess — what would those six realms be? What are the different kinds of existence, the different conditions beings can be in?"

Take responses freely — this is a generative moment, not a test. Students may say: "heaven, hell, earth, animals, spirits..." Accept the logic of the guesses. Then: "Here is what the Wheel shows."

Fallback (if students are quiet): Ask: "What is the best possible existence? What is the worst possible existence? And what is the in-between one — the one we're in right now?" This always generates the axis (highest/lowest/human) and the remaining three realms can be built from there.

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

The teacher moves through all six realms in order from top to bottom, pointing to each section in the image as it is named. The tone is panoramic — this is a flyover, not a landing. (Unit 2 goes deep into each realm.)

For each realm, the teacher gives: its name (Tibetan), its characteristic experience, its key insight, and its gesture (created together with students, explained below). Move with pace — do not rush, but do not dwell so long that the pattern of six is lost.

1. God realm — Lha (ལྷ):

"The gods. Beings in the god realm are comfortable — ease, beauty, pleasure, no immediate suffering. They are proud. They live a long time. But they don't know their good fortune will end. They are so absorbed in comfort that they forget to practise, forget to prepare. And when their merit runs out and they begin to fall — they have no resources for it. They don't know how to be in difficulty.

"Key insight: comfort is not safety. A life of ease with no preparation is its own kind of danger."

Gesture (students create together with teacher): hands raised at the sides, leaning back, luxuriating — the posture of ease and pride. Confirm with students: "Does this feel like the god realm? Yes?"

2. Demi-god realm — Lhamin, Asura (ལྷ་མིན):

"The demi-gods, or asuras. They are powerful — strong, capable, fighters. But they are consumed by jealousy. They can see the gods above them, and they want what the gods have. They spend their existence in conflict — always fighting the gods, always wanting what is just out of reach. They are never satisfied by what they have.

"Key insight: competitive jealousy is its own kind of hell. You can be powerful and capable and still suffer intensely if you only measure yourself against what you don't have."

Gesture: fists raised — the posture of struggle, of fighting. "Demi-gods."

3. Human realm — Mí (མི):

"The human realm. Us. Both suffering AND the capacity for awakening — in the same realm. Human beings have enough suffering to motivate change: we know loss, we know sickness, we know death. We are not so comfortable that we forget to look for something better. And we have enough wisdom and reflection to actually pursue a path.

"This is why, in Buddhist teaching, human birth is called 'precious.' It is rare — and it is an opportunity. The human realm is the realm of the middle: not pure ease, not pure suffering. Just enough of both to make practice not only possible but necessary.

"Key insight: difficulty is not just an obstacle. It is what makes practice possible. The suffering we wish away may be the very thing that keeps us looking."

Gesture: hands placed gently in the lap, upright, alert — the meditation posture. "Human realm. Our realm."

4. Animal realm — Dü (དུད་འགྲོ):

"The animals. Beings driven largely by instinct — hunger, survival, reproduction, threat response. There is very little space in the animal realm for reflection, for choice, for stepping back and asking 'what is actually happening here?' Life is immediate: eat or be eaten, survive or don't.

"Key insight: when we act purely on instinct, without reflection — when we react without pausing, when we only respond to immediate need or threat — we enter the animal realm's quality, regardless of the body we're in."

Gesture: hands placed on the floor (or desk) in front of the body — the posture of being on all fours. Just a brief, light gesture, not a performance. "Animal realm."

5. Hungry ghost realm — Yidag (ཡི་དྭགས):

"The hungry ghosts — preta in Sanskrit; yidag in Tibetan. This is one of the most psychologically vivid realms in the whole Wheel. Beings here have enormous stomachs — large, swollen bellies — and tiny, constricted mouths and throats. They are perpetually, intensely hungry. But they cannot eat. Whatever they reach for turns to fire or ash as it enters the mouth. The craving can never be satisfied, no matter how much they pursue what they want.

"Key insight: more of what you crave does not reduce the craving. The hungry ghost realm is the realm of addiction, of insatiable wanting — where getting more of the thing makes the wanting worse, not better. You know this realm."

(Pause after that last sentence. Let it land.)

Gesture: hands wide at the stomach (indicating the large belly), thumb and forefinger brought close together at the mouth (indicating the tiny opening). "Hungry ghosts."

6. Hell realms — Nyalwa (དམྱལ་བ):

"The hell realms. There are many described in the texts — hot hells, cold hells. Beings here experience intense, sustained suffering — not a moment of pain but a prolonged, overwhelming condition. In the Buddhist analysis, the hell realms are the consequence of sustained, powerful hatred and ill-will. Of violence turned outward with full force.

"Key insight: the mental states that create the most suffering in others are also the ones that create the most suffering in the one who holds them. Hatred harms the one who hates. This is not punishment — it is how the system works."

Gesture: arms crossed tightly over the chest, braced — the posture of being in extreme cold, or bracing against intense heat. "Hell realms."

Now run the full sequence of gestures, realm by realm:

"Let's do all six. I'll name the realm — you make the gesture."

Teacher names each in sequence; students gesture. Repeat twice — the second time slightly faster. Watch for which gestures are confident and which are uncertain; this maps understanding.

The dual reading — held together:

"Here is something important. These six realms are understood in two ways — and in the Tibetan Buddhist tradition, both are held to be true at the same time.

"The first reading is literal: these are actual modes of existence — cosmological states in the Buddhist understanding of the universe. Beings pass through them across lifetimes.

"The second reading is psychological: these six realms are also states of mind. States you and I enter many times a day. The god realm is the moment of unchecked comfort where you forget to pay attention. The hungry ghost realm is the moment of insatiable craving — wanting one more, and then one more, and it never satisfies. The hell realm is the moment of blind rage.

"We do not choose between these readings. We hold both. The Wheel maps existence — and it maps the mind. Both are true. Which is more useful to you right now is a different question."

Vocabulary Moment:

"The phrase 'precious human birth' — in Tibetan, དལ་འབྱོར་གྱི་མི་ལུས, dal jor gyi mi lü. 'Precious' here is not a compliment. It is an assessment. The human realm is called precious because it offers the conditions for liberation that other realms do not: enough suffering to motivate practice, enough intelligence and reflection to pursue a path, and — for students in this room — access to the teachings themselves. 'Precious' is a call: use this life for what it is uniquely capable of. Don't spend it in the god realm's comfort or the demi-god realm's jealousy. You are here, in this realm, at this time, in this classroom. That is not an accident — it is a conditions question."

Write dal jor / དལ་འབྱོར on the board alongside "precious human birth."

Body Moment: The six gestures already performed above are the body moment of this class. If time permits, one additional embodied move: students briefly make the gesture for the realm they feel they visit most often as a state of mind — silently, just for themselves, without explaining to anyone. Ten seconds of private acknowledgment.

Emotional Hook: The slight discomfort — and the recognition — of seeing your own familiar inner weather organised into an ancient cosmological taxonomy. The moment of "oh — I know that realm." Not shame, but precise self-seeing.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: which realm do you think you visit most often — as a state of mind, not a literal place? Not where you live in some other lifetime, but what quality of experience you move through most in your daily life. Be honest, and be specific — not just 'hungry ghost realm' but 'hungry ghost realm when I keep watching one more video even when I don't want to' or 'god realm when everything is going well and I stop paying attention.' Specificity is honesty."

Partners talk for two to three minutes. Teacher circulates and listens. This is personal — do not push for whole-class sharing unless students choose it.

What to listen for: Students who can name a specific, concrete instance of the realm they visit most. Students who notice that they move between realms ("sometimes god realm, sometimes hungry ghost"). Students who identify the human realm as the most frequent — this is interesting and worth noting: it suggests self-awareness about the opportunity-quality of their experience. Any student who says "hell realm" quietly and specifically — that student may need follow-up.

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Return each student's in-progress Wheel drawing (with the hub completed).

"Today: add all six realms to your Wheel drawing. Draw them in the correct approximate positions — use the image on the wall as your guide. Label each one with its name, and next to each name, write one word or phrase that describes the characteristic experience of that realm."

Students work from the displayed image. Teacher circulates with specific prompts:

  • "Where is the god realm — at the top or the bottom?" (Top — conventionally.)
  • "Which realm is directly across from the god realm?" (Hell realms — at the bottom or opposite.)
  • "What is the characteristic experience of the animal realm — write that in one word."
  • "Can you indicate in some way, visually, the difference between the realms? Line weight, shading, the expressions of the beings inside?"

Extension: Students who move quickly add a small symbolic figure inside each realm — a tiny drawing that represents the characteristic being of that realm. (A being seated in ease for gods; a figure with a large stomach and tiny mouth for hungry ghosts; a figure on all fours for animals.) These small figures are entirely the student's own interpretation — no single correct answer.

Debrief — 5 minutes

"Notice that the human realm is in the middle of the Wheel — neither at the very top nor at the very bottom. Not pure ease, not pure suffering. The middle realm. And it is the only realm where the Buddha's pointing finger really points for us. Why the middle? Why not the top?"

Take two or three responses. Students often arrive at: because suffering motivates, because the middle has enough difficulty to keep us looking. Hold and affirm these.

"The god realm beings don't need to practise — they're comfortable. The hell realm beings can't practise — the suffering is too overwhelming. The human realm is the one where the conditions are just right. Difficulty. Reflection. Access. That combination is called precious."

End with: "Next class, we draw. Not from looking — from knowing. The whole Wheel, your understanding of it, in one image. What do you know? What does your hand know? We'll find out."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set, student leader:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

Teacher's closing line:

"You have now seen the whole map. The engine at the center. The six places the engine drives beings. And the figure standing outside, pointing. Next class, that map goes from your eyes into your hands."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author):

Carry forward: C3 is intentionally panoramic — all six realms in one pass, without deep-diving into any single one. Resist the pull to spend more time on the hungry ghost realm (the most vivid and the one students will most recognise). That depth belongs to U2.C2, which is specifically designed for it and which prepares the ground carefully. The six-realm gesture warmup (one gesture per realm, called by name) is worth using as an opening retrieval at the start of C4 and as a brief warmup at the opening of Unit 2. It costs sixty seconds and returns the whole framework to working memory. Students who are already asking "which realm am I in right now?" — spontaneously, without prompting — are tracking the psychological reading of the Wheel alongside the literal. This is exactly right; it is the analytical capacity this year is building toward. Encourage it without making it obligatory for students who are not yet there. Spiral note: the hungry ghost realm's connection to the Grade 3 work on reciprocity (acting without recognition, the insatiable desire for return from one's giving) should be named explicitly in Unit 2, not here. Let the panorama settle first. The connection will be more powerful for having been held. Note in Your Reflection any student who identified the "hell realm" quietly and specifically in the turn and talk — this warrants gentle follow-up outside class time. The debrief question ("Why the middle? Why not the top?") is designed to draw out the logic of "precious human birth" without the teacher stating it — students who arrive at it themselves have genuinely understood, not just received. Wait for them to find it.
