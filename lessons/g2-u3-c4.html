Our Helpers

Sharing the helper drawing and speaking about a real person or being who helps when things feel too big → gratitude arrives when we name, look at, and speak about the helpers already in our lives; the circle of care that began with family has expanded to include all beings, and it also — still — includes the person who sits beside you when you're scared.

Bloom's levelUnderstand with early Apply
LessonG2.U3.C4

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Sharing the helper drawing and speaking about a real person or being who helps when things feel too big → gratitude arrives when we name, look at, and speak about the helpers already in our lives; the circle of care that began with family has expanded to include all beings, and it also — still — includes the person who sits beside you when you're scared.

Cognitive Principle Featured: Elaborative interrogation through public articulation. When a student explains their thinking aloud — especially in a social context where others are listening — they deepen and consolidate their own understanding. The share structure ("This is my helper... They help me by... They are like Guru Rinpoche because...") is a scaffolded version of this: it asks students to move from description to comparison, which is the beginning of analysis. The comparison to Guru Rinpoche need not be accurate to be valuable — the attempt is the learning.

Bloom's Level: Understand with early Apply — Students apply what they know about Guru Rinpoche's qualities (compassionate steadiness, courage that doesn't look like force) to a real person in their life. The comparison is the cognitive work; it requires holding two things at once. Synthesising the year's themes (widening circle, Bodhichitta, Sem kye) in the debrief is early Analyze, held lightly for this age.

Prior Class Summary: In C3, students learned the plain-language meaning of the Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer line by line, heard it chanted in Tibetan, began to join in, and wrote one line onto their helper drawing. The prayer was chanted one final time after the closing prayers, as the class ended in sound.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW how to articulate who their helper is, what their helper does, and how their helper resembles Guru Rinpoche in some way.

Students will UNDERSTAND that the circle of care — which has expanded outward across Grade 2 to include animals, strangers, and all beings — also flows back inward: the person who helps you is part of the same circle.

Students will BE ABLE TO share their completed helper drawing with the class, using the three-part share structure, and receive others' shares with attention.

Students will FEEL gratitude — not as a concept, but as the specific warmth that arrives when you look at a drawing of your helper, remember what they do for you, and speak their name out loud in a room where people are listening.

Materials Checklist

  • Students' completed helper drawings from C3 (stored from last class)
  • Coloured pencils — one set per student or shared on tables (for final details in Phase 1)
  • Pencils
  • The three-part share structure written on the board before class:
  1. "This is my helper. Their name is ___."
  2. "They help me by ___."
  3. "They are like Guru Rinpoche because ___."
  • A teacher-prepared "helper" — the teacher should decide before class who or what they would draw, and be ready to model the share authentically and briefly

Plan B — for students who cannot speak in front of the whole group: Allow a student to share with one partner instead of the full class, and the partner reports back on their behalf: "My partner's helper is ___." This is equally valid. No student should be compelled to speak publicly. Note in your reflection if any student used this option and how it went.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader for today leads the full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa. Teacher settles the room with particular care today — this is the last class of Grade 2, and the opening deserves its full weight. One breath together before the student leader begins. Unhurried throughout. The teacher may notice students are more alert or more restless than usual — both are responses to endings. Let the prayers absorb some of that energy.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

"This is the last class of Grade 2. Let's look back for a moment — not at everything, just at the shape."

"At the beginning of the year, we asked: who do we include in our circle of care? We started with the people closest to us — family, friends. Then we kept expanding. We expanded to animals, to strangers, to all beings. We practised Sem kye — the wish to benefit all beings — every single class. We sent that wish outward. Every class."

"Then we heard the story of the Buddha — a man who asked one hard question and didn't stop until he found the answer. And then Guru Rinpoche — a teacher who went toward frightening things with kindness instead of force."

Pause. "Today you share your helper. The person or being who helps you when things feel too big. They are part of the circle too — the circle that starts right here, between us."

Mini Lesson — 7 minutes

Brief. Teacher models the share — showing their own helper or describing who they would draw:

"I'm going to go first. I want to show you what the share looks like, and I want to do it for real — not as an example, as an actual share."

Teacher holds up their drawing (or describes it without a drawing if one was not prepared):

"This is my helper. Their name is ___ [teacher fills in honestly]. They help me by ___ [specific — not 'being kind' but an actual behaviour: 'sitting quietly with me when I'm overwhelmed,' or 'asking me what's wrong before I've said anything,' or 'making me laugh when I'm taking something too seriously']. They are like Guru Rinpoche because ___ [a genuine connection — not forced: 'they don't try to fix what I'm feeling — they just stay near,' or 'they're not afraid of the difficult thing; they turn toward it with me']."

Then: "That's the whole share. It's not long. Three things. Who, what they do, and how they remind you of Guru Rinpoche. The third part — the Guru Rinpoche part — you can be as loose as you want. There's no wrong answer. If your helper calms you down, if they stay when others would leave, if they make you braver — that's enough."

Vocabulary Moment: No new vocabulary today. This is a synthesis class. The teacher revisits, briefly: "We've used these words this year: Bodhichitta. Compassion. Sem kye — the wish to benefit all beings. Ngowa — the dedication of our merit to all beings. Your helper is someone who already lives some of this — maybe without knowing the words."

Body Moment: Before the shares begin: "Stand up. Take one breath. Think of your helper — just for a moment. Picture their face. Feel what it's like to know they exist."

Five seconds of real quiet. Then: "Sit."

Emotional Hook: Gratitude is not named. It does not need to be. The act of standing, breathing, and picturing the helper — followed immediately by sitting and beginning the shares — creates the condition. Do not say the word. Let the feeling do its work.

Turn and Talk — embedded in Activity Phase 2

The share circle IS the turn and talk — structured, public, and received by the whole group. See Activity below.

Activity — 18 minutes

Phase 1 — Final details (8 minutes) Return students' drawings. "You have about eight minutes to finish. Final colours. Final details. Look at your drawing and ask: is there anything missing? Is there anything you want to add before you share it?"

Quiet work. Teacher circulates — no prompting, just presence. Students who finish early: "Look at your drawing one more time. Think of the three things you're going to say. Practise them in a whisper."

Phase 2 — Share circle (10 minutes) "We're going to share now. I'll call on each person. Hold up your drawing so everyone can see it. Say the three things. We'll listen — no interrupting, no commenting. When someone shares, your job is to really look at their drawing and really hear what they say."

Call students one at a time. Teacher does not comment between shares — just: "Thank you. Next." Warmth is in the listening, not in evaluative responses. Do not correct the Guru Rinpoche comparisons. Receive each one exactly as offered.

If a student says "I don't know how they're like Guru Rinpoche," the teacher says simply: "Tell us something about them — what they do when you're scared. We'll find the connection together." Then, after the student speaks: "That sounds like Guru Rinpoche to me — someone who stays when it's hard."

Extension for students who share quickly and have more to say: allow one additional sentence. "Is there anything else you want us to know about your helper?"

Debrief — 5 minutes

"Every person in this room has a helper. We just heard that. Every single person has someone — or something — that makes them less afraid, or more able to keep going."

"And every person here is probably someone else's helper. Maybe you don't know it. Maybe you'll never know it. But in a room of people who practise Bodhichitta — the wish to benefit all beings — that's already happening."

"That's the circle of care. It doesn't just go outward to strangers and all beings. It starts here, in this room, between us."

Pause.

"Next year, in Grade 3, we'll go further. We'll ask: what if we could do something real — not just wish, not just feel — to actually reduce suffering? What would that look like? That's where we're heading."

"For today — that's Grade 2."

Closing — 3 minutes

The student leader leads the full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa. Unhurried. The teacher chants the Seven-Line Prayer once more after the closing set — students join in as they can.

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "You have learned a lot this year. What you carry: that kindness can expand. That your circle can grow. That there are teachers who walked this path before us — and that you already have helpers of your own. That is not nothing."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Grade 2 is complete. The helper drawings go into student portfolios or are displayed. The three making-thread artefacts for Grade 2 are: the concentric-circles diagram from Unit 1 (the widening circle), the four-panel Buddha comic from Unit 2, and the helper drawing from Unit 3. These three images together tell the story of the year: the circle expanded, a great teacher showed one path through it, another great teacher showed a different path, and each student named someone who helps them walk their own path. This is not a small curriculum. The thread into Grade 3 is compassion as action — not just wishing, not just feeling, but doing. In Grade 3, students carry out a real Tsethar: a life-release practice, freeing living beings from harm. The inner circle from Unit 1 becomes the foundation: if we can send wishes outward to all beings, we can also act to protect them. The students who said "I don't know" during the Guru Rinpoche comparison are not behind — they are being honest, which is the starting point of everything. Spiral note: Students now know the Sem kye prayer (Bodhichitta aspiration) and Ngowa (dedication of merit) as automatic Grade 3+ standing practice — these are internalized this year and need no further introduction. The Seven-Line Prayer continues to deepen in Grade 3 toward memorisation; note which students have already begun internalizing phrases — these students will be natural leaders in Grade 3's prayer-learning sequence.
