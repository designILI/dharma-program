Finishing the Flags

Completing and hanging prayer flags → Choosing who we wish good things for

LessonG2.U1.C2

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Completing and hanging prayer flags → Choosing who we wish good things for

Cognitive Principle Featured: Elaborative interrogation — asking "who do we wish good things for, and why?" prompts students to generate their own connections between the act of sending a wish and the choice of who receives it. This self-generated reasoning embeds the concept more durably than being told the answer.

Bloom's Level: Understand. Students are connecting a concrete act (finishing, hanging the flag) to an emerging concept (that a wish is directed, not random — that we can choose its recipients, and that the circle of recipients can expand). This is the first conceptual step toward the unit's essential question.

Prior Class Summary: In C1, students revisited the mantra syllables, connected sound to image, and began painting their flags. The lesson closed with an open question: "Who is the wish for?" That question arrives with students today, unanswered.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW that a wish, like a prayer flag, can be intentionally directed toward specific people or beings — and that "who we include" is a choice.

Students will UNDERSTAND that the circle of people we wish good things for can be larger or smaller, and that noticing who is in our circle is the first step to expanding it.

Students will BE ABLE TO complete their flag and name at least one person they are sending a wish to; some will name several; some will begin to wonder about people they haven't thought of before.

Students will FEEL the particular satisfaction of finishing something they started — a sense of completion — followed by a first, gentle stirring of curiosity about who else might belong in the wish.

Materials Checklist

  • Flags from C1 (retrieved from drying rack)
  • Same paint supplies as C1: brushes, paint, water cups, protective table covering
  • Aprons or paint shirts — Plan B: Newspaper collar-and-lap covering as in C1
  • Reference cards with syllables (same as C1)
  • String or twine for hanging, pre-cut in lengths — one length per student, or one shared classroom line
  • Clips, pegs, or a prepared hanging rod near a window or along a wall
  • Be Kind by Pat Zietlow Miller (picture book) — one copy for the teacher to read aloud
  • Plain paper or index cards (one per student) for the "wish list" writing moment at the end

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader for today leads the Grade 1 prayer set:

  1. Kyap Dro (Refuge)
  2. Om Mani Padme Hung (7 times)
  3. Manjushri Prayer

Note on prayers: Sem kye and Ngowa are introduced in C3. Use only these three for C1 and C2.

After the prayers, the teacher says: "Last week you left a question hanging — like a flag that isn't up yet. Who remembers what we ended with?" Allow a student to name it: Who is the wish for? "That's the question we're going to work on today."

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

"Before we get the flags, let me ask you something. Imagine you have one wish — a really good wish, the kind that will actually reach someone. You can only send it to one person today. Who do you send it to?"

Give students ten seconds of silence to think.

"Hold that person in your mind. Don't say their name out loud yet — just hold them."

Pause.

"Now: why that person? What made you choose them?"

Take several responses. The teacher does not evaluate. "Each of you chose someone specific. You didn't just send the wish into the air — you aimed it. That's important. We're going to think more about that today."

Then: "Now think bigger. Who else might need a wish? Someone you don't always think about?"

Take one or two responses. Let the question breathe. Don't answer it.

Mini Lesson — 10 minutes

*Read-aloud: Be Kind by Pat Zietlow Miller*

"Before we finish the flags, I want to read you something. It's a short book. Listen for one thing: who does the kindness reach — and how far does it go?"

Read Be Kind aloud in full. Read slowly. Allow illustrations to be seen. Do not stop to ask comprehension questions during the reading — let the story complete itself.

After the final page: hold the book closed for a moment of silence.

Then: "The girl in this book didn't know how far her kindness would go. She did one thing. And it moved."

"Your flag is like that. When it goes up in the wind today, you won't be able to track where the wish goes. You can't follow it. But you can decide who you're aiming it toward when you put it up."

Vocabulary Moment: "In Himalayan Buddhist teaching, the word for the people and beings we include in our care is sometimes described as a circle — like rings in water when you drop a stone. Today your flag goes up. Next class, we'll begin drawing that circle. You'll see what I mean."

Body Moment: "Stand up for a moment. Face the window — or the door, wherever outside is from here. Close your eyes. Think of the person you held in your mind at the start of class. Stretch one hand toward the window. That's where your wish is going. Hold it there for three breaths." The teacher does this alongside students. After three breaths: "Sit down."

Emotional Hook: The moment a student's flag is clipped to the line and moves — even slightly — in the air from an open window or a fan's breath.

Turn and Talk — 4 minutes

"Turn to the person next to you. Tell them two things: who you are sending your wish to today, and one person you wouldn't normally think to send a wish to — someone outside your usual circle."

Allow three minutes. The teacher circulates and listens.

What to listen for: Students naming family members for the first person (expected and right). For the second person — listen for stretches: the school cook, a child in another village, a person who is unkind to them, an animal. These are the seeds the unit will grow. Note any remarkable responses to return to.

Activity — 16 minutes

Part 1 — Finish the flags (10 minutes)

Return flags from the drying rack. Students complete any unfinished syllables, touch up colours, add borders or symbols as desired. The teacher circulates. Brief, targeted prompts: "Is there anything you want to add before this goes up?"

Note on oral storytelling: If the teacher wishes to narrate anything while students work — the tradition of prayer flags, what the five colours represent — this can be read verbatim from written notes. Memorisation is not required.

Part 2 — Hang the flags (3 minutes)

Flags are attached to the string or rod one by one. The teacher may invite each student, as they clip their flag, to say quietly — not aloud to the class, but to themselves — the name of one person they're sending a wish to. "You don't need to say it out loud. Just know it."

Part 3 — Write a wish list (3 minutes)

Each student receives a plain index card. "Write down three names — or draw three faces, or three symbols. These are people or beings you want to send a wish to. Keep this card. Next class, we'll need it."

This card carries forward into C3 as the seed for the concentric circles diagram.

Debrief — 5 minutes

"Look at the flags. They're up. They're moving."

Give a moment of looking.

"Here's what I noticed today: every one of you, when I asked you who the wish was for, had an answer. You didn't hesitate for long. You already have people in your circle. The question for this year is: can that circle get bigger? Can you keep adding people to it — further and further out — and still really mean the wish?"

"Next class, we're going to start finding out. We're going to start drawing that circle."

Closing — 3 minutes

The student leader leads the Grade 1 prayer set:

  1. Kyap Dro
  2. Om Mani Padme Hung (7 times)
  3. Manjushri Prayer

After the closing prayers, the teacher says: "The flags are up. Every time the wind moves them today, a wish goes out — to the people you were thinking of when you hung them. You chose those people. That choice matters."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The index cards from Part 3 of the activity are small but important — they are the first version of the student's "circle of care" and should be brought into C3 as a reference when students draw their centre ring. Collect them at the end of class and redistribute in C3, or ask students to store them in their Dharma folders. The read-aloud of Be Kind plants the ripple metaphor (kindness spreading outward) — use "rings in water" language deliberately when introducing the concentric circles diagram in C3. Strictly No Elephants (the second anchor text) will be read in C5 when the class expands to animals. Spiral connection: Grade 1, Unit 1 (Prayer Flags) — today's flag-hanging is the direct continuation and culmination of the Grade 1 activity; the new layer is intentionality about the recipient.
