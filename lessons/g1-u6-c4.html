Jataka: The Fish King

A great fish king, trapped in a drying lake as all the creatures around him die of thirst, calls on his own accumulated virtue and speaks a true oath — and rain falls, the lake refills, and all are saved → truthfulness is not just a moral rule; it is a force in the world that can change circumstances.

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleNarrative structure
Bloom's levelUnderstand / Apply
LessonG1.U6.C4

Note: Standalone rotating session — can be taught at any point in the year.

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): A great fish king, trapped in a drying lake as all the creatures around him die of thirst, calls on his own accumulated virtue and speaks a true oath — and rain falls, the lake refills, and all are saved → truthfulness is not just a moral rule; it is a force in the world that can change circumstances.

Cognitive Principle Featured: Narrative structure — the story builds a mounting crisis (the lake shrinks, creatures die, the fish king runs out of options) until the one remaining resource is not physical but internal: his own honesty, held perfectly over a lifetime. The structure teaches students that the invisible virtues we cultivate have real-world effects, a concept made concrete through a vivid, consequential narrative.

Bloom's Level: Understand / Apply

Prior Class Summary: "This session may occur at any point in the year. Students carry cumulative year experience. The settle→story→discuss→draw ritual structure is familiar."

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the story of the Fish King — the drying lake, the crisis, and how truthfulness saved the creatures. Students will UNDERSTAND that honesty is not just about following rules — it is a quality that builds up over time, like a kind of strength, and that strength is real. Students will BE ABLE TO name a time they told the truth when it was difficult, and recognize that act as adding to their own inner strength. Students will FEEL the gravitas and power of truthfulness — not as an obligation but as something that belongs to them.

Materials Checklist

  • Source: Traditional Jataka tale (Saccakiriya / "act of truth" tales; Jataka No. 75 and related oral tradition); teacher's oral telling from this plan
  • Drawing supplies: crayons, colored pencils, paper squares for the wisdom wall
  • Optional prop: a bowl of water and a stone — hold the stone, place it in the water, let students see that a heavy thing can rest in water; truthfulness is the stone that holds
  • Wisdom wall: space and tape ready for today's drawings

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader stands at the front. Class settles — feet flat, hands in lap, one breath together.

Student leader leads:

  • Kyap Dro (Refuge prayer) — recited together
  • Om Mani Padme Hung — seven times, slow and full
  • Manjushri Prayer — recited together

the teacher: "Thank you. One breath — in... and out. Today's Jataka story is about a fish. A very great fish — a king among fish — and about what he did when everything was going wrong and he had nothing left but his own honesty. Let's find out what that means."

Story Preparation — 2 minutes

"I want to ask you something before we begin. If you have always told the truth — if you have never lied, not once — does that count for something? Does all that honesty go somewhere, like savings in a jar? Or does it just disappear?" (Let students think — don't answer it yet.) "Hold that question. Today's story answers it."

The Story — 8–10 minutes

Tell with rising gravity. The emotional peak — the fish king's oath — should be delivered with complete stillness and weight. Don't rush it.

Long ago, there was a great lake in the heart of a forest — wide and clear and full of life. In that lake lived the Fish King. He was very old, and very large, and he had been the protector of the lake for as long as anyone could remember. Under his watch, no creature harmed another without need. The lake was a place of safety.

And most importantly: the Fish King had never, in all his long life, told a lie. Not a small one, not a convenient one, not a kind one to avoid difficulty. Every word he had ever spoken had been true. He had been building this quality — this truthfulness — his entire life, the way a lake builds depth: slowly, quietly, completely.

Then the rains stopped.

First for one season. Then two. The lake began to shrink. The edges pulled back. The mud banks appeared, cracked and white. The creatures who lived near the edges began to leave. Then the fish who needed deep water moved toward the center, and the center grew crowded and dark.

The birds who came to drink every morning began to find only mud. Some flew away. Some did not fly away in time.

Creatures began to die.

The Fish King moved through what remained of his lake. He saw his world shrinking. He saw his people suffering — the fish, the frogs, the water-insects, the creatures who depended on the lake. And he felt something he had almost never felt: helplessness. He could not make it rain. He could not call the water back. He could not solve this with strength or wisdom or leadership.

He had only one thing left.

He rose to the surface of the water — what was left of it — and he looked up at the sky. The dry, white sky.

And he made a vow. Not a prayer, exactly. Something older than a prayer. He called on the truth.

"By the power of the truth I have lived," he said, "by every honest word I have spoken, by every lie I did not tell when it would have been easier to lie — by all of this, accumulated over a lifetime — let rain fall. Let this lake fill. Let these creatures live."

He closed his eyes. He held still. He waited.

For a moment — nothing.

Then, at the edge of the sky, a darkness. A cloud. Then more clouds, pressing together, building. A wind moved through the trees. A smell — the sharp, clean smell of rain coming.

And then it came. Not a drizzle. Rain, real and full, drumming on the surface of what remained of the lake, running in rivers down the hillsides, pouring into the cracks in the mud. The lake rose. The edges filled. The creatures who had moved to the center spread back out, gasping with relief.

It rained for three days.

When it was over, the lake was full again. The Fish King floated in the deep, cool water, and said nothing, and breathed.

He had used his whole life's worth of truth, and it had been enough.

The Discussion — 8 minutes

the teacher: "Be still for a moment."

(Ten seconds of quiet.)

"What is the first thing in your mind from that story?"

(Take 3–4 open responses.)

"The Fish King had no magic. He couldn't fly, or call lightning, or move the clouds. What was the one thing he had?"

(Guide toward: his truthfulness. His whole life of being honest.)

"Here is something I want us to think about carefully. The Fish King said something very specific. He didn't say 'I hope it rains.' He didn't say 'Please, someone, send rain.' What did he say?"

(Help students recall: he called on the truth he had lived. He pointed to it, named it, offered it.)

"Do you think that truthfulness was real? Like — was it really a kind of power? Or is that just a story?"

(This is the heart of the discussion. Let students debate it. Don't resolve it too quickly. the teacher should hold the question open.)

"Let's think about it this way. Every time you told the truth when it was hard — maybe you got in a little trouble, but you said what really happened — does that go somewhere? Does it become something? Or does it just disappear?"

(Guide toward: virtues we practice build up. They become part of who we are. The Fish King's truthfulness was real because he had made it real, over and over, across his whole life.)

Vocabulary Moment: Arrived through discussion — "The word is truthfulness. Not just 'telling the truth once' — but choosing it again and again until it becomes part of who you are. The Fish King didn't decide to be truthful when the lake was drying. He had been building that quality his whole life. And when he needed it most, it was there."

Body Moment: "Put one hand on your chest — right on your heart. Think of one time you told the truth when it was hard. You don't have to say it out loud. Just remember it. Feel it." (Ten seconds of quiet.) "That moment is real. It's still in you. That's what the Fish King had." (Lower hands.)

Emotional Hook: Don't explain — just ask: "When the Fish King made his vow and then waited — and nothing happened for a moment — what do you think he felt in that waiting?" (Let students name it: scared, still, trusting, alone. Accept everything.) "And then the rain came. What changed in his body right then?" (Receive those answers too: relief, wonder, fullness. Let the contrast live.)

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Assign fixed pairs.

Prompt — read slowly:

"Tell your partner about a time you told the truth when it was hard — when it would have been easier to lie, or stay quiet, or blame someone else. What happened? How did it feel afterward?"

One person talks, one listens, then switch.

What to listen for: students naming their own real moments of honesty — and the cost, and the feeling that came after. When they stop describing the fish and start describing themselves, transfer is happening.

Bring the class back. Invite one or two pairs to share.

Adding to the Wisdom Wall — 8–10 minutes

"Now we add to our wisdom wall. Today you'll draw one moment from the Fish King's story that stayed with you. It might be the lake drying up, or the Fish King rising to the surface to make his vow, or the moment the rain began, or the full lake at the end."

"Choose the moment that felt most true to you."

Distribute paper squares and supplies. Students draw in quiet or soft conversation. the teacher circulates: "What moment did you draw? What is the Fish King doing in your picture?"

After 6–7 minutes, students bring drawings to the wisdom wall under the label: "The Fish King — Truthfulness."

Debrief — 3 minutes

Gather at the wisdom wall.

"One last question. If you could ask the Fish King one question after the rain came and the lake was full again — what would you ask?"

(Take 2–3 responses. Examples: "Were you surprised it worked?" / "Did it hurt to use all your truth at once?" / "Could you do it again?" Receive each one.)

the teacher — one closing sentence: "The Fish King didn't borrow power from somewhere else — he called on what he had built himself, quietly, one honest word at a time. That is what truthfulness becomes when it is practiced long enough."

Closing — 2 minutes

Student leader returns to the front.

  • Kyap Dro — together
  • Om Mani Padme Hung — seven times
  • Manjushri Prayer — together

the teacher connects the story to the mantra: "Om Mani Padme Hung contains the quality of the jewel — pure, clear, unbroken. Truthfulness is like that jewel. Each time we say the mantra with our full attention, without pretending, we are building what the Fish King built."
