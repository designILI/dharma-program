Learning the Movements

Three specific physical movements (walk, mudra, turn) → intentionality as the quality that transforms ordinary action into practice

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleProcedural encoding through repetition
Bloom's levelAnalyze (embodied delivery
LessonG6.U4.C3

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Three specific physical movements (walk, mudra, turn) → intentionality as the quality that transforms ordinary action into practice

Cognitive Principle Featured: Procedural encoding through repetition — movement sequences learned with full attention and repeated multiple times are stored differently from movement sequences learned casually. Attention during the learning is the variable that determines durability.

Bloom's Level: Analyze (embodied delivery — the analytical understanding that intentionality transforms movement is the cognitive work of this lesson; Apply is the delivery mode, not the primary level)

Prior Class Summary: C2 introduced the five key Cham figures — students confirmed their mask choices and wrote meaning profiles. They carried the concept of wrathful compassion and the experience of briefly embodying a figure. They carry their figure's quality into this movement class.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW three specific Cham movement elements — the Cham walk, one hand mudra, one quarter-turn — and their traditional meanings. Students will UNDERSTAND that movement done with full intention differs qualitatively from casual movement, and that this difference is itself the teaching. Students will BE ABLE TO perform the combined sequence (walk + turn + mudra) and begin adding their figure's specific quality to the movement. Students will FEEL the shift in the quality of their own attention that deliberate, formal movement creates — what changes in the body between ordinary walking and the Cham walk.

Materials Checklist

  • Fully cleared floor space — desks to the walls, or class held in an assembly space, courtyard, or other open area. This must be arranged before students arrive. The room must be ready when they walk in.
  • No paper needed for the lesson body (this is a movement class — portfolio writing, if any, is brief and at the end)
  • Teacher's own reference for the three movements — confirmed with the resident teacher before this class if the teacher is uncertain of any element
  • Plan B: If the full floor cannot be cleared, the Cham walk and quarter-turn can be adapted to movement in place (stepping weight-to-weight, turning in place), and the mudra practice can be done seated. This is significantly less effective but preserves the essential learning. Do everything possible to clear the space before this class.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set including Dolma: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer

Student leader leads the prayers. No desk — student leader stands if possible.

Immediately after the closing prayer: "Before we sit — let's warm up the body from C1 and C2. Do the deliberate step, once around the space. Follow me."

Teacher leads one circuit of the deliberate step. Students follow. Settle.

Prior Knowledge Activation — none

Teacher: "No prior knowledge check today — this is a movement class. Begin in the body."

Students stand. "Space yourselves so you can move without touching each other. Turn and face me. We are going to learn three things today: the Cham walk, one hand gesture, and one turning movement. Those three elements are the foundation of the performance. Everything you add from here is built on these three."

Mini Lesson — 15 minutes (The embodied practice portion that runs beyond 15 min is absorbed into the Activity — see Activity section.) (delivered through demonstration and practice, not lecture)

Element 1 — The Cham walk (7 minutes):

Teacher demonstrates, narrating: "Feet parallel. Weight shifts fully onto one foot before the other lifts. Each step is placed, not dropped. The body is upright — not stiff, but present. The gaze is level. Nothing is casual."

Walk once across the space, slowly. Students watch.

"Now: join in. Walk with me."

Students walk. Teacher observes and gives precise, quiet adjustments: "Full weight shift before the step — feel when your weight has completely moved." "Level gaze — not down at the feet." "Each step is chosen."

One circuit. Stop. "What is different from how you walked into this room today?"

Take two brief responses. Then: "This walk says: I am fully here. Each step is deliberate. That quality is what makes it a practice."

Element 2 — One mudra (6 minutes):

Teacher demonstrates one hand gesture — a protective gesture (palm raised toward the front, fingers upright, arm extended at shoulder height) or an earth-touching mudra appropriate to the tradition. Confirm which gesture is used with the resident teacher before class.

Explain its meaning: "This gesture — in the tradition — means: protection. Or: I witness this. Or: this is my offering. [Use the specific meaning of the gesture chosen.] The gesture is not decorative. Each finger position has a meaning. When you hold this gesture in the performance, you are making a statement with your hand."

Students practise until the gesture is clean — elbow soft, wrist aligned, fingers intentional. Not gripping; not slack. "The mudra is a hand that means something."

Element 3 — One quarter-turn (4 minutes):

Teacher demonstrates: from a standing position, pivot a full quarter-turn — to face a new direction — with a single, deliberate movement. Not a shuffle. Not a drift. One clean turn.

"In Cham, the dancer faces the four directions in sequence — offering to each direction, purifying each direction, protecting each direction. When you turn: turn as if the direction matters. Because in the ritual — it does."

Students practise: stand, face front, quarter-turn right, quarter-turn right again (now facing back), quarter-turn right (now facing left), quarter-turn right (returned to front). Slowly. Deliberately.

Combined sequence:

"Now: the three elements together. Walk — turn — mudra. Follow me."

Teacher leads the combined sequence slowly. Students follow. Multiple passes.

Teacher observes and offers corrections: specific, brief, quiet. "Your turn — feel the four directions, not just one." "The mudra — hold it through the turn, then release."

Vocabulary Moment: "The concept of embodied practice — practice that lives in the body, not only the mind. The mala practice from Grade 4, the water-bowl offering, the careful pour: these are embodied practices. The body learns what the mind knows, and then the body can carry it when the mind is distracted or afraid. Cham is embodied practice at the level of the full body — not one hand, but every part."

Body Moment: Between the second and third rounds of the combined sequence — "Stop. Stand still. Feel what is in your body right now, after that movement. What is different from when you walked in? Thirty seconds."

Thirty seconds of stillness. Students do not move. Teacher stands still.

"That quality — that is what deliberate movement creates."

Emotional Hook: The discovery that moving with full intention feels different from moving casually — not better or worse, but different in quality. That difference is the point of Cham. Students encounter it in their own bodies, not as a description.

Turn and Talk — replaced by partner feedback

"Instead of talking today: show your partner the three elements. Walk. Turn. Mudra. Your partner watches. Then: tell them one thing they do well, and one thing to make more deliberate. Be specific. Then switch."

Partner pairs practise and give feedback. Teacher circulates: "What did you notice in your partner? What would make it more deliberate?"

Activity — 15 minutes

Extended practice — multiple rounds of the combined sequence. Students circulate so they can observe each other and receive feedback from the teacher.

Rotation structure: half the class practises, half observes and gives one-word feedback ("deliberate / more deliberate") — then switch.

Extension for students who are comfortable: "Add your figure's quality to the movement. If you are Yama: what does gravity feel like in the walk? If you are Citipati: what does lightness feel like in the turn? If you are Mahakala: where does fierce presence gather as you hold the mudra?" Students who take up the extension should be noted — they will demonstrate in C5 with this extra dimension.

Debrief — 3 minutes

Teacher: "Next class: you make your masks. The class after that: you perform. The movements are in your body now. Between these classes: practise at least once. In your room, in a corridor, anywhere private. Do the walk, the turn, the mudra. The body needs repetition to hold what it knows. Don't let it go cold."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set including Dolma. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer at close. If possible: the prayer set is chanted standing — students have been in movement for this entire class, and returning to stillness for prayer is itself a transition worth marking.

Teacher's closing line: "The movements are yours. Practise them. The body that practises is different from the body that only knows."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Students should practise the movement sequence between C3 and C5. Remind them explicitly — write a reminder on the board or in a note sent home. The performance in C5 is real: it has an audience, masks are worn, and it carries the quality of ceremony rather than classroom exercise. Prepare the performance space before C5: arrange seating for any audience, set up a simple shrine or altar, have the prayer set ready to open the performance. The performance opens with prayer — not with announcement, not with teacher explanation. If the teacher is not fully confident of the movements, run this class with the resident teacher present or leading. It is better to share the role than to teach imprecise movements that students then practise incorrectly between classes. Note which students take up the extension (adding figure-specific quality to the movements) — these students may be invited to demonstrate or lead in C5. Spiral connections: the concept of embodied practice connects directly to the mala practice (Grade 4), the mindful water offering (Grade 3), and the torma-making (Grade 4). Grade 6 brings this concept to its fullest form: the whole body in deliberate motion as practice.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child learned the basic Cham movements — the deliberate Cham walk, a hand mudra, and a quarter-turn facing the four directions — and began practising the combined sequence. They have been asked to practise these movements at home before the performance. You can ask them to show you: 'Show me the Cham walk. Show me the mudra. What is the difference between doing it deliberately and doing it casually?'"
