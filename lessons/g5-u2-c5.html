Grade 5  ·  Unit 2  ·  Class 5

Sharing and Reflecting

Presenting one detailed realm drawing → being witnessed in something personally examined and honestly held

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleElaborative retrieval through public articulation
Bloom's levelAnalyze and Evaluate
LessonG5.U2.C5

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Presenting one detailed realm drawing → being witnessed in something personally examined and honestly held Cognitive Principle Featured: Elaborative retrieval through public articulation — presenting understanding to others requires a different kind of consolidation than private reflection. The student must organize, sequence, and justify their understanding aloud, which deepens encoding and surfaces gaps. Being witnessed also creates a social anchor for the learning that purely private work cannot. Bloom's Level: Analyze and Evaluate — students synthesize their understanding of one realm into a coherent two-minute presentation (Analyze), and make and defend a personal evaluative judgment — why this realm, why it speaks to them most (Evaluate). Prior Class Summary: C4 was the drawing completion class — students finished their three detailed realm drawings (plus the "Way Out" page) and chose which realm to present. They carry the intention to present one realm and explain why it calls to them most.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the structure of the realm presentation: what the realm is, what it feels like from inside, which klesha rules it, one example, and why they chose it. Students will UNDERSTAND that the purpose of presenting is not performance — it is the act of making something privately examined available to the community, which changes both the presenter and the witnesses. Students will BE ABLE TO present their chosen realm clearly and honestly, with at least one specific personal or observed example. Students will FEEL the particular quality of being genuinely witnessed in something they have examined with honesty — and the corresponding experience of witnessing others with real attention.

Materials Checklist

  • Completed realm drawings (collected from students at the end of C4 — teacher returns them at the start of this class)
  • The Wheel of Life image displayed at the front of the room
  • Bell (for the close of the unit)
  • Space arranged for a share circle or standing gallery — whichever suits the room

Plan B: If a student is absent for C5 and hasn't presented: note their name and arrange a brief individual sharing with the teacher in the following week — do not skip them. If a student arrives without their drawing (e.g., it wasn't collected in C4): they present from memory and their drawing is displayed when it is recovered. The presentation matters more than the artefact.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Student leader opens the full prayer set. Teacher chants with the class.

Full Grade 5 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

(Food Offering Prayer is closing only.)

Prior Knowledge Activation — 3–5 minutes

Teacher leads the three-poison → three-antidote gesture sequence once through, without commentary. Then:

"Which realm are you presenting today? Hold it in your mind — the image, the characteristic experience, the klesha. You have everything you need. We'll begin in a moment."

Thirty seconds of quiet. No talking. This is preparation, not warm-up.

Mini Lesson — 2 minutes

Brief presentation structure — say it once, clearly:

"Tell us the realm. Show your drawing. Explain what this realm is — the characteristic experience and the ruling klesha. Give one example — personal or observed. Then: why this realm? Why did you choose it above the other two you drew? That last question is the most important."

"Two minutes each. No evaluation — witnessing. When someone is presenting, your whole attention is on them. That's the practice."

Vocabulary Moment: "No new vocabulary today. You have all the words you need."

Body Moment: Before any presentation begins — the full class stands. The teacher leads one full round of Dechen Monlam, standing. Slow. Together.

"We chant the aspiration for all beings before we look at the states that cause suffering. This is why the prayer exists. It holds the purpose of what we're doing."

The class sits after the chanting. Presentations begin.

Emotional Hook: The experience of being genuinely witnessed — not evaluated, not corrected, not compared — in something personal and honestly examined.

Turn and Talk — not applicable this class

The sharing circle itself is the structured sharing. No separate Turn and Talk.

Activity — 20–25 minutes

Presentations.

Each student presents for approximately two minutes. The sequence is flexible — students can volunteer, or the teacher can move through the room in a natural order.

The teacher's role during presentations:

  • Full attention on the presenter — model the witnessing the class is asked to give
  • After each presentation: one specific, genuine observation. Not evaluation — recognition. Examples:
  • "I notice you chose the hungry ghost realm, and your drawing shows the food turning to flame in the ghost's mouth — that's a detail from the traditional accounts, and it adds something to the teaching."
  • "You said you chose the human realm because it's the one where you can keep going even when it's hard. That's a precise reading — that's exactly what the teaching says makes it precious."
  • "Your drawing of the demi-god realm shows two figures, not one — and one is looking sideways at the other. That's the whole teaching in a single image."

Keep each observation brief — one or two sentences — so the next presentation can begin.

If a student is nervous: "Take a breath. Show us the drawing first, then tell us about it. The drawing can speak first if you need it to."

If a student's presentation runs short: hold the silence rather than fill it. Then: "Is there anything else you want us to know about why you chose this one?"

Debrief — 5 minutes

After the last presentation, the teacher asks:

"We've spent two units with the Wheel of Life. What do you know now — specifically — that you didn't know when you walked into Grade 5?"

Take 3–4 responses. The teacher receives them without adding to or correcting them. Hold the last response briefly — a genuine pause.

Then: "Next unit: we meet the teacher who came to Tibet specifically to give people the tools to step out of the Wheel. Not just the map — the tools. You've been encountering him since Grade 1. His name is Padmasambhava. Next class, you'll learn the full story of why he came and what it cost him."

Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader closes the full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

Teacher rings the bell once at the end of the closing set. A moment of silence.

Teacher's closing line, the arc of Units 1 and 2 in one sentence: "You've looked at the Wheel from the outside and from the inside — as a map of the cosmos and as a map of your own mind — and you've found that both readings are true."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The three detailed realm drawings (portfolio artefact for Unit 2) are returned to students after C5 or held for year-end portfolios — follow the school's portfolio policy. Together with the Wheel of Life drawing from Unit 1, these constitute the Grade 5 visual artefacts through the first two units. Unit 3 begins: Padmasambhava and the coming of the Dharma to Tibet. Students have been encountering Padmasambhava since Grade 1 — his birth story (G1.U3), the taming narrative (G2.U3), the Seven-Line prayer meaning (G2.U3.C3), and the prayer memorisation begun in Grade 3. Grade 5 is the full historical and lineage introduction: who he was, when and why he came to Tibet, what he brought, and how the chain of transmission has kept the Dharma alive into this classroom. This unit is less psychologically intense than Units 1–2 — it is grounding, narrative, and connective. Students have been living inside the map; now they meet the mapmaker. The debrief transition into Unit 3 should feel like both an arrival and a beginning: students know the Wheel, they know what the antidotes are, and they are now ready to meet the teacher who brought the antidotes within reach of an entire culture.
