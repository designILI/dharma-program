Reading a Stupa

The stupa read element by element, connected to specific curriculum content → the recognition that the eight-year curriculum is visible in the structure of a single object

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleElaborative encoding
Bloom's levelEvaluate / Create
LessonG8.U3.C2

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): The stupa read element by element, connected to specific curriculum content → the recognition that the eight-year curriculum is visible in the structure of a single object

Cognitive Principle Featured: Elaborative encoding — connecting new information (the stupa's symbolic structure) to a rich web of prior learning (the full eight-year curriculum) creates deeper retention and genuine integration; the stupa becomes a retrieval cue for the entire curriculum, and the curriculum becomes the key for reading the stupa

Bloom's Level: Evaluate / Create — students synthesise eight years of curriculum content into the stupa's symbolic structure, make specific connections across grades, and finalise the design for their model

Prior Class Summary: C1 introduced the stupa structure — base, dome, harmika, spire, pinnacle — and each element's meaning in the tradition. Students circumambulated a real or visualised stupa, received the Tibetan term chorten, and sketched a design draft with basic labels. They carry the recognition that the stupa encodes everything they have studied, and the instruction: look at every stupa you pass with different eyes.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW at least one specific connection from each stupa element to a specific year or unit of the Dharma curriculum — not generic ("the base means compassion") but located ("the base's four immeasurables connect to Grade 2's widening circle of compassion and Grade 7's Medicine Buddha metta") Students will UNDERSTAND that the stupa's iconography is a complete synthesis of the Buddhist path — and that their eight years of study have moved through the same structure, from the foundation outward and upward Students will BE ABLE TO finalise their stupa design with: the type chosen, the proportions sketched, the curriculum connections annotated on each element, and preparatory notes for the C4 presentation Students will FEEL the sense of a completed circuit — seeing the whole curriculum present in the structure of a single object and recognising, with surprise or with satisfaction, how much they have actually carried

Materials Checklist

  • Students' stupa sketches from C1 (carried from last class — confirm students have them)
  • Reference images of stupas — the same set as C1, available for reference during design finalisation
  • Reference list of the eight types of stupa — for students who have not yet chosen their type
  • Pencils or pens for annotation and design finalisation
  • Whiteboard or projected space for the teacher to work through the curriculum connections during the mini-lesson
  • Materials checklist for C3 — the teacher should confirm all C3 materials are secured before the end of this class: clay or salt dough (one portion per student), shaping tools if available, armature materials (cardboard, wire, or wooden dowels if students plan tall or complex spires), paint if finishing in C3, reference images for C3 building
  • Plan B: If students' C1 sketches are unavailable, they redraw from memory (5 minutes maximum) before beginning the annotation — the redrawing is also a retrieval and encoding exercise

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 8 prayer set, led by the student leader of the day. Sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Tsog Due pa. Student leader conducts.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

"Read your stupa sketch from last class. Which element are you least certain about — where you drew it and labelled it, but aren't confident you know what it means? One person each."

Take responses. Common areas of uncertainty: the harmika (what exactly does it represent?); the relationship between the thirteen rings and the bodhisattva path stages; the difference between the dome as "Buddha's body" and the dome as "womb of enlightenment."

"Good. By the end of this class you should be able to read a stupa the way you read a text — going element by element and finding the meaning in each part. That is what we are doing today."

Mini Lesson — 15 minutes

Return to the stupa structure element by element — now with explicit, specific connections to curriculum content. Move between the stupa image and the class's own knowledge.

Base → Four immeasurables → the curriculum's foundation:

"The base of the stupa: the four immeasurables — loving-kindness, compassion, empathetic joy, equanimity. Where did you first encounter these? Grade 2 — the widening circle of compassion that starts with yourself and extends outward to all beings. You drew the circles. In Grade 7, the Medicine Buddha metta practice returned to these four as a healing intention directed outward. The base of the stupa is what you began building in Grade 2. You have been working on the base for eight years."

Dome → Buddha-nature, potential for awakening in all beings:

"The dome encloses the seed of awakening in all beings. Where did you first encounter the teaching that all beings carry this potential? Grade 1 — the circle of kindness, the idea that every being wants to be happy and free from suffering. Grade 5 — the teaching of precious human birth, and the Wheel of Life showing the realms that all beings move through, each with the capacity to arrive at awakening. Grade 7 — Teaching Day, where you stood in front of younger students and transmitted something. You were treating them as beings with the full capacity to understand and receive. That is the dome's teaching in action."

Harmika → Buddha's mind, the transmission of teaching:

"The harmika is the throne of the teaching — the Buddha's mind from which the Dharma radiates. Where in the curriculum did you encounter the idea of transmission — of a living understanding passed from person to person? Grade 1: Guru Rinpoche, who brought the Dharma to Tibet. Grade 5: Padmasambhava, the detail of that transmission. Grade 7: Teaching Day, where you yourselves attempted transmission — standing in front of younger students and passing on what you had understood. The harmika is that act: the teaching-mind from which understanding radiates outward."

Spire with thirteen rings → the bodhisattva path, the eight-year curriculum:

"The thirteen rings of the spire: the stages of the bodhisattva path, from the first arising of the intention to benefit all beings to complete enlightenment. This curriculum is the first stages of that path. Eight years of this curriculum are the base of the spire. Every year: a wider capacity, a deeper understanding, a larger view. The spire is what you have been climbing. You are on it now."

Pinnacle → nature of mind, open and luminous:

"The pinnacle: the space element. Open. Luminous. Unobstructed. Where in the curriculum did you encounter the teaching about the nature of mind at this level? Grade 6 — the Bardo unit, the ground luminosity, the clear light of awareness that is present at death and accessible in practice. Grade 8, Unit 1 — the mind the Twelve Links point toward: not the conditioned mind described by the chain, but the awareness underneath the conditioning. The pinnacle is what all the other elements are pointing toward. It has been in the curriculum since Grade 6. You have arrived at the top of the stupa."

Display the whole structure now as a unified image — all elements visible simultaneously.

"The stupa is the curriculum in stone."

Vocabulary Moment: "The term iconography — from the Greek eikon, 'image,' and graphein, 'to write.' The visual symbols and imagery that encode meaning in a tradition. Stupa iconography is not decorative — it is precise. Every proportion, every element, every colour in a painted stupa encodes a specific teaching. Learning to read iconography is learning to read a different kind of text — one written in three-dimensional space and proportion rather than in words. After this class, every stupa you encounter is a text you can read."

Body Moment: Students hold their stupa sketches with both hands and read them from bottom to top — aloud, quietly to themselves, moving through each element.

"Start at the base. Which grade did you first study the four immeasurables? Name it. Now move up to the dome. When did you first learn about the potential for awakening in all beings? Name the grade or unit. Move to the harmika. Where did you first encounter the idea of transmission? Move to the spire. Eight years of this curriculum — that's you, on the spire. Move to the pinnacle. Grade 6 — the clear light. Grade 8 — the mind underneath the conditioning. Now you are at the top."

Students move through this at their own pace — some quickly, some slowly. Let the silence hold.

Emotional Hook: The recognition of the completed circuit — the whole curriculum visible in the structure of a single object — and the particular quality of that recognition: unexpected, specific, quiet.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: what is the most meaningful connection for you — one element of the stupa and one specific thing you studied in this curriculum. Not the most impressive-sounding connection — the most genuinely meaningful one to you, personally. Why that one?"

Take two or three connections from the room after pairs have spoken.

Activity — 12 minutes

Students finalise their stupa design. The finalised sketch should show:

  • Which of the eight stupa types they are building (or the standard general form), and why that choice
  • The proportions of each element — rough but considered: the spire should be taller than the dome; the base should be clearly wider than the dome; the harmika should be distinct and readable
  • Annotations on each element: name, traditional meaning, and the specific curriculum connection (grade or unit)
  • Any decorative or surface choices they intend to apply during the building session in C3
  • A note on materials they plan to use (clay, salt dough, cardboard — based on what will be available in C3)

Teacher circulates: "What will you say about the spire when you present in C4? Not generally — what specific teaching does each ring represent, in your understanding?" "Is the connection you've written for the dome actually specific, or is it general? 'Buddha-nature' is the traditional meaning — what from your own study of it do you bring to this element?" "Why this type of stupa? What draws you to commemorating that specific event in the Buddha's life?"

Students who have not chosen a type by the end of this session should be guided before C3 — the building session cannot begin without a design decision.

Debrief — 3 minutes

"When you build your stupa in C3, you are not making a model. You are making a teaching object — an object that encodes in three-dimensional space what this curriculum has been teaching in words and practice for eight years. Every element you shape with your hands should carry its meaning in your mind as you shape it. That is the quality of attention the session asks for."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full Grade 8 prayer set. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer added at close.

Teacher's closing line: "Next class: materials, hands, clay. Come knowing what you are building."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Confirm all materials before the end of this class — clay or salt dough (sufficient for one model per student), shaping tools (butter knives, popsicle sticks, or actual clay tools if available), armature materials for students planning tall or structurally complex spires (wire, cardboard inner forms, wooden dowels), paint if the plan is to finish and paint in C3, and reference images of stupas available throughout the building session. Students who have not finalised their design by the end of C2 need brief one-on-one guidance before C3 begins — even a simple design choice (standard stupa form, focus on proportions and labelling) is sufficient. The building session in C3 should not begin with unresolved design decisions. The C4 presentation requires each student to hold their completed stupa and explain each element from base to pinnacle, with at least one specific curriculum connection per element. Students should be reminded of this at the end of C2 so they practise the explanation between classes — it should not be improvised in C4. The presentation is not a school report — it is a transmission. Frame it this way.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child worked through the stupa's symbolic structure in depth — connecting every element, from the base to the pinnacle, to specific things they have studied across eight years of the Dharma curriculum. They finalised the design for the model stupa they will build next class. You can ask them: 'What does the pinnacle of a stupa represent — and which year of your Dharma study connects to it? What type of stupa are you building, and why?'"
