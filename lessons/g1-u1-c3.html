Flags in the Wind

A physical prayer flag — its colours, cloth, and markings → the idea that a wish can be written down, lifted by wind, and sent outward without a person present to carry it

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleConcrete-to-Abstract Progression (Bruner's Enactive/Iconic/Symbolic sequence)
Bloom's levelRemember and Understand
LessonG1.U1.C3

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): A physical prayer flag — its colours, cloth, and markings → the idea that a wish can be written down, lifted by wind, and sent outward without a person present to carry it

Cognitive Principle Featured: Concrete-to-Abstract Progression (Bruner's Enactive/Iconic/Symbolic sequence) — students first handle something physical (or see vivid photographs of real flags), then describe what they observe, then are offered the concept that the physical object is doing invisible work. The abstract idea (wishes travelling on wind) arrives only after students have touched and described the concrete thing. Reversed, this lesson fails — always concrete first.

Bloom's Level: Remember and Understand — students are acquiring new factual knowledge (what prayer flags are, where they come from, what is on them) and building toward understanding (why someone would write a wish on a flag and put it in the wind). The "why" is the lesson's centre of gravity.

Prior Class Summary: In C2, students learned the six syllables of Om Mani Padme Hung and what each points toward, using finger-counting and illustrated cards. They read Moody Cow Meditates in full and chanted with hands on hearts. The mantra now carries meaning for them, not just sound. Today that mantra meets a new form: it is written on cloth, lifted by wind.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW what a prayer flag is: cloth printed with mantras and prayers, hung in the wind, used in Himalayan Buddhist communities.

Students will UNDERSTAND that hanging a prayer flag is a way of sending a wish out into the world — the wind carries the prayers, so you don't have to be present for the wish to keep travelling.

Students will BE ABLE TO describe a prayer flag (colours, markings, how it moves) and explain in their own words why someone would hang one.

Students will FEEL a sense of recognition — these are part of their world, their mountains, their culture — alongside genuine curiosity about what will be written on their own flag.

Materials Checklist

Option A (preferred): 1–2 real prayer flag strings — the kind hung between structures, with multiple coloured flags on a line. Even worn or faded ones are better than photographs alone. If the school has flags on the building, take the class outside briefly to look at them up close. If a family has donated used flags, handle them carefully and with respect.

Option B: A printed set of close-up photographs of prayer flags — minimum 6 images showing: the five colours side by side, the Lung-ta (wind horse) motif, mantra text, flags at a mountain pass, flags on a monastery wall, flags moving in wind. Print A4 size or display on a screen if a projector is available.

  • Singing bowl (1)
  • Six syllable cards from C2 (already made — retrieve from storage)
  • One plain white cloth rectangle per student — approximately 15 cm × 20 cm, cotton or similar. This is the flag each student will paint in C4. Today they see it and hold it, but do not paint it yet. Advance prep: Cut and hem one cloth piece per student before class. Light hemming prevents fraying during painting. Label each on the back with the child's name in small letters.
  • A small stone or smooth pebble — one for each student (or one to pass around the circle). Advance prep: Collect one smooth stone per student, roughly palm-sized. These will anchor the flags during painting in C4 — today students simply hold them.
  • Beeswax crayons or chalk — a few pieces, available at tables for the activity (not yet distributed)

Room setup: Circle for opening, prior knowledge activation, and mini lesson. Tables set up for activity (15 minutes before class end). On each table: 2–3 photographs of flags or direct access to the real flags if available. Cloth rectangles stacked at teacher's station, not yet distributed.

Student leader: Brief before class. The leader is now a practised role — reinforce that chanting slowly is better than chanting fast.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Students enter the circle. If real prayer flags are available, they are visible — hanging across the room or draped over a chair at the centre. If photographs, they are face-down on the teacher's chair, not yet revealed.

Student leader steps forward.

Kyap Dro (Refuge): Leader recites, the teacher alongside softly.

Om Mani Padme Hung: Seven times. By C3, this chanting should feel grounded and unhurried. If it rushes, the teacher slows visibly — no verbal correction needed. The group will follow the body.

Manjushri Prayer: Leader recites.

Strike the bowl once.

"Thank you." Student leader sits.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

"Before we do anything else — has anyone seen prayer flags? Anywhere? At home, on the way to school, at a monastery, up on a mountain?"

Wait. In a Himalayan school context, most students will have seen them. The question is whether they have ever stopped and looked.

Strong-response example: A child says, "My uncle has them at his house, tied from the roof." "Have you ever touched one? What did it feel like?" If yes, invite briefly: "What did you notice about it?" Probable answers: old, faded, rough, the writing was there. Receive each observation.

Surface response: A child says, "I saw them but I don't know what they are." "You've seen them — that's the most important thing. Today we're going to look very carefully at what's on them. And then I think you'll see them differently every time after this."

If no student has seen them: This is unlikely in this context but possible. In this case: "Has anyone ever seen something that moves in the wind and isn't alive? Not a kite — something that people put up on purpose, to send a message?" If still no response, move directly to showing the flags and let the look precede the question.

After two or three students have shared: "Here's my question — and it's an interesting one: why would someone write words on a piece of cloth and hang it up in the wind? Why not just say the words themselves?"

Let this question sit without answering it. Move into the mini lesson.

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Reveal the flags — either bring the real string to the centre of the circle, or distribute photographs to small groups of two or three students.

"Just look. What do you see?"

Allow thirty to forty-five seconds of quiet observation. Let students look without being told what to see. Then begin inviting observations:

"What colours are there?"

Students will name: blue, white, red, green, yellow. the teacher lists them on fingers: "Five colours. Always these five. Anyone know why five?"

Take one or two guesses warmly. Then: "Five elements — sky, wind, fire, water, earth. The flags carry all five. The wind is already one of them."

"What else do you see on the flags?"

Students will notice: a horse, writing, patterns, other animals or symbols. Point out:

"That horse in the middle — this is the Lung-ta. It means Wind Horse. He's galloping — carrying the prayers. Every time the wind blows, the Wind Horse runs, and he carries the wishes further."

"And the writing — does anyone know what it says?"

A student may guess correctly: Om Mani Padme Hung. If so: "Yes. Exactly. The mantra you've been saying — it's written right here on the flag. So every time the wind moves it, the mantra goes out."

If no one guesses: point to the text carefully. "Look closely at these letters. They're in Tibetan script. But what do you think they might say, given what we've been learning?" Let it arrive naturally.

Now hold up one of the plain white cloth rectangles.

"This is what we're going to make. Each of you will get one. Next class, you'll paint your mantra on it. Then — in our last class — we'll hang them together."

Pass the cloth pieces around the circle so each student can hold one for a few seconds before passing it on. No instructions — just feel the material.

"That cloth in your hands — imagine your wish is going to live in it. Whose wish? For whom?"

Vocabulary Moment: "Lung-ta — Wind Horse. In our tradition, he's the one who carries prayers on his back. When you see flags moving in the wind, that's not just the wind — that's Lung-ta running."

Body Moment: Students stand. "Spread your arms out — like a flag. Now — I'm the wind." the teacher walks slowly around the inside of the circle, blowing gently past each student. Students let their arms sway. "Your arms are the flag. The wind is moving you. Where is the wind taking you?"

This takes thirty seconds. Students sit back down.

Emotional Hook: You are working toward the felt sense of being moved by something invisible — that the wind is not random, that it is doing something. Watch for students who visibly imagine it — who look out the window, or who settle into the swaying and don't want to stop. The emotional hook in this lesson is the child's dawning sense that the world around them is already full of wishes in motion, that the flags have been working all this time. Do not explain this. Let the Lung-ta image do the work.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Turn to your partner. Tell them: if you could write one wish on your flag — for one person — who would it be for? You don't have to tell me. Just tell your partner."

This question is the first time students are asked to choose a recipient for their flag. It is private between partners for now. the teacher circulates but does not lean in to overhear. Presence without intrusion.

Signal with the bowl to reconvene.

"You don't need to share unless you want to. But hold onto that person in your mind — we'll come back to them in C4."

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Students move to tables. Each table has 2–3 flag photographs and a few pieces of beeswax crayon or chalk.

"Today we're not going to paint yet — that's next class. But we are going to do something to get ready. Look at one of the photographs. Choose one thing you see on the flag — a shape, a pattern, a letter — and copy it lightly onto the cloth with your crayon. Just one thing. It doesn't have to be perfect."

This is a slow-down activity by design. The purpose is to get students looking very closely at actual flag details, and to give their hands the experience of the cloth before the painting begins. Light crayon marks can be erased or will be covered by paint in C4.

Teacher circulates and sits briefly with each table group:

  • Ask: "What did you choose? Why that one?"
  • Receive answers without evaluation.
  • If a student is stuck: "Try the horse. Just the outline of his body — can you see his four legs? Start there."

Scaffold: For a student who is overwhelmed by choice, point to the Wind Horse and say: "Just this one. Trace its outline with your finger first on the photograph, then do the same on the cloth." Tracing before drawing is a reliable scaffold for hesitant hands.

Extension: A student who completes this quickly can look at multiple photographs and find: How many different mantras or prayers appear? Are they all the same? (They are not — different flags carry different texts.) This student is doing observational research. Acknowledge it as such: "You're reading the flags. That's what scholars do."

At the five-minute warning, begin a quiet wrap-up. Students hold their cloth pieces.

"Next class, we paint. Think about the colours you want to use. There are five traditional ones — but your flag, your choice."

Collect cloth pieces and store carefully with names facing out. They should not go home yet.

Debrief — 5 minutes

Reconvene the circle. Students sit without their cloth pieces.

"Here's something I keep thinking about. These flags have been hanging on mountains and rooftops for hundreds of years. Wind blows in winter, sun fades them in summer, rain soaks them. They get old and frayed. But people keep putting new ones up."

Pause.

"Why would they keep doing that? What are they so sure of?"

Do not answer this. Let one or two students speak. You are listening for: they believe in it; they want the wishes to keep going; someone taught them to; it matters.

"I think they're sure the wishes are real. Even when no one is watching. Even when the flag is just moving in the wind by itself — alone on a mountaintop — the Lung-ta is still running."

Let that settle.

"Our question: how can a wish travel to someone far away? What do you think now?"

Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads: Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer.

Strike the bowl once.

"When you go home today — look for flags. If you see one moving, notice: Lung-ta is working."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Students have now held their cloth flag pieces and made light marks on them. In C4, each student should find their piece with their name already on the back — no searching or confusion. Ensure cloth pieces are stored flat. Also: students have privately decided (in Turn and Talk) who their flag wish is for. In C4's activity, gently ask each student to carry that person in mind while they paint. You do not need them to say the name aloud until C5's sharing.
