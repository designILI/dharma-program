LessonG1.U4.C2

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): A small insect moving across a surface → the question of whether other creatures experience their lives from the inside

Cognitive Principle Featured: Perspective-taking through guided inference. Children cannot know what a bug feels, but they can reason from what they know about themselves. This lesson scaffolds that reasoning: I move away from danger → the bug also moves away from danger → perhaps the bug does not want to be hurt, just as I do not want to be hurt. This is not biology — it is the first exercise in moral imagination.

Bloom's Level: Understand / Apply. Students move from simply hearing facts (Understand) to applying a simple ethical framework to their own behaviour: if bugs feel, what should I do when I see one? (Apply). The class is structured as a guided inquiry, not a lecture.

Prior Class Summary: Last class students heard the Jataka tale of the Brave Little Parrot — a story of persistent care for small creatures. They made story maps and discussed why the parrot kept going. They end that class with a question left open: do small things matter? Today's class is the philosophical answer — not delivered as a conclusion, but discovered through discussion.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW that insects are living beings — they move, they react to danger, they seek food, they avoid pain — and that they share the basic condition of all living things: they want to continue living.

Students will UNDERSTAND the concept of ahimsa in simple form: not causing harm to any living being, starting with the smallest and most easily overlooked.

Students will BE ABLE TO give a reason — in their own words — for why a bug should not be squashed: not because they were told so, but because they worked it out.

Students will FEEL genuine curiosity — the philosophical discomfort and wonder that comes from not being completely sure of the answer to a hard question, and the satisfaction of thinking carefully about it anyway.

Materials Checklist

  • Three to four images of common local insects (ants, beetles, small spiders, moths, butterflies — choose species the students will actually encounter in and around the school). Print these large enough to see clearly — A5 or A4, laminated or on cardstock.
  • One short video clip of an insect moving, if a device and screen are available in the classroom — optional but effective. An ant carrying food or a beetle righting itself after being flipped are ideal. Keep it under 90 seconds. Download it in advance — do not rely on internet connection.
  • Whiteboard and marker — for the "What do we know?" T-chart
  • Student story maps from C1 (returned for reference during the activity)
  • Blank half-sheets of paper (one per student) for the activity
  • Standard prayer card
  • A live insect in a container, if one can be gently collected before class — a single ant or small beetle in a clear jar with a few small holes in the lid, with a tiny piece of fruit or leaf. This is entirely optional but transforms the lesson if present. Handle the jar with obvious care. Return the insect to outdoors at the end of class.

Advance prep: Think about how you personally relate to the question "Do bugs feel?" You do not need an answer. What you need is genuine uncertainty — and the willingness to wonder alongside the children. If you walk in with the answer already given, the inquiry collapses into a lecture. Walk in with the question. The question is the lesson.

Note on the live insect: if you bring one, it will be the most powerful thing in the room. Handle the jar gently, speak of the creature with care, and never put it at risk. When you release it at the end of class, make a small event of it — carry the jar outside, set it in the grass, let the bug walk out on its own. The children will remember it.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Students settle in the semicircle. The insect images are visible at the front but not yet the focus.

Student leader of the day comes forward.

Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer. Together, unhurried.

Student leader returns. Teacher sits.

"Last class, the parrot cared about other birds who were suffering. The parrot kept going even though it was small. Today I want to ask a different question. The question is this:"

Teacher writes on the board in large letters: DO BUGS FEEL?

"That's our question for today. And I want to tell you honestly — I'm not entirely sure of the answer. So we're going to think about it together."

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

"First — tell me what you already think. Don't think too hard. Just say what comes to your mind. Do bugs feel? Yes, no, maybe — and why?"

Go around the circle quickly — every student says one word or one sentence. Teacher writes key words or phrases on the board. Do not evaluate any answer. Do not say "good answer" or "interesting." Just write. The board might fill with: "no — they're too small," "yes — they run away," "maybe — I don't know," "yes — because they move," "no — they don't have feelings like us."

"Look at all of these. Some of us think yes. Some think no. Some aren't sure. That's exactly the right place to start. Let's think more carefully."

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

This is a Socratic dialogue, not a lecture. Teacher poses questions and receives responses. The goal is to guide students toward reasoning from what they know about themselves.

Part 1: What do bugs do?

Post the insect images or show the video clip (if available). If a live insect in a jar is present, place it where students can see it.

"Let's look at what bugs actually do. What do you see?"

Discuss briefly: bugs move, they react when you touch them, they run away from danger, they seek food, they build homes.

Write on the board under a simple T-chart: one side "What HUMANS do," one side "What BUGS do."

Fill it in together:

  • Humans move / Bugs move
  • Humans eat / Bugs eat
  • Humans run from danger / Bugs run from danger
  • Humans make homes / Some bugs make homes (nests, webs, hives)

"So here's my question: when you run away from something — a dog that's barking, a person who's going to hit you — are you doing it because you feel something? Or just because your body does it automatically?"

(Children will say "because I feel scared.") "Right. So when the bug runs away from your foot — what do you think might be happening?"

Allow the tension. Do not resolve it. Let several students respond.

Part 2: Can we know for sure?

"Here is the honest answer: we can't be completely sure. We can't get inside a bug's mind. We don't know for certain what a bug experiences.

But here is what the Dharma teaches us. It says: all living beings want to live. All living beings want to avoid pain. Not just humans. Not just mammals. All living beings. That is what makes something 'living' — it moves toward life and away from death. Toward comfort and away from pain.

A bug is living. It moves toward life. So we treat it accordingly."

Part 3: Introducing ahimsa

"There is a word from Sanskrit — the language of many sacred texts — that means: not harming. The word is ahimsa. Let's say it: ah-HIM-sa." (Class repeats.) "Ahimsa means: I will try not to cause pain to any living thing. Not just to people I love. Not just to animals I think are cute. To all living things. Even the small ones. Even the ones that are easy to overlook.

This is not easy. We don't always manage it. But it's a direction we try to go."

Vocabulary Moment: "Ahimsa. Write it on your hand if you want to remember it." (Many six-year-olds will do this.) "Ah-him-sa. Not harming. We'll use this word the rest of the unit."

Body Moment: "I want to try something. Imagine there is a tiny ant on your desk right now — right in front of you. It's walking slowly across. What does your hand want to do?" (Pause — some children will make a squishing gesture.) "Now — I want you to practice a different movement. Instead of squishing — use one finger, very very gently, and imagine you are just moving the ant to the edge of the desk. Very slow. Very careful." Students practice this slow, deliberate, gentle finger movement. "That's ahimsa in action. That's your hand practicing gentleness."

Emotional Hook: If a live insect is present in the jar: place it gently on the desk. "Look at this creature. Just look at it for a moment." Silence for fifteen to twenty seconds. Students looking at a living thing without talking — this is the hook. The insect becomes real in a way that words about insects never can.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Fixed pairs. Students turn to their partner.

"Here is my question. It's a real question — not one I know the answer to. If a bug somehow could understand what was happening to it when a person squished it — what do you think it would want that person to know? What would the bug say, if it could talk?"

Two minutes. Teacher circulates. Listen for empathy, humour, imagination, genuine engagement. This question requires perspective-taking and will produce vivid, surprising answers.

Reconvene: take three or four responses. Receive each one with care. "Interesting. What else?"

Then: "I think the bug would probably just want to keep going. It had somewhere to be. It had something to do. It wanted its life."

Activity — 15–20 minutes

"Bug Thoughts" — The Perspective Journal Entry

Each student gets a blank half-sheet of paper.

"Here is your task. Draw a bug — any bug you want. It can be an ant, a beetle, a butterfly, a spider — whatever comes to mind. Then around your bug, write or draw its thoughts. What is it thinking about? What does it want? Where is it going? What is it afraid of?"

"If you were the bug — what would be on your mind?"

This is a perspective-taking activity. Students project inner life onto a creature they cannot actually know. This is exactly the right exercise — it builds moral imagination.

Teacher circulates. Ask: "What is your bug doing?" "What does it want?" "Is it scared of anything?" Prompt with specificity: "Is it looking for food? Is it going home? Is it looking for its family?"

Scaffold for a stuck student: Start with the drawing. "Draw the bug first. What does it look like — big eyes, lots of legs, wings?" Once the bug exists on paper, ask: "Where is it going right now?" That question alone unlocks the thought bubbles.

Extension for an early finisher: "Can you write a tiny story — just three sentences — from the bug's point of view? Start with: 'Today I was walking across the floor and...'"

Collect or keep — these become part of the bug rescue observation journal in C3.

Debrief — 5 minutes

Students seated in the semicircle.

"I want to ask you: did anything change in how you think about bugs today? Even a little?"

Take three or four responses. Do not evaluate. Then:

"The thing I want to leave you with today is this: it doesn't take much to decide to be gentle. It costs you almost nothing to step around an ant instead of on it. It takes one extra second to carry a bug outside instead of squishing it. And for the bug — that one second is everything.

Next class, we're going to go and actually observe a bug. Really look at one — for a long time. And we're going to draw what we see."

If the live insect is present, now is the moment to release it: "Before we close — let's return this one to where it belongs." Take the jar outside briefly, open it, set it in the grass. Students watch. Teacher says nothing except: "There. Off it goes." Return inside for closing prayer.

Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader to the front.

Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer. Together.

Teacher's closing sentence, specific to today:

"Ahimsa. Not harming. That's the word we're going to keep living with. Watch how many chances you get to practise it — just today, between now and dinner."

Dismiss per school custom.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child thought about a genuinely hard question: do bugs feel? They considered what insects do — moving, eating, running from danger — and learned the Sanskrit word ahimsa, meaning not harming. They drew a bug and imagined its thoughts. You can ask them: 'Do you think bugs feel? What does ahimsa mean? What would you do now if you saw a bug on the floor?'"
