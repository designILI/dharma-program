Teaching Day

Walking into a room of younger students with a lesson plan in hand → the threshold experience of being the teacher; the identity shift from student to transmitter

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleTransfer
Bloom's levelCreate
LessonG7.U2.C6

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Walking into a room of younger students with a lesson plan in hand → the threshold experience of being the teacher; the identity shift from student to transmitter

Cognitive Principle Featured: Transfer — knowledge and skill built in a supported context (the Grade 7 classroom, the rehearsal, the design process) is applied in a new, real context (the younger students' classroom). Transfer is the ultimate test of understanding; it cannot be faked.

Bloom's Level: Create — the full realisation of the unit's creative arc. Students do not present or perform — they teach. The lesson they designed, rehearsed, and revised is now enacted for a real audience whose learning depends on it.

Prior Class Summary: C5 was the full rehearsal — each student taught their lesson to a partner in the younger-student role, received feedback, and identified the one change to make before Teaching Day. Students carry their lesson plans, their concrete anchors, all materials, and the instruction: "Stay present. The plan is a guide, not a script."

Format note: This class takes place in a different location — the younger students' classroom or an arranged teaching space. The Grade 7 class travels together. The schema is present and followed; the Activity section describes the teaching event itself.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW from direct experience what it feels like to be responsible for someone else's understanding — to stand in the role of teacher before a real audience and bear the responsibility of transmission Students will UNDERSTAND that the lesson they designed belongs to them fully — including its successes and its stumbles — and that both outcomes are equally valuable information about the gap between intention and effect Students will BE ABLE TO collect a verbal response from the younger students after their lesson ("What did you learn? What was the most interesting part?") and bring that response to C7 as data Students will FEEL the threshold experience of entering the room as the teacher — the specific shift in identity, weight, and presence that crossing that threshold produces

Materials Checklist

  • Final revised lesson plans (each student carries theirs)
  • All concrete anchors and lesson materials (each student has confirmed these before leaving Grade 7 room)
  • Any physical materials the younger students will use in the activity (students carry these)
  • Teacher's observational notes framework (the teacher carries this — see Teacher Notes)
  • Receiving class teacher confirmed: room location, arrival time, duration, format (sequential or parallel groups)
  • Plan B (if a student is acutely distressed before entering): One breath together. The teacher walks in beside that student. Their lesson plan is in their hand. Begin. A student who is nervous and begins teaching almost always finds that the nervousness resolves within the first sixty seconds. A student who cannot begin at all is rare — if it happens, the teacher briefly introduces the student to the younger class and invites the younger students to ask a question first. This redirects the energy from "I must perform" to "I am responding" and usually frees the student to begin.
  • Plan B (if Teaching Day must be postponed — illness, scheduling conflict): Reschedule immediately to the next available class. Do not substitute a simulated teaching session — the younger students are essential to the experience. If rescheduling is not possible before the end of the unit, the teacher facilitates a structured peer-teaching session with a different Grade (not Grade 7 peers) and notes in the curriculum record that the near-peer element was modified.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 7 prayer set — led in the Grade 7 classroom, before travelling to the teaching space: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader.

The teacher, before leaving the Grade 7 room: "We open here because this practice belongs to us, wherever we are. The Sang offering travels with us — we carry the intention outward into the room we're going to. Bring it."

Food Offering Prayer at closing — said upon return to Grade 7 classroom.

Prior Knowledge Activation — during the walk (informal)

No formal PKA. The teacher walks beside students on the way to the teaching space and asks quietly, conversationally:

"What is the one thing you most want the younger students to take from your lesson? Not what you want them to know — what you want them to feel, or understand, or remember next week?"

Students answer individually as the teacher moves through the group. These answers are not shared with the class — they are said aloud to help each student locate their intention before they begin.

Mini Lesson — none

Students are the teachers. No mini lesson today.

Vocabulary Moment: None today.

Body Moment:

At the door of the younger students' classroom — before entering — the teacher stops the group.

"Stop. One breath. In together."

Students and teacher breathe in together, once.

"You know this material. You have rehearsed. Now: be present for them — not for your plan, not for me, not for how it looks. For the younger students in that room. In."

Students enter.

Emotional Hook: The threshold — the doorway between being a student and being a teacher. The shift in identity that occurs when entering a room as the responsible adult. This is a real shift, and it should be experienced as real, not minimised.

Activity — 20–25 minutes (depending on arrangement)

The teaching session.

Each student teaches their lesson in sequence or in parallel groups, depending on what has been arranged with the receiving class teacher:

Sequential format: One student teaches at a time to the whole younger class while the other Grade 7 students observe from one side. After each lesson: brief transition (the teacher facilitates a smooth handover). This format allows the Grade 7 students to observe each other's teaching, which adds a secondary learning layer. Better for smaller groups.

Parallel format: Grade 7 students each take a small group of younger students to a different area of the room (or different rooms) and teach simultaneously. This format is more private and reduces performance pressure. Better if the younger class is large or if the teaching space is limited.

The Grade 7 teacher observes without intervening. This is the students' teaching, not a demonstration lesson. The teacher takes quiet observational notes on each student's delivery — these inform the C7 reflection conversation. Notes on: what went well, what stumbled, how the student responded to unexpected moments, what the younger students did with the lesson.

Do not rescue a student whose lesson is stumbling. Let the experience be real. A stumble in Teaching Day is more valuable to the C7 reflection than a smooth lesson that required adult support to stay smooth.

After the lesson: Each student collects a brief verbal response from their younger students before the Grade 7 class leaves the room. The teacher (Grade 7) prompts: "Before we go — ask your students: 'What did you learn? What was the most interesting part?' Listen to the answers. You will need them next class."

Students listen and remember (or note briefly). These responses are the data for C7.

Debrief — 5 minutes (in the corridor or upon immediate return to classroom)

Brief and immediate — before the experience has been processed or rationalised.

"What happened that you didn't plan for? Just one thing each."

Go around quickly — one sentence per student. Do not comment on each answer. Receive them all.

Then: "What worked better than you expected?"

One sentence each.

"Write these two things down right now — before the memory changes. You will use them in C7's reflection."

Students note their immediate responses. Then: travel back to Grade 7 classroom.

Closing — 3 minutes (back in the Grade 7 classroom)

Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader.

Teacher's closing line: "The Sang offering goes outward. Today you sent something outward — you put understanding into other people's minds. That understanding doesn't stop moving. What you gave them today, they may give to someone else. The tradition moves this way: person to person, teacher to student. You are now part of that chain."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Teaching Day is one of the peak experiences of the eight-year curriculum. Treat it accordingly — unhurried arrival at the teaching space, the threshold moment given real time, the observational notes taken carefully. The teacher's observational notes are the most important preparation for C7.

Observational framework — carry this during Teaching Day:

For each student, note:

  • What worked in the lesson (specific — not "it went well" but "the concrete anchor was immediately engaging and the younger students picked it up quickly")
  • What stumbled (specific — "the activation question produced no response and the student didn't have a fallback")
  • How the student responded to the unexpected (did they freeze, adapt, stay present?)
  • What the younger students seemed to take from the lesson (based on their verbal responses and body language)
  • One question to ask this student in C7: a specific question that opens the reflection rather than evaluating the outcome

Some lessons will work beautifully. Some will stumble. Both outcomes are equally valuable for C7's reflection. The stumble is often more instructive than the smooth lesson — do not rescue it, and do not treat it as a failure in C7. Frame it as what it is: data about the gap between intention and effect, which is the unit's essential question.

The younger students' responses ("what did you learn?") are data, not evaluation. If a younger student says something unrelated to the lesson's main idea, that is information — it may mean the lesson's main idea didn't land as the primary takeaway. Help students understand this in C7.

Spiral connection forward: C7's three-part reflection (What I imagined / What happened / What I understand now) is the unit's final portfolio artefact alongside the lesson plan itself. Together, the lesson plan and the reflection constitute the most substantial student-generated documents in the Grade 7 portfolio. Unit 3 (Medicine Buddha) follows — students turn inward again after the outward-facing arc of Units 1 and 2, but with the earned authority of someone who has transmitted the teaching. Medicine Buddha's practice for the healing of others connects directly to Unit 1's empathy work.
