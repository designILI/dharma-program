Six Kinds of Life

Six realm drawings and gesture review → the recognition that ancient cosmological categories describe states of mind from this morning

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleRetrieval + elaboration
Bloom's levelAnalyze
LessonG5.U2.C1

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Six realm drawings and gesture review → the recognition that ancient cosmological categories describe states of mind from this morning

Cognitive Principle Featured: Retrieval + elaboration — the six-realm gesture warmup activates prior knowledge rapidly and at low stakes, creating mental hooks onto which the expanded psychological reading can attach. Meaning builds on already-encoded form.

Bloom's Level: Analyze — students are not merely recalling the six realms (Remember) or explaining their features (Understand) but identifying which realm-quality is active in a given moment of their own experience, naming the state accurately, and distinguishing it from adjacent states.

Prior Class Summary: C5 of Unit 1 was the reflection class — students completed their personal Wheel of Life drawings, compared them to initial sketches, and wrote the three-part reflection (before → what happened → what I know now). The unit closed with the question: "The realms are not only cosmological — they are psychological. You are about to meet yourself in some very uncomfortable mirrors."

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the name, characteristic image, and ruling klesha of each of the six realms. Students will UNDERSTAND that the six realms describe recurring states of mind as well as cosmological planes of existence — and that these states arise and pass multiple times in a single day. Students will BE ABLE TO identify at least one specific moment from their own recent experience that corresponds to a realm-quality and articulate why. Students will FEEL the slightly uncomfortable — and also quietly exciting — recognition that something ancient and cosmological describes something personal and immediate.

Materials Checklist

  • Drawing paper (3 sheets per student — for the three detailed realm drawings begun this class)
  • Pencils, coloured pencils or crayons
  • Student portfolios or folders (to hold the drawings across C1–C4)
  • The Wheel of Life image displayed at the front of the room (carried over from Unit 1)
  • Board or chart paper for the teacher to write realm names during PKA

Plan B: If drawing materials are limited, students can sketch in a dedicated section of their dharma notebooks. Three pages reserved and labelled. If the Wheel of Life display is unavailable, the teacher draws a rough sketch of the outer ring on the board — the visual anchor matters.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader for this class opens the full prayer set. The teacher does not lead — the student leads, the class follows.

Full Grade 5 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

(Food Offering Prayer is closing only — not included in the opening set.)

Dechen Monlam: by this point in the year, most students should be joining from memory. The teacher chants with them, not ahead of them.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher leads the six-realm gesture warmup. This comes from Unit 1, Class 3 — students should have the gestures in their bodies. Move through them quickly and with some energy:

  • Gods: arms relaxed, posture easy, expression satisfied — ease, fullness, slight boredom
  • Demi-gods: fists raised, competitive stance — striving, comparison, agitation
  • Humans: hands resting in lap, posture alert — engaged, suffering-but-continuing
  • Animals: hands toward the ground, head slightly bowed — instinct, immediate need, no reflection
  • Hungry ghosts: hands wide at the stomach, fingers nearly touching at the lips — enormous craving, tiny intake
  • Hell realm: body braced, expression tight — total engulfment, no room to think

Teacher calls the realm name — students gesture. Move quickly: "Gods. Demi-gods. Animals. Hungry ghosts. Humans. Hell." Then scrambled: "Hell. Gods. Animals." Watch for confusion and use it: "Which one do you mix up? That's interesting."

Then the turn: "Which realm have you visited in the last 24 hours? Just gesture — you don't have to say it aloud." Students gesture privately. The teacher pauses. "Look around — there's honesty in this room."

Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

The psychological reading of all six realms — expanded from Unit 1, now with specific mundane examples.

Teacher speaks with specificity and some dry warmth. These are not abstract categories — they are descriptions of ordinary experience.

God realm: "Think about the moment after you get exactly what you wanted. Not the reaching-for — the moment after. You've eaten the thing, won the game, received the praise. You feel full, satisfied, slightly… nothing. Like there's nothing left to want right now. That slight blankness is the god realm. It feels good — until it fades, and then the wanting starts again."

Demi-god realm: "Someone in your class gets something you wanted — a prize, a compliment, an opportunity. You feel something. It isn't quite happiness for them. It might look like happiness on the outside, but inside there's a tightness. That's the demi-god realm. The constant comparison. The sense that someone else's gain is somehow your loss."

Human realm: "You're sitting with a difficult homework problem — or any hard thing. You've been at it for a while. You want to give up. But you don't give up, quite. You sit there, frustrated, and you keep going anyway. That is the human realm. Not despite the suffering — the suffering is part of what makes it the human realm. The frustration and the continuing are both present."

Animal realm: "You're so hungry that you can't think about tomorrow, or your friends, or anything abstract at all — only the immediate need. Or you're so exhausted that survival is the whole of your attention. Pure instinct. No reflection. No asking 'should I?' Just the need and the moving toward it."

Hungry ghost realm: "You finish the meal — the meal you were craving — and immediately you want more. Or it doesn't taste the way you thought it would. The thing you kept reaching for turns out to be slightly less than you imagined. And instead of feeling satisfied, you feel the next craving beginning. We'll spend a full class on this one next time. But notice: most of you have a version of this that you return to regularly."

Hell realm: "Something enrages you — not moderately, but completely. Everything else disappears. You say something you regret. Or you feel something so sharp it's like heat. It doesn't last long — but while it's happening, there's no room for anything else. That's the hell realm. Brief, but total."

Vocabulary Moment: Write on the board: moment-to-moment arising.

"The Buddhist teaching is that our experience is not fixed. We don't live in one realm permanently — we move through these states many times in a single day. You might wake up in the god realm, move into the hungry ghost realm by mid-morning, touch the human realm when you struggle through something difficult in the afternoon. What changes our state? Largely: the three poisons. We'll come back to the antidotes in Class 3. For now: notice the movement."

Body Moment: "Right now — just this moment, not five minutes ago, not this morning — which realm quality is most present? One. Not a story about it. Just the quality." Ten seconds of genuine internal check. Eyes can be open or closed. No sharing required. The teacher does this too.

Emotional Hook: The slightly startling recognition that a cosmological map drawn over two thousand years ago describes something from this morning. Not as metaphor — as description.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Prompt: "Tell your partner: give a specific example from your own life of when you were clearly in one of the six realms — as a state of mind. Be specific enough that your partner can picture it. Not 'I was angry' — what happened, when, what it felt like from inside."

Structure: each partner gets 90 seconds. The teacher listens without intervening.

What to listen for: students who find the psychological reading genuinely recognizable — and students who resist it ("but the realms are real, not just states of mind"). Both responses are valuable. If the second arises, hold it without resolving it: "The Buddhist teaching holds both readings simultaneously. We'll come back to that."

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Students choose three realms to draw in detail — one per page. This is the beginning of the Unit 2 making element, carried forward across C2 and C3, completed in C4.

Each drawing should include:

  1. Image of the beings — what they look like, their posture, their expressions, what surrounds them
  2. Annotation of the characteristic experience — what it feels like from inside this realm
  3. The ruling klesha — which of the three poisons drives this realm (ignorance, desire, or aversion — or a compound)
  4. One personal example — one moment from their own life that touched this realm's quality

Teacher circulates with specific, quiet attention. Ask individual students: "Tell me about this one. What does the hungry ghost look like to you? What are the beings reaching for in your drawing?" Do not evaluate — inquire.

Students who haven't chosen their three realms yet: ask "Which one did you recognize most from the PKA gesture? Start there."

Debrief — 5 minutes

Teacher to the class: "The Wheel is not a judgment — it's a map. Maps don't blame you for where you are. They show you where you are, so you can find the way out. That is the only purpose of this teaching."

Brief pause. Then: "You'll continue these drawings over the next two classes. Next class, we go deep into one realm in particular — the one I said we'd spend a whole class on. The hungry ghost realm. Come ready to look at it honestly."

Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader closes the full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "You've been in six different places in the last twenty-four hours. That's not a problem — that's what it means to be alive inside the Wheel. The question is: can you notice?"

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The three detailed realm drawings begun today are the portfolio artefact for Unit 2. They continue in C2 and C3 and are completed in C4 — make sure students have a consistent place to store them (portfolios or folders kept in the classroom). The hungry ghost class (C2) is the most psychologically intense class in Unit 2 — read it carefully before teaching it. Some students will connect the hungry ghost realm to experiences of addiction, scarcity, or anxiety in their own families. The goal in C1 is recognition at the level of ordinary daily experience — keep the examples mundane and honest, not dramatic. The Body Moment (ten-second internal check) is not a discussion prompt; it is a practice in direct observation. Do not ask students to report on it. The gesture warmup is a retrieval tool — not a performance. If students are uncertain of gestures, that is information: note which realms were least consolidated in Unit 1 and give them slightly more attention in the C1 mini lesson. Spiral connection: Unit 1, C3 introduced all six realms in brief — this class is the transition from panorama to depth.
