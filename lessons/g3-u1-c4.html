Planning Our Release

Making the booklet that will hold the record of our release → intention as the direction of the heart before an act

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleGenerative Processing
Bloom's levelApply
LessonG3.U1.C4

Duration: 40 minutes

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Making the booklet that will hold the record of our release → intention as the direction of the heart before an act

Cognitive Principle Featured: Generative Processing — assembling the reflection journal is not merely craft; it is a form of review that forces students to re-encounter their C2 fish piece and C3 planning notes in a new context (alongside each other, inside a single object that is theirs). The act of placing these elements together, physically, consolidates learning more durably than re-reading alone. The intention-setting moment uses the same principle: writing an intention is not the same as thinking it.

Bloom's Level: Apply — Students are synthesising everything from C1–C3 into a single planned act. The journal is not a record of something past; it is a preparation for something coming. The intention-setting in the Body Moment asks students to do the first act of the release before the release has happened.

Prior Class Summary: C3 covered what Tsethar is — its meaning, traditional basis, and the key principles of how to do it well. Students received the full conceptual framework: merit, dedication of merit, appropriate habitat, the sequence of the release. They wrote initial planning notes answering four questions: what creature, where, what prayers, what we need. They carry the image of both hands at heart level and the phrase "both are changed."

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW the confirmed logistics of the release: the creature, the site, the date, the sequence of events on the day.

Students will UNDERSTAND that an intention is not the same as a plan — it is the direction of the heart before an act begins.

Students will BE ABLE TO assemble and personalise their Tsethar reflection journal, integrating their work from C2 (fish piece) and C3 (planning notes) into a single object.

Students will FEEL anticipation that is grounded rather than excited — the weight of readiness, not eagerness, as they understand that the act is real and nearly here.

Materials Checklist

  • Pre-folded booklets — one per student. Minimum four pages: cover, interior page 1 (for the C2 fish piece), interior page 2 (for C3 planning notes and intention), two blank pages for C5 and C6 entries. Teacher prepares these in advance using folded A4 or A3 paper, stapled at the spine. Simple is correct — this is a working journal, not a display piece.
  • Scissors and glue sticks — for pasting C2 pieces and C3 planning notes into the journal
  • Students' C2 first-person fish pieces (kept by the teacher since C2)
  • Students' C3 planning notes (in their notebooks, or collected by the teacher)
  • Pencils, optional coloured pencils for cover decoration
  • Teacher confirmation of: the specific creature (confirmed), the release site (confirmed), the date of C5 (confirmed), adult supervision arranged

Plan B: If pre-folded booklets are not ready, students fold and assemble their own from a sheet of A3 paper — teacher demonstrates. This adds 3 minutes to the activity; trim the PKA or debrief accordingly. The journal can also be a simple folded single sheet if materials are limited — it needs only to hold the essential elements.

Plan B (logistics): If any logistics for C5 remain unconfirmed — creature, site, date, permission, transport, supervision — resolve them before teaching this class. C4 tells students that everything is confirmed. If anything is not confirmed, this class cannot be taught as written. Do not improvise logistics in front of students.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 3 prayer set, led by the student leader for today:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer (at closing)

Teacher: "Let's begin. Seven-Line prayer together — I hear more voices joining in now. That's the practice working." Chant it together.

(Food Offering Prayer recited at closing, as established from C3.)

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "Last class you answered four planning questions. Let me read back what we know together." Teacher summarises or reads back the main responses from C3 planning notes (either from collected work or from notes made while circulating).

"So: we know the creature. We know the site. We know the prayers. We know what we need to bring." Name each concretely — "We will release [the specific creature confirmed with school admin]. We will go to [the confirmed site]. The date is [the confirmed date]." Students need to hear this as fact, not possibility.

Then: "What's still missing? Is there anything we haven't thought of?" Take 2–3 responses. Address anything real. If nothing is missing, say so: "I think we are actually ready. The planning is done. What's left is the act."

Mini Lesson — 5–7 minutes

Teacher walks through the sequence of C5 — the release day — step by step. This is not a long lesson; it is a briefing. Students should leave knowing exactly what to expect.

"On the day of the release, here is what will happen:

  1. We will travel to the release site together as a class — [name transport method if relevant].
  2. When we arrive, we will settle. A brief prayer to open.
  3. We will approach the water together.
  4. We will hold the container — both hands, at heart level.
  5. Together, we will release the creature. As it enters the water, we begin chanting Om Mani Padme Hung.
  6. We chant for as long as it feels right — I will lead.
  7. Then we close with Ngowa: we dedicate the merit to all beings.
  8. We sit quietly for a few minutes at the site.
  9. Then: we write in our journals — immediately, at the site. You will have 8–10 minutes for this. I will ask you one question; you will write or draw your answer.
  10. Then we return."

"Any questions about the sequence?" Take questions. Answer them concretely.

Vocabulary Moment: "The word intention. An intention is not a plan — a plan is logistical, it describes what you will do. An intention is the direction of the heart: why are you doing it, and for whom. Before any Buddhist practice, we set an intention. We did it at the start of C1 without naming it — 'what would it look like if we acted instead of wished?' — that was an intention taking shape. Today, we name it clearly."

Body Moment: "Sit for a moment. Close your eyes. The release is next class — it is coming. Right now, in your mind, set your intention for it. Don't plan the logistics — just feel the direction: why are you doing this? For whom? What do you want to happen — not to you, but for the creature, for all beings? Hold that."

30 seconds of quiet. Then: "Open your eyes. You've just done the first act of the release. The compass is pointed."

Emotional Hook: Anticipation that has weight rather than excitement. The release is real and coming. The creature exists right now. That is not an abstraction. Students should feel the ground beneath the act — not nervousness, not enthusiasm, but the quality of readiness.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Teacher: "Tell your partner your intention for the release. Just the intention — not the logistics, not what you'll do. The direction of your heart."

Structure: 90 seconds each. Remind students that an intention is often quiet: "If your intention is simple — 'I want this creature to be free' — that is complete. Intentions don't need to be elaborate."

What to listen for: specificity of heart-direction versus generic goodwill. "I want it to be free" is complete; "I want to feel good" is not yet an intention for the creature.

After: "You don't need to share your intention with the whole class. It can stay between you and your partner."

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Assemble the Tsethar reflection journal.

Teacher distributes the pre-folded booklets. "This is your Tsethar reflection journal. By the end of class, it will have your name on the cover, your C2 fish piece inside, your planning notes inside, and your intention written on a page. Then it stays with me until the release — and you'll write in it at the site, and one more time in C6."

Walk students through the assembly:

Step 1 — Cover: "Write your name. Then decorate the cover — this can be simple or elaborate; 5 minutes either way. Think about what image belongs on the cover of a journal about life release."

Step 2 — Paste in the C2 fish piece: Teacher distributes the C2 first-person fish pieces (collected after C2). Students cut or trim and paste them onto Interior Page 1. If the piece is loose paper, glue it in. If it was in a notebook and cannot be removed, students copy a short excerpt (3–5 sentences) into the journal.

Step 3 — Paste in the C3 planning notes: Students paste or copy their four planning questions and answers onto Interior Page 2. Below the planning notes, they write their intention in a few words or sentences. Teacher models briefly: "My intention for this release: to give this creature's life back to it, fully and without keeping anything for myself." Students write their own — no template, just the instruction.

Step 4 — Remaining pages: Two pages stay blank for now. "These are for C5 and C6. Don't write on them yet."

Teacher circulates throughout: "What does your cover tell me about what you think this release is?" "What did you write as your intention?" "How does your fish piece from C2 feel to you now, reading it again?"

Note: the journal stays with the teacher after this class — bring it to C5.

Scaffold: For students who are uncertain what to draw on the cover: "Draw the moment you are most looking forward to in the release. Or draw the creature. Or draw the place where we are going." Any specific image is correct.

Extension: Students who finish early may write on the blank Page 4 (designated for C6): "What do I think I will know after the release that I don't know now?" This will be compared to their actual C6 reflection.

Debrief — 5 minutes

"Hold up your journal for a moment. Look at it." Pause.

"That booklet has your imagining of the fish (C2), your plan for the release (C3), and your intention (today). What's missing?" Pause for responses. "The act itself. And what the act revealed. Those come next class and the one after."

"You are ready. The planning is done. The only thing left is to do it."

"One last question before we close: what do you think will surprise you?"

Take 2–3 responses. Receive each one without commentary.

Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full prayer set:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer

Teacher's closing line: "The journals stay with me. I'll bring them to the release. Carry your intention — it's already set."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author):

Carry forward: The assembled reflection journals travel to C5 with the teacher — do not return them to students before the release. They write in them immediately after the release, at the site. Bring pencils for each student to C5. After C6, the completed journals become the portfolio artefact for Unit 1.

Final logistics confirmation before C5: This class is the last opportunity to resolve any outstanding logistics before the release. Creature confirmed? Yes/No. Transport confirmed? Yes/No. Release site accessible and appropriate? Yes/No. Permission from school administration in writing? Yes/No. Adult supervision arranged? Yes/No. Date confirmed with the school schedule? Yes/No. If any of these is No, resolve it before C5. There is no improvising a life release.

Spiral connections: The intention-setting practice introduced today is a precursor to the formal "setting of motivation" (Tibetan: kün long) that students will encounter more formally in Grade 6 when they begin studying the six paramitas. In Grade 3, name it simply as "the direction of the heart." The journal format — imagining first, acting next, reflecting last — is the reflection structure named explicitly in C6 Curriculum guidance as one students will use again in Grade 5 and Grade 7.
