Presenting the Stupa

Each student presents their completed stupa → the act of transmission — passing living understanding from one person to others through a held object and a spoken explanation

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleTeaching as the highest form of learning
Bloom's levelCreate / Evaluate
LessonG8.U3.C4

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Each student presents their completed stupa → the act of transmission — passing living understanding from one person to others through a held object and a spoken explanation

Cognitive Principle Featured: Teaching as the highest form of learning — presenting one's understanding in a form that can be received by others requires a quality of clarity and integration that silent or private understanding does not demand; holding the stupa and speaking about it completes the encoding that building began

Bloom's Level: Create / Evaluate — students present and explain an original artefact encoding complex religious and philosophical content; the group evaluates through genuine questions

Prior Class Summary: C3 was the full building session — 35 minutes of sustained, largely silent making. Students completed their model stupas. They carry the instruction: "Look at it. Read it. Know what you have made." They have been asked to practise the presentation between classes.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW what is meant by transmission in the Buddhist sense — the passing of living understanding from one being to another, not as information transfer but as the sparking of recognition Students will UNDERSTAND that presenting the stupa to the group is an act of transmission — the same quality of intention as Grade 7's Teaching Day, but now directed not at a younger age group but at peers who share the same knowledge base Students will BE ABLE TO hold their stupa and explain each element from base to pinnacle — name, traditional meaning, and at least one specific connection to the eight-year curriculum — with clarity and without reading directly from notes Students will FEEL the specific quality of being fully seen in an act of knowledge: presenting something made with sustained attention, in a room that understands its meaning, and being met with genuine questions rather than polite silence

Materials Checklist

  • Students' completed stupa models (brought to class — confirm with students in advance; if stored at school, have them collected before the opening prayer)
  • A central surface (a table, a cloth on the floor, or a shelf) where all stupas can be displayed together during the opening prayer and throughout the class — large enough to hold one stupa per student
  • If any students need to paint or finish their model before presenting: have paint and brushes available for the first 5 minutes before the opening, not during the class time
  • Plan B: If a model was damaged or broken between C3 and C4, the student presents from their design sketch and describes what the completed model would have shown — the knowledge is what is being transmitted, not the physical perfection of the object; the teacher says simply: "The knowledge is in you. Present from that."

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Before the opening prayer — students bring their stupas and place them on the central surface. The stupas are present in the room during the entire session, including the opening prayer.

Full Grade 8 prayer set, led by the student leader of the day. Sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Tsog Due pa. Student leader conducts.

After the closing of the opening prayer — before the PKA — the teacher speaks: "We are about to present something we have made. This is not a show-and-tell — it is a transmission. Bring the same quality of intention you brought to Teaching Day in Grade 7. The difference: then you were transmitting to younger students who did not yet have what you had. Today you are transmitting to people who have been on the same path. They will notice if you are precise. They will notice if you are not."

No PKA today — the stupas are already in the room. The presence of the stupas is the prior knowledge activation.

Mini Lesson — 3 minutes

Frame the presentation briefly and clearly.

"Each of you will come forward — or present from your seat — holding your stupa. You will explain it from base to pinnacle. For each element: its name, its meaning in the tradition, and one connection to something specific from this curriculum — from any grade. You have eight years of material. The connection should be specific. 'The base represents the four immeasurables' is the traditional meaning — the curriculum connection is: 'which grade first gave me these, and what did I do with them there?'"

"After each presentation: two minutes for the group to ask one question about a specific element. The question should be genuine — something you actually want to understand better — not an affirmation."

Vocabulary Moment: "The word transmission — in the Buddhist sense: the passing of living understanding from one being to another. Not the transfer of information, which any book can do. The sparking of recognition — helping another being see what you have seen, so that their own seeing is activated. When Milarepa received transmission from Marpa, it was not because Marpa gave him facts about the nature of mind. It was because Marpa transmitted the living quality of understanding to a student who had prepared, through years of practice, to receive it. When you present your stupa today — you are attempting that. Not because you are enlightened, but because you have genuinely understood something. Genuine understanding can be transmitted. That is what today asks for."

Body Moment: The teacher holds an example stupa (or gestures to one nearby) — demonstrating the holding posture: both hands, at chest height, slightly tilted so the group can see the form. "Hold it so everyone can see it. Your hands are part of the transmission — you built this. The marks of your hands are in it. Hold it with that."

Emotional Hook: The specific experience of being fully present with what you have made and what you know, in a room that shares the context to receive it.

Activity — 30 minutes

The presentations.

Each student presents in turn. The teacher holds time — roughly 2–3 minutes per student's presentation, 1–2 minutes for a question from the group. With the full class, the timing must be tracked carefully: adjust question time if needed to ensure everyone presents.

Teacher's role during presentations:

  • Hold the space — no evaluation of the content during the presentation; receive each explanation as a transmission
  • After each presentation, invite one question from the room: "One question — about a specific element. What do you genuinely want to understand better?"
  • If a question is an affirmation ("I thought it was really good"), redirect gently: "Can you turn that into a question? Something you actually want to know?"
  • If a student's explanation of an element is unclear or incomplete, the teacher may ask a clarifying question — not as correction but as an invitation to elaborate: "Tell me more about what you said about the harmika — what does it mean to you, specifically?"
  • If a student stumbles or goes blank: "Take your time. Start at the base. What does the base mean?" Simple re-anchoring. Do not rush.

After all presentations:

If time allows — 3–4 minutes — the teacher invites the group to look at the collection of stupas together, all on the central surface:

"Look at them all together. Each one is different — different type, different proportion, different maker's hands. Each one encodes the same thing. What do you notice?"

Take two or three observations. No extended discussion — just seeing.

Debrief — 3 minutes

"In the Himalayan Buddhist tradition, a stupa is consecrated before it is placed — prayers are said, relics are placed inside, the object is formally dedicated to the benefit of all beings. The consecration is not magic — it is the explicit recognition that this object carries meaning and is being offered for the use of others."

"Your stupas are consecrated by your understanding of what they mean. That is enough. You built them knowing what they encode. You explained them to this room. The understanding has moved from your minds into your hands and now into this space. That is transmission."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full Grade 8 prayer set. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer added at close. The stupas remain on the central surface during the closing prayer.

Teacher's closing line — for Unit 3 as a whole, and for the first three units of Grade 8: "Three units. The nature of mind described. The nature of transformation demonstrated. The mind made visible in form. The year is turning toward its final movement."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: Stupas are taken home as the Unit 3 portfolio artefact — each one is unique and encodes its maker's understanding. Before they leave: photograph each stupa if possible (a simple photograph with the student's name noted), so the portfolio record is preserved even after the physical object goes home. Together with the Unit 1 philosophical argument, the Unit 2 final "Can anyone change?" response with its C1 before-position, and the Unit 1 Twelve Links chain drawing, these three artefacts are the first movement of the Grade 8 portfolio. Unit 4 begins immediately: the full Pajapati story. Students have carried her name since Grade 2. They encountered a partial telling in Grade 6. The full arc — seven classes — is now given. Read the Unit 4 brief in full before teaching U4.C1. The opening of U4.C1 is one of the most significant moments in the curriculum: the teacher does not open with "today we are studying Pajapati." The teacher opens with the question: "You have been carrying a name for six years. I am going to tell you who she was." Know the full story before you say that sentence. The weight of the moment depends on the teacher's preparation, not on any technique.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child presented their completed stupa to the class — holding it and explaining each element from base to pinnacle, with its traditional meaning and its connection to what they have studied over eight years of the Dharma curriculum. The stupa is coming home as one of their Grade 8 portfolio pieces. You can ask them to show it to you and explain it — from base to pinnacle, as they explained it to the class. Ask them: 'What does the pinnacle mean? What curriculum connection surprised you most?'"
