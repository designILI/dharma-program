Dance as Teaching

A single deliberate movement → the body as a vehicle for Dharma teaching

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleEmbodied transfer
Bloom's levelApply
LessonG6.U4.C1

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): A single deliberate movement → the body as a vehicle for Dharma teaching

Cognitive Principle Featured: Embodied transfer — when students learn that a body in motion can carry a teaching they have already received in conceptual form, the learning shifts registers. The same knowledge inhabits a different medium, and the transfer deepens both.

Bloom's Level: Apply — students begin applying their existing knowledge of the Dharma figures (Yama, Tara, Padmasambhava) to a new medium: the body in motion.

Prior Class Summary: C4 of Unit 3 was the "Our Taras" sharing — students drew their personal Tara figure and spoke their connection to the class. They completed a full unit. They carry the warmth and relief of a unit's arrival and the Dolma prayer newly settled into the prayer set.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW what Cham is — its traditional context, who performs it, and when it is performed. Students will UNDERSTAND that Cham is not performance but practice — the body trained to embody specific figures and qualities becomes a vehicle for the Dharma, and the dance is simultaneously ritual, teaching, and meditation in motion. Students will BE ABLE TO attempt one basic Cham movement; write a portfolio entry naming the figure they most want to embody and why. Students will FEEL the difference that intentional, formal movement produces in the body — specifically the quality of attention that deliberateness creates.

Materials Checklist

  • Clear floor space for the body moment (desks pushed to the sides or class held in a larger space — arrange before students arrive)
  • Images of Cham — printed or displayed: masked dancers, festival context, individual Cham figures (Yama, Citipati, Mahakala preferred for this class)
  • Video of a real Cham performance if available — ideally 3–5 minutes, from a reputable monastery source. Preview the clip before class to confirm it is appropriate and high quality.
  • Portfolio notebooks (one per student)
  • Pencils
  • Plan B: If video is unavailable, images alone with narrated description of the movement is sufficient. If the teacher does not know Cham movements from personal practice, seek guidance from the school's resident teacher or a community member with knowledge of the tradition before this class — this consultation should happen before C1, not after.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set including Dolma: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Food Offering Prayer (closing only)

Student leader leads. After the prayers, the teacher speaks: "This year we've studied death, the Bardo, rebirth, Tara. All with words and maps and drawings and discussion. Today: what if the body could be the teaching? What if instead of reading about Yama, the Lord of Death — you wore him?"

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

Teacher: "What if the body could be the teaching? What would that mean? Has any of you ever felt that your body understood something before your mind had words for it?"

Take three or four responses — these may be from sport, from dance, from a moment of fear, from grief. Receive them. Do not rush to the content; the question is worth sitting with briefly.

Then: "That is what Cham is. Let me show you."

Mini Lesson — 15 minutes

What Cham is:

Cham (ཆམ) is a form of sacred masked dance performed in Tibetan Buddhist monasteries. It is not entertainment. It has been part of the tradition for centuries. It is performed at major festivals — Losar (Tibetan New Year), Saga Dawa (the celebration of the Buddha's birth, enlightenment, and death), and other ceremonies specific to particular lineages and communities.

Those who perform Cham — traditionally, trained monks — spend years learning the movements and their meanings. Each step, each turn, each gesture of the hand corresponds to a specific teaching or intention. Nothing is casual.

Who the figures are:

Show images as each figure is named:

  • Yama — the bull-headed Lord of Death, holding the Wheel of Life. Students have spent six classes with Yama and the Wheel. Now they see him danced.
  • Citipati — the skeleton dancers. Two figures in full skeleton costume, moving with strange lightness. They are the memento mori — the reminder of death — but they dance, because even death is part of the Dharma's completeness.
  • Mahakala — the Great Black One, a wrathful protector figure, many-armed. He protects the Dharma and the community.
  • Guru Rinpoche (Padmasambhava) — sometimes present in Cham as the teacher who brought the Dharma to Tibet.
  • Various Dharma protectors — animal-headed figures who protect the ceremony.

What the dance does:

Cham is simultaneously several things: a ritual performance (it purifies the environment, protects the community, marks a sacred time); a teaching (each figure and movement embodies a principle students can now recognise); and a meditation (the dancer holds the figure's quality throughout the dance while remaining aware that they are the dancer — a form of double awareness).

Show the video clip if available. Watch without commentary. Afterward: "What did you notice? What did you recognise?"

Key connection to the year:

"You have been studying the figures of this tradition all year — the six realms, Yama holding the Wheel, Tara at the threshold, Padmasambhava in the lineage. Cham puts those figures in a body. The dancer who wears the Yama mask is not pretending to be Yama — they are invoking the quality that Yama represents: the inescapable truth of impermanence. The body becomes the teaching."

Vocabulary Moment: Write on the board: cham (ཆམ) — sacred masked dance. Also: drubchen (སྒྲུབ་ཆེན) — a great accomplishment ceremony, often including extended ritual practice, of which Cham is frequently part. "The dance is practice. The dancer is practising. That is the distinction that matters."

Body Moment:

Teacher demonstrates one simple Cham movement — the basic deliberate step used in Cham: feet parallel, weight shifting fully before each step is placed, body upright, nothing casual. Or: a simple protective hand gesture (palm raised, facing out) from the Cham tradition.

Students stand and try. Teacher circulates. "Feel how the body changes when the movement is deliberate, formal, meaningful — rather than casual. This is not walking to get somewhere. This is walking as an act."

One round of the movement together. Thirty seconds of stillness afterward. "What changed?"

Emotional Hook: The recognition that Yama — a figure students have spent weeks with in the Bardo material — can be worn, embodied, danced. The same knowledge, different register. Something becomes real in a new way.

Turn and Talk — 4 minutes

"Tell your partner: what figure from this year's study would you most want to embody in the dance — and why? What do you think it would feel like to wear that figure's mask?"

Pairs work. What to listen for: specificity (not just "Yama" but "Yama because..."); students who choose unexpected figures; students who feel some resistance to embodying certain figures. All of this is useful data for the mask-making choice in C2.

Brief whole-group share: two or three pairs.

Activity — 8 minutes

Students write in their portfolio: three things.

  1. What Cham is and why it exists — in their own words.
  2. Which figure they most want to embody in the class performance — and why.
  3. What they think it would feel like to wear that figure's mask during the performance.

This is the first of the Unit 4 portfolio entries. It is also a diagnostic for C2: the teacher reads these before the next class to understand which figures students are gravitating toward and whether any students seem uncertain.

Debrief — 4 minutes

Teacher: "Next class: you will learn what each Cham figure represents and confirm which one you will embody. The class after that: you will learn the movements. Then you will make your masks. And in the final class of Grade 6 — you will perform. That performance is not a classroom exercise. It is a ceremony. Come to each class ready to be in your body."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set including Dolma. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer at close.

Teacher's closing line: "The tradition has always known that the body can carry what the mind has learned. That is what we are doing for the rest of this year."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The body moment in C1 (the Cham movement demonstration) sets the physical and intentional tone for the entire unit. If the teacher is uncertain of the correct movement, the resident teacher consultation must happen before C1 — not as a courtesy but as a necessity. The performance in C5 should be treated as real: if possible, invite an audience (another class, parents, school staff, the resident teacher). The more genuine the performance stakes, the more engaged the preparation. Read the portfolio entries before C2 — students' figure choices and reasoning will inform how the C2 lesson proceeds. Physical space: the room needs to be rearranged before C3 (movement class) and before C5 (performance). Begin coordinating now. Spiral connections: Yama — encountered in the Bardo material (G6.U2) and on the Wheel of Life (Grade 5) — is now available as a dancer's figure. This is a significant moment of integration. The Cham figures connect directly to the Dharma protectors and lineage figures students have encountered at multiple grade levels.
