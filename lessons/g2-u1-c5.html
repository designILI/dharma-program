The Third Ring (Animals)

Drawing animals in the third ring of the diagram → Discovering that the wish for wellbeing is not only for humans

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleCognitive dissonance as productive learning
Bloom's levelApply / early Analyze. Students apply the meditation structure they know to a non-human subject
LessonG2.U1.C5

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Drawing animals in the third ring of the diagram → Discovering that the wish for wellbeing is not only for humans

Cognitive Principle Featured: Cognitive dissonance as productive learning — most students at age 7–8 have never been asked to formally include animals in their circle of moral care, or to notice that animals feel fear and pain in the same way humans do. The gentle disruption of Strictly No Elephants — a story in which the "who belongs?" question is asked directly — creates the opening for a genuine conceptual shift. Productive dissonance prompts students to revise their implicit boundary of "who counts."

Bloom's Level: Apply / early Analyze. Students apply the meditation structure they know to a non-human subject — itself a meaningful act of application. Early analysis begins when a student asks the natural question: "If animals want to be okay too, what else might be outside my circle that I haven't thought about?" This is the bridge to C6.

Prior Class Summary: In C4, students expanded the meditation to friends and neighbours — people they see regularly but hadn't explicitly wished well for. They added a second ring to their diagram. The meditation was slightly harder than C3's, and most students noticed the difference in feeling, which is exactly right.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW that animals — birds, insects, fish, dogs, wild creatures — also experience something like wanting to be safe, fed, and free from pain, and that this is why the circle of compassion extends to them.

Students will UNDERSTAND that the practice of sending a wish does not require words or language, because what is being sent is a feeling — an image of the animal, well and unafraid — rather than a sentence.

Students will BE ABLE TO extend the guided meditation to an animal of their choosing and add drawings (not names) to the third ring of their diagram; some will feel genuine concern for the animal they picture; some will feel curious or uncertain; both are fine.

Students will FEEL the surprise of discovering that they actually can feel something for a creature they've never considered before — and that the feeling, however small, is real.

Materials Checklist

  • Strictly No Elephants by Lisa Mantchev — one copy for the teacher to read aloud
  • Concentric circles diagrams from C4 (returned by teacher)
  • Pencils and coloured pencils (coloured pencils especially useful today — students draw animals rather than write names)
  • Tibetan singing bowl or bell — Plan B: teacher's audible breath as opening/closing signal
  • Optional: a small natural object placed at the front of the room — a feather, a stone, a dried leaf, a flower — something from the non-human world. Not explained; just present

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader leads the full Grade 2 prayer set:

  1. Kyap Dro
  2. Om Mani Padme Hung (7 times)
  3. Manjushri Prayer
  4. Sem kye — teacher leads; most students are beginning to join at least partway
  5. Ngowa — teacher leads at closing (see Closing section)

Before the prayers: "Five classes in, and you are all leading and joining better every week. Today we go somewhere new — past people entirely. But first, let's open with what we know."

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

"Who remembers the two rings you have in your diagram so far? Tell me what they are."

Take responses: centre (family) and second ring (friends/neighbours).

"Right. Two rings. Two different kinds of wishes — one very close, one a little further out. The meditation got slightly harder as we moved out, and most of you noticed that. That noticing is the practice."

"Now I want to ask you something before we read today: who is the furthest kind of person in your second ring? Not your closest friend — your furthest second-ring person. The one it took the most effort to include."

Take one or two responses.

"Now: is there any creature — not a person — that you sometimes think about? An animal you've seen, worried about, or wondered about?"

Take responses without directing. Note who offers animals and what kinds — pets, stray dogs, birds, insects. All are valid.

"Hold that creature in the back of your mind. We'll come back to it."

Mini Lesson — 10 minutes

*Read-aloud: Strictly No Elephants by Lisa Mantchev*

"Before the meditation today, I'm going to read you a story. The boy in this story has a pet that doesn't fit in — a pet that the Pet Club says isn't allowed. Listen for one question: who gets to belong?"

Read Strictly No Elephants aloud in full, slowly, with the illustrations shown. Do not pause for comprehension questions during the reading.

After the final page, close the book. One moment of silence.

"Who got to belong, in the end?"

Take responses. Students will likely say: everyone. The boy and his elephant. All kinds of pets.

"Why did the boy make a new club instead of just walking away?"

Take responses. Let students wrestle with it briefly: because he wanted to include the ones who were left out. Because leaving someone out felt wrong.

"Now here's the question I want you to sit with: the boy's elephant — did the elephant know it was being excluded? Did it understand the sign on the door?"

Pause. Students may say no — the elephant didn't understand language.

"Probably not. The elephant didn't read the sign. But the elephant still felt something when it was turned away. The story shows us that. The elephant leans against the boy. It wants to be with him. It's not happy being excluded — even if it can't say so."

"Animals don't use words. But they want to be okay. They want to feel safe. They want to be with the ones they trust. They don't want to suffer. That is enough to be in the circle."

Vocabulary Moment: "'Sentient being' — this is a Tibetan Buddhist term that means any being that can feel. Feel pain. Feel fear. Feel something like comfort or peace. Humans are sentient beings. Animals are sentient beings. When the mantra and the meditation talk about 'all beings,' this is what they mean. Today, your third ring is for sentient beings who are not human."

Body Moment: "Close your eyes for five seconds. Picture the animal you thought of at the beginning of class. See it — its body, how it moves, where it is right now. Open your eyes." Do not discuss — move directly to Turn and Talk.

Emotional Hook: The moment in the book when the elephant leans against the boy at the door. Read that moment slowly.

Turn and Talk — 3 minutes

"Turn to the person next to you. Tell them: what animal came to your mind just now, and where is it — where do you picture it being? In the school garden? In a river? At someone's house? In a cage?"

Allow two minutes. Listen for the range: pets, birds, stray animals, wild animals, insects. All of these are correct — and the variety is the point.

What to listen for: Students who choose insects or very small creatures — spiders, ants, worms. If this comes up in sharing, amplify it: "Even that small? Does a spider want to be okay?" Let the class hold the question.

Activity — 18 minutes

Part 1 — Guided Meditation: The Third Ring (5 minutes)

Ring the bell (or audible breath). Students close eyes or look at their hands.

Teacher reads aloud, slowly and clearly. This can be read verbatim.

"Settle your body. Let it be heavy and still."

Pause 5 seconds.

"One slow breath in. Let it go."

Pause 5 seconds.

"Go back to the centre — find the family member you held in your mind in C3. Just a glimpse. You don't need to stay."

Pause 6 seconds.

"Step outward — find your second ring. The friend, the classmate, the neighbour. See their face for a moment."

Pause 6 seconds.

"Good. Both rings are there. Both wishes went out. Now go further."

Pause 5 seconds.

"Step past the people you know. Go out to the edge of what you can see from here — out the window, down the path, past the gate."

Pause 5 seconds.

"Think of an animal. Any animal nearby. A bird on the roof right now. A dog sleeping in the sun. The insects moving through the school garden — the ones you walk past every day without thinking. Choose one."

Pause 10 seconds.

"Don't use words for this one. Just picture the animal. See its body. See how it moves, or rests, or feeds. Where is it right now?"

Pause 8 seconds.

"Now: does it want to be okay? Does it want to feel safe — unafraid, not in pain, not hungry? You don't need to know the answer for certain. Just ask yourself: does it want that?"

Pause 8 seconds.

"Send it something — not words, because it doesn't use words. Send it an image. The image of that animal, safe, comfortable, free. Not afraid. Well."

Pause 10 seconds.

"Hold that image. Let it be steady."

Pause 10 seconds.

"Before you open your eyes — notice: what is bigger than this ring? If there are birds outside and dogs in the village and insects in the garden — what is even further out than those?"

Let the question hang, unanswered, for 8 seconds.

"Take one breath. Open your eyes."

Ring bell or exhale.

Thirty seconds of quiet.

"That question you just heard — 'what is bigger than this ring?' — we will answer that next class."

Part 2 — Draw the third ring (10 minutes)

Return diagram sheets. "Open to the third ring. For this one — draw. Don't write names. Draw the animals you thought of during the meditation, or any animal you want to include in your circle. They can be ones you know or ones you've never seen. Draw them in the ring."

Students draw. Coloured pencils encouraged. The room should feel like quiet absorption. The teacher circulates without interrupting unless a student is stuck.

Scaffold for students unsure what to draw: "Draw one animal you see near this school. One bird. One insect. That's enough."

Extension: Students who finish quickly can draw a small continuous line of animals around the outer edge of the third ring — like a border of creatures. Or they may write the word "sentient beings" around the ring in any language they choose.

The teacher collects diagrams at the end of class.

Debrief — 5 minutes

"Look at the third ring of your diagram. You put animals in it. Animals that can't read, can't say thank you, can't know that you thought of them."

"Does that change whether the wish is worth sending?"

Take responses. Allow the question to sit without a neat answer.

"The meditation asked: what is bigger than this ring? I didn't answer it. I won't yet. But I want you to go home today and look at one creature — a bird, an insect, whatever you see — and just think: is that being in my circle?"

"Next class, we find out what's in the outermost ring."

Closing — 3 minutes

The student leader leads:

  1. Kyap Dro
  2. Om Mani Padme Hung (7 times)
  3. Manjushri Prayer
  4. Sem kye
  5. Ngowa — teacher leads

After closing: "The mantra you just chanted — Om Mani Padme Hung — is sent out for all beings. Not just the people in this room. Every creature. It has always been that wide. Now you know what that means from the inside."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The question left open at the end of the meditation — "what is bigger than this ring?" — is the bridge to C6's "all beings" ring. Do not answer it today. Let it travel home with students. In C6, open by asking: "Did anyone notice an animal this week and wonder about it?" Even one student who did is enough. The key conceptual shift in this lesson is from directed compassion (toward someone with a face and a name) to non-verbal, image-based compassion — a felt image of a creature, well and unafraid, rather than a verbal wish. This is a genuine pedagogical step toward the vast, non-pictorial compassion of C6. Do not rush it. The Strictly No Elephants read-aloud is doing real philosophical work — the question "who gets to belong?" is not cute; it is the unit's central question applied to a new domain. Read it slowly. Spiral connection: Grade 1 (the mantra as sent to "all beings" — students may recall hearing this language in Grade 1 but not having a felt sense of what it meant). This lesson begins to build that felt sense.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child extended their circle of care to animals — using a short meditation to picture one creature and send it a wordless wish for safety and wellbeing. We read a book called Strictly No Elephants, about who gets to belong. You can ask them: What animal did you think of? Do you think animals want to be okay? What's bigger than that ring?"
