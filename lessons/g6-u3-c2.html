Grade 6  ·  Unit 3  ·  Class 2

Two Faces of Compassion

A walk across the room → what it costs a human being to keep going when everything says stop

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleNarrative as conceptual anchor
Bloom's levelAnalyze
LessonG6.U3.C2

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): A walk across the room → what it costs a human being to keep going when everything says stop

Cognitive Principle Featured: Narrative as conceptual anchor — the story of Pajapati, told with enough historical detail that it becomes specific and real, anchors the abstract distinction between archetype and history in a lived example. Students remember particular people more durably than general principles.

Bloom's Level: Analyze — students examine the relationship between archetype (Tara) and history (Pajapati), asking what each one offers that the other cannot. They are not being asked to prefer one; they are being asked to see why both are necessary.

Prior Class Summary: C1 introduced Tara — her origin story, the two forms (White and Green), the vow to be present at every threshold of difficulty in female form. Students took both postures in their bodies and sketched both figures. They carry the question: what is the vow you are building?

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW who Pajapati was — her relationship to the Buddha, the early life she lived, the loss she faced, and the structural obstacle that stood between her and the practice she sought — with enough historical weight to hold the name fully. Students will UNDERSTAND the distinction between archetype and history: that Tara makes the quality of swift compassion graspable and portable, while Pajapati demonstrates that a human being actually embodied that quality in historical time. Students will BE ABLE TO write a journal entry that holds both Tara and Pajapati, naming what each one offers that the other does not. Students will FEEL the specific weight of a name they have carried since Grade 2 finally arriving with real historical particularity — a person, a life, a constraint, and an unfinished story held open for Grade 8.

Materials Checklist

  • Portfolio notebooks or lined writing paper (one per student)
  • Pencils
  • Clear floor space for the walking body moment (desks arranged so students can walk the length of the room — rearrange before class begins)
  • Written reference on the board: Pajapati Gotami and lay practitioner
  • Plan B: If floor space cannot be cleared, the walking moment can be adapted — students walk in place, slowly and deliberately, for ninety seconds, with the same instruction and reflection. This preserves the embodied quality, though with less spatial impact.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set (Dolma not yet introduced — that arrives in C3): Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

Student leader leads the set. The teacher holds the opening with full attention.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

Teacher: "Last class, Tara made a vow — to attain enlightenment in female form and to be present at every threshold of difficulty, forever, until samsara is emptied. Today: what does that vow look like when a human being actually carries it out? Not a bodhisattva on a lotus — a woman, in history, on foot."

Take two or three responses — students may name historical figures they know, or say they are not sure. Receive all of it.

Then: "There is one woman in the tradition whose story the curriculum has been holding for you since Grade 2. Today it arrives. Her name is Pajapati Gotami."

Mini Lesson — 15 minutes

Part 1 — Brief Tara review (3 minutes):

Tara is an archetype. The word archetype means a symbolic figure that makes a quality graspable — it gives a face, a posture, a name to something that would otherwise remain abstract. The Tara archetype makes the quality of swift, active compassion portable across centuries and cultures. When you invoke Tara, you are not calling on an external power — you are calling the quality into presence. Archetypes are not less real than historical figures; they operate at a different level. They hold a truth that does not depend on a single time or place.

Part 2 — Pajapati as historical Tara-figure (12 minutes):

Now: what does it look like when a human being actually embodies that quality — not through divine birth, but through human life, with grief and loss and perseverance?

The teacher tells the story, slowly, with full presence. Do not rush it.

Who was Pajapati? She was born into a noble family — sister to Maya, the woman who became the Buddha's mother. When Maya died days after Siddhartha was born, Pajapati raised him as her own. She loved him fully. When Siddhartha grew up and left to become the wandering teacher who would transform the world, Pajapati remained — first as wife, then as widow. Her husband died. She was left with the question that had always been with her: how to live wisely, how to practise deeply, in a world that had not yet made room for that. She could hear the teaching. She could follow the precepts of a lay practitioner. But the deepest form of practice — full ordination — was not yet available to women. She wanted what the tradition had to offer in its fullest form. The world was not yet ready to give it. That is where we leave her today. What she did next — what she asked for, how many times she was refused, who finally helped, and what she attained — that story belongs to Grade 8.

After the story — do not immediately move on. Let it sit for a few seconds.

Then: "Tara shows what is possible — she is the symbol of swift compassion in female form, present at every threshold. Pajapati shows that it is what a real human life actually looks like — grief, love, loss, and a question that would not leave her."

Vocabulary Moment: Write on the board: lay practitioner. "A lay practitioner is someone who follows the Buddhist teachings and observes ethical precepts but lives in the world rather than as a monastic renunciant. Pajapati was a lay practitioner. She meditated. She studied. She kept the precepts. The tradition also offered a deeper form of commitment — full ordination as a nun, with vows and a life entirely given to practice. That path was not yet open to her. Not because of who she was, but because of the world she lived in. What she did with that constraint is the story that belongs to Grade 8."

Body Moment:

"Stand up. Space yourselves so you can walk to the back of the room."

Students stand. The teacher does not perform energy or urgency here — the instruction is quiet, deliberate.

"Walk to the back of the room and return. Do not rush. As you walk — even this short distance — hold this: Pajapati faced something the world was not yet ready to give her. She stayed with the question anyway. She kept practising. What is it like to keep moving toward something when the path is not yet open?"

Students walk the length of the room and return. They sit.

"What did you feel?"

Take three or four responses. Receive them without sorting.

Emotional Hook: The cumulative weight of the name Pajapati — held since Grade 2, given its first real content in G6.U2.C2, now arriving with full historical gravity. For students who have carried this name for four years without knowing her story, today is the moment of arrival. The teacher does not point at this — it will happen on its own. For female students in particular, both the structural constraint Pajapati faced and the unfinished quality of her story — held open for Grade 8 — may carry specific weight. Receive what comes.

Turn and Talk — 4 minutes

"Tell your partner: what do you think it felt like for Pajapati to want to practise fully — and to live in a world that had not yet made room for it? What do you think kept her going?"

Pairs work. This is an interpretive question; there is no single correct answer. What to listen for: students who distinguish between external motivation (wanting ordination) and internal motivation (love, conviction, something she could not abandon); students who express frustration or recognition at the structural constraint Pajapati faced and what it reveals about the tradition; students who connect Pajapati's persistence to something in their own experience.

One or two pairs share briefly.

Activity — 10 minutes

Students write a journal entry in their portfolios titled: "Tara and Pajapati — archetype and history."

Prompt: What does each one offer that the other doesn't? Why do we need both?

This is a portfolio piece — it will be collected. The teacher circulates, asking: "Which one do you find more powerful right now? Why do you think that is?" These questions are not rhetorical — the answers are data.

Students who finish early: "Add one more paragraph: what do you think Pajapati's early life — raising someone else's child, loving him fully, watching him leave — prepared her for? What did those experiences give her?"

Debrief — 4 minutes

Teacher: "Pajapati is not a historical footnote. She is a real person who lived a real life, faced a real obstacle, and did not let it be the end of her story. This curriculum has been holding her name since you were seven years old. You have been ready for longer than you knew. Her full story — what she asked for, how many times she was refused, who helped, and what she attained — that comes in Grade 8. When it arrives, you will understand it differently because you have been carrying her name for so long."

Do not give more of the story now. If students ask: "Her full story is Grade 8. I am not holding it back because it is too difficult — I am holding it because you will meet it better when you have had more time. That is how the curriculum works."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set — with Food Offering Prayer at close. Student leader.

Teacher's closing line: "Tara is the archetype — she shows us what is possible. Pajapati is the history — she shows us it has been done. Both of these are true at the same time."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The Pajapati journal entry (Tara + Pajapati, archetype + history) is a portfolio piece — collect it before or after class, or ensure students know it will be held. This is one of four Grade 6 portfolio artefacts. The Pajapati section of this lesson covers her early life and the historical constraint she faced — and stops there. The three refusals, Ananda's intervention, her ordination, her enlightenment, and her death are all reserved for Grade 8 Unit 4 (G8.U4.C1–C7), where students encounter the complete story after six years of carrying her name. Do not add any of this material here, even if students ask. If a student asks what happened, the correct response is: "That's the right question to carry. You'll hear it in Grade 8." If a student knows more of the story from family or personal knowledge, receive it warmly and say: "Yes — you already know some of her story. Hold that. It will matter in Grade 8." Female students note: This class is particularly significant for female students. Observe during the body moment, the journal activity, and the debrief — note in Your Reflection which students are most moved by the Pajapati material. This is not sentiment; it is important pedagogical data for the teachers who will receive these students in Grades 7 and 8. C3 introduces the Dolma prayer — it should feel like an arrival after the weight of C1 and C2. The floor must be clear for the walking body moment; arrange the room before students arrive. Spiral connections: Pajapati's name was first introduced in Grade 2 and held across the curriculum. The theme of perseverance under refusal connects to the bodhisattva path material from Grade 5. The ordination consequence — a single act that changes the entire tradition — connects to the discussion of karmic momentum from G6.U2.

Parent Connection

"This week in Dharma, your child began to learn who Pajapati Gotami was — the Buddha's foster mother, a woman of great love and perseverance who faced a structural obstacle in her pursuit of full practice. They connected her story to the figure of Tara through the idea of archetype and history: Tara as the symbol of swift compassion, Pajapati as a historical woman who embodied that quality in her own way. They wrote a journal entry exploring the difference. You can ask them: 'What did Pajapati want, and what stood in her way? What does it mean that both Tara and Pajapati are part of the same teaching?'"
