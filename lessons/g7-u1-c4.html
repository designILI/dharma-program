Researching Responses

Three specific frameworks for bystander response → what it actually costs to act, and what practice asks

Duration40 minutes
Cognitive principleEvaluative analysis
Bloom's levelEvaluate
LessonG7.U1.C4

Topic (Concrete and Invisible): Three specific frameworks for bystander response → what it actually costs to act, and what practice asks

Cognitive Principle Featured: Evaluative analysis — students assess frameworks not for abstract correctness but for specific adequacy in specific situations. The move from "which is right" to "which is most likely to reduce suffering here, with these constraints" is the cognitive skill the lesson trains.

Bloom's Level: Evaluate — students judge the adequacy of three response frameworks across varied scenarios, construct a reasoned position on bystander responsibility, and begin drafting a personal position paper. This is explicit Bloom's Evaluate: assessing quality, building a defensible claim.

Prior Class Summary: C3 was the Three Chairs exercise — students inhabited all three perspectives and sat with the bystander question from the inside. They produced a list of specific obstacles to acting (fear of becoming the target, social contract anxiety, diffusion of responsibility, not knowing what to say). They carry that list and the felt understanding of all three chairs.

Learning Objectives

Students will KNOW three frameworks for bystander response — direct intervention, lateral support, and systemic action — including the specific advantages and costs of each Students will UNDERSTAND that Buddhist practice does not provide a formula; it provides a principle (reduce suffering, including one's own, without creating new harm), and the application of that principle requires situated judgment, not rule-following Students will BE ABLE TO evaluate which framework is most adequate in a given scenario, defend the evaluation with reasons, and begin drafting a personal position paper that holds their own view Students will FEEL the specific discomfort of discovering that all three frameworks are genuinely limited — that there is no clean, risk-free, always-adequate response — and that this limitation is not a failure of the frameworks but a feature of what cruelty actually demands

Materials Checklist

  • Board and chalk or marker for vocabulary and framework comparison
  • Scenario cards for small groups — three scenarios, one per group or pair (scenarios written below in Activity section; print or write on board)
  • Journals or paper for position paper draft, one per student
  • Pens or pencils, one per student
  • Grade 7 prayer set visible
  • Plan B (for small-group scenario analysis): If groups are moving slowly or the discussion is shallow, bring it back to the whole class. Work through one scenario together as a model before releasing groups to the remaining two. The three-scenario format is ideal but one well-analysed scenario is more valuable than three superficial ones.
  • Plan B (for position paper draft): If the drafting time is consumed by the scenario discussion, assign the draft as preparation for C5 — students begin at home. They must bring a working draft to C5 or the writing class will not work.

Lesson Body

Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 7 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer at closing only.

Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

"Last class ended with: 'Who is the someone who goes first?' Today we look at what going first actually looks like. What are the real options for a bystander in a bullying situation?"

Generate a list with the class — write everything offered on the board. Accept all: say something directly, tell a teacher, go sit with the person, walk away, laugh along, pretend not to notice, send a private message, talk to a friend about it later.

"Good. Now: we have a list. Today we're going to do something harder than listing options — we're going to evaluate them. Which of these actually works? Under what conditions? What does each one cost?"

Mini Lesson — 10–12 minutes

Three frameworks for bystander response.

The class's list likely contains many versions of three core approaches. Organise the board into three columns:

Framework 1: Direct Intervention. Stepping in — naming what's happening, standing physically with the target, speaking directly to the person doing the bullying. "That's not okay. Stop." Or to the target: "Hey — come sit with us."

  • Advantages: immediate, clear, directly disrupts the harm in the moment.
  • Costs: requires courage; can escalate (the person doing the bullying may turn on the intervener); not always physically safe; depends on the relationship and power dynamics in the specific situation.
  • When it's most likely to work: when the intervener has some social standing, when there is a relationship, when the bullying is verbal rather than physical, when there is more than one person willing to act together.

Framework 2: Lateral Support. Not confronting the person doing the bullying directly, but visibly and warmly moving toward the person being bullied. Sitting with them. Talking to them. Making them less alone. Not naming the bullying — just refusing to let them be isolated.

  • Advantages: lower risk than direct intervention; direct and immediate benefit to the target; does not depend on confronting power.
  • Costs: does not address the bully's behaviour directly; the behaviour may continue; some students find it uncomfortable to cross an invisible social line ("going to sit with the wrong person").
  • When it's most likely to work: when direct intervention feels unsafe; when the target needs visible human solidarity more than confrontation; when the bullying is social exclusion rather than direct verbal or physical harm.

Framework 3: Systemic Action. Reporting to an adult — a teacher, a parent, a school authority — to activate a formal response.

  • Advantages: involves adult authority and accountability; may produce consequences; takes the burden off the bystander.
  • Costs: slow — the formal response rarely happens in the moment; in peer culture, widely perceived as betrayal ("snitching"), which may increase retaliation against both the reporter and the target; effectiveness depends entirely on whether the adult responds well.
  • When it's most likely to work: when the harm is serious and sustained; when the bystander genuinely cannot act safely otherwise; when the adult involved can be trusted to respond effectively.

"Now: the Buddhist framing. All three can be acts of practice — of placing oneself on the side of reducing suffering. The right response is not the same in every situation. The question is: what is most likely to reduce suffering overall, in this specific situation, without creating new harm? That question requires judgment, not a formula."

Vocabulary Moment:

Write on the board: evaluate — to assess the quality, significance, or adequacy of something in context.

"When we evaluate a response framework, we're not asking 'is this good or bad in the abstract.' We're asking: what does this do well, what does it cost, and when is it adequate to the specific situation in front of you? That is evaluation — and it is a harder skill than simply knowing the options."

Body Moment:

"Stand up. Find a position in the room that represents where you feel yourself standing in relation to this question — not literally beside one of the columns, but physically: where are you in this?"

Students move to positions in the room. The teacher does not direct them.

"Look at where you ended up. Why there? What does your position in the room tell you about which framework you find most natural — and what you're most resistant to?"

Take 3–4 responses. Students remain standing for thirty seconds, then sit.

Emotional Hook: The recognition that all three frameworks are genuinely limited — that there is no clean, risk-free, cost-free response to cruelty. The discomfort of this is important: students who want a simple answer will find that this class does not provide one. The discomfort is the content.

Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: which framework do you find most compelling — and what does it cost? Not cost in the abstract — what does it specifically ask of the person who uses it? What do they have to be willing to give up, risk, or endure?"

Two minutes of genuine conversation. Then take 2–3 responses to the whole group.

What to listen for: Specificity about cost. Students who say "direct intervention" without acknowledging the risk are being abstract. Students who say "lateral support seems easier but it doesn't actually stop it" are being precise. Push toward precision: "What exactly does it cost? Name the specific fear or sacrifice."

Activity — 15–20 minutes

Phase 1: Small-group scenario analysis (10 min).

Groups of two or three. Each group receives one scenario (rotate if time allows):

Scenario A: A student is consistently mocked in class when they give a wrong answer. The teacher does not stop it. Four people hear it each time. The mockery is quiet — never loud enough for the teacher to notice clearly.

Scenario B: A student finds that their belongings have been moved or hidden repeatedly. It's not clear who is doing it. Several people seem to know. No one has said anything directly.

Scenario C: A student is excluded from a WhatsApp group that most of the class uses for homework coordination. They find out because someone accidentally shows them the screen. No one mentions it directly to them.

Groups discuss: Which framework applies most clearly to this scenario? Why? What does the most adequate response cost the bystander? Is there a response that reduces suffering without creating significant new harm?

Groups report back (3–4 min): Teacher facilitates. "What made you choose that framework? What did you have to give up to get there?"

Phase 2: Position paper draft (8–10 min).

"Begin your position paper. This is a personal statement — what you believe about cruelty, empathy, and what practice asks of you as a bystander. Not what you think is the right answer. What you actually believe, after sitting in three chairs and evaluating three frameworks."

Position paper prompt: "What do I believe about cruelty, empathy, and what practice asks of me?"

The draft today does not need to be finished. Students need: a clear opening claim, at least one piece of evidence from what we've studied (not just assertion), and a note on what feels most difficult to hold.

Teacher circulates: "What is your actual claim? Is this what you believe, or what you think you're supposed to believe? What's the hardest argument against your position — have you written it down?"

Debrief — 5 minutes

"Buddhism does not offer a formula. It offers a principle: reduce suffering, including your own, without creating new harm. How you apply that principle — with which framework, in which moment, at what cost — is the practice. Not the theory. The practice. Next class you finish your position paper and share it. Come with a claim you're willing to defend."

Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader.

Teacher's closing line: "The Sang offering carries our intention outward. Today we did not find a clean answer — we found an honest one. Carry the honest answer into next class. It is more valuable than the clean one."

Teacher Notes

Curriculum guidance (read before class — written by curriculum author): Carry forward: The small-group scenario analysis is the analytical core of Unit 1. By the end of C4, every student should have a working position they are willing to defend. C5 is the writing and sharing class — give students the full activity time there to complete their position papers. If any student's draft is still entirely open-ended after C4 (no claim, no direction), give brief individual guidance before C5 begins: "What do you believe — even tentatively? Start there."

The position papers are collected at the end of C5 as the Unit 1 portfolio artefact. Alert students to this now: what they write in the draft matters, because they will be completing and submitting it next class.

Spiral connection: the question of what practice asks of a bystander connects directly forward to Unit 2. Teaching younger students is itself a bystander-to-active-participant transformation. A student who has genuinely worked out what they believe about cruelty and bystander responsibility arrives at Unit 2 with earned seriousness — they understand what it means to step forward rather than wait. Name this bridge at the start of C5.

Spiral connection: Grade 6 — Tara does not abandon beings in difficulty. The three frameworks (direct intervention, lateral support, systemic action) can all be understood as forms of Tara-like action: moving toward suffering rather than away from it. The question of which form to use is the situated judgment the lesson trains.
