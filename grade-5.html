The Year in One Sentence

Grade 5 is the year students move from knowing the map to inhabiting it: the Wheel of Life becomes a mirror of their own inner states, and the lineage that has kept the Dharma alive becomes a chain they can trace from the Buddha to their own name.

Central question: What keeps beings turning in the Wheel, and what is the way out?

The Connective Thread

Grade 5 operates at three scales simultaneously, and the teacher should hold all three throughout the year.

The first scale is cosmic. Unit 1 gives students the full structure of the Wheel of Life — the three poisons at the hub, the inner ring of cause and effect, the six realms, the twelve nidanas on the rim, and the Buddha standing outside pointing toward liberation. This is the map as a map: a picture of samsara and its logic. Students produce a personal Wheel drawing and write the three-part reflection. By the end of Unit 1, they carry the map in their hands and the question in their minds: are the realms only cosmological, or is something else being described here?

The second scale is interior. Unit 2 answers that question directly. The six realms are states of mind as well as cosmological planes, and Grade 5 students are old enough to recognize them as such without losing the traditional cosmological reading. The God realm is the blankness after satisfaction. The hungry ghost realm is the pattern of craving that cannot be resolved by getting more. The human realm is the one where both suffering and reflective capacity are present — which is why it is the only realm where liberation is accessible. The three antidotes — wisdom, generosity, compassion — are introduced as the responses to the three poisons, and students discover that the whole curriculum has been practising these antidotes since Grade 1. The realm drawings are the artefact here: personal, annotated, and held with the same quality of care as the water bowls in Grade 4.

The third scale is historical. Unit 3 zooms out from the interior reading to the question of how the map reached this classroom at all. Padmasambhava made an arduous journey to Tibet in the 8th century. The lineage of teachers who followed him — who translated, transmitted, and preserved — is real, named, and traceable. Students draw that chain all the way to their own name and discover that they are not passive recipients. They are custodians, temporary holders of something very old.

The arc is coherent: cosmic map → inner life → historical chain. Three scales, one question.

Bloom's Level Guidance

Grade 5 operates primarily at the Understand, Apply, and Analyze levels. Students at ages 10–11 can hold literal and psychological readings simultaneously — they do not need to choose between "the realms are real places" and "the realms are states of mind." This is a genuine cognitive achievement and should be named as such. When students demonstrate this dual holding, affirm it explicitly: "You've just done something that requires real sophistication. You're holding two true things at once."

The evaluative and synthesis levels are touched in the culminating classes (U2.C5 and U3.C4) but not insisted upon. The culminating question — "What keeps beings turning, and what is the way out?" — invites synthesis, and many students at this age will produce genuine synthesis. Receive it as such.

The Prayer Set

Grade 5 uses the full ten-prayer set at every opening and closing:

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

At closing only, add: Food Offering Prayer.

The one new addition in Grade 5 is the Dechen Monlam — the aspiration prayer that begins "May all beings be free from suffering. May all beings find great happiness." This prayer was introduced in Unit 1, Class 1, with meaning first: students received the meaning of the prayer before they received the words, because they are studying the Wheel of Life and need to understand why this aspiration exists. Introducing the prayer in the context of the Wheel grounds it in the year's question rather than presenting it as another item to memorize.

By Unit 2, most students should be chanting the Dechen Monlam from memory. The teacher should listen for this and note it without making it a public performance goal.

The Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer takes on particular significance in Unit 3, when students discover that the prayer is addressed to Padmasambhava — the same teacher whose historical story they are now studying. In U3.C1, chant it with particular care and say so. In U3.C4 (the final class of the year), the teacher chants the Seven-Line prayer once more alone after the standard closing, without announcement. This gesture is not theatrical; it is devotional. It closes the year in the presence of the figure who made the year's central content possible.

New Prayer Protocol: Dechen Monlam

The Dechen Monlam is introduced in U1.C1 — the first class of Grade 5 — using the meaning-first protocol. This means: before students receive the words of the prayer, they receive its meaning. In the context of the Wheel of Life study, the meaning lands immediately: if beings are turning in the Wheel through no fault of their own, driven by poisons they cannot see, then the aspiration for all beings to be free from suffering is not pious sentiment — it is the only sane response to what the Wheel describes.

Having heard the meaning, students receive the words. They chant it together for the first time. The teacher does not rush through this moment. The prayer should feel like a genuine aspiration, not an addition to the routine.

By Unit 2, when the hungry ghost class creates a moment of sharp personal recognition, the Dechen Monlam at the end of class carries a different weight. Let it. No framing required.

Student Leader Protocol

The student leader protocol is inherited unchanged from Grades 1–4. One student leads the opening and closing prayer sets each class. Rotation is the teacher's responsibility — ensure every student leads at least once across the year. The student leader opens and closes; the teacher chants with the class, not ahead of it. The leader's role is real, not ceremonial.

The Making Thread

Grade 5 has three artefacts, produced across three units. Together they constitute the Grade 5 portfolio.

The personal Wheel of Life drawing (Unit 1) is the panoramic view — the whole structure of samsara held in one image, with a three-part reflection written alongside. Students annotate their Wheel and compare it to an initial sketch made before teaching, so they can see what has changed.

The three detailed realm drawings (Unit 2) are the close-up view — three realms examined with depth, annotated with the characteristic experience, the ruling klesha, and a personal example. The fourth page is the "Way Out" page, showing the human realm, the three antidotes, and the Buddha outside pointing. The hungry ghost drawing is encouraged for every student, even those who did not choose it as one of their three.

The lineage map (Unit 3) is the historical view — a visual diagram of the chain of transmission from the Buddha through Padmasambhava to the school and the student. The student's own name is written at the end of the chain. Below the name: one sentence about what they intend to carry forward.

These three artefacts move from outside to inside to relationship. Cosmic structure, inner experience, historical chain. The same movement as the year's three units.

The Sensitive Content Note: G5.U2.C2

The hungry ghost class (G5.U2.C2 — "The Hungry Ghosts") is the most psychologically intense class in the entire curriculum to date. The teaching is precise and honest: the hungry ghost realm describes the structure of craving that cannot be resolved by getting more of what is craved. For some students, this will land at a safe intellectual level. For others, it will describe something real in their home environment — a family member's addiction, a pattern of scarcity, a compulsive behavior they have observed or experienced.

The class is designed with this in mind. The body moment and the personal annotation are private — students are explicitly told they do not have to share. The turn-and-talk prompt offers the option of speaking about "life more broadly" or "patterns you've observed" rather than personal disclosure. These are not social niceties; they are protective structures.

The practical guidance for teachers: do not push. The goal of this class is recognition and illumination, not excavation. If a student makes a disclosure during or after class that warrants pastoral follow-up, note it in the reflection section and follow the school's care process. This class is not a therapy session — it is a precise examination of a state of mind using an ancient and accurate image. The distinction between those two things matters for how the teacher holds the room.

The Pajapati Thread

The name Pajapati has been present in the curriculum since Grade 2, Unit 2, Class 1, where it was planted without explanation. Do not explain this name to Grade 5 students. Do not draw attention to it. It returns in Grade 6, Unit 3 (Tara), where students encounter a fuller version of her story. The complete telling is reserved for Grade 8.

This is a deliberate slow-release structure: a name held for years before its full context is given. When students encounter it again in Grade 6, it should feel familiar — a thread they carried without knowing why. Teachers should trust the structure and resist the temptation to anticipate it.

Practical Notes for Teachers

The Wheel of Life image should be displayed throughout Unit 1 and remain available in Unit 2. By Unit 3 it can be set aside, though some teachers prefer to leave it up all year as a visual anchor for the central question. Either is right.

Lineage information must be gathered before Unit 3, Class 2. The teacher needs the specific names of the lineage relevant to this school's tradition — not a generic list, but the real chain. Contact the school's senior teacher or consult founding documentation. The exercise of writing one's own name at the end of a real, named chain is qualitatively different from writing it at the end of a generic diagram. Do this work before C2 so the information is ready when students need it.

The hungry ghost class is not a therapy session. This is worth stating plainly as a practical note, not only as a sensitive-content caution. The teacher's job in G5.U2.C2 is to be a clear, warm, unhurried presence — not a counselor, not a confessor, not a facilitator of disclosure. The image does the teaching. The teacher holds the room. Trust the image; do not over-explain or over-process it.

The bell closes Unit 3, Class 4 — the final class of Grade 5 — as it closed Grade 4. One ring. Silence. The teacher waits until the silence is genuinely complete before speaking the closing line. This is a pacing decision, not a dramatic one. Silence after a bell carries something that words immediately following it do not.
