Read the complete Grade 1 teaching overview

Read this before teaching the first class. It gives you the year's shape, the making thread, how prayers work at this level, how the student leader role runs, and what Grade 1 plants for the years ahead.

The year in one sentence

Children in Grade 1 spend the year learning that kindness is not only a feeling — it is something that can be made, sent, spoken, and practised with the whole body.

Central question: How do we send kindness to others we cannot see?

The connective thread: Each unit picks up from the one before. Unit 1 asks where kindness goes when we send it. Unit 2 shows what giving without return looks like. Unit 3 introduces a being who embodied both. Unit 4 brings it down to the smallest scale — if bugs matter, everything does. Unit 5 returns to a great life, but shows it began ordinary. Unit 6 runs through the year, giving students a story every two weeks to carry the lessons outward.

The making thread: Every unit produces something physical. The flags go up outside. The crown is worn and shared. The drawing is presented to the class. The journal is kept. The book is assembled and read. The story-wisdom wall grows all year. By June, the room itself is evidence of what students have learned.

Bloom's level for this grade

Remember, Understand, Apply. Everything is made, moved, or told.

At this age, the highest and most honest form of understanding is doing — chanting, drawing, observing, releasing, sharing. The lessons are designed around that. Do not push toward analysis or abstraction. Trust that the doing, done well, is sufficient.

The prayer set

Grade 1 uses three prayers at the opening and closing of every class:

  1. Kyap Dro (Refuge prayer)
  2. Om Mani Padme Hung (mantra of compassion — chanted seven times)
  3. Manjushri Prayer

For pronunciation, meaning, and context of each prayer, see /curriculum/PRAYER-REFERENCE.md in the main curriculum folder.

In Unit 1, Class 1: students hear the prayers for the first time. The teacher chants; students listen and join when ready. No pressure, no correction. By Class 2, most students will be attempting to follow along.

From Class 2 onwards: one student leader leads the prayers at opening and closing (see Student Leader Protocol below).

Note on pace: Chant Om Mani Padme Hung slowly — approximately one full repetition every 4–5 seconds. If you rush, students will rush. Your pace sets the room's temperature.

The student leader protocol

Each class, one student leads the opening and closing prayers. This role:

  • Develops ownership and confidence
  • Gives students a model of what "leading" looks like in a contemplative setting
  • Rotates so every child leads multiple times across the year

Rotation: Keep a simple class list and rotate in order. Mark who led. When a child is absent on their day, skip to the next name and return to the absent child the following week.

Preparing the leader: At the end of the previous class, quietly tell the next student they will lead. Say: "Tomorrow you will stand at the front and lead our prayers. I'll be right beside you the whole time. Just do what you've been doing — I'll help if you need it." No further preparation is required.

If a child is reluctant: Never force it. Simply say: "That's fine — we'll come back to you another time." Move to the next child on the list. Return to the reluctant child after two or three weeks; most settle into it.

What "leading" means at this age: The student stands at the front, faces the class, and begins the prayers. The teacher joins immediately, chanting alongside. The student does not chant alone — the teacher is always there.

Assessment — what to look for

No grades, no exams. At year's end, students present a portfolio. During the year, the evidence of learning is:

  • The prayer flags (made, named, hung)
  • The leaf crown (worn and shared in the gratitude circle)
  • The lotus drawing (presented in the gallery)
  • The bug journal (taken outdoors, drawn in)
  • A page of the class book (contributed and "read")
  • Drawings on the story-wisdom wall (accumulated all year)

Watch for: which students engage most readily with the physical practices. Which students need more time before they join in. Which students show unexpected depth in Turn and Talk. These observations go in your My Reflection field after each class — they are the real assessment record.

Practical notes

Timing: Lessons are written for 40 minutes. The Opening (5 min) and Closing (3 min) are fixed. If time runs short, the Debrief can absorb a cut — but protect the activity. Making is never the optional part.

Outdoor classes: Unit 4, Class 3 (Watching Closely) and Class 4 (The Rescue) require outdoor time. Walk the schoolyard the morning of these classes to locate insects. Know two or three spots before students arrive.

Oral storytelling: Several lessons and all Jataka sessions rely on the teacher telling or reading a story aloud. The written retellings in the Jataka session files are detailed enough to read verbatim — you do not have to tell them from memory. Practice once aloud before class.

Plan B thinking: Any lesson with a materials-dependent activity (painting, outdoor observation, book assembly) includes a Plan B in the Materials Checklist. Read it before class so you are not surprised.
