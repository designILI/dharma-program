Grade 1 · Ages 6–7 · The Circle of Kindness

Grade 1 —
The Circle of Kindness

Central question: How do we send kindness to others we cannot see?

Children in Grade 1 spend the year learning that kindness is not only a feeling — it is something that can be made, sent, spoken, and practised with the whole body. Every unit produces something physical. By June, the room itself is evidence of what students have learned.

This is the foundation year. The prayers, stories, and practices introduced here will be carried forward and deepened across every grade that follows.

Units 6 (5 fixed + 1 rotating)
Lessons 27 total
Bloom's Level Remember / Understand / Apply
Prayer Set Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung · Manjushri
01

The year at a glance

Each unit picks up from the one before. The connective thread: Unit 1 asks where kindness goes when we send it. Unit 2 shows what giving without return looks like. Unit 3 introduces a being who embodied both. Unit 4 brings it down to the smallest scale — if bugs matter, everything does. Unit 5 returns to a great life, showing it began ordinary. Unit 6 runs all year as a rotating library.

02

How lessons work at this age

Remember, Understand, Apply. Everything is made, moved, or told.

At this age, the highest and most honest form of understanding is doing — chanting, drawing, observing, releasing, sharing. The lessons are designed around that. Do not push toward analysis or abstraction. Trust that the doing, done well, is sufficient.

"Making is never the optional part of a lesson. It is the lesson."

Each class runs 40 minutes and follows the same arc: Settle → Remember → Hear → Talk → Make → Surface → Close. The Opening (5 min) and Closing (3 min) are always the prayer set, led by a rotating student leader. If time runs short, the Debrief absorbs the cut — never the activity.

Opening (fixed) 5 minutes — prayers, student leader
Lesson body 32 minutes — flexible, activity protected
Closing (fixed) 3 minutes — prayers, one takeaway sentence
Unit 1 · 6 classes

Om Mani Padme Hung
& Prayer Flags

How can a wish travel to someone far away?

Students hear Om Mani Padme Hung for the first time, learn its six syllables, explore how sound carries intention, then paint their own cloth prayer flags and hang them in a small ceremony. The unit ends with Hoisting Day — flags going up outside, wishes sent out.

Students make: Painted cloth prayer flags — hung outside and kept all year

Unit 2 · 4 classes

The Giving Tree &
Unconditional Love

What does it mean to love with no reward?

Through Shel Silverstein's The Giving Tree, students encounter the idea of love that keeps giving with nothing asked in return. They identify their own "giving trees," make a leaf gratitude crown, and close with a circle of spoken thanks.

Students make: A leaf gratitude crown — worn and shared in the closing circle

  • C2 Giving and Getting 40 min →

Unit 3 · 4 classes

Guru Rinpoche's Birth

Where do special, wise people come from?

Students hear the story of Guru Rinpoche appearing as a child of light on a lotus in the middle of a lake. They explore what it means for someone to arrive in the world with a purpose, then make their own lotus-birth drawings for a class gallery.

Students make: A lotus-birth drawing — presented in a class gallery

Unit 4 · 4 classes

Patience & the Bugs

Do the smallest creatures matter?

Starting from the Jataka tale of the brave little parrot, students explore whether all living things — even insects — have feelings and deserve care. They go outside to observe creatures closely, then carry out a small "rescue" practice. The inquiry introduces ahimsa in its most concrete, touchable form.

Students make: A bug rescue observation journal — drawn outdoors, kept all year

  • C1The Brave Little Parrot40 min→
  • C2Do Bugs Feel?40 min→
  • C3Watching Closely (outdoor class)40 min→
  • C4The Rescue (outdoor class)40 min→
Unit 5 · 4 classes

Buddha's Birth

What makes a life special from the very beginning?

Students hear about the Buddha's birth — the signs, the prophecy, the wonder — but also that he was once small, young, learning, like them. The unit closes with each student contributing a page to a class book, which is then read aloud together.

Students make: One page each for a class book — assembled and read together in C4

Unit 6 · Rotating library · 5 standalone sessions

Jataka Stories

What can animals teach us about being good?

These five standalone sessions can be taught in any order, interwoven across the year — approximately one every two weeks. Each follows the same pattern: settle → hear the tale → discuss → draw on the story-wisdom wall. The wall grows all year as a visual vocabulary of moral courage.

Students make: Drawings on a running story-wisdom wall — accumulates all year
