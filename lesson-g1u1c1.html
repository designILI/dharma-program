Grade 1 · Unit 1 of 6 · Class 1 of 6 · First class of the year

The Sound of Kindness

Students hear Om Mani Padme Hung for the first time — not as information, but as an experience. The sound is offered like a gift, not taught like a lesson.

Duration 40 minutes
Cognitive Principle Dual Coding
Bloom's Level Remember
Anchor Text Moody Cow Meditates
Learning Objectives
  • Know The sound and approximate pronunciation of Om Mani Padme Hung, and that this sound has a name: a mantra.
  • Understand That some sounds are made on purpose — to send a feeling out into the world, the way a bell rings and the sound travels.
  • Be Able To Join in chanting Om Mani Padme Hung with the group, following the teacher's lead, at a slow and steady pace.
  • Feel The gentle surprise of making a sound together and noticing it in the body — something between wonder and settledness.
Materials Checklist
  • Moody Cow Meditates by Kerry Lee MacLean — mark two pages in advance: the Grandfather teaching breath, and the mind-jar settling. Read only these two sections today.
  • Small hand bell or singing bowl (preferred) — resting on cloth in the centre of the circle before students arrive
  • Chairs arranged in a full circle — no tables visible. The room should look different from an ordinary classroom.
Opening 5 minutes

Students enter and find seats in the circle. The teacher is already seated, still, the bowl in front of them.

"Before we start, I want to show you what we always do at the beginning and end of this class. We say some prayers together. Today, I'll do them first so you can hear what they sound like. After that, whenever you feel ready, you can join in."

Strike the bowl once. Let the sound fully die before speaking.

Recite Kyap Dro at a pace that allows students to hear each syllable. Hands folded in prayer position — model it without instruction.

Chant Om Mani Padme Hung slowly, seven times. The teacher chants alone, eyes slightly lowered, fully present. Some students will begin to murmur along by round 4 or 5 — let that happen without comment or encouragement. Do not praise them for joining. The joining itself is the reward.

Recite the Manjushri Prayer gently. Strike the bowl once to close.

"Good. That's how we begin and end every class. You don't need to remember anything yet — your ears are already doing the work."
Note on student leader

Starting from Class 2, one child will lead the opening and closing prayers each class. Today, the teacher leads alone — students need to hear the prayers correctly before they are asked to lead them.

Prior Knowledge Activation 5–8 minutes
"I want to ask you something before we do anything else. Has anyone ever heard a sound — a really beautiful sound — and felt something happen inside you? Like your body did something?"

Wait. Give it genuine time. These are six-year-olds and the question is unusual — some will look blank, some will look inward.

Strong response

A child says: "When my aama sings at night, I feel sleepy and happy." Receive this without elaborating: "Yes. The sound did something inside you. That's exactly it."

Surface or misdirected

A child says: "I heard a truck and it was really loud." Go deeper: "And when you heard it, did your body do something? Did it jump? Did it go tense?" Help the child notice that any sound lands somewhere in the body.

If no one responds

"Think about this: when someone sings a lullaby to a baby, the baby doesn't understand the words. But what happens?" Let them answer. "The sound did that. Not the words — the sound."

"Today we're going to make a sound together, and I want you to notice what it does inside you."
Mini Lesson 10–15 minutes
"We just said some words at the start — did anyone hear them? Can anyone try to say them back?"

Let two or three children attempt. Receive all attempts warmly. Do not correct pronunciation at this stage — the goal is engagement, not accuracy.

"The sound we kept saying, over and over — Om Mani Padme Hung. I'm going to say it one more time, slowly."

Say it once, very slowly: Om — Ma — ni — Pad — me — Hung.

"Now — just once — try it with me."

Chant it together, once. Slowly.

"How many pieces did you hear? Let's count on our fingers."

Hold up one finger per syllable. OM. MA. NI. PAD. ME. HUNG. Six fingers up.

"Six little pieces. Six sounds. And together, they make one thing: a wish."

Pause. Do not explain further yet. Let "a wish" hang in the air.

Now open Moody Cow Meditates. Do not introduce the book formally — simply begin reading from the Grandfather's first appearance. Read to where the Grandfather shows the child how to breathe. Pause the reading.

"Let's try this — just like in the book."

Body Moment: Guide a slow breath together. In through the nose — count to three — out through the mouth with a long, soft exhale. Three times. The teacher breathes visibly: chest rising, shoulders dropping. After the third breath, speak quietly.

"Notice what happened. Did anything change in your body?"

Do not require answers. Let one or two students speak if they want to. Continue reading to the page where the mind jar settles — the glitter swirls and slowly comes to rest. Close the book.

"That's what the sound can do. When we chant Om Mani Padme Hung together — it's like the glitter settling. Things inside us get a little quieter."
"Om Mani Padme Hung — that's what we call a mantra. You don't need to remember that word today. But a mantra is a special sound that's been said by kind people for a very, very long time. Hundreds of years. Maybe thousands. And every time someone says it, they're adding their voice to all those other voices."

You are building a sense of belonging to something ancient and large. Do not name it. Watch for the child who goes quiet and looks slightly inward — that is the sign the hook has taken. Do not call attention to it. Let it work.

Now chant Om Mani Padme Hung together, seven times, slowly. By round three or four, let your voice drop slightly so the children's voices are more present. Listen for when the group finds a shared rhythm — this usually happens around round four or five. That moment of shared rhythm is the lesson's peak. Let it breathe.

Turn & Talk 3–5 minutes

Assign pairs before the question — tap two neighbouring children to indicate their pair. Do this for the whole class quickly.

"Turn to your partner and tell them: when we were chanting just now, did you notice anything? In your body, or in the room? Tell your partner one thing you noticed."

Signal to begin. Circulate slowly. Listen for: chest, belly, vibration, warmth, stillness, loud, quiet, together. You are not correcting — you are hearing.

Signal to reconvene with one gentle strike of the bowl. Invite one or two pairs to share briefly. Receive without over-praising. "Yes. You noticed that." is enough.

Activity 15–20 minutes
Note on today's activity

There is no making activity in Class 1. The activity today is the chanting itself, structured as a gentle practice. This is intentional — Class 1 is about arriving into the sound before anything is made from it.

"We're going to chant together one more time — but this time, I want to try something. I'm going to chant and you listen. Then you chant and I'll listen. Then we'll do it together."

Round 1 — Teacher only: Chant Om Mani Padme Hung, seven times, unaccompanied. Students sit and listen. Eyes can be open or closed.

Round 2 — Students only:

"Now you. I'll just sit here. You chant. As many times as you like, until it feels done."

This is unusual and possibly slightly uncomfortable — that slight discomfort is valuable. Students will look to the teacher; the teacher nods gently and is silent. Let them chant. Let them find when to stop. If they trail off after two rounds, that is fine.

Round 3 — Together: Join in and chant together seven times. This third round will feel fuller and more confident. End by letting the last "Hung" fade naturally.

After the final chant: complete quiet for approximately fifteen seconds. No speaking. The teacher sits still. Then, very quietly:

"Who were you thinking about, when you chanted? Even if it was no one — that's fine too. Just notice."

Do not ask students to share this. It is private for today.

Scaffold — student who won't chant

Some children will sit silently through all three rounds. Do not draw attention to this. Simply model and chant. If a child looks distressed, catch their eye and give a small nod — no words. The chanting being available without pressure is the scaffold.

Extension — deeply engaged student

After class, not during, invite privately: "Would you like to be the one who leads the chanting next time? You don't have to — but I saw you really listening." This is the seed for the student leader role in Class 2.

Debrief 5 minutes

Remain in the circle. Students do not move.

"Here is something I want to ask you, and you can think about it even after you leave today. When you ring a bell —"

[Strike the bowl once. Let the sound die completely.]

"— the sound goes out into the room. You can't see it. But it's really there, isn't it? You can hear it."
"Om Mani Padme Hung is like that. When we say it, the sound goes out. Where it goes — that's something we'll talk about more. But today, you made that sound. All of you, and me. That sound was real."

Pause.

"This whole year, we're going to think about one question together. I'll tell it to you now, and we'll keep coming back to it:"

"How can a wish travel to someone far away?"

Repeat the question once, slowly. Do not answer it. Do not invite answers today. Let it sit.

Closing 3 minutes
"Let's close the same way we opened."

The teacher leads the prayer set again: Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung (seven times), Manjushri Prayer. Strike the bowl once to close.

"Before you go — take that question with you. How can a wish travel? Maybe you'll notice something before next class."
Teacher Notes
Curriculum guidance (read before class)

Students now have the sound of Om Mani Padme Hung in their bodies. In Class 2, begin by asking them to reproduce it — don't play it for them first. Trust that it is there.

Also carry forward: the question "How can a wish travel?" should be left completely unanswered going into Class 2. Resist the urge to answer it in closing conversation.
