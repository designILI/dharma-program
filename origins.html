Teachings on intention, compassion, and the art of meeting each being where they are

When you make clothes for your body, you have to make them according to the size and shape of your body. You can't just say — square here, long there. You have to make it according to what it has to fit.

— Rinpoche

Six Essential Teachings

I

The Mind is Everything

All actions — positive, negative, and neutral — arise from intention. In Dharma, this is kun-long: kun meaning "all," long meaning "to rise." From intention, everything rises. Train the mind and all else follows.

"How do you create a mind that works for universal good?"

II

Meet Each Being Where They Are

Don't teach university concepts to first graders. Watch what a child is drawn to — that interest is a window into their nature, a trace of their previous life. Teaching through it lands. Teaching past it is lost.

"If a tree is crooked, it can still be useful. Crooked branches are sometimes the most useful of all."

III

Words Point — They Are Not the Destination

You need words to access meaning, but words alone are not enough. Spiritual language is for an inward journey, not for navigating the world. The same word — yeshe, rigpa — can mean something entirely different in sutra and tantra. Framework is everything.

"It's like a person without fingers trying to climb up a rock."

IV

Ethics as Foundation

Before enlightenment, simply live by clear principles. Rinpoche returned to Golok — a community known for stealing — and offered four or five simple vows: no drinking, no killing, no lying, no stealing. He did not force. He invited. Now the doors stay open.

"If people live according to ethics, bad people become the minority."

V

Everything Grows from a Seed

In dharma, nothing happens by accident. All comes from a seed — gyu — but you also need manure and water (khyen) for the seed to grow. Conditions matter as much as potential. The teacher's role is to tend the conditions.

"Buddha never said, 'I'll force my teachings down your throat.' He said, 'I'll provide them — it's up to you to practice.'"

VI

We Are at a Tipping Point

According to Guru Rinpoche, it is not time that has changed but people's minds — their mindset and priorities. The balance has tipped toward worldly existence. The task now is to graft dharma back into the mind, especially in how we teach the next generation.

"Things could go either way — happiness and the dharma tradition could go way up, or way down."
