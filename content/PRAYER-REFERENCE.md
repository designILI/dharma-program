# The Dharma Program — Prayer & Pronunciation Reference
## All Grades · Complete Repertoire

*This document travels with the full curriculum. Every prayer in the graduated ladder is listed here: its Tibetan name, English meaning, when it first enters the program, approximate pronunciation guidance, and its role in the classroom. The actual Tibetan text and preferred transliteration should be confirmed with the resident teacher and filled into the bracketed fields below.*

*The graduated ladder works cumulatively: each grade practices all previous prayers plus its new additions. A Grade 6 class opens and closes with every prayer from Grades 1 through 6.*

---

## How to use this reference

- **Before teaching a new grade:** Read the entries for all prayers that grade will use.
- **Pronunciation guides** use simple phonetic rendering for non-Tibetan-speaking teachers. The symbol **'** marks a light glottal stop. Syllables in **CAPS** carry the main stress.
- **Pace:** All prayers and mantras should be recited slowly — more slowly than feels natural at first. Let each syllable land before the next begins.
- **Correction policy:** In Grades 1–3, correct nothing during the prayer itself. After class, if a child is consistently mispronouncing, model the correct sound privately, warmly. In Grades 4–8, gentle correction during practice is appropriate.
- **Actual Tibetan text:** The transliteration fields below are left for the resident teacher to confirm and fill in. Do not rely on internet sources alone — regional variation in chanting tradition matters.

## Grade 1 prayers

### 1. Kyap Dro — Refuge Prayer
**First introduced:** Grade 1, Unit 1, Class 1
**Tibetan name:** སྐྱབས་འགྲོ (Kyap Dro)
**English meaning:** "I take refuge." The prayer of placing oneself under the protection and guidance of the Three Jewels: the Buddha (the awakened one), the Dharma (the teachings), and the Sangha (the community of practitioners).
**Pronunciation guidance:** *Kyap* rhymes with "yap" with a soft k. *Dro* rhymes with "go."
**Tibetan text / preferred school transliteration:** *(fill in with resident teacher)*
**In the classroom:** Recited at the opening of every class. For Grade 1 students hearing it for the first time, describe it simply: *"This is how we begin — by saying we want to learn, and we trust the teachings to help us."*

### 2. Om Mani Padme Hung — Mantra of Compassion
**First introduced:** Grade 1, Unit 1, Class 1
**Sanskrit / Tibetan name:** ཨོཾ་མ་ཎི་པདྨེ་ཧཱུྃ (Om Mani Padme Hung)
**English meaning:** Often translated as "the jewel in the lotus" — though the meaning is considered inexhaustible. Traditionally understood as the complete teaching of the Buddha in one sound: the transformation of body, speech, and mind into the body, speech, and mind of Chenrezig (Avalokiteśvara), the bodhisattva of compassion.
**Pronunciation guidance:**
- **Om** — like "home" without the h
- **Ma** — as in "mama"
- **Ni** — as in "knee"
- **Pad** — as in "pod"
- **Me** — as in "may"
- **Hung** — as in "hoong" (not "hung" as in English past tense)
**Chanting pace:** One full repetition approximately every 4–5 seconds. Chanted seven times at opening and closing in Grades 1–3; the count may extend in higher grades.
**In the classroom:** Introduced in Grade 1 as "the sound of compassion." Students meet it by ear in Class 1 before its meaning is explained in Class 2. In all grades, chanting together is a practice, not a recitation — the teacher chants with genuine attention, not as a formality.

### 3. Manjushri Prayer
**First introduced:** Grade 1, Unit 1, Class 1
**Tibetan name:** འཇམ་དཔལ་གཞོན་ནུར་གྱུར་པ (Jampel Shyönnu) — a prayer to Manjushri, bodhisattva of wisdom
**English meaning:** A prayer invoking the wisdom and clarity of Manjushri — the bodhisattva depicted with a flaming sword that cuts through ignorance and a lotus holding a wisdom scripture.
**Pronunciation guidance:** *(fill in with resident teacher)*
**In the classroom:** Recited as the closing element of the opening set. For Grade 1 students, describe Manjushri simply: *"He is the one who helps our minds become clear and sharp, like a lamp turning on in a dark room."*

## Grade 2 additions

### 4. Sem kye — Bodhichitta Prayer
**First introduced:** Grade 2
**Tibetan name:** སེམས་བསྐྱེད (Sem kye / Semkye)
**English meaning:** "Generating the awakening mind." Bodhichitta is the aspiration to attain enlightenment not for oneself alone, but for the benefit of all beings. The prayer is the formal expression of that aspiration.
**Pronunciation guidance:** *Sem* rhymes with "gem." *kye* rhymes with "yay" with a soft k.
**In the classroom:** Introduced in Grade 2, where the unit on expanding compassion (concentric circles) gives students the experiential ground for this prayer. Before introducing it, explain: *"This prayer is a promise — that we want to become wise and kind not only for ourselves, but for everyone."* This prayer is deeply connected to the Grade 2 year theme: "The Widening Circle."

---

### 5. Ngowa — Dedication of Merit
**First introduced:** Grade 2
**Tibetan name:** བསྔོ་བ (Ngowa)
**English meaning:** "Dedication of merit." At the close of any practice, the merit (positive energy, goodness) generated is dedicated outward — not kept for oneself but offered to all beings. This prevents spiritual practice from becoming self-centred.
**Pronunciation guidance:** *Ng* as in the end of "sing." *Ngowa* — the ng is the first sound.
**In the classroom:** The Ngowa is the closing dedication. When a student leader guides the closing, the Ngowa is the final element. For Grade 2 students, explain: *"Whatever good came from this class — we don't keep it. We send it out to everyone, everywhere."* This is closely connected to the prayer flag unit (Grade 1 / Grade 2): the wish going outward on the wind.

## Grade 3 additions

### 6. Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer (7-Line Prayer)
**First introduced:** Grade 3 (heard as listening practice in Grades 1–2; memorised in Grade 3)
**Tibetan name:** ཚིག་བདུན་གསོལ་འདེབས (Tshik Dün Soldebs)
**English meaning:** A seven-line invocation of Guru Rinpoche (Padmasambhava), describing his miraculous birth on a lotus in Danakosha Lake and invoking his blessings. One of the most widely recited prayers in Nyingma and Tibetan Buddhist traditions.
**Pronunciation guidance:** *(fill in with resident teacher — this prayer has regional variation)*
**In the classroom:** Students in Grade 1 and 2 *hear* this prayer chanted by the teacher; they do not recite it. In Grade 3, they begin learning it with meaning first. Never introduce memorisation before meaning.

### 9. Dusum Sangay — Buddha of the Three Times
**First introduced:** Grade 4
**Tibetan name:** དུས་གསུམ་སངས་རྒྱས (Dusum Sangay)
**English meaning:** "Buddha of the three times" — a prayer to Guru Rinpoche understood as the embodiment of all buddhas of past, present, and future.
**Pronunciation guidance:** *Du* as in "do." *sum* as in "some." *Sang* as in "song." *Gye* as in "yay" with a soft g.
**In the classroom:** Introduced in Grade 4 alongside deeper study of ritual language. By Grade 4, students have the cognitive framework to understand "three times" (past, present, future) meaningfully.

## Grade 5 addition

### 10. Dechen Monlam — Prayer of Great Bliss
**First introduced:** Grade 5
**Tibetan name:** བདེ་ཆེན་སྨོན་ལམ (Dechen Monlam)
**English meaning:** An aspiration prayer for the happiness and freedom of all beings — "Monlam" means aspiration or wish; "Dechen" means great bliss or great happiness.
**Pronunciation guidance:** *De* as in "day." *chen* as in "chen." *Mon* rhymes with "bone." *lam* as in "lum."
**In the classroom:** Introduced in Grade 5 alongside the Wheel of Life unit, where students are grappling with the suffering of different realms. The prayer is a response to what they have understood: a wish that all beings be free from the suffering the Wheel describes.

## Grade 6 addition

### 11. Dolma — Tara Prayer
**First introduced:** Grade 6
**Tibetan name:** སྒྲོལ་མ (Drolma / Dolma) — Tara
**English meaning:** A prayer to Tara, the female bodhisattva of swift compassion. "Dolma" means "she who liberates." The prayer invokes her protection, healing, and swift action on behalf of beings in need.
**Pronunciation guidance:** *Dol* as in "dole." *ma* as in "ma."
**In the classroom:** Introduced in Grade 6 alongside the White Tara and Green Tara unit. Students learn the prayer with meaning first — who Tara is, what she represents, why she is depicted in two forms. This prayer is especially significant for the female students in the class: Tara is a model of awakening in a female form. See the spiral touchpoint note in G6.U3.C2 about pairing Tara-as-archetype with Pajapati-as-history.

## Grade 7 addition

### 12. Rewo Sang Chod — Smoke Offering Prayer
**First introduced:** Grade 7
**Tibetan name:** རི་བོ་བསང་མཆོད (Riwo Sang Chod)
**English meaning:** "Mountain smoke offering." A purification practice in which incense or fragrant substances are burned and offered to the local spirits, protectors, and all beings in the area, while prayers are recited for their benefit and the purification of obstacles.
**Pronunciation guidance:** *Ri* as in "ree." *wo* as in "woe." *Sang* as in "song." *Chod* as in "choe."
**In the classroom:** Introduced in Grade 7 alongside the Panglhabsol unit. This prayer has a strong embodied quality — the smoke, the outdoors, the sense of offering to an unseen community. Grade 7 students are ready for the practice's full dimension: honouring place, acknowledging what protects us, recognising that we exist within a web of relationships that extends beyond the visible.

## Grade 8 addition

### 13. Tsog Due pa — Tsok Offering Prayer
**First introduced:** Grade 8
**Tibetan name:** *(fill in with resident teacher — Tsok prayers vary by tradition and lineage)*
**English meaning:** The Tsok (or Ganachakra) offering is a feast offering practice in which food, drink, and sacred substances are offered to the Three Jewels and to all beings. It is both a purification practice and a celebration of the inseparability of the sacred and the ordinary.
**Pronunciation guidance:** *Tsog* — the ts is pronounced together, as in "bits." *Due* as in "doo-ay." *pa* as in "pa."
**In the classroom:** This is the final prayer added to the repertoire, completing the eight-year ladder. Grade 8 students who have been in the program since Grade 1 now hold the full set. The first time this prayer is introduced, acknowledge what that means: *"You started with Om Mani Padme Hung — one sound. Now you carry the full repertoire. That took eight years."*
