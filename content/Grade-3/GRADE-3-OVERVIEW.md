# Grade 3 — Dharma Program Overview
## Compassion in Action: What does kindness look like when it becomes a deed?

---

### To the Teacher

Grade 3 is the year compassion stops being an idea and becomes something students have actually done. They will end the year having released a living creature, built a wall-sized record of deliberate kind acts, and participated in a real Saga Dawa observance. Each of those three things happened because students planned, prepared, and followed through — not because they were told that compassion is good. The year's central question — *What does kindness look like when it becomes a deed?* — is answered three times, in three different registers: ceremonial, everyday, and sacred-seasonal. None of the three answers is the complete one. Together they form a picture.

The students arriving in Grade 3 are eight and nine years old. They are old enough to hold a genuine commitment and be asked honestly what happened to it. They are old enough for the gap between intention and action to be named as a real gap — not a moral failure, but the honest terrain of a life being built. Bloom's level for this year is Apply, with early Analyze beginning to emerge. That means students are not just receiving ideas — they are being asked to use them in real contexts and, increasingly, to examine why the using is hard.

### The Connective Thread

The three units of Grade 3 are not sequential topics that happen to share a year. They are three answers to the same question, and they are arranged deliberately to build on each other.

Unit 1 is the deep foundation. The Tsethar — a real life release, planned and carried out across six classes — establishes what the year is about at the level of lived experience. Students learn what it feels like to plan a compassionate act over weeks, to encounter all the practical and emotional friction that real preparation involves, and to carry it through. The key phrase from Unit 1 is this: "Acting for a creature's welfare is different from wishing for it — it has weight." That phrase carries into everything that follows.

Unit 2 asks: if compassionate action has weight in a ceremonial context, does it have the same weight in ordinary life? Can an eight-year-old carry the same quality of attention to a Tuesday morning that they brought to a six-class Tsethar? The answer is: not automatically — it has to be trained. The kindness-is-a-muscle metaphor carries the unit: compassion is not only for special occasions, it requires daily exercise, and the exercise is small, deliberate, and honest. The Kind Acts map on the class wall is the visible record of that exercise — it should be full and specific by Unit 2's end.

Unit 3 introduces the third register: sacred and seasonal. Saga Dawa is the most sacred month of the Himalayan Buddhist calendar, and it is the container in which the ordinary practice of Unit 2 is held with amplified intention. The unit asks: what is different about acting compassionately during sacred time? How does the collective holding of a community change what an individual act means? Students prepare and present a real offering, participate in a simple ceremony, and dedicate the merit — and the year closes in that ceremony.

The three together are the picture: a Tsethar is not more important than a Tuesday kind act, which is not more important than a Saga Dawa offering. Each is a different expression of the same underlying practice. By the end of Grade 3, students have done all three.

### Bloom's Level Guidance: Apply / Growing Analyze

Apply means students take a concept and use it in a real context. In Grade 3, this means carrying out an actual life release, not just learning what a Tsethar is. It means committing to a specific kind act and reporting honestly what happened, not just describing what kindness is. It means making a real offering in a real ceremony, not studying what offerings mean in the abstract.

Growing Analyze means students begin to examine their own process. Why did the kind act fail? What made it harder than expected? Was the motivation pure, or partly about recognition? These questions are not expected to produce settled answers at age eight — they are expected to be taken seriously. The teacher's job at this level is to honour honest uncertainty. The student who says "I'm not sure why I didn't do it" is doing more real analytical work than the student who gives the expected answer fluently. Listen for the honest ones.

When observing students, watch for the difference between performed understanding and genuine grappling. Both are real, but they need different responses. The performed response needs a gentle follow-up question that asks for specificity. The genuine grappling needs a teacher who receives it without rushing to resolve it.

### The Prayer Set

Grade 3 carries the full seven-prayer set that will continue through the programme. Every class opens with the first six prayers, and every class closes with all seven including the Food Offering Prayer.

The seven prayers, in order:

1. **Kyap Dro** — taking refuge in the Three Jewels; the foundational orientation of the practice
2. **Om Mani Padme Hung (7×)** — the mantra of Chenrezig, the bodhisattva of compassion; central to this year's theme
3. **Manjushri Prayer** — the prayer of wisdom; supports the growing analytical capacity of this age group
4. **Sem kye** — the aspiration for bodhicitta; the wish to attain awakening for the benefit of all beings
5. **Ngowa** — dedication of merit; what we generate, we give away
6. **Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer** — new to Grade 3 as a memorisation target (the meaning was held in Grade 2)
7. **Food Offering Prayer** — introduced in U1.C3; closing only

Two prayers require specific notes for Grade 3 teachers.

The **Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer** was encountered in Grade 2, where students held its meaning without being required to memorise it. In Grade 3, it becomes a memorisation milestone. The prayer should be chanted daily — every class — until it is genuinely in the body: not recited from memory by effort, but carried the way a familiar song is carried. The distinction matters. Chanting from memory by effort is not the same as having the prayer in the body. Aim for the latter. A student who has the Seven-Line in the body carries something that will serve them for decades.

The **Food Offering Prayer** was introduced in U1.C3, once students had encountered the Tsethar and the theme of generosity was alive in the room. Confirm with the resident teacher that the school's accepted version of this prayer is the one being used — there are variant forms and the school's tradition takes precedence.

---

### New Prayer Introduction Protocol

When a new prayer enters the set, the sequence is always the same: meaning first, then text, then memorisation. In Grade 3, the Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer follows this arc across the year. Students hear its meaning before they are asked to carry its words. Once the words are introduced, the prayer is chanted every class — not for fluency alone, but for embodiment. The goal is a student who, years from now, finds the prayer arising in a moment of need. That does not come from studying the text. It comes from ten thousand repetitions across a childhood.

The Food Offering Prayer was introduced mid-Unit 1. Its introduction should feel organic — it is the prayer that closes every class, and it was added to the set at the point in the year when students were deep in the question of what it means to give. The timing was pedagogically deliberate.

---

### Student Leader Protocol

The student leader role rotates each class, inherited unchanged from Grades 1 and 2. The leader sets the pace for the prayer set and opens and closes the class alongside the teacher. No student is skipped — every student leads before the rotation returns. The teacher notes who has led and tracks the rotation. If a student is absent on their turn, they lead the following class. The leader's role is not ceremonial — it is a real leadership responsibility, and students at this age feel the weight of it.

### The Making Thread: Three Artefacts

Grade 3 produces three portfolio artefacts, one per unit. Together they are the Grade 3 record.

The **Tsethar reflection journal** (Unit 1) is a written and drawn account of the life release: what the student imagined the Tsethar would be like, what actually happened, and what they know now that they didn't know before. It is the first formal use of the before → during/what happened → what I know now reflection structure.

The **Kind Acts map entries** (Unit 2) are each student's contribution to the class wall display: the documented kind acts across four classes, with a note about what each act cost. This is the most public of the three artefacts — it is visible on the classroom wall and builds across the whole unit.

The **Saga Dawa observance booklet** (Unit 3) is assembled across four classes: a calendar page, a three-moments page, an offering preparation page, and a final "what I carry from this" page. It is the most intimate of the three — a small personal document of the student's entry into sacred time.

Photograph the Kind Acts map before it comes down. Keep all three artefacts together in the Grade 3 portfolio record. These documents are not just evidence of learning — they are the learning, given material form.

---

### The Reflection Format: A Portable Practice

The reflection structure introduced in Grade 3 — before → what happened → what I know now — is not unique to the Tsethar. It appears again in Unit 2 (what I noticed → what I chose → what happened → what it cost) and implicitly in Unit 3 (what I brought → what I offered → what I carry from it). These are the same structure wearing different clothes, and they are a version of a structure students will use throughout the programme and beyond it.

By Grade 8, this structure should be second nature — students should be able to apply it without being taught it, to any new experience they encounter. Grade 3 is where it is introduced explicitly as a pattern. Name it to students when you use it the second time: "You've done this before. In Unit 1, you reflected on the Tsethar this way. Now you're doing it again." Naming the pattern helps students see that they are building a skill, not just completing an assignment.

### Practical Notes

The Tsethar that anchors Unit 1 requires logistical coordination before the unit begins — not during it. The teacher should confirm the following before Grade 3 starts: what creature will be released (fish, insects, or another appropriate animal), where the release will take place, what permissions or community coordination are required, and who will accompany the class for the release itself. Six classes are built into Unit 1 precisely because this coordination takes time. Do not begin Unit 1 without this in place.

Unit 3 should be aligned as closely as possible with the actual Saga Dawa month — the fourth month of the Himalayan Buddhist lunar calendar. Confirm the dates for the current year before planning the unit schedule. Even one class that falls within the month adds authentic weight to the observance. If the school holds a community Saga Dawa puja or event, connect Unit 3 explicitly to it — the class observance is not separate from the community observance; it is part of it.

The Food Offering Prayer should be confirmed with the school's resident teacher before it enters the Grade 3 prayer set in U1.C3. Use the school's accepted version. This is not negotiable — the school's liturgical tradition takes precedence over any version used in other contexts.

The Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer should be chanted every class, every time, from the moment it enters the Grade 3 set. Not practised — chanted. The distinction is that practice implies a future moment of readiness; chanting implies it is real now, even imperfectly. Both the teacher and the student leader chant it at full participation. When the teacher is seen to carry the prayer, students learn to carry it too.

Grade 3 is a demanding year. It asks students to do real things — not simulated, not imagined — and to reflect on them honestly. The teacher who holds that demand warmly, without flinching from the honest answer, is the essential ingredient this curriculum cannot provide. Everything else is on the page. What happens between the page and the child is the teacher's art.
