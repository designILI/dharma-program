# G3.U1.C2 — If I Were the Fish

## Grade 3 · Unit 1 · Class 2 of 6 · If I Were the Fish

*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Imagining what it is like to live in a small bucket of water → perspective-taking as the foundation of genuine compassion

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Embodied Cognition — the body knows things before the mind names them. The curl/unfold exercise encodes constraint and release physically before students write about it conceptually. When students later recall "what it felt like to be the fish," their body memory is available to them — they are not guessing; they are reporting.

**Bloom's Level:** Apply / early Analyze — Students are not just reading about empathy; they are practising it as a skill, then examining what the practice revealed. The first-person fish writing asks them to apply a perspective shift, and the debrief asks them to step back and analyse what "seriously trying" to take another being's perspective actually is.

**Prior Class Summary:** C1 introduced Tsethar through the Fish King story. Students encountered the word for the first time, drew their first imaginations of what a life release looks like, and felt the weight of the word "actually." They carry the phrase: "This year, we are going to turn a feeling into an act."

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** the word *perspective* and be able to use it to describe what they did in this class.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that trying to inhabit another being's experience — seriously, not as a game — is a form of practice that lies at the heart of compassion, and that it is difficult.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** write or draw a short first-person piece from the perspective of the creature they will release.

Students will **FEEL** the discomfort of constraint — of being small, in a small space, with no language for what lies beyond it — and from that discomfort, a more honest motivation for the release than abstract goodwill.

---

### Materials Checklist

- Notebooks or writing paper (one per student)
- Pencils
- Optional: a small clear container of water placed visibly at the front of the room — not yet explained, just present. (This is for atmosphere; students will notice it.)

> **Plan B:** If the water container is unavailable or feels out of place, omit it. The guided imagination does not depend on a prop.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 3 prayer set, led by the student leader for today:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer**

Teacher chants the Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer together with the class as in C1 — slowly, teacher leading. "Let's chant it again together. If more words are coming back to you, let them come. If you're still following by sound, that's right too."

*(Food Offering Prayer not yet added — joins from C3 closing.)*

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "Last class, you drew what you imagined a Tsethar — a life release — might look like. Who remembers what they drew? What did you picture?"

Take 3–4 responses. Receive each one. If students remember vividly, let the variety land — some will have imagined something dramatic, some something quiet, some something they're not sure about.

Then: "Today we're not going to think about what *we* will do. Today we're going to think about the creature — not from our side of the release, but from theirs. What is it like to *be* the fish?"

Pause. "That's a strange question. Can we actually know? Let's try."

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Teacher: "Close your eyes. I'm going to guide you through something. Just follow along — there are no right answers."

**Guided Perspective-Taking (spoken slowly, with pauses):**

"You are a fish. Not a large fish — a small one. You live in a bucket. The bucket is plastic — you can feel the curved walls if you swim to the edge. It is not very wide. You turn, and you reach the wall. You turn again. You reach the wall again. The water is not very clean — it has a taste to it, a heaviness. You have been in this bucket for a day, maybe longer. You don't know.

"There are other fish near you. You can feel them moving — little currents, little disturbances in the water. You are not alone, but the space is still very small.

"You don't have words. You don't think in sentences. But something in your body — in your fins, in the movement of water across your gills — something in your body knows that this is not all there is. There is something your body remembers, or longs for, even if you have no name for it. A larger movement. A different kind of water. Something in you knows there is more space somewhere.

"You don't know what will happen. You just live in this bucket, this moment, this water."

*(Pause for ten seconds.)*

"Now — slowly — come back into the room. Take a breath. Open your eyes."

Teacher: "What was that like? What did you notice?" Take 4–5 responses. Receive them without evaluation. Students may say: "I felt claustrophobic," "I felt sad," "I couldn't really imagine it," "I kept forgetting I was a fish." All of these are honest. "Couldn't really imagine it" is particularly valuable — hold it: "That difficulty is real. Let's stay with it."

**Vocabulary Moment:** "The word *perspective* — it means the view from inside a particular experience. When we take the perspective of the fish, we are practising a particular kind of attention: trying to see the world from inside a life that is not ours. This is not a small thing. Buddhist practice has asked people to do this for thousands of years — to take the welfare of other beings as seriously as their own. That begins with trying to see from where they stand. Or swim."

**Body Moment:** "Stand up for a moment." Wait until everyone is standing.

"Now — make yourself as small as you can. Curl your spine, pull your arms in, make your body into the smallest shape it can be. Like a creature in a small space." Give students about ten seconds in this shape. Hold it without commentary.

"Now — slowly — unfold. Open your arms. Stretch your spine tall. Take up the most space you can. Open." Let this take five seconds.

"Sit back down."

"You didn't need me to explain what release feels like. Your body already knew it." Leave this without further commentary.

**Emotional Hook:** The discomfort of constraint. Most eight-year-olds can access the feeling of being too small in a too-small space viscerally — it is close to a feeling they actually know. The exercise does not need drama; the body does the work. Let the room be quiet for a moment after the unfold.

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Teacher: "Turn to the person beside you. Here is the question: *If you were the creature we are going to release — what would the moment of release feel like? Describe it in detail. Where would you go first?*"

Structure: Partner A speaks first (90 seconds), then Partner B (90 seconds). Tell pairs to be specific — not "I would feel happy" but "I would feel the cold water and then I would go toward the darker part of the river because it would feel safer."

What to listen for: whether students move from vague emotional language into sensory specificity. Specificity is the marker that perspective-taking is happening rather than just sentiment.

After pairs: "What details came up that surprised you?" Take 1–2 responses.

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

"Now you are going to write — or draw — from the fish's point of view. Start with these words: *I am the fish. Today is the day of my release.* Then continue. Write what the fish notices first when it enters the water. What does it do? Where does it go? What does its body feel?"

Distribute paper or open notebooks. Teacher circulates, crouching beside students rather than standing over them: "What does the fish notice first — before anything else?" "What does the water feel like coming in from every side?" "Where does the fish go after that first moment?"

Teacher should look for: sensory detail (temperature, movement, light), physical sensation (fins, gills, the shift from constriction to openness), behaviour (where the fish swims, how fast, whether it pauses).

**Scaffold:** For students who are stuck: "Just write what the fish feels in the first five seconds. Only five seconds." This makes the task small enough to enter.

**Extension:** Students who finish may write a second piece from the fish's perspective: *The day before the release — what the fish was feeling in the bucket.* This creates a before-and-after arc inside the fish's own experience.

Teacher collects these pieces at the end of class — they will be included in the Tsethar reflection journal assembled in C4. Tell students: "I'm keeping these — you'll see them again."

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

"We don't actually know what a fish feels. That's true. But notice what we did today — we tried to imagine it seriously. Not as a game, not just for a moment, but slowly, with detail, with our bodies as well as our minds. That is what empathy is: a serious effort to take another being's experience as real."

"The Buddhist tradition has a teaching about this: every being wishes to be happy and to be free from suffering. Not just humans — every sentient being. A fish, an insect, a bird. The tradition asks us to take that seriously. Today was a practice in taking it seriously."

"One question before we close: *Was it easy to imagine being the fish? Or hard?* And what does the difficulty, if there was difficulty, tell you?" Take 2–3 responses. End with: "The difficulty is not a failure. It is information. It tells us how much practice this kind of attention takes."

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full prayer set:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer**

*(Food Offering Prayer not yet added — joins from C3 closing.)*

Teacher's closing line: "You practised today something that cannot be taught — only attempted. The attempt is the practice."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*

Carry forward: The first-person fish pieces from today's activity are kept by the teacher and will be pasted into the Tsethar reflection journal in C4. Store them safely, labelled with each student's name. The body exercise (curl/unfold) is a prototype for the embodied practice work that will appear again in Grade 5 (Wheel of Life realms — students inhabit the physical logic of each realm). You can name this later: "Remember in Grade 3 when we made ourselves small like the fish?" — the body memory will be there.

Note which students found the perspective exercise easy (those who entered the guided imagination readily and produced sensory detail in their writing) and which resisted (either couldn't access it or wrote abstractly or didn't try). Resistance is not a failure and should not be treated as one — it is honest information about where the child is with empathy-as-practice. Follow up quietly with students who seemed closed off: "What was hard about that today?" is enough. Do not push.

Logistics check: The release (C5) must be planned before C3. Confirm with school administration this week: permission to purchase and release animals, appropriate habitat, transport, adult supervision, and a date. If this is not yet done, it must happen before C3. C3 introduces the full logistics to students — if the logistics aren't confirmed, the class cannot be taught honestly.

Spiral connection: G1.U4.C2 asked "Do bugs feel?" and held the question philosophically. Today's class is the practice version of that same question — not asking it philosophically, but trying to answer it experientially, in the body.
