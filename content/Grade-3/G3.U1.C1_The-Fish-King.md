# G3.U1.C1 — The Fish King

## Grade 3 · Unit 1 · Class 1 of 6 · The Fish King

*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A story about a fish king who saved a lake full of creatures → compassion that moves from feeling into act

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Retrieval + New Lens — students already hold the Fish King story in memory (from G1.U6.C4). Revisiting a known story with a deliberately shifted question ("not just truthfulness — but what he did") allows prior knowledge to become a foundation rather than a repetition. The new lens (Tsethar) is what makes this retrieval generative rather than mere recall.

**Bloom's Level:** Apply — Students are not just remembering the Fish King story; they are being asked to apply its logic to themselves: *what would it look like if I acted rather than wished?* The drawing/writing activity at the close asks students to project that logic into their own imagined future act.

**Prior Class Summary:** This is the first class of Grade 3. Students carry Grade 2's full year — the concentric circles of compassion (Bodhichitta), the Buddha's life, Guru Rinpoche. They have practised Sem kye and Ngowa as standing prayer. Today is their first encounter with compassion as a deed rather than a wish.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** the word *Tsethar* and its literal traditional meaning (tshe = life; thar = liberation), and that this unit will culminate in a real life release.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the Fish King's story, seen through this new lens, is about a being who moved from caring to acting — and that this movement is the central question of Grade 3.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** draw or write their first, unfiltered imagination of what a life release looks like, as a record of where they start.

Students will **FEEL** the weight of the word "actually" — that Tsethar is not metaphor or poetry but a real creature, a real river, a real act — and sit with both the possibility and the responsibility that carries.

---

### Materials Checklist

- Class prayer cards (one per student, if used) — for the Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer introduced today
- Drawing paper or notebooks (one per student)
- Pencils and optional coloured pencils
- The Fish King retelling (included in this plan — teacher reads or tells from memory)
- A safe place to store students' C1 drawings/writings — these will be revisited in C6

> **Note:** No Plan B needed for this class — all activities are classroom-based and logistics-independent. However, if prayer cards for the Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer are not yet printed, the teacher chants it alone and students listen; cards can be added next class.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 3 prayer set, led by the student leader for today:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa**

Then — the **Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer**, introduced for the first time in the Grade 3 prayer set:

Teacher addresses the class before chanting: "We're adding a prayer today that you have heard before — some of you learned its meaning line by line last year. In Grade 3, we bring it fully into our body — we memorise it, we chant it together every class. Today I will lead it; just follow by sound. If you remember the meaning, let that meaning be underneath the sound." Teacher chants the prayer slowly, clearly. Students who remember it from Grade 2 will recognize it; those who are new follow the melody. No pressure to join fully yet — the first encounter in this setting is enough.

**Food Offering Prayer: not yet introduced — joins the closing from C3 onwards.**

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "In Grade 2, you spent a whole year sending wishes outward — to your family, to animals, to all beings. You learned the word Bodhichitta — the wish for all beings to be free from suffering. You practised Sem kye: 'May all beings be happy. May all beings be free from suffering.' You know those words. Here is a question for today — a question this whole year will live inside: *What if instead of wishing, we did something? What would that look like?*"

Pause. Wait five full seconds. Then: "Take a moment to think. Then: share one idea."

Take 3–4 responses. Receive each one without evaluation. If students say things like "help someone," "give food to an animal," "not step on a bug" — hold them: "Yes. Exactly that kind of thing." If no one responds: "Think about the last time you helped something — a person, an animal, a small creature. What did you *do*? Just describe the action."

Hold the question in the room: "That question — what does it look like when the wish becomes an act — is what Grade 3 is about. Keep it with you as we go."

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

**The Fish King Retelling** (teacher tells aloud from memory, or reads this version verbatim):

---

*Long ago, the Bodhisattva — the being who would one day become the Buddha — was born as a fish. Not an ordinary fish: a great fish, a king among fish, living in a lake that was vast and full of life. The lake held thousands of creatures — small fish and large, water birds, frogs, turtles, insects that danced on the surface. All of them were part of that world.*

*Then a drought came. The rains stopped. Week after week, the lake shrank. The water grew shallow and hot. The creatures grew frightened. Many began to die.*

*The Fish King watched this. He felt it — not from a distance, not as an idea. He felt the dying of the lake in his own body. And he understood: if he did nothing, all of these thousands of beings would perish. His feeling of care was not enough. Something had to be done.*

*The Fish King called out to the god of the clouds — Pajjunna. He had never done this before. He had only his merit — the goodness built from many lifetimes of care, of honesty, of compassion. He staked it all. He spoke a truth: "By the power of the goodness I have accumulated — all my lives, all my acts of care — let it be used now. Let rain come. Let these beings live."*

*And the clouds gathered. And the rain came. And the lake filled. And the creatures lived.*

---

Teacher, after a moment of quiet: "You've heard that story before — some of you in Grade 1, when the lesson was about truthfulness. The Fish King spoke a truth so powerful it changed the weather. But today I want you to notice something different: the Fish King didn't just feel sad about the dying lake. He *acted*. He put everything — his entire life's accumulated goodness — into the situation. He risked it. He used it. That is different from wishing."

**Vocabulary Moment:** "The word *Tsethar* — it is a Dharma term. *Tshe* means life. *Thar* means liberation. Tsethar: liberating a life. Not as a poem. Not as a metaphor. Actually — a real creature, a real act of release, a real place in the world where that creature now lives free. Over the next six classes, we are going to do a Tsethar. A real one. Today is the beginning of that."

**Body Moment:** "Close your eyes for a moment. Think of a creature — any creature, small or large. A fish. A bird. An ant on a path. Hold it in your mind — give it a shape, a size, a colour. Now — imagine that creature being harmed. Someone reaching toward it. A net. A bucket. The water running out. What happens in your body right now, as you imagine that?"

Pause for five seconds. Then: "Open your eyes. That reaction — that thing that happened in your body — that is the feeling that Tsethar comes from. This unit, we are going to take that feeling and turn it into an act."

**Emotional Hook:** The weight of the word "actually." Let it land without elaboration. The Fish King's truth was actual — it changed the weather. Our release will be actual — a real creature. Teacher can simply let the word sit in the room rather than explaining it further.

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Teacher: "Turn to the person beside you. Tell them: *What is the smallest creature you have ever helped? What did you do? What happened?*"

Structure: Partner A speaks first (1 minute), then Partner B (1 minute). Pairs decide who is A and who is B.

What to listen for: specific, real memories of care — moving a worm off a path, carrying a spider outside, giving water to a bird. The more specific the better. Transfer is happening when students name their own acts, not hypothetical ones.

After pairs finish: "Did anyone hear something from their partner that surprised them?" Take 1–2 responses.

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

Teacher introduces the unit: "Over the next six classes, we are going to do a real Tsethar — a life release. We will plan it together, prepare for it, and carry it out. We will each make a small journal about it. And at the end of this unit, you will reflect on what the doing revealed."

"But today — before you know what it will really be like — I want you to draw or write what you *imagine* a life release looks like. Not what you've been told. What you picture. What comes into your mind when you hear the words: 'We are going to release a living creature into the wild.' Draw it, or write it, or both."

Distribute paper (or students open notebooks). Teacher circulates: "What did you draw? Tell me what's happening in your picture." Note which students imagine the release as dramatic, which as quiet, which are confused about what to picture.

These first imaginings — drawings or writing — are kept by the teacher. They will be revisited in C6, when students compare their first imagination to what the release actually was. Do not tell students this yet — just say: "I'm going to keep these safe."

**Scaffold:** For students who are not sure what to draw, teacher says: "Draw the moment — the exact moment — when the creature is released. What does it look like?" This gives them a frame without telling them what to imagine.

**Extension:** Students who finish early may write: "What do you think the creature will feel at the moment of release? Describe it."

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

"The Fish King's act was, in one sense, very simple: he spoke a truth he had spent a lifetime building, and creatures were saved. Our act will be simpler still — we will find a living thing and give it back to its life. We will not be saving a whole lake. We will be saving one creature, in one river, on one morning. That is the whole practice."

"Can someone say back to me: what does the word *Tsethar* mean?" (Take one response.) "And what are we going to do over the next six classes?" (Take one response.)

Hold the essential question of the unit for a moment before closing: "What does it mean to act — not just wish — for the welfare of another being? That is the question we are going to live inside for the next six weeks."

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full prayer set:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer**

*(Food Offering Prayer not yet added — joins from C3 closing.)*

Teacher's closing line, spoken after the prayers: "You carried a wish last year. This year, we are going to learn what it feels like to carry an act."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*

Carry forward: Students' first imaginations of Tsethar (drawings and/or writing from today's activity) must be stored safely — they are revisited in C6 as part of the culminating reflection. Label each piece with the student's name before storing. The Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer is introduced today for the first time in the Grade 3 prayer set — it will deepen across the unit with daily repetition; don't push for memorisation yet. The Fish King retelling in this plan is intended for this age level and can be read verbatim if the teacher prefers; it is slightly more mature in register than the G1.U6.C4 version.

Spiral connections: Students first met the Fish King in G1.U6.C4 (lens: truthfulness — the power of an honest life to change the world). They met the Brave Little Parrot in G1.U4.C1 (lens: Ahimsa — a creature risking itself for others). G1.U4.C2 posed the question "Do bugs feel?" — Grade 3 now provides the lived answer: yes, and here is what we do about that. The Body Moment in this lesson (imagining a creature being harmed, noticing what happens in the body) is a direct extension of that G1 philosophical inquiry.

Before C2: Begin coordinating with school administration immediately if not already done — permission to purchase and release animals, appropriate release site (a nearby stream, pond, or garden), transport and containers, and a confirmed date for C5. This planning cannot wait until C3.
