# G3.U1.C5 — The Release

## Grade 3 · Unit 1 · Class 5 of 6 · The Release

*Duration: 40 minutes*

> **Format note:** This class takes place at the release site, not in the classroom. The structure is modified to reflect the outdoor, mobile context — but all 13 schema fields are present. The prayer set is abbreviated at the site but complete in spirit. The act is the lesson.

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Releasing a living creature into its appropriate habitat → what it means — in the body and not just the mind — to act for the welfare of another being

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Enactment — students do not simulate, discuss, or imagine the release; they perform it. The physical act of holding a living creature, feeling its movement, and opening the hands is qualitatively different from any prior lesson. The immediate journal writing at the site captures the unrepeatable first response — before interpretation, before narrative. This is the evidence of learning the portfolio is built to preserve.

**Bloom's Level:** Apply at its fullest — and the beginning of genuine Analyze. Students perform the practice they have prepared for across four classes. The 30-second silence after the release, and the one-word prompt, are the first Analyze moves: standing outside the act and reporting what it felt like from the inside.

**Prior Class Summary:** C4 was the full planning and preparation class. Students assembled their Tsethar reflection journals — cover decorated, C2 fish piece pasted in, C3 planning notes included, intention written. They set their intention in the Body Moment: 30 seconds of quiet, compass pointed. They carry readiness.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** the full sequence of a Tsethar practice — prayer, holding, release, Om Mani Padme Hung, Ngowa — as something they have now done, not just heard described.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that "both are changed" — heard first in C3 — has a specific, personal meaning that only emerges after the act is done.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** write or draw an immediate response to the release in their journal — what they noticed, what surprised them — before interpretation settles.

Students will **FEEL** what eight-year-old hands feel when they release a living creature into moving water: the texture, the weight, the moment of opening. And then: whatever comes after. No one can predict it. That is the point.

---

### Materials Checklist

**Confirmed logistics — all items must be verified before this class:**

- Confirmed creature + appropriate transport container (sealed, aerated if needed, water from the creature's current environment, not overcrowded)
- Confirmed release site — clean, appropriate habitat for the specific creature; accessible on foot or by arranged transport
- Written permission from school administration to purchase and release an animal
- Transport arranged (if the site requires it)
- Adequate adult supervision for outdoor travel and the release itself — minimum one additional adult beyond the teacher
- Students' assembled Tsethar reflection journals (held by the teacher since C4)
- Pencils for each student (carried by the teacher — students should not need to remember their own)
- No additional materials required

> **Plan B — If the release must be postponed:** If the release cannot occur on the planned date due to weather, logistics failure, or administrative issue, do not cancel — reschedule immediately. The class session becomes: a guided meditation and visualisation of the release (teacher leads students through the full sequence in imagination, step by step, slowly), followed by journal writing (students write what they *imagined* in the same spaces they will later fill with what *happened*). Set a concrete rescheduled date before students leave. When the actual release occurs, students return to their journals and write on a new page: "What I imagined vs. what actually happened."

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

*(Conducted at the release site, immediately upon arrival, or in the final moments before departure from school.)*

Full Grade 3 prayer set, abbreviated for the outdoor context. Teacher leads, no student leader for the opening today — the teacher holds this one:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer**

Teacher, before beginning: "We planned this together. We imagined it. We set our intention. Now we do it. Let's open with our prayers."

Chant the Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer together. Outdoors, the sound carries differently. Let that be noticed.

*(The Food Offering Prayer is part of the closing set — recited at school upon return, or adapted at the site if returning to school is not possible.)*

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 2–3 minutes

*(Brief — at the site, before the release. This is not a discussion; it is a last moment of interiority before the act.)*

Teacher, quietly: "Before we release — hold your intention. The one you set last class. Don't say it aloud. Just feel it. Where is it in your body right now?"

30 seconds of quiet. The class stands together at the release site. The container with the creature is present.

Then: "Good. Let's begin."

#### Mini Lesson — 2–3 minutes

*(This is a briefing, not a lesson. The act is the lesson.)*

Teacher: "Here is the sequence. We move through it together. No one rushes.

"First, we approach the water as a group. Then we pause — just for a moment — and feel the place we're in. Then I will hold the container, at heart level, with both hands. Anyone who wants to place their hands on the container too — you may. Then, together, we release the creature. The moment it enters the water, we begin Om Mani Padme Hung. Chant for as long as it feels right — I will lead and I will end it.

"Then: Ngowa. We dedicate the merit. Not to ourselves. To all beings.

"Then: 30 seconds of silence. Then I will ask you one question. You answer in one word, just to the person beside you.

"Then we sit and write in our journals.

"That is the whole thing."

**Vocabulary Moment:** "The word *dedicate* — to give something away, completely, without keeping it. After the release, we dedicate the merit. We don't hold on to the feeling, the goodness, the satisfaction. We open our hands again — and give it outward."

**Body Moment:** The release itself. The sensation of holding a living creature — its weight, its movement, the water it lives in — and then opening the hands. This is the body moment. There is nothing to add.

**Emotional Hook:** What happens in a student's face at the moment the creature enters the water. The teacher should not name it, explain it, or manage it. Let it be what it is — for each student, it will be different. Do not reach for a particular emotional response. Witness.

#### Turn and Talk — 2–3 minutes

*(After the release, at the site. Do not rush this.)*

Teacher: "Don't talk yet. Stand quietly for 30 seconds. Just be here."

Silence. 30 full seconds.

Then: "Tell the person beside you — one word. One word for what you feel right now."

Students whisper their word. Teacher should not ask for whole-class sharing here. This is between pairs, and that is enough.

#### Activity — 8–10 minutes

*(At the release site.)*

Students sit — on the ground, on rocks, on the bank — wherever they can sit safely. Teacher distributes the reflection journals and pencils.

Teacher, spoken quietly: "Write or draw what just happened. Not what you planned — what actually happened. What did you notice? What surprised you? Start anywhere."

Teacher does not circulate actively during this writing time — allow the space to be quiet and private. Sit nearby, available but not hovering. If a student is stuck: lean close and say quietly, "Just describe what you saw." Or: "What did your hands feel?"

This is not assessed for craft. It is a record of the unrepeatable first response. Even three words are valuable.

When time is up: collect journals and pencils. "I'm keeping these — you'll finish them next class."

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

*(Back at school, or at the site if time allows.)*

Teacher: "What did you know after the release that you didn't know before?"

Take 3–4 responses. Receive each one without commentary — without "yes, exactly" or "good answer." Just: "Yes." Or: nod. Or: let the response sit in the room. The answers belong to the students.

If no one speaks: "That's okay. Some things take time to become words. We'll come back to this question next class."

End with: "The creature is in the river right now." Let that be the last thing said in the debrief.

#### Closing — 3 minutes

*(At school, upon return. Or adapted at the site if time and circumstance permit.)*

Student leader resumes their role for the closing:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer**

Teacher's closing line — one sentence, specific to this day: "We did what we said we would do."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*

Carry forward: Some students will be visibly moved — tears, silence, a kind of solemnity. Some will seem matter-of-fact, or even chatty. Both responses are valid, and the matter-of-fact response does not indicate shallowness — processing often comes later, in a different register. Do not engineer or push for a particular emotional response. Your job during the release is to hold the space, not to perform emotion or suppress it.

The journal entry written at the site is the core of the culminating assessment. What the student noticed — not what they planned — is the evidence of learning. "The water was cold" and "I didn't want to let go" are both evidence. "I felt compassion for all beings" — without a specific, sensory anchor — is a performance. Specificity is the mark.

Note in your My Reflection which students seemed deeply affected, which were matter-of-fact, which seemed reluctant. These observations belong in the portfolio context. C6 is the integration class — bring all observations to it.

If any student cannot hold the creature or approaches the water with distress (fear, not reluctance — actual distress), honour it. They can stand at a slight distance and still chant. Inclusion in the ceremony is not the same as identical physical participation.

Spiral connections: The dedication of merit (Ngowa) connects forward to Grade 5's study of Tonglen — giving and taking. The gesture of opening the hands and releasing is the same gesture, at a more elementary level. In Grade 5, when students encounter Tonglen, the teacher can say: "Remember Grade 3 — releasing the fish — that opening of the hands? That is the gesture we are building on."
