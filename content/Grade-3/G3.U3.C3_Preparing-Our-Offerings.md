# G3.U3.C3 — Preparing Our Offerings
## Grade 3 · Unit 3 · Class 3 of 4 · Preparing Our Offerings
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Filling a water bowl or preparing a butter lamp → discovering that the gesture of care in making an offering is itself a practice, before the object is even placed
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Embodied cognition — the activity of preparing an offering (cleaning a bowl, filling it with care, holding it at heart level) changes understanding in a way that description alone cannot. The hands teach what the mind would only catalogue.
**Bloom's Level:** Understand / Apply — students understand what offerings mean in Himalayan Buddhist practice and why we make them; Apply is fully present as they prepare a real offering for C4
**Prior Class Summary:** C2 examined the three great moments — birth, enlightenment, and parinirvana — as teachings about arriving, waking, and releasing. Students added a three-moments page to their observance booklet.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** why offerings are made in Himalayan Buddhist practice: as expressions of generosity and letting go, not as transactions
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the mind that gives the offering — the quality of attention brought to the preparation — matters as much as the object itself
Students will **BE ABLE TO** prepare a simple offering with care and attention, and write one sentence about what they are putting into it
Students will **FEEL** the quiet, focused quality of making something with care for something beyond themselves — the experience of preparing-as-practice

---

### Materials Checklist
*Coordinate with the school's resident teacher and facilities before this class. Determine which type of offering is appropriate and available.*

**Option A — Water bowls:**
- Seven small bowls (or however many the school's tradition uses), cleaned and polished
- Clean water for filling
- A simple shrine or offering table, set up before students arrive
- Cloths for drying bowls if needed

**Option B — Butter lamps:**
- Butter lamp holders, wicks, and butter or ghee
- Safe lighting equipment (matches or lighter, held and used by the teacher or a supervising adult only)
- Fireproof surface and safe storage arranged in advance
- Adult supervision throughout — students participate in placing and preparing but an adult handles all flame contact

**Option C — Simple puja preparation:**
- Flower offerings, incense, or other materials the school's tradition supports
- Small trays or plates for arranging

**All options:**
- Students' Saga Dawa observance booklets (carried from C2)
- Pencils for the booklet entry
- **Plan B:** If none of the above can be arranged due to logistics, materials, or school policy — students prepare a symbolic offering: a small folded paper, or a drawing of an offering, prepared with the same quality of attention. The teacher is explicit: "The object matters less than the mind that gives it. Today we practise the mind." This is not a lesser version of the class — it is a direct teaching.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full prayer set, led by the student leader for this class.
Prayers in order: **Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer.**
*(Food Offering Prayer at closing only.)*

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
The teacher begins: "We have been learning about Saga Dawa — what it is, what it commemorates, how communities observe it. Now the question is: how do we observe it? What do we actually do? What does this school do? What will we do, in this room, on this day?"

Take responses. Connect to what the school actually does during Saga Dawa — if students know of pujas, offerings, or community practices, name them and connect to those. "What we do today is not separate from that. It is the same thing, at our scale."

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes
"In Himalayan Buddhist practice, offerings are a central part of how we show up for what is sacred. You have seen offerings on shrines — water bowls, butter lamps, flowers, incense, fruit. Why do we offer these things?"

Pause. Take 2–3 student responses before giving the answer.

"An offering is not a transaction. We are not paying for something — not buying merit, not purchasing the Buddha's attention. An offering is an expression of generosity: I value this thing, and I am letting it go. I am giving it, without expecting anything back. The Three Jewels — the Buddha, the Dharma, the Sangha — are worthy of our best, and we offer something of value to acknowledge that. The offering matters less than the mind that gives it."

Pause. "Let me say that again: the offering matters less than the mind that gives it. A simple bowl of clean water offered with full attention is more valuable than an elaborate offering prepared without care."

"The different types of offerings have meanings:
- Water bowls — purity, clarity, the offering of water for drinking and washing, seven times, representing the seven factors of enlightenment in some traditions.
- Butter lamps — the light of wisdom in the darkness, the dispelling of ignorance.
- Flowers — impermanence, beauty that does not last."

Then: "Today you will prepare your offering for next class — for our Saga Dawa observance. The preparation itself is practice."

**Vocabulary Moment:** "The word *tsok* — ཚོགས — a feast offering. In a tsok, food and sacred substances are offered and then shared among the community. What goes to the Three Jewels comes back to the community. Sharing is part of the offering. We will do a simple version of this principle at our observance next class — what we give, we give together."

**Body Moment:** The teacher demonstrates the gesture of making an offering — holding the bowl or object in both hands at heart level, with care and full attention. Students try this with whatever is in front of them — even an empty pair of hands, held at heart height, will do. "Feel the weight of what you're holding. Even if your hands are empty right now — feel the intention in the gesture. That intention is the practice. The object comes next."

**Emotional Hook:** The experience of making something with care for something beyond yourself. Even preparing the offering — cleaning a bowl carefully, filling it without spilling, holding it with both hands — is a practice in itself. This class is not building toward the ceremony; it is already the ceremony. Name this: "There is no moment when the practice begins and the preparation ends. The preparation is practice."

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
"Tell your partner: what is one thing you are willing to give as an offering? It doesn't have to be a physical thing — it can be time, a habit you are willing to give up, your full attention. What are you willing to let go of, during Saga Dawa, as an offering?"

Allow 2 minutes. Take 3–4 responses — receive whatever students offer genuinely. If a student says "my phone time" or "my habit of fighting with my sibling," both are equally valid as offerings of intention.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes
Students prepare their offerings. Depending on school resources and the options chosen above:

**If water bowls:** Students clean their bowl with a cloth, fill it with water carefully — not rushing, not spilling — and place it with both hands. The teacher circulates in near-silence, or with Om Mani Padme Hung chanted softly in the background. This is quiet, attentive making-time.

**If butter lamps:** Students prepare the lamp holder, place the wick, and hold the lamp ready. The teacher or supervising adult handles the actual lighting. Students hold the lit lamp with both hands briefly before it is placed safely.

**If symbolic offering:** Students fold or draw their offering with deliberate attention. Same quality of quiet applies.

After the offering is prepared: each student writes a short entry in their Saga Dawa observance booklet — "My offering for Saga Dawa: what it is, and what I am putting into it." This is not a description of the object — it is a description of the mind: "I am putting care into it. I am putting the intention to let go. I am putting my attention, for once, entirely on one thing."

Teacher circulates: "What are you putting into this? What quality are you trying to bring?"

The offerings are set aside safely for C4. They are ready.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
"The offering is prepared. Next class, we will present it. Tonight, and between now and then — let it be ready. Don't un-make it. The preparation has happened."

One reminder: "Next class is a ceremony, not a lesson. Come ready for that. Come with the quality of attention you brought to making the offering."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full prayer set. Student leader leads. **Food Offering Prayer included.**
Teacher's closing line: "You prepared an offering today with care. That care was already the practice — the ceremony next class will receive it. May what you prepared go toward the welfare of all beings."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The prepared offerings must be stored safely between C3 and C4. If butter lamps: the school must have appropriate storage and fire-safety arrangements — confirm this before C3 begins. If water bowls: refresh the water before C4 if more than a day passes. The Saga Dawa observance in C4 should feel like a real ceremony — not a classroom exercise. Arrange the room differently: remove extra furniture from a central space, or use the school's prayer room if available. Prepare the shrine before students arrive for C4. C4 is a ceremony, not a lesson, and the room should announce that before the students sit down.

Spiral connection: The gesture of making an offering with both hands at heart level — introduced here — will become the subject of direct study in Grade 4's "Language of Ritual" unit, where students will learn why offerings are held this way, what the different positions of hands mean, and how the body carries meaning in ritual. What is learned as practice here becomes curriculum there. Note this briefly to students if it arises naturally: "In Grade 4, you will learn the language behind what your hands are doing right now."
