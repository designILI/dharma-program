# G3.U2.C4 — Sharing Our Actions
## Grade 3 · Unit 2 · Class 4 of 4 · Sharing Our Actions
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Presenting one kind act to the class → experiencing what it means to be witnessed in something real
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Elaborative interrogation and synthesis — students articulate their experience of the unit across four parts (what I noticed, what I chose, what happened, what it cost), which deepens encoding and consolidates the unit's learning
**Bloom's Level:** Apply
**Prior Class Summary:** C3 deepened the practice — what makes a kind act genuinely kind versus performative, and what it costs. Students carry the word *reciprocity* and the felt experience of checking their own motivation.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the four-part structure for reflecting on a kind act: what I noticed, what I chose to do, what happened, what it cost
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the unit as a whole — across four classes — has produced something visible: a documented practice, not just good intentions
Students will **BE ABLE TO** share one honest account of a kind act from the unit in front of the class, using the four-part structure
Students will **FEEL** the quality of being witnessed — of saying something real in a room that is paying attention

---

### Materials Checklist
- The Kind Acts map, fully built from C1–C3, mounted and visible
- Space for students to walk the map (gallery walk) — ensure it is accessible from both sides if possible
- No new materials required for this class — everything needed is already on the wall and in the students' experience
- **Plan B:** If the room cannot accommodate a gallery walk (too small, or the map cannot be moved to allow circulation), run a seated map-reading first: pass around photographed sections of the map, or have students read their neighbour's entry aloud. The gallery walk is preferable but not essential — what matters is that the class sees the whole map as a body of work before the presentations begin.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full prayer set, led by the student leader for this class.
Prayers in order: **Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer.**
*(Food Offering Prayer at closing only.)*

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
The teacher invites the class to look at the Kind Acts map as a whole. "Look at everything on this wall. All of it, together. What do you notice?"

Take 3–4 responses. Students may notice: how many entries there are; specific acts that surprise them; the variety; the words "cost" appearing again and again. Do not lead the responses — let students bring what they actually see. Then: "This is what this unit produced. Not the idea of kindness — the actual record of it."

---

#### Mini Lesson — 3–5 minutes
Brief framing only — this is a synthesis class, not a new-concept class.

"Today each of you will share one act from this unit. Not the easiest one — the most honest one. The one that taught you the most about what kindness actually is, as opposed to what you thought it was. Here is the structure — four parts:

1. What you **noticed** — the moment you spotted.
2. What you **chose** to do — the deliberate act.
3. What **happened** — what actually occurred.
4. What it **cost** — time, effort, comfort, recognition.

That's the whole share. One to two minutes."

The teacher models once, briefly, using a specific and honest example — not a perfect one. Show a moment where the outcome was ambiguous, or where you noticed you also wanted recognition. The model should feel real, not instructional.

**No new vocabulary today.** This is a synthesis class.

**Body Moment:** Before shares begin: "Stand up. One full breath. Sit down." Simple, unhurried arrival. Students are now in a different mode — not listening-to-a-lesson mode, but present-and-attending mode.

**Emotional Hook:** The experience of being witnessed — of saying something true in a room where people are actually listening — is not nothing. Name this without over-announcing it: "This room is going to pay attention to what each person says. That is the container for this class. When someone is sharing, we give them our full attention."

---

#### Turn and Talk — embedded in the share structure
Before full-class presentations, run a structured pair-share (3–4 min): each student shares their four-part account with a partner. This is a rehearsal and a settling — it gives students a chance to hear their own story out loud before the full class.

Then: open to the full class. Invite 4–6 volunteers to share. The teacher listens actively and responds to specifics — not praise, but genuine attention: "You said the act cost you embarrassment — can you say more about that? What exactly felt embarrassing?" These follow-up questions are not tests; they are the sound of the room paying attention.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes
**Gallery walk (5–7 min):** Students walk the Kind Acts map, reading each other's entries. Quiet or near-quiet — no commentary required during the walk. Students can point to entries that strike them, but the primary mode is reading.

**Full-class presentations (10–12 min):** 4–6 volunteers share their four-part account with the class. After each share, the teacher names one specific thing they heard: "I noticed you said you did the act when no one was watching. That's the practice we were working toward." Keep the full-class presentations unhurried — four meaningful shares are better than eight rushed ones.

If time is short, the pair-share is sufficient as the assessment. The full-class share is richer but not strictly required for every student.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
"Unit 2 asked: how do we bring the same quality of care from the Tsethar into ordinary, non-ceremonial moments? What is your answer now — after four classes, after the map, after the shares?"

Take 3–4 responses. Hold the last response without resolving it — let it be the final word from the class, not a neat conclusion from the teacher. Then:

"Unit 3 begins next class. It is about a different kind of action — not the small, daily kind we've been practising, but the sacred and seasonal kind. Saga Dawa. The most sacred month in the Tibetan Buddhist calendar. The same quality of care — a different container. Neither one is more important. They complete each other."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full prayer set. Student leader leads. **Food Offering Prayer included.**
Teacher's closing line: "Unit 2 is complete. You carried the Tsethar's quality of care into ordinary life — and you documented it. May the good from everything on that wall go outward to all beings, and may it continue past this room."

---

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The Kind Acts map can be kept on display through Unit 3 and the end of the year, or photographed and archived. Either way, note that the map is a portfolio artefact for Unit 2 — each student's entries (documented kind act + what it cost) belong in the Grade 3 portfolio record.

Spiral connection — name this explicitly in the debrief: "You've now used a reflection structure twice. In Unit 1, it was: what I imagined it would be like → what happened → what I know now. In Unit 2, it was: what I noticed → what I chose → what happened → what it cost. These are the same structure, in different clothes. By the time you're in Grade 8, this structure will be second nature — you'll use it without being taught it." Name the pattern: the curriculum is building a portable reflective practice, not just content.

Unit 3 transition: The bridge from Unit 2 to Unit 3 is explicit. Unit 2 was the non-ceremonial, daily version of compassion in action. Unit 3 is the ceremonial, seasonal version. Saga Dawa is the container — sacred time — in which the same practice lives at a different scale. Both are necessary. The debrief question ("What is your answer now?") carries this forward.
