# G3.U3.C4 — Observing Together
## Grade 3 · Unit 3 · Class 4 of 4 · Observing Together
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Presenting offerings on a shrine and dedicating merit together → experiencing the amplifying quality of sacred time held collectively
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Consolidation through ceremony — the ceremony does not introduce new content; it consolidates everything the unit has built. The body, the room, the silence, and the collective attention do the work.
**Bloom's Level:** Apply — students apply everything they have understood across U3.C1–C3 in a real, embodied observance; early Evaluate is present as they reflect on how this felt different from an ordinary class
**Prior Class Summary:** C3 was the preparation class — students made their offerings and wrote a page in their observance booklet about what they are offering and what they are putting into it. They carry the instruction: "Let it be ready. Don't un-make it."

---

*Format note: This class is structured as a ceremony, not a conventional lesson. The schema is adapted to honour that — all fields are present, but the Lesson Body follows the arc of the ceremony.*

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the basic structure of a simple Saga Dawa observance: gathering, offering, prayer, dedication
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that merit dedicated outward through Ngowa is not diminished by being given away — it is completed by it
Students will **BE ABLE TO** present an offering with both hands at heart level, in the context of a real ceremony, with full attention
Students will **FEEL** the particular quality of a room in which everyone is quiet, present, and attending to something real together — what collective sacred attention actually feels like from the inside

---

### Materials Checklist
- All prepared offerings from C3, retrieved from safe storage and placed near the shrine before students arrive
- A shrine or designated offering space, arranged before students arrive — cloth on a surface, incense lit if appropriate, image or statue of the Buddha if available in the school
- The classroom arranged as ceremony space: chairs in a circle or arc facing the shrine, extra furniture moved to the side, or the school's dedicated prayer space used if available
- Students' Saga Dawa observance booklets, available for the final entry
- Pencils for the final booklet entry
- **Plan B:** If any offering from C3 was lost, damaged, or cannot be presented — the student brings themselves and their full attention. The teacher says quietly: "Your presence is the offering." Do not make this a moment of embarrassment; receive it simply and move on.

*Room setup is the teacher's responsibility before students arrive. The room should communicate the ceremony before anyone speaks.*

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Students enter a room that is already arranged as a ceremony space. The difference is visible before anyone speaks.

Full prayer set — chanted more slowly than usual, with the full weight of Saga Dawa. The student leader leads, but the teacher participates fully and sets the tone of pace and care.
Prayers in order: **Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer.**

The Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer is chanted with particular care today — this is the prayer that has been growing in the students' mouths since Unit 1. Let it take its full time.

*(Food Offering Prayer at closing only.)*

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 2 minutes
*(Not a conventional PKA — a settling.)*

The teacher: "Today is our Saga Dawa observance. We have prepared — three classes of preparation. Now we enter it. One breath together."

Full minute of silence. No instruction, no fidgeting allowed to derail it. Let the silence be a little longer than comfortable. Then, quietly: "We're ready."

---

#### Mini Lesson — 2–3 minutes
*(Framing only — not a teaching class.)*

"What we do today is small compared to what communities do during Saga Dawa across the Himalayan world. But it is real. We will present our offerings. We will recite our prayers together. And we will dedicate the merit. The dedication — the Ngowa — goes outward to all beings, in all directions, in all times. That is how this ceremony ends."

**Vocabulary Moment:** "The word *merit* — and the word *multiply*. Acts of compassion and practice performed during Saga Dawa are understood to generate merit that is multiplied many times over. We do not hold this as a mathematical claim. We hold it as a teaching about what happens when sacred time and collective intention meet. When this room is quiet and present together, something different is possible than when each of us acts alone."

**Body Moment:** The whole ceremony is a body moment. The specific gesture: holding the offering in both hands at heart level, walking forward, and placing it on the shrine. No rush. Both hands. Full attention. Every student does this.

**Emotional Hook:** The quality of a room in which everyone is quiet and genuinely present together for something real — not performed, not pretended — is not common in a child's life. This class is built to create it, briefly, and let students feel it from the inside.

---

#### The Observance — 20 minutes
*(This is the Activity, renamed to honour its form.)*

**1. Presentation of offerings (10 min):**
Students come forward one by one — no rush, no hurry between students — and place their offering on the shrine. Both hands at heart level. Then the bow, or a moment of stillness, before returning to their seat.

While students come forward: Om Mani Padme Hung is chanted softly, continuously, by the class — not loudly, not mechanically, but as a murmur that holds the room. The teacher sustains the chant and students join in as they are able.

No commentary during this portion. The silence and the mantra do the work.

**2. Full prayer set — recited together (7–8 min):**
After all offerings are placed, the class recites the full prayer set together, slowly, with full attention. More slowly than any previous class. The student leader leads. The teacher participates fully.

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer.**

**3. Teacher's reflection (1–2 min):**
After the prayers, the teacher offers one sentence about what this unit has been. Not a summary — a recognition. Something specific and true, in the teacher's own words. This is not scripted — it is the teacher's genuine observation of what this class and this unit have been. It might be: "You came into this unit having released a living creature. You leave it having offered something — your attention, your care, your time — to what is sacred. That is not nothing." Or it might be simpler, shorter, truer. The teacher must find the sentence themselves.

**4. Ngowa — dedication of merit (1 min):**
The class chants Ngowa together with particular intention. Before they begin, the teacher: "We send whatever good came from this unit — from the Tsethar, from the Kind Acts, from this observance today — outward. To all beings, everywhere, in all directions. We don't keep it. We give it."

Then: Ngowa, together.

---

#### Debrief — 3–5 minutes
Transfer question: "How did it feel to do a kind act during Saga Dawa, compared to doing it on an ordinary Tuesday? What was different — if anything?"

Take 2–3 responses. Do not resolve the question. Let it remain open — some students will feel a difference clearly; some will not; some will be uncertain. All three responses are honest and worth honouring. "This question can stay with you. You don't have to settle it today."

---

#### Closing — 5 minutes
*(The observance is the closing. The final element is the booklet entry.)*

Students open their Saga Dawa observance booklets to a final, fresh page. One prompt, written on the board: **"What I carry from this."**

Students write or draw for 3–4 minutes. No instruction beyond the prompt. This is their final entry — the booklet is now complete.

The booklets are collected for the portfolio or returned to students to keep, according to the school's practice.

The teacher offers a final line: "Grade 3 is complete. May everything you have learned this year — in Unit 1, Unit 2, and Unit 3 — go into the world with you, and serve beings you haven't met yet."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The completed Saga Dawa observance booklet is the portfolio artefact for Unit 3. Together with the Tsethar reflection journal (Unit 1) and the Kind Acts map contribution (Unit 2), these three artefacts constitute the Grade 3 portfolio — the record of a year in which compassion in action was practised in three forms: ceremonial, everyday, and sacred-seasonal.

Grade 3 is complete.

Thread into Grade 4: Grade 4 is "The Language of Ritual" — students will learn the seven-bowl water offering, make tormas, and study ritual objects. The transition: Grade 3 was about compassion in action in three registers — ceremonial (Tsethar), daily (Kind Acts), and sacred-seasonal (Saga Dawa). Grade 4 is about the language of the sacred: how ritual objects, gestures, and offerings encode meaning that the tradition has carried for centuries.

Name this transition explicitly to students: "In Grade 4, you will learn why we hold offerings with both hands, why the water bowls are placed in a specific order, and what the instruments of the shrine mean. The knowledge you build there will make what you already know — what you did today — deeper. It won't replace it. It will explain it."

The offering gesture students practised in C3 and performed today will become explicit curriculum content in Grade 4. The Saga Dawa ceremony they participated in today will be the lived experience that Grade 4's conceptual work is grounded in.
