# G3.U1.C3 — What Is Tsethar?

## Grade 3 · Unit 1 · Class 3 of 6 · What Is Tsethar?

*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** The practice of purchasing or rescuing a living creature and releasing it into appropriate habitat → merit, karma, and compassion made concrete through an act

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Elaborative Interrogation — students arrive having felt Tsethar from the inside (C1: imagination, C2: embodied perspective-taking). This class gives them the framework that makes sense of those feelings. Presenting the conceptual structure *after* the experiential foundation means concepts land on soil that is already prepared. The "both are changed" phrase gives students a generative question, not a finished answer.

**Bloom's Level:** Apply / early Analyze — Students receive the full conceptual and historical framework of Tsethar (Knowledge), then immediately apply it to their specific situation by writing planning notes. The debrief question ("what changes in the person who does the release?") begins the Analyze move — examining the practice from the outside, not just inside it.

**Prior Class Summary:** C2 took students inside the perspective of the creature they will release — curling small, imagining constraint, writing from the fish's point of view. Students carry a felt sense of what it is to live in a small space with a body that knows there is something larger. They carry the word *perspective* and the gesture of the body moving from constraint to openness.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** what Tsethar is — its traditional meaning, its basis in Buddhist ethics, the traditional elements (merit, karma, compassion for sentient beings, prayer, mantra, dedication of merit), and the principle that appropriate habitat is non-negotiable.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that Tsethar is a practice that operates in two directions simultaneously — it changes the creature (freedom) and the person doing it (something harder to name, the subject of the unit's transfer question).

Students will **BE ABLE TO** write initial planning notes answering four concrete questions: what creature, where, what prayers, what we need.

Students will **FEEL** the gravity of placing their specific act inside a tradition that is over a thousand years old — not as intimidation, but as a sense of belonging to something larger than this classroom, this week, this age.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 3 prayer set, led by the student leader for today:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer**

Before beginning: "Today I want to introduce a new prayer — but it goes at the *closing*, not the opening. It is called the Food Offering Prayer. I will explain it and teach it at the end of class. For now: let's begin."

Chant the Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer together as established. Teacher notes any students joining more confidently now, after two classes.

*(Food Offering Prayer introduced at closing today — see below.)*

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "We've been talking about Tsethar for two classes now. What do you know? What is it?" 

Take 4–5 responses. Hold back any corrections or additions — just collect. Write key words on the board as they come: *life release, fish, river, Dharma term, act, prayer.*

Then: "What do you *not* know yet? What are you still wondering about Tsethar?" This question matters more. Take 3–4 responses. These wonderings open the lesson. Hold them visibly — write them on the board too if possible — and return to them in the debrief: "Did we answer any of your questions today?"

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Teacher: "Today is the most information-heavy class of this unit. I'm going to give you the full picture of what Tsethar is — where it comes from, how it works, and the key principles for doing it well. Stop me with questions whenever you have them."

**Full explanation of Tsethar — given conversationally, not as a lecture:**

"Tsethar has been practised in the Himalayan Buddhist tradition for over a thousand years. The idea is ancient and simple: you find a living creature that would otherwise be killed — a fish in a market, a bird in a cage, an insect in distress — and you purchase or rescue it, and you release it into appropriate habitat. Not any habitat — the right one. A fish goes to a clean river. A bird goes to open sky. An insect goes to the garden or the forest edge. This part is not negotiable. A fish released into a concrete bowl is not Tsethar — it is cruelty wearing the clothes of kindness."

*(Pause. Let that land.)*

"Three reasons the tradition gives for doing Tsethar:"

"First: *compassion for sentient beings*. Every being wishes to be happy and free from suffering. When we release a creature, we are acting on that wish — for a specific being, in a specific moment, with our actual hands."

"Second: *merit* — or the Dharma term Sönam (བསོད་ནམས). Merit is the positive energy that accumulates from compassionate action. It is not a reward from outside — more like the natural consequence of acting with genuine care, the way warmth is the natural consequence of fire. When we do Tsethar, merit is generated."

"Third: *Ngowa* — the dedication of merit. You already know this prayer. When we dedicate the merit after the release, we do not keep it for ourselves. We offer it outward — to all beings. This is the same movement you practised in Grade 2 with the circles of compassion: start close, go wider, let it reach everything."

"During the release itself, we chant Om Mani Padme Hung. You already know this mantra. Chanting it while the creature enters the water consecrates the act — puts a blessing inside it."

Key point to name clearly: "Both are changed. The creature is changed — it is free. What changes in the person who releases it? That is the question this unit is holding. We won't answer it today. You will know after you have done it."

**Vocabulary Moment:** "The word *merit* — Sönam in Dharma term: བསོད་ནམས. It is not the same as a prize or a reward. It is more like the consequence of a cause. When you plant a seed in good soil and water it — the plant grows. Not because you deserve it; because that is what seeds and soil and water do together. Merit is like that: the natural growth that comes from acting with care. And Ngowa — the dedication — is the act of giving that growth away, outward, to all beings. Not keeping it. Giving it."

**Body Moment:** Teacher holds up a small glass of water with both hands, at heart level. "Before I offer this — I hold it here. Both hands. Heart level. That is the gesture of offering. It is different from holding something for yourself — when you hold something for yourself, it's lower, or one-handed, or close to the body in a different way."

"Try it — hold whatever is in your hands right now — a pencil, your notebook — with both hands, at heart level. Feel the difference." Students try. Give them ten seconds.

"The gesture of offering is in the body. When we do the release, we will hold the creature — in its container, or in our cupped hands — at heart level, with both hands. The practice is in that gesture as much as in any word."

**Emotional Hook:** "Both are changed." The creature is changed — that is visible. What changes in the person is invisible and is the real subject of the unit. Don't explain it. Just name it, and let it be a question students carry.

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Teacher: "Tell your partner: what do you think changes in the person who does the release? What might you know, after, that you don't know now? Be specific — not 'I'll feel good.' What would you actually know that you don't know now?"

Structure: Partners discuss freely (3 minutes, no assigned A/B this time). What to listen for: whether students move toward the experiential ("you'd know what it's like to hold something alive," "you'd know what a river sounds like up close") rather than the abstract ("you'd know that releasing is good").

After: "One response from the room — what might change in the person?" Take 1–2 responses.

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

Teacher: "We're going to start planning our Tsethar today. I will confirm all the logistics in the next class — but today, you're going to answer four planning questions. Write them in your notebook."

Write the four questions on the board — or read them and have students copy:

1. *What creature will we release?*
2. *Where will we release it?* (Describe the place — what kind of water? What kind of habitat?)
3. *What prayers or mantras will we say during the release?*
4. *What do we need to prepare or bring?*

Teacher: "Write your best answers now — based on what you've learned so far. These are not final; we will refine them together in C4. But I want to know what you're thinking."

Circulate. Key questions to ask individual students:
- "Why that place? Why is it appropriate for the creature?"
- "What are you still not sure about?"
- "What's on your list of things we need?"

**Scaffold:** For students who are uncertain, narrow the question: "Start with question 3 — you already know the prayers. What would you say during the release?" This gives them a toehold.

**Extension:** Students who finish all four questions may write a fifth: *What do you expect to feel at the moment of release? Describe it as specifically as you can.* (This will be compared to the actual experience in C6.)

Collect these planning notes at the end of class or let students keep them in their notebooks — they will be pasted into the Tsethar reflection journal in C4. If kept in notebooks, remind students to bring their notebooks every class.

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

"Did we answer any of the questions you had at the start of class?" Look back at what was written on the board. Take a response for each question that was addressed.

Then: "Tsethar has been practised in the Himalayan Buddhist tradition for over a thousand years. Thousands of people, before us, have held a living creature in both hands at heart level and let it go. We are about to add our act to that line. That is not small." 

Pause: "One thing to remember before next class: the creature we will release exists right now, somewhere. It is alive right now. That's true whether we think about it or not."

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full prayer set:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer**

Then — **introduce the Food Offering Prayer** for the first time:

Teacher: "Now we add the Food Offering Prayer to our closing. This prayer is said before eating — we offer the food to the Three Jewels and to all beings. Even our meals become an act of care. I will chant it once through slowly, explaining each phrase as I go. Just listen today — the words will come."

Teacher chants the Food Offering Prayer from the authorised school version (PRAYER-REFERENCE.md), pausing briefly to give a plain-language gloss of each line. After: "From today, this closes our class. You don't need to know it perfectly yet — just be present with it."

Teacher's closing line: "The next two classes are preparation and practice. After that: the act itself."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*

Carry forward: The planning notes from today's activity are the foundation for C4's full planning session — have students bring their notebooks, or collect the notes yourself. The Food Offering Prayer joins the standing set from today's closing onward. By C6 students should be joining in naturally; do not pressure them to memorise it on a schedule.

**Critical logistics note:** The release (C5) requires confirmed logistics before C4 is taught. Before teaching C4, the teacher must have confirmed with school administration: (1) permission to purchase and release a living animal; (2) appropriate release site — a clean stream, pond, or garden; (3) transport arrangements; (4) adequate adult supervision; (5) the specific date of C5. C3 is the moment students understand the full shape of the practice — if logistics are unresolved, address them this week without fail. C4 tells students that everything is confirmed; that must be true.

Spiral connections: The concept of merit (Sönam) and Ngowa connects directly to Grade 2's work on Bodhichitta and the concentric circles of compassion — the Grade 2 movement was sending wishes outward; Grade 3's Ngowa is giving merit outward. Students who made that connection in Grade 2 will feel its continuity here. Name it if it comes up: "In Grade 2 you practised sending wishes outward in circles — Ngowa is that same movement, but after an act, not just in the heart."
