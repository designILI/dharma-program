# G2.U2.C1 — The Prince in the Palace
## Grade 2 · Unit 2 · Class 1 of 5 · The Prince in the Palace
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A prince growing up inside a beautiful, walled palace → the way protection from suffering can also be protection from truth

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Narrative anchoring — students build an initial mental model of Siddhartha through sensory-rich story. The contrast between the palace's comfort and the concealed world beyond its walls creates productive cognitive tension that will persist across the unit. Children do not resolve this tension today; they sit inside it.

**Bloom's Level:** Remember / Understand

**Prior Class Summary:** In U1 C7 (Sharing the Circle), students presented their concentric-circles diagrams, hung their prayer flags, and named the practice Bodhichitta — a sense of unit completion and expanded care. They carry a felt sense of the widening circle. This class opens Unit 2 by asking: who first walked that path? Who first modelled expanding compassion by facing suffering directly?

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** that Siddhartha Gautama was born a prince; that his father King Suddhodana tried to hide all suffering from him; that his mother Queen Maya died when he was a baby and he was raised by his aunt Pajapati; and that he grew up inside palace walls without knowing old age, sickness, or death.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that being shielded from difficulty does not make the difficulty disappear — and that a life without contact with suffering cannot yet understand it or help others through it.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** describe two or three details about Siddhartha's early life in the palace and say, in one sentence, why the king kept the gates closed.

Students will **FEEL** a gentle, specific unease — the sensation of a comfortable world that might also be incomplete; the sense that something real lies just beyond what one is allowed to see.

---

### Materials Checklist
- *Stories of the Buddha* or teacher's preferred illustrated anchor text (preview before class; mark pages for the birth narrative and early palace life)
- Whiteboard or large chart paper + marker (for beginning the unit timeline — "Palace [gates closed]" is today's first entry)
- Drawing paper, one sheet per student
- Crayons or coloured pencils
- **Plan B:** If the anchor text is unavailable, the story can be told orally near-verbatim from the narrative in the Mini Lesson section below. The oral version is full and complete — no additional preparation required.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
The rotating student leader leads the full Grade 2 prayer set:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa**

The teacher sits with the class, unhurried, fully present. The prayer set is not rushed or abbreviated.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
The teacher says: *"Last class, we hung our flags and talked about Bodhichitta. Before I say anything else — take one breath. Now: what does Bodhichitta mean? Don't look around. Just see what's in your own mind."*

The teacher waits. Takes three or four responses without evaluating. Then: *"You said [student's word]. And you said [student's word]. So Bodhichitta is about growing the circle of who we care about. Good. Hold that."*

*"One more question: what was at the very centre of the circle?"* (The self, this person.) *"And what was at the very outside?"* (All beings, everyone.)

*"Today we begin a new story. It's going to last us five classes. And it begins with a question: who first walked the path of that widening circle? Who modelled this kind of expanding compassion? We're going to find out — starting from the very beginning."*

**Fallback if energy is low:** *"Turn to your neighbour. Thirty seconds — tell them one thing you remember about our flags from last time."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes
*"Today we begin one of the most important stories in Buddhism — and in the world. It begins a very long time ago, in a kingdom in the foothills of the Himalayas. Not so far from here."*

[Open anchor text or begin oral narration:]

*"A king and queen lived in a beautiful palace. The king's name was Suddhodana. The queen's name was Maya. And they had a son. His name was Siddhartha."*

*"When Siddhartha was born, a wise man came to the palace and looked at the baby. He said: 'This child will be either a great king — or a great holy teacher.' The king heard this and thought: a great king. My son will be a great king. And he made a decision: nothing will stop that from happening."*

[Pause. Let that decision land.]

*"The king loved his son. More than anything. And because he loved him, he gave orders: the gates of the palace will stay closed. Siddhartha will have everything inside these walls — beautiful gardens, delicious food, music, friends, games. He will never be cold. He will never be hungry. He will never see anyone who is old. He will never see anyone who is sick. He will never see anyone dying."*

*"So Siddhartha grew up inside the palace. He had everything a child could want. He was healthy and strong and happy — or something that looked like happiness. The king gave him teachers and elephants and horses. He had everything — except the world."*

[At this point in the narrative of Siddhartha's earliest life:]

*"His own mother, Queen Maya, died when Siddhartha was just a baby. So he was raised by her sister — his aunt, Pajapati — who loved him as her own. She has her own story, and one day you'll hear it."*

[Move on immediately. Do not pause for questions about Pajapati. If a child asks, say: *"We'll hear it when the time is right — it's a good question to hold."* And continue.]

*"Siddhartha grew up. He grew up happy — or something that looked like happiness. He never saw an old person. He never saw someone sick. He didn't know that bodies wear out. He didn't know that people die. His whole world was beautiful and safe — and the gates stayed closed."*

**Vocabulary Moment:** *"The word for what his father built for him is a kind of shelter — not just a building, but a protected world. A place where the hard things of life were kept outside. Has anyone ever had the feeling that something was being kept away from you — to protect you? We'll hold that question."*

**Body Moment:** *"Let's try something. Sit very comfortably. Like you're resting on the softest cushion you've ever felt. Lean back a little. Relax your hands. Close your eyes if you want. You have everything you need. You're safe. You're warm."* [Ten seconds of silence.] *"Now — what don't you hear? What don't you see? What's outside? What's on the other side of the wall?"* [Brief pause. Eyes open.] *"That's where Siddhartha lived. Inside the silence."*

**Emotional Hook:** The king loved his son. That is real — he was not a cruel man. He was a father who was afraid. Most children know what it feels like when someone who loves them tries to protect them from something. Sometimes that protection is needed. And sometimes — we need to find out for ourselves.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
**Prompt:** *"If someone who loved you very much tried to keep every difficult thing away from you — would that be kind? What might they be missing? Talk to the person next to you."*

Partners talk for two minutes. The teacher circulates and listens — not correcting, just hearing. Then: *"I heard someone say [quote something real from the room]. That's interesting. Anyone else?"*

**What to listen for:** "You can't learn if you don't know what's real." / "Maybe they just don't want you to feel sad." / "Maybe you'd wonder even more about what's out there." All of these are valid. Do not resolve the tension — let it stand.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes
*"We're going to start a class timeline today — we'll add to it every class. First: let's draw."*

*"On your paper, draw what you imagine Siddhartha's palace looked like. Give yourself five minutes — any details you want. Big gates? Gardens? Animals? Music? Put it in."*

[Five to seven minutes of drawing. Teacher circulates — asks: "What's one thing inside the palace?" and "What do you imagine is right outside the gates that Siddhartha has never seen?"]

*"Now — on the back of your paper, or in the corner: draw or write what's outside the walls. What is Siddhartha not seeing?"*

[Five minutes. Then pairs share briefly. Teacher sketches "Palace — gates closed" as the first entry on the class timeline and posts it where it will remain for the full unit.]

**Extension:** *"If you finish early: what do you think Siddhartha wonders about? What questions might grow inside someone who can never go outside?"*

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
*"Here's what I want to ask: the king built this beautiful world for his son. He thought: if Siddhartha is always comfortable and happy, he will become a great king. Do you think it worked? Do you think Siddhartha was actually happy — or was there something else mixed in with the happiness?"*

Take three or four responses. Do not resolve the question. Let complexity stand.

*"We'll find out what happened next time."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
The rotating student leader leads the full Grade 2 prayer set:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa**

Teacher sentence, specific to today: *"Today we met a prince who had everything — and next class, we'll find out what he couldn't stop looking for."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*

Carry forward: This class plants two things that must not be resolved prematurely. First: the productive discomfort of the palace — comfort that conceals. The four sights in C2 land much harder if students have genuinely sat inside the protected world first; don't skip the Body Moment or rush past the description of palace life. Second: the Pajapati seed has been planted. Do not return to it or explain it further in this or any later class until the designated curriculum moment arrives. If students ask about her in later classes, honour the question briefly and redirect: *"Her story is coming — it's worth waiting for."* This thread pays off in Grade 6 (Bardo unit and Tara unit) and fully in Grade 8 (novel study). — The unit timeline begun today should be posted on the wall and added to in C2, C3, and C4. Keep it visible throughout the unit. — Spiral connections: U1 C1 (first encounter with the Buddha as a figure, Grade 2 students heard his name); U1 C7 (Bodhichitta as expanding circle — this unit shows who first modelled that path). — If telling the story orally rather than from the text, the narrative above can be read near-verbatim. Trust the story; don't embellish or modernise.
