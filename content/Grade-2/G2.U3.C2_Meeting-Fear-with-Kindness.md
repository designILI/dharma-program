# G2.U3.C2 — Meeting Fear with Kindness
## Grade 2 · Unit 3 · Class 2 of 4 · Meeting Fear with Kindness
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Standing steadily beside someone who is afraid → kindness is not the same as fixing; a calm presence can shift what another person is feeling, even when the frightening thing doesn't go away.

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Embodied simulation. The body exercise in this lesson — standing still and calm across from a partner who is wearing a worried face — creates a felt understanding of "compassionate steadiness" that no amount of discussion can replicate. Students don't just hear that being near a calm person can change your own fear; they feel it happen. The concept is in the body before it is in the words.

**Bloom's Level:** Understand — Students revisit the Shelging Karmo story through their own experience, asking: what did Guru Rinpoche's steadiness actually do, and have I ever done that or had that done for me? The body exercise moves this from abstract understanding to felt recognition. Early Apply surfaces when students name a real worry in the Turn and Talk.

**Prior Class Summary:** In C1, students heard the Shelging Karmo story for the first time and began their helper drawings — an outline of someone who helps them when they're scared. They encountered the concept of transformation through compassion, and did a brief body moment of sitting as Guru Rinpoche sat.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** that Guru Rinpoche's response to the frightening spirit was to stay present — not to attack, escape, or fix.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that being calm and present beside someone who is afraid can shift their fear, even when the source of fear remains.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** name a real, current worry — briefly and without elaboration — and imagine what it might look like to have someone sit with them in it.

Students will **FEEL** the specific, physical sensation of being steadied by another person's calm — experienced directly in the body exercise, not just understood as an idea.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Students' helper drawings from C1 (stored from last class)
- Coloured pencils — one set per student or shared on tables
- Pencils
- Enough floor or standing space for pairs to stand facing each other (desks can be pushed slightly if needed)

**Plan B — if the classroom cannot accommodate standing pairs:** Do the body exercise seated. Partners sit facing each other. One puts on a worried expression and posture (hunched, eyes down); the other sits tall, open, eyes steady. The exercise works just as well seated. Note in your reflection if the standing version was unavailable.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
The student leader for today leads the full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa. Teacher settles the room before handing to the student leader — one breath together. Unhurried throughout.

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes
*"Last class, Guru Rinpoche faced something frightening. He didn't fight it. He didn't run. What did he do?"*

Take 2–3 responses. Listen for: he sat down; he looked at her; he stayed. Gather these on the board or simply repeat them aloud as students offer them. Then:

*"Why do you think that worked? Why did sitting and looking change what happened?"*

Take 3–4 responses. Do not resolve the question yet. Responses may include: because she was surprised; because no one had ever done that before; because he wasn't afraid; because he saw her. Hold them all. *"Let's think about this more today — not just about Guru Rinpoche, but about us."*

#### Mini Lesson — 12 minutes
*"We're going to think about fear today — not Guru Rinpoche's — ours. Has anyone here ever helped a friend who was scared of something? Not by fixing it — not by making the scary thing go away — just by being there? What did you do?"*

Take 2–3 stories. After each: *"Did the scary thing go away?"* (Sometimes yes; often no.) *"But something changed anyway. What changed?"*

Listen for: the friend felt less alone; they calmed down a little; they could keep going even though they were still scared.

**Vocabulary Moment:** *"Here is something important: courage is not the absence of fear. Courage is what you do while you are still afraid. You've all been courageous. Every single one of you — you've done hard things while still being scared. That is courage."*

Pause. Let it settle.

**Body Moment:** *"We're going to try something now. Stand up, and find a partner. Stand facing each other — arms-length apart."*

Wait for students to be in pairs and standing.

*"One of you — the one closer to the windows — you are the worried one. Think of something that feels a little worrying right now. It doesn't have to be big. Let that show on your face and in your body. You can look down a little, or hold your arms close. Just feel something that feels a bit much."*

*"The other person — don't say anything. Don't smile, don't make a funny face. Just stand tall. Look at your partner steadily — like you're really there. Not staring — just present. Fifteen seconds."*

Count quietly to fifteen. Then: *"Switch. Now the other person is the worried one."*

After both rounds: *"Sit down. What happened? What did you notice — when you were the worried one, with someone standing calm in front of you?"*

Take 4–5 responses. This is the heart of the lesson. Students may say: I felt a little less worried; I didn't want to move; it was strange; it was actually calming; I kept looking at them; it helped. Receive each one.

**Emotional Hook:** *"There is a kind of bravery that doesn't look brave from the outside. It looks like just being still. It looks like ordinary. But it isn't. That's what Guru Rinpoche showed the spirit."*

Leave that without further comment.

#### Turn and Talk — 5 minutes
*"Tell your partner: what's one thing that feels a little scary right now — something real in your life, not something imaginary. Just name it. You don't have to explain it."*

Give adequate time. Then:

*"Now: what would it look like if someone sat with you in that — the way Guru Rinpoche sat with the spirit? Not to fix it. Just to be there."*

Circulate and listen. Do not intervene unless a student is distressed. What you are listening for: students naming real, specific worries (a test, a difficult friendship, something at home, something they're uncertain about). These concrete anchors mean the concept has found a real home. Students who stay vague or stuck on imaginary scenarios may need the teacher's quiet presence beside them during this turn: *"Even something small counts — something that made you tense this week."*

#### Activity — 10 minutes
Return students' helper drawings from C1.

*"Today you add the setting. Where is your helper? Where are you? What is happening in the picture? Your helper doesn't have to be doing something dramatic — they might just be near you. Add the place, and what's happening."*

Students work. Teacher circulates: *"What's your helper doing in this picture?"* This question is simple and generative — most students will answer it immediately and the answer will reveal whether they are representing active intervention (helping do something) or quiet presence (just being there). Both are worth noting.

Extension: *"Look at your helper's hands. What are they doing? Draw that."*

#### Debrief — 4 minutes
*"Guru Rinpoche saw something frightening and didn't treat it as the enemy. He treated it as something that could change. Do we ever do that?"*

Take 3–4 responses. Receive them.

*"When? Tell me a time."*

Students may offer examples: when a sibling is upset; when a friend is crying; when a younger child is scared of something. Receive each one. Then:

*"You were already doing this — before you knew this story. The story just has a name for it now."*

#### Closing — 3 minutes
The student leader leads the full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa. Unhurried.

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: *"Being steady for someone else when they're scared — that takes something real. You found out today what it feels like from both sides. That's not a small thing to know."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The body exercise — standing steadily across from a worried partner — is the prototype for what students will encounter more formally in Grade 4 and 5 in the context of difficult emotions and the Wheel of Life. It is worth being specific in your reflection about what happened in the room during those thirty seconds. Which students were able to hold a genuinely steady presence? Which became giggly or broke eye contact — and was that nervousness or something else? Which students, as the "worried" partner, noticeably settled? These observations are the real assessment record for this class. They are not grades; they are portraits. The helper drawing now has a figure and a setting. In C3, students will add a line of the Seven-Line Prayer. In C4, they will add final details and share. Keep drawings safe between classes. Spiral note: The vocabulary word "courage" as "what you do while you're still afraid" is first introduced here. It will be revisited in Grade 3 in the Tsethar unit, where students take a real action on behalf of living beings — an act that requires some courage from some children. Planting the definition now, in the body and in story rather than as a dictionary entry, gives it a felt home to return to.
