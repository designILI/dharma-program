# G2.U2.C3 — Leaving Everything
## Grade 2 · Unit 2 · Class 3 of 5 · Leaving Everything
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Siddhartha riding out of the palace gates at midnight, leaving his sleeping family behind → the nature of a sacrifice made not from coldness but from something that cannot be stopped

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Perspective-taking and cognitive dissonance — students are asked to hold two valid and contradictory positions at once: Siddhartha's departure is both necessary and a loss. The emotional complexity is not resolved. Holding it without forcing a verdict is the intellectual and moral work of this class.

**Bloom's Level:** Understand

**Prior Class Summary:** In C2, students witnessed the four sights alongside Siddhartha — old age, sickness, death, the holy seeker. The class ended with the question alive: what will he do now? Students carry the weight of four encounters with reality that cannot be undone.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** that Siddhartha left the palace in the middle of the night; that he left behind his wife Yasodhara, his baby son Rahula, and his father the king; that his servant Channa saddled his horse Kanthaka and rode out with him; and that this departure is called the Great Renunciation.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that some choices are both right and painful at the same time — Siddhartha's departure is not a triumphant scene but a wrenching one; the people he left behind also mattered, and their grief is real and valid.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** retell the departure scene in their own words, naming at least two people Siddhartha left behind.

Students will **FEEL** the specific ache of a complicated choice — the sense that love and leaving can coexist; that something can be both necessary and a loss.

---

### Materials Checklist
- *Stories of the Buddha* / illustrated anchor text (mark the departure passage)
- Class timeline (posted — add today)
- Drawing paper or reflection journals, one per student
- Crayons and pencils
- **Plan B:** If students seem particularly unsettled by the departure story, offer the drawing paper as a drawing-only option — students process the story through image rather than words. Do not rush anyone toward verbal response. If a child sits quietly with the story for the full activity time, that is a valid response.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
The rotating student leader leads the full Grade 2 prayer set:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa**

Teacher present, unhurried.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
*"What did Siddhartha see the last time we met? Start anywhere — what comes to you first?"*

Take three or four responses. Then:

*"At the end of last class I asked: what do you think he did when he got home? What did you think — what did you picture?"*

Take two or three responses.

*"Today we're going to find out. And I'll tell you now: it's a hard story. Not scary-hard. Honest-hard. So settle in."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes
*"After the four sights, Siddhartha went back to the palace. That night there was a feast — music, dancing, everyone celebrating around him. But he couldn't enjoy it anymore. He looked at all the people laughing and he felt — separate. Like he was watching from behind glass."*

*"The question the four sights had put inside him wouldn't go away: why do people suffer? Why is there old age, sickness, and death? And what — if anything — can be done about it?"*

*"Late that night, the music stopped and everyone fell asleep. Siddhartha got up very quietly. He walked to the room where his wife, Yasodhara, was sleeping. Their baby son, Rahula, was sleeping in her arms."*

[Pause. Take a full breath before continuing.]

*"He stood there. He looked at them for a long time. He did not wake them. He thought: if I wake her, if I say goodbye, she will beg me to stay. And maybe I won't be able to go. And the question will never be answered, and I will live the rest of my life behind these walls not knowing."*

*"He turned. He walked out. He went to the stables. His servant Channa saddled his horse, Kanthaka. They rode out through the gates — into the dark — and kept riding until the palace was far behind them."*

*"Siddhartha looked back once. Just once. Then he rode on."*

[Silence. Ten seconds. Do not fill it.]

*"His father woke in the morning and found him gone. Yasodhara woke up and found him gone. Rahula — too young to understand anything — didn't know yet that his father had left."*

**Vocabulary Moment:** *"The word for what Siddhartha did is renunciation — giving something up. Not because it was bad or worthless. But because something else called louder. Renunciation is harder than giving up something you don't care about. He gave up things he loved."*

**Body Moment:** *"Put your hand on your heart for a moment. Think of something you love — a person, a place, an animal. Don't say it out loud. Just feel it. Now imagine leaving it — not because you wanted to, but because something inside you said you had to. What happens in your body? Where do you feel it?"* [Five seconds.] *"That's a piece of what Siddhartha felt when he looked back that once."* [Hands down.]

**Emotional Hook:** This is not a triumphant moment, and the teacher should not make it one. Some tellings of this story cast the departure as heroic and clean. It is not. It is a person doing something that costs him everything, because something inside him has made staying impossible. These are two different things, and both must be honoured.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
**Prompt:** *"Can you think of a time when someone had to leave — or when you had to give something up — not because it was bad, but because something else mattered more? Or: was Siddhartha right to go? Was he wrong? Can he be both?"*

Structure: Two minutes with a neighbour. Then a brief whole-class share. What to listen for: students who resist the binary (right/wrong) and reach for something more complex. Both verdicts — right and wrong — are available and worth naming. The goal is not consensus but genuine engagement with a hard question.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes
**"Two Windows"**

*"On your paper, draw a line down the middle."*

*"On the left side: draw or write what Siddhartha was riding toward — the question, the search, the truth he couldn't stop needing to find."*

*"On the right side: draw or write what he left behind — his wife, his son, his father, the only home he had ever known."*

[Ten to twelve minutes of work. Teacher circulates — asks quietly: "What's on your left side? Tell me one thing." / "Who did you put on the right side?"]

*"Now — look at both sides. Is one side heavier than the other?"*

[Brief partner share, then two or three responses to the class. Teacher does not adjudicate — receives.]

[Add to class timeline: **"Great Departure — midnight."**]

**Extension:** *"If you finish early: what do you think Yasodhara said or felt when she woke up? You can draw or write her side of the story."*

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
*"Here's the question I want to leave with you: was Siddhartha selfish — or was he brave? Is it possible to be both at the same time?"*

Take three or four responses. Do not close the question down — let it remain open. Then:

*"Some of the greatest questions don't have one answer. This is one of them. It's worth holding."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
The rotating student leader leads the full Grade 2 prayer set:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa**

Teacher sentence, specific to today: *"Today Siddhartha left everything. Next time, we'll find out what he found."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*

Carry forward: Do not sanitise the departure. The teacher's willingness to hold the grief of Yasodhara and the king — without resolving it in Siddhartha's favour — is what makes this story honest and therefore worth teaching. Students will sense if the story is being tidied up. The departure is wrenching because it is also right; that combination is the entire moral weight of C3. Neither side of the Two Windows activity should be dismissed or minimised. — The Two Windows drawings are among the strongest portfolio pieces of the unit. Collect them at the end (or photograph them). — If any student raises the question "Did he abandon his family?" receive it fully: *"Yes. That's the hard thing. He did leave them. And the reason he gave was that he had to find the answer to something bigger than his own life. You can decide how you feel about that."* Do not resolve it for them. — Spiral connections: Grade 5 Ethics unit will return to the ethics of sacrifice and duty (dharma vs. self-interest); this moment in Grade 2 plants the affective seed that makes the Grade 5 analysis meaningful. — The name "Rahula" means "fetter" or "bond" — Siddhartha named him this, knowing. This is worth knowing as a teacher but need not be introduced to students in Grade 2 unless they ask.
