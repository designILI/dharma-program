# G2.U1.C3 — The Inner Ring (Family)
## Grade 2 · Unit 1 · Class 3 of 7 · The Inner Ring (Family)
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Drawing one person's name in the centre of a circle → Sending a specific, felt wish to someone who needs it right now

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Concrete-to-abstract anchoring — the guided meditation is made emotionally accessible by keeping it rigorously *particular* (one person, one face, one place they might be right now) before any movement toward abstraction. Students who can picture a specific face can generate a real wish; students asked to feel "love for family" often feel nothing. Particularity is the engine.

**Bloom's Level:** Apply. Students take the concept introduced in C2 (a wish can be aimed) and apply it inwardly — locating a specific person, generating a specific wish, and then externalising that wish by drawing it in the diagram. Some students begin early Analyze when they notice what the wish feels like inside them.

**Prior Class Summary:** In C2, students hung their completed flags and were introduced to the idea that who we include in our circle of care is a choice we can make consciously. Each student wrote or drew three names on an index card — the first sketch of their personal circle. That card arrives with them today.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** what the centre ring of their concentric circles diagram represents — the people who are closest to them, who they carry with them all the time.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that Sem kye is the aspiration to become wise and kind not just for oneself, but for everyone — and that the meditation they are learning is one way of practising it.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** sit through a 3–4 minute guided meditation and hold one specific person in mind; some will generate a felt wish for that person; some will feel willing to try.

Students will **FEEL** the particular quiet that comes from holding one real, specific person in mind with care — a feeling that is somewhere between tenderness and effort, and that is new and slightly unfamiliar at this age.

---

### Materials Checklist

- Index cards from C2 (one per student — redistribute from teacher's collection, or have students retrieve from Dharma folders)
- Large concentric circles diagram sheets, pre-drawn (one per student): A3 or tabloid-sized paper with five concentric circles drawn in light pencil or printed — a small centre circle, then four expanding rings. Label the rings lightly in pencil: Centre (Family), Ring 2 (Friends & Neighbours), Ring 3 (Animals), Ring 4 (All Beings). Students will fill these over C3–C6
- Pencils (one per student) — for the diagram, not paint
- Coloured pencils or crayons (available but not required)
- A quiet, settled classroom — desks or floor space arranged so students can sit comfortably with eyes closed without touching neighbours. **Plan B:** If the room cannot be rearranged, students remain at desks; ask them to push books aside and rest hands on the desk in front of them
- Himalayan singing bowl or a small bell (for opening and closing the meditation) — **Plan B:** If no bell is available, the teacher uses a slow, audible inhale and exhale as the signal to open and close
- Soft, even lighting — natural light preferred; overhead fluorescents dimmed if possible

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader for today leads the full Grade 2 prayer set for the first time. Before beginning, the teacher says:

"Starting today, we add two new prayers to our opening and closing. The first one is called Sem kye. I will say it first so you can hear it. You don't need to join in yet — just listen."

The teacher chants or recites Sem kye once, slowly and clearly.

"Sem kye means: 'I want to become wise and kind — not just for myself, but for everyone.' That is what this whole year is about. We'll say it every class from now on. Join when it feels right — there is no rush to memorise."

The student leader then leads:
1. **Kyap Dro** (Refuge)
2. **Om Mani Padme Hung** (7 times)
3. **Manjushri Prayer**
4. **Sem kye** — teacher leads; students may join or listen

*Note: Ngowa (Dedication of Merit) is introduced at today's closing. See Closing section below.*

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

Distribute the index cards from C2.

"You wrote or drew three names on this card last week — three people or beings you wanted to send a wish to. Take a moment to look at your card."

*Pause 20 seconds.*

"Who is the first name on your list? Or the first face you see when you look at it?"

Take three or four responses. Listen for: family members (very likely), friends, pets, community figures.

"These people — the ones at the very top of your list, the ones you thought of first — they're already in your circle. They've been there all along. Today we're going to make that circle visible."

Distribute the concentric circles diagram sheets. "This is a map of your circle of care. We're going to fill it in over the next four classes — one ring at a time. Today: just the centre."

---

#### Mini Lesson — 8 minutes

Point to the centre circle on the diagram.

"The centre — the smallest circle, right in the middle — is for the people who are closest to you. Family. The people you live with, the people who would worry if you were sick, the people you think of when you're far from home."

"But here's what we're going to do today that's different from just writing names: we're going to *find* one of those people. Inside. In your mind. And we're going to send them a wish."

"This is a kind of meditation. You've probably sat with your eyes closed before. But today, instead of just sitting still, we're going to use our imagination like a hand — reaching out toward someone specific."

"Why? Because a wish with a real person behind it is stronger than a wish into the air. It's the difference between shouting 'someone be okay!' and looking at a specific face and saying, 'I want *you* to be okay.'"

**Vocabulary Moment:** "The prayer we learned at the opening — Sem kye — means wanting to become wise and kind for everyone. The meditation we're about to do is a small practice of exactly that. It starts with one person. It won't end there."

**Body Moment:** "Before we begin the meditation, put both hands flat on your knees. Feel the weight of your own hands. Now take one slow breath — in through the nose, out through the mouth. That breath is the door. We walk through it together."

**Emotional Hook:** The moment of silence just before the meditation begins — the teacher's voice drops, the room settles, and students feel themselves shift into a different register of attention. Do not rush past this moment.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3 minutes

"Before we go inside, turn to the person next to you. Tell them: is there one person in your family — not just who you love most, but someone who might be struggling or worried or tired right now? You don't have to say why. Just: is there someone?"

Allow two minutes. The teacher listens but does not require disclosure.

"You don't have to tell me their name. But hold it — you'll need it in a moment."

---

#### Activity — 18 minutes

**Part 1 — Guided Meditation: The Inner Ring (3–4 minutes)**

Ask students to close their eyes or look down at their hands. Ring the bell (or take a slow, audible breath).

*Teacher reads the following aloud, slowly, with pauses. Do not rush. The pauses are as important as the words. This can be read verbatim — no need to memorise.*

---

"Settle your body. If you're sitting, feel the chair or the floor underneath you. Feel how it holds you."

*Pause 5 seconds.*

"Take one breath in — slow. And let it go."

*Pause 5 seconds.*

"Now, bring one person to your mind. Someone in your family — not necessarily the person you love most, but someone who might need something right now. Someone who might be carrying something heavy. A parent who is tired. A grandmother who is unwell. A younger brother who got into trouble. Someone real."

*Pause 8 seconds.*

"Picture their face. Actually see it — the shape of their eyes, whether they're smiling or not. Picture where they are right now — sitting somewhere, or working, or lying down to rest. Make it specific."

*Pause 8 seconds.*

"Now notice what they might be feeling. You don't have to know for certain. Just imagine. Are they okay? Is there something they need?"

*Pause 8 seconds.*

"Now, from where you're sitting — from inside your chest — send them something. Not words. Just a feeling. The feeling of: *I want you to be okay. I want you to feel less heavy today. I want things to go well for you.*"

*Pause 10 seconds.*

"Stay with that. Just hold it — like you'd hold something fragile."

*Pause 10 seconds.*

"Before you open your eyes — what does that wish feel like inside you? Not in your head. In your body. Is there warmth? Is there ache? Is there both?"

*Pause 8 seconds.*

"When you're ready, take one breath. And slowly open your eyes."

*Ring bell or exhale.*

---

*Give students thirty seconds of quiet before speaking.*

"That — what you just did — is the centre ring."

---

**Part 2 — Draw the centre ring (10 minutes)**

"Open your diagram. Look at the centre circle. Write the name of the person you were thinking of — or draw their face — or a symbol that means them to you. You can add more than one person. But start with that one."

Students work in quiet. The teacher circulates without interrupting. Coloured pencils available if wanted.

"When you've added what feels right to the centre, you can look at your index card from last week. Are there others from your innermost circle who belong here? Add them if so."

The teacher collects diagrams at the end of class and stores them safely for C4–C6.

**Extension:** Students who finish the centre ring quickly may write one sentence around the outside of the centre circle — the wish they sent during the meditation, in their own words.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

"How was that — the meditation? What happened for you?"

Take several responses. Receive whatever is offered: confusion, quiet, warmth, restlessness. None of these are wrong. "It's okay if your mind wandered. It always does. What matters is that you tried to find that person."

Then: "That feeling — even a small one — of wanting someone to be okay: that's what Sem kye is pointing toward. Not a big dramatic love. Just: *I want you to be okay.* Small and real."

"Next class, we're going to go out one ring further. We're going to add someone who isn't family — someone a little further out — and see if the wish can still be real."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Before the student leader begins, the teacher introduces Ngowa:

"Today we add the second new prayer — the closing one. It's called Ngowa. Ngowa means: instead of keeping the good we made here today, we send it outward. We don't hold onto it — we offer it, like the flags offer the mantra to the wind. That's the Dedication of Merit."

"I'll lead it first. Listen."

The teacher leads Ngowa. Then the student leader leads:
1. Kyap Dro
2. Om Mani Padme Hung (7 times)
3. Manjushri Prayer
4. Sem kye
5. **Ngowa** — teacher leads; students may join or listen

After closing: "The wish you sent today — to that one person — don't forget it. It was real. You made it."

---

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The quality of C3's meditation sets the tone for C4, C5, and C6. The most important thing to protect is the particularity — one person, one face, one specific wish. If students rush to name many people or use vague language ("I sent love to everyone"), gently return them: "Can you picture one face? Just one?" The concentric circles diagrams must be collected and stored by the teacher between classes — students should not carry them home until C7. The index cards can be returned to students at the start of C4 as a reference for the second ring. Spiral connection: Grade 1 meditation practice (sitting still, following the breath) — this lesson builds on that foundation by adding directed attention toward a specific person. If your students have not done sitting practice before, add one extra minute at the start of the meditation to establish settling. Sem kye and Ngowa are both introduced today — keep the explanations brief and warm, not doctrinal. Meaning first. Always meaning first.
