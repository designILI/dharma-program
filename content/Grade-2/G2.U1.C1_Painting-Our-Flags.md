# G2.U1.C1 — Painting Our Flags
## Grade 2 · Unit 1 · Class 1 of 7 · Painting Our Flags
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Painting Tibetan syllables on cloth → A wish made visible, ready to be carried by wind

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Retrieval practice — asking students to reconstruct what they already know (the syllables, their sounds, their meaning) before they act on that knowledge strengthens the memory trace and gives the new year's central question something solid to build on.

**Bloom's Level:** Understand. Students are not simply recalling the six syllables — they are re-engaging with *why* those syllables matter, connecting sound to image to intention. This is the conceptual ground the rest of the unit will stand on.

**Prior Class Summary:** This is the first class of Grade 2. Students carry their Grade 1 experience — the mantra, the prayer flags they made and hung, and the question "How can a wish travel?" — into a year that will expand that question outward, ring by ring.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** the six syllables of Om Mani Padme Hung by name and sound, and that each syllable carries a meaning.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that painting a syllable with care and intention is itself a form of sending a wish — not merely decorating cloth.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** paint at least one Tibetan syllable on their flag with visible attention; some will paint all six.

Students will **FEEL** the quiet steadiness of returning to something they already know — the mantra — and sensing that it now opens into something larger than last year.

---

### Materials Checklist

- Pre-cut flag fabric or heavy cotton paper (one piece per student), approximately 20 cm × 30 cm, in any of the five traditional flag colours: blue, white, red, green, yellow — or a mix
- Fabric paint or washable acrylic paint, minimum black plus two or three additional colours
- Brushes: one medium, one fine per student
- Water cups: one per table group
- Newspaper or protective table covering for all surfaces
- Aprons or designated paint shirts — **Plan B:** If aprons are unavailable, have each student tuck a folded newspaper strip into their collar and lay additional sheets across their lap before any paint container is opened
- Reference cards showing the six Tibetan syllables in large, clear print: OM · MA · NI · PAD · ME · HUNG (one card per table, or one posted clearly at the front)
- One completed Grade 1 prayer flag for display — borrow from the hallway or ask the Grade 1 teacher. If none is available, a teacher-made example works equally well
- Drying rack or a clean section of floor lined with newspaper for flags to rest at the end of class

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader for today leads the Grade 1 prayer set:
1. **Kyap Dro** (Refuge) — the class follows
2. **Om Mani Padme Hung** (mantra, chanted together 7 times)
3. **Manjushri Prayer**

*Note on prayers: Sem kye and Ngowa are not introduced until C3. Use only these three prayers for C1 and C2.*

Before the prayers begin, the teacher says: "This is the first time in Grade 2 that one of us leads the class. We will rotate that role all year — every class, one person leads and the rest of us follow. Today, [student leader by rotation] begins." After the prayers: "Welcome to Grade 2. Let's start."

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

"Before we touch any paint, I want to know what your memory has kept. Close your eyes — don't answer yet. Just look for something."

*Pause ten seconds.*

"Picture the flags you made last year. Where are they hanging? What colour were yours? What's on them?"

*Pause ten seconds.*

"Open your eyes. Who wants to share what they saw?"

Take several responses. Then: "Those flags are still out there. Right now, while we are in here, they are in the wind. They have been sending wishes out all year while you were growing. They didn't stop."

Then: "What are the six syllables? Can someone say them?" Allow students to volunteer. If hesitation, invite the whole class to say them together. Point to each syllable on the reference card as it is named. Then: "What do they mean?" Take all responses without correction — affirm each piece offered. "All of that is true. The mantra holds a lot."

**Fallback:** If the class is quiet, the teacher chants the mantra once, slowly and clearly — just once — and says: "See if your body remembers." Then asks again.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10 minutes

The teacher holds up the Grade 1 example flag:

"Last year you asked: *how can a wish travel?* You painted flags. You put them where the wind would find them. The answer was: like this — carried outward by something bigger than you."

*Pause.*

"This year the question is bigger. Not just *how* does a wish travel — but *how far* can it reach? Can a wish reach someone you have never met? Can it reach an animal? Can it reach beings you cannot even picture?"

"We are not going to answer that today. We are going to start where you always start when you want to go somewhere far: you start close. You start with the syllables. You start with your hands."

The teacher picks up a brush and paints one syllable — slowly, deliberately, without rushing. The teacher speaks while painting: "I am not painting a letter. I am painting something that is going to go outside, into the wind. It deserves this much care."

**Vocabulary Moment:** "'Syllable' — each of the six parts of Om Mani Padme Hung is one syllable. OM is one syllable. MA is one syllable. Six syllables, six wishes wrapped inside one sound. Today you will make them visible."

**Body Moment:** The teacher leads the class in tapping the syllables on their knees while chanting together — left knee, right knee, alternating: OM (left) — MA (right) — NI (left) — PAD (right) — ME (left) — HUNG (right). Repeat three times at a steady pace. "Now your hands know the rhythm. Let them carry it into the brush."

**Emotional Hook:** The moment the brush touches the cloth for the first time. The teacher pauses before demonstrating this — allows a brief, genuine silence — and does not speak during it.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3 minutes

"Before you start painting, turn to the person sitting next to you. Tell them: which syllable do you want to paint first, and why? There is no wrong answer."

Allow approximately two minutes. The teacher circulates and listens but does not redirect or correct.

*What to listen for:* Students connecting syllables to meaning ("I want OM because it's the beginning"), to aesthetics ("I like how NI looks"), or to personal feeling ("HUNG sounds powerful"). All are valid entry points into intention.

---

#### Activity — 17 minutes

Distribute materials. Display the reference cards. Quickly review: "Water cup is for rinsing between colours. Lay your cloth flat before you start. Begin with the syllable you chose. You have room for all six, or you can paint fewer, larger ones. This is your flag — no two will look the same."

Students paint. The teacher circulates with minimal interruption — presence, not instruction. Quiet affirmations where genuine: "Take your time with that edge." "Look at how that one is coming."

*Note on oral storytelling:* If the teacher wishes to narrate the story of the first prayer flag while students work — the monk, the cloth, the wind, the first day it flew — this story can be read verbatim from a written version. It does not need to be memorised.

**Scaffold:** Students uncertain how to form a syllable may use a pencil to lightly trace the shape from the reference card onto their cloth before painting over it.

**Extension:** Students who complete their syllables may add a border, a simple symbol (sun, lotus, cloud, mountain, wave), or a small image of something they wish to send a wish toward. Ask: "What would you add if you had more room?"

Flags are placed on the drying rack or floor with care. Students clean brushes before the debrief.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

"Hold up your flag if you would like to show it." Allow several students to share briefly. The teacher does not evaluate the painting quality — responds to intention: "You took your time with that. What were you thinking while you painted?"

Then: "Next class, we will finish these and hang them. And we will begin to ask a question that is going to stay with us all year: *who* is this wish for? Who do we want it to reach?"

Leave the question open. Do not answer it.

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

The student leader leads the Grade 1 prayer set:
1. Kyap Dro
2. Om Mani Padme Hung (7 times)
3. Manjushri Prayer

After the closing prayers, the teacher says: "The mantra you just chanted and the one you painted today are the same. The wish goes out in the sound. It goes out in the ink. Both are real."

---

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The central move of this lesson is the shift in question — from Grade 1's "how does a wish travel?" to Grade 2's "how far can it reach?" Make sure students hear that shift clearly in the mini lesson. Acknowledge Grade 1 warmly and briefly, then move. Flags must be physically drying by end of class so they can be finished and decorated in C2. The question "who is the wish for?" is planted in the debrief but not answered — C2 opens with it directly. Spiral connection: Grade 1, Unit 1 (Prayer Flags) — this lesson is an explicit extension of that unit, not a repetition. Students built the mantra into muscle memory in Grade 1; here they are asked to think about what that mantra *does*.
