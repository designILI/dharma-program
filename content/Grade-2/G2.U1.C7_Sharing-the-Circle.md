# G2.U1.C7 — Sharing the Circle
## Grade 2 · Unit 1 · Class 7 of 7 · Sharing the Circle
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Presenting the completed diagram and hanging the prayer flags → Naming the practice that has been built across the unit: Bodhichitta

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Retrieval through articulation — explaining what is in each ring of the diagram to another person is the strongest form of retrieval: it forces the student to reconstruct not just what they put there, but *why*, which deepens and consolidates the learning far more than silent reviewing. The presentation is the assessment *and* the deepest learning act of the unit.

**Bloom's Level:** Apply

**Prior Class Summary:** In C6, students completed the guided meditation with the widest ring — all beings — and finished drawing the outer ring of their diagram. The lesson ended with a deliberate tease: "It has a name — a very old one. We'll name it next class." That promise is fulfilled today.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** the word Bodhichitta — that it means the aspiration to become wise and kind for the benefit of all beings, and that the expanding circle they have built across this unit is a child-accessible expression of it.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the practice they have been doing — ring by ring, week by week — is not only a classroom exercise but a real and ancient practice that teachers and monks have used for thousands of years to grow their compassion.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** present their completed diagram to at least one other person, explaining who is in each ring and something about what the meditation felt like; some will speak fluently and at length; some will point and say a few words; both are presentations.

Students will **FEEL** the particular pride of having made something complete and shown it — not performance anxiety, but the satisfaction of recognition: *I built this. It is real. Someone sees it.*

---

### Materials Checklist

- Completed concentric circles diagrams (returned by teacher at the start of class)
- Completed prayer flags (already hanging — or, if not yet hung outside, brought in briefly for the ceremony)
- Additional string or twine for hanging new flags in the ceremony, if needed
- The room arranged for a small gallery or presentation: desks pushed to edges, or chairs in a circle, so students can move through each other's displayed diagrams — **Plan B:** If the room cannot be rearranged, presentations happen in pairs at desks, with a brief whole-class sharing at the end
- A small bell or singing bowl for the ceremony moment
- Optional: a brief teacher-written note placed on each diagram before class, something specific: "I noticed you put ___ in your outer ring. Tell me about that." (Not required, but powerful — takes five minutes the night before)

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

The student leader leads the full Grade 2 prayer set:
1. **Kyap Dro**
2. **Om Mani Padme Hung** (7 times)
3. **Manjushri Prayer**
4. **Sem kye** — most students joining meaningfully; some now leading the words
5. **Ngowa** — teacher leads; students joining

Before the prayers: "This is the last class of Unit 1. Before we begin, I want to say something: over the last seven weeks, you made flags, you sent wishes, you sat in meditation four times, and you built a map of your own circle of care. That is not a small thing. Let's open with full attention."

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

"Last class I told you the practice has a name. An old name. I want you to try to guess what it might be — not the word itself, but what kind of word it might be."

Take responses. Students may guess: a Dharma term, a Buddhist word, a long word, a prayer name. Don't confirm yet.

"Here's what I want you to do first. Before I give you the name — look at your diagram. Pick up your circle. Look at the four rings. Think about the person in the centre. Think about what the fourth ring felt like. Hold all of it."

*Pause 30 seconds.*

"When you look at the whole diagram — all four rings together — what does it feel like? One word or phrase. Don't think too long."

Take several responses: big, wide, hard, real, like a map, like a flower, like the mantra, like something I actually did. Receive all of them.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 8 minutes

"The practice you have been doing has a name in Sanskrit — the ancient language of Buddhist teaching. The name is: *Bodhichitta*."

Write it on the board: **Bodhichitta** (and, if the teacher knows it: **བྱང་ཆུབ་སེམས།** in traditional script).

"*Bodhi* means awakening — becoming fully wise and kind. *Chitta* means mind or heart. So Bodhichitta means: the mind that wants to awaken — not for itself alone, but for the benefit of all beings."

"That's the prayer Sem kye. That's what Sem kye has been saying every class since C3: 'I want to become wise and kind, not just for myself, but for everyone.' Sem kye *is* Bodhichitta — said out loud as a wish."

"And the circle you built — ring by ring, starting with your family and ending with all beings — that is Bodhichitta practised with your imagination and your care."

"This practice is thousands of years old. Teachers spent their whole lives working on exactly what you have been doing in these seven weeks. They started where you started — with the people they loved — and they kept expanding outward. They never stopped asking: *who else can I include?*"

"That is the question of this entire year. And you have already begun."

**Vocabulary Moment:** "Bodhichitta — write it on your diagram, somewhere on the outside, if you want to. It is the name of what you built."

**Body Moment:** "Stand up. Hold your diagram out in front of you, like an offering — both hands, palms up, diagram resting on them. Stand with it for a moment. This is what you made." *Allow five seconds of holding. Then: "Sit down."*

**Emotional Hook:** The moment a student hears "Bodhichitta" and their face changes — the moment of recognition. "You already knew this. You just didn't have the name."

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3 minutes

"Find one person in the room — not your closest friend, someone you don't usually sit with. That's your presentation partner. Go sit next to them."

Once paired: "You have two minutes each. Show your partner your diagram, ring by ring. Tell them: who is in the centre, and why. Who is in the second ring. What animals you drew in the third ring. What you put in the outer ring and what the fourth meditation felt like."

*This is the culminating assessment. The teacher circulates, listens, and takes brief mental notes (or a quick written note) on each student's ability to explain their diagram — not for a grade, but for carry-forward information.*

*What to listen for:* Can the student explain the outer ring, not just the inner ones? Do they connect the meditation experience to what they drew? Do they use any of the vocabulary — sentient beings, Bodhichitta, Sem kye? These observations inform C7's carry-forward notes.

---

#### Activity — 17 minutes

**Part 1 — Gallery walk or full presentation (10 minutes)**

If the room is rearranged: diagrams are placed on desks around the room. Students walk through, look at each other's circles, and leave a small mark — a star or dot — on the ring of another student's diagram that surprised or moved them. Students may not write words; only the dot. This preserves the diagram for the student.

If the room cannot be rearranged (Plan B): students remain in pairs. Each pair finishes the full two-minutes-each presentation, and then two or three volunteers share one thing from their outer ring with the whole class.

*The teacher ensures every student presents to at least one person.*

**Part 2 — Ceremony: Hanging the flags (7 minutes)**

"Now we do something that only happens at the end of a unit. We take the flags outside."

The class moves together to where the flags will be hung — a new spot, distinct from the Grade 1 flags, or extended along the same line. Each student clips or ties their flag.

Before the first flag goes up, the teacher says: "Before you hang your flag — look at it. You painted the mantra on it. The mantra goes out for all beings. Now you know who 'all beings' means from the inside."

Students hang their flags, one by one, without rushing. No words are required during this. The bell may be rung once as the last flag goes up.

*Note: If flags were already hung in C2, students may add a small ribbon, tie, or additional mark to their existing flag as a sign of completion.*

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

The class returns to the room. Students sit.

"Look at your diagram one more time. I want you to tell me — or tell yourself — one thing: who is in the outer ring of your circle who you don't usually think about? Just one. And can you send them a wish right now — just for a moment?"

*Allow 30 seconds of quiet.*

"That question — 'can you keep expanding the circle?' — is not just a Unit 1 question. It is a Grade 2 question. It is a question we will carry into every unit this year."

"Next unit, we're going to learn about a person who spent his entire life expanding his circle. Ring by ring. His whole life. You'll meet him next class."

Leave it there. Do not name the Buddha yet — let the curiosity carry forward.

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

The student leader leads the full Grade 2 prayer set for the last time this unit:
1. Kyap Dro
2. Om Mani Padme Hung (7 times)
3. Manjushri Prayer
4. Sem kye
5. **Ngowa** — teacher leads; students joining

After closing, the teacher says — slowly, without fanfare:

"Bodhichitta. The mind that wants to awaken for everyone. You practised it. You know what it feels like from the inside. That is enough for now. We carry it forward."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: This class should feel like a ceremony, not a lesson. Protect the pacing — resist the urge to squeeze more teaching in. The three most important moments are: (1) the naming of Bodhichitta, (2) the presentation of the diagram, and (3) the flag-hanging. Each deserves its own full weight. The diagrams may be sent home after today, or kept in the classroom until parents can see them at the next family visit — decide based on your school's culture. The culminating assessment is the student's ability to explain their diagram ring by ring; use the gallery walk or presentations to gather your observations, not a separate quiz or checklist. Unit 2 begins next class: the Life of the Buddha. The thread from Unit 1 to Unit 2: the person who became the Buddha also spent his life expanding his circle of compassion, ring by ring — first his family, then his community, then all beings. He is the most famous practitioner of exactly what these students have been doing. Name this explicitly at the start of C8 (U2.C1): "Remember the outer ring? He tried to fill it for his whole life." Spiral connection: Grade 1, Units 1 and 2 (mantra, flags, kindness practices) — everything from Grade 1 has been spiralled into this unit and extended. Students are no longer asking "how does a wish travel?" They have begun asking "how far can it reach?" — and experiencing the answer from the inside.
