# G8.U2.C2 — The Hard Teacher
## Grade 8 · Unit 2 · Class 2 of 5 · The Hard Teacher
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** The Marpa-Milarepa relationship — years of labour, the forged letter, eventual transmission → the question of what a teacher owes a student, and whether suffering endured with awareness can be transformative
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Narrative complexity resisting premature closure — the Marpa-Milarepa relationship cannot be evaluated by a single moral framework; it requires students to hold "Marpa was right for Milarepa" and "no teacher should treat a student this way generally" simultaneously, without collapsing one into the other
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluate — students analyse the Marpa-Milarepa relationship from multiple ethical perspectives, assess the concept of penance in the Buddhist sense, and write an argued evaluation of Marpa's pedagogy
**Prior Class Summary:** C1 told the beginning of Milarepa's story — his family's dispossession, his mother's instruction, the training in black magic, and the mass deaths he caused. Students wrote their initial position on whether transformation is possible for someone who has done this. They carry the moral weight of that beginning and the question: what would it take?

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the major episodes of the Marpa-Milarepa relationship: the years of impossible labour, Damema's forged letter, the transmission, and Marpa's declaration
Students will **UNDERSTAND** the concept of penance in the Buddhist sense — not punishment to appease a divine authority, but a practice undertaken to exhaust the momentum of negative karma through suffering endured with awareness
Students will **BE ABLE TO** write a short analytical response to Marpa's pedagogy that evaluates it from at least two positions — what the tradition says and what the student themselves believes — and argues for a considered view
Students will **FEEL** the genuine complexity of the Marpa-Milarepa relationship: Marpa's apparent cruelty, Damema's compassion, and the possibility that both responses were, in their own way, a form of care

---

### Materials Checklist
- No projected materials — oral narrative and writing class
- Paper and pencils for the analytical response
- Teacher should know the Marpa-Milarepa section of the biography well enough to narrate it clearly and with the appropriate weight; know Damema's role in particular — she is easily underplayed and should not be
- **Plan B:** If time runs short in the narrative, the teacher can compress the detailed account of the tower episodes into a single representative description ("Marpa sent him to build a tower, then tear it down and rebuild it elsewhere — again and again, for years") rather than telling each tower episode individually; the emotional logic of the narrative must be preserved even if the details are compressed

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full Grade 8 prayer set, led by the student leader of the day. Sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Tsog Due pa. Student leader conducts.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes
*"Milarepa had caused mass death. He lived with what he had done. Eventually he heard about a great teacher named Marpa, and he went to him. Marpa knew who Milarepa was — what he had done — and he received him. Before I tell you what Marpa did: what do you think a teacher should do with a student who carries what Milarepa carried? What does a teacher owe someone who has done terrible things — and who wants to learn?"*

Take two or three responses. Do not resolve. Hold the question open.

*"The tradition gives one answer. It may not be the answer you would give. Let's see."*

**Fallback:** "Put it this way — does the severity of what a person has done change what they deserve as a student? Or does every student deserve the same thing from a teacher, regardless of what they've done?" This grounds the question concretely before the narrative.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 15 minutes
The teacher narrates the Marpa-Milarepa relationship. Tell it as story.

---

*"Marpa the Translator was not known for gentleness. He had studied in India at great cost — personal, financial, physical — and he had brought the teachings back to Tibet. He was a fully realised practitioner. He was also a difficult man."*

*"When Milarepa came to him and asked to receive teachings, Marpa did not embrace him or offer reassurance. He put him to work. Build a tower, he said. Milarepa built a tower. When it was nearly done, Marpa said he had been confused — he hadn't meant that spot, or that design, or that shape. Tear it down. Build it again, somewhere else. Milarepa tore it down and built again. Again, when it was nearly done: I misremembered. Start over."*

*"This went on for years. Milarepa's back was raw from carrying stone. The skin of his hands was broken. He wept. He was in constant physical pain, constant psychological uncertainty — never knowing when the task would be accepted, never knowing if it would ever end. He received no teaching. He received nothing except the next impossible instruction."*

*"Marpa's wife, Damema, watched this. She could not bear it. She was a practitioner herself — wise, compassionate, and convinced that what was being done to Milarepa was too much. So she forged a letter. She wrote it in Marpa's name, introducing Milarepa as a student with permission to receive teachings, and sent him to another teacher in the tradition. That teacher — receiving what appeared to be Marpa's letter — gave Milarepa the teachings."*

*"When Marpa found out, he was furious. He confronted Milarepa directly. He did not punish him — but he told him the truth. The teaching given under a forged letter would not take root. Not because of magic or punishment, but because the conditions were wrong. Milarepa had not been ready. Marpa had known he was not ready. The years of labour were not cruelty — they were purification. The only way through the karma of what Milarepa had done, Marpa told him, was to exhaust it through suffering endured with awareness and without bitterness. Not performed suffering — real suffering, held with consciousness."*

*"Eventually — when Marpa judged the time had come — he gave Milarepa the full transmission. When he did, he wept. He told Milarepa: 'You will be a great light in this world.'"*

---

**Vocabulary Moment:** *"The concept of* penance — *and specifically its meaning in Buddhist practice, which is different from its meaning in other traditions. Penance in the Christian sense can mean punishment assigned by an authority to appease a divine judge. In the Buddhist sense, penance is a practice undertaken to exhaust the momentum of negative karma — the accumulated force of harmful action. It is not about satisfying an external authority. It is about changing what is inside. Milarepa's years of building towers were penance in this sense: not because Marpa was cruel and wanted to make him suffer, but because there was no other route through what Milarepa carried. The suffering was not the point — the awareness he brought to it was the point."*

**Body Moment:** *"What does it feel like in the body to be told — again — to do something hard, painful, and apparently pointless by someone you have chosen to trust completely? Not someone who has forced you into this. Someone you came to, freely, because you believed they could help. Hold that for twenty seconds."*

Wait. Twenty seconds.

*"That is the pedagogical moment Milarepa was inside for years. The question is not only whether Marpa was right. The question is also: how do you hold that? What kind of quality of mind does it take to do what Milarepa did — endure it without becoming bitter, without abandoning the trust, without breaking?"*

**Emotional Hook:** The complexity — held without resolution — of Marpa's apparent cruelty and Damema's apparent compassion, and the unsettling possibility that both were, in their different ways, responding to the same person with a genuine form of care.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
*"Tell your partner two separate questions — and keep them separate, because they have different answers: Was Marpa right to treat Milarepa the way he did? And: was it compassionate? Can something be the right response to a situation without being compassionate in the ordinary sense? Two minutes."*

---

#### Activity — 12 minutes
Students write a short analytical response — not a full argument, but a genuine evaluation. The prompt:

*"Marpa's pedagogy: what was it, why did he use it, and was it the right response to what Milarepa carried? Argue a position — don't just describe. What do you think? Use at least two perspectives: what the tradition says, and what you believe."*

Teacher circulates: *"Don't just describe — evaluate. Was this the right approach? What is your argument? What is the strongest case against your view?"*

This is shorter than C4's philosophical argument — it is analytical writing, not a five-part philosophical argument structure. Honest and argued, not polished.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
*"The tradition holds Marpa as a great teacher. It does not hold him as a model for how ordinary teachers should treat ordinary students. This distinction matters. Marpa knew Milarepa's exact karmic situation — his specific burden — and acted from that knowledge. You cannot apply Marpa's method generally, any more than you can apply a surgeon's method to ordinary care. The tradition is not endorsing cruelty or hardship as a general pedagogical principle."*

*"But the story raises a question that matters regardless: is there a form of suffering that can only be worked through by passing through suffering — not avoiding it, not managing it, but enduring it consciously? And if so, what is the relationship between that kind of suffering and practice? That question follows us into C3."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full Grade 8 prayer set. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer added at close.

Teacher's closing line: *"Damema wept for Milarepa's pain. Marpa wept when he gave him the teaching. Both of those things are true. Keep them both."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The Marpa-Milarepa discussion should not resolve too easily — neither in the direction of "Marpa was right, this is just what it takes" nor in the direction of "what Marpa did was abusive and unjustifiable." The tradition holds Marpa as a great teacher and his methods as appropriate to Milarepa's specific karmic situation, while simultaneously not holding this up as a universal model. Students should be able to hold both: "Marpa was the right teacher for Milarepa in a specific and unrepeatable situation" AND "no ordinary teacher should treat a student this way." If students flatten this into one position, push back: "Can something be right in a specific situation and wrong as a general principle? Give me an example from outside this story." Damema deserves attention — she is often underplayed in tellings of the Milarepa story, but her role is important: she represents ordinary compassion responding to visible suffering, while Marpa represents a deeper and harder form of compassion operating at the level of what Milarepa actually needs. Both are compassion. Both are real. C3 is the cave years — where Milarepa goes after transmission. This is the heart of his transformation. Prepare the two-minute meditation moment in C3's body section carefully: it needs to be held without rushing, and the room needs to be genuinely still. If the room is difficult to quiet in C2, note this and plan to extend the settling at the start of C3's opening.
