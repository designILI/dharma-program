# G8.U2.C1 — The Sorcerer
## Grade 8 · Unit 2 · Class 1 of 5 · The Sorcerer
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** The beginning of Milarepa's life — family, dispossession, black magic, mass death → the question of whether any person, having done the worst, is capable of transformation
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Moral discomfort as cognitive activation — presenting a morally complex narrative without premature resolution activates genuine evaluative thinking; students who are uncomfortable have engaged the ethical weight of the story; discomfort is not a problem to manage but a sign that the teaching has landed
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluate — students assess the moral weight of Milarepa's actions, form an initial position on the possibility of transformation, and produce a before-response that will be compared to their final position in C5
**Prior Class Summary:** C5 of Unit 1 was the Socratic discussion — students defended their philosophical arguments about the Twelve Links and the nature of the mind. They carry the recognition that genuine philosophical exchange produced something in the room: a different quality of thinking than the one the Twelve Links describe as conditioned. Unit 2 opens with a complete tonal shift — from philosophical analysis to story.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the major events of Milarepa's early life: his father's death, his family's dispossession by the uncle and aunt, his mother's instruction, his training in black magic, and the mass deaths he caused
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the Himalayan Buddhist tradition tells this story without sanitisation — Milarepa is not presented as a flawed person who made mistakes but as someone who committed acts of great harm, and the teaching begins there
Students will **BE ABLE TO** write an honest initial position — not a polished argument but a genuine first response — on whether transformation is possible for someone who has done what Milarepa did
Students will **FEEL** the genuine moral discomfort of being asked to follow the story of someone who caused mass death — not resolving that discomfort prematurely, but holding it as the question the unit is built around

---

### Materials Checklist
- No projected materials needed — this class is oral narrative; the teacher tells the story
- Paper and pencils for the initial response writing (one per student)
- Teacher should have the story prepared in advance — know it well enough to tell it as narrative, not read from notes; the tradition of oral transmission matters here
- **Plan B:** If the oral narrative is not flowing well (this is rare but possible — the story is stark and teachers sometimes feel the need to soften it), the teacher reads directly from a printed version of the traditional biography; what must not happen is softening of the moral weight

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full Grade 8 prayer set, led by the student leader of the day. Sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Tsog Due pa. Student leader conducts.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
Before the story begins — a question designed to prime the moral evaluative frame.

*"Before I tell you anything about today: what is the worst thing a person can do — and still be redeemable? Is there a limit? Or is transformation always possible, for anyone, from anything?"*

Take two or three responses only — do not debate, do not resolve. Listen. Note positions.

*"Hold whatever you just said. Or whatever you think but didn't say. Today's story will test it. I am going to tell you about Milarepa. You have heard his name. You have not heard the beginning of his story. It does not begin where you might expect."*

**Strong response if a student says "anyone can change":** "I'd like to believe that too. Let's see what you think at the end of class."

**Fallback if students are reticent:** "If the question is too abstract — try this one instead. Can you think of a person — from history, from a story, from anywhere — who did something terrible and then genuinely became different? What made the difference possible?" This grounds the question before the story begins.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 15 minutes
The teacher tells the story of Milarepa's early life. This is oral narrative. The telling is direct, unhurried, and not softened.

---

*"Milarepa was born in Tibet in the eleventh century. His name at birth was Thöpaga — 'he who is pleasant to hear.' His father died when he was still young. On his deathbed, the father entrusted the family's property — their land, their animals, their wealth — to a brother, Milarepa's uncle, and to his uncle's wife. The understanding was that they would hold the property in trust until Milarepa came of age, and then return it."*

*"The uncle and aunt did not return it. They kept everything. Milarepa, his mother, and his sister were reduced to servants in what had been their own home — working for the people who had stolen from them, eating the leftovers, wearing worn-out clothing. This went on for years."*

*"Milarepa's mother was not a person who accepted this quietly. She was consumed by grief and rage. When Milarepa was old enough, she scraped together what little money she could and sent him away with a specific instruction: learn black magic. Learn it well. Come back and make them pay."*

*"He did. He found a teacher of black magic and he studied. And when he had learned enough — he used it. At the wedding feast of his uncle's son, with nearly the entire extended family gathered, he caused the roof of the house to collapse. Thirty-five people died. His aunt and uncle were among the survivors."*

*"He then sent a hailstorm that destroyed the crops of his uncle and aunt — the crops they would have needed to survive the winter."*

*"He had done exactly what his mother asked of him."*

Pause. Let the story sit.

*"The tradition does not tell this story quietly or in passing. It does not say 'Milarepa made some mistakes in his youth.' It says: he caused mass death. He caused it deliberately, with skill, in response to a real injustice — and that is the beginning of who Milarepa is."*

---

**Vocabulary Moment:** *"The word* remorse *— and the distinction the tradition makes between remorse and guilt. Guilt is self-focused: 'I am a terrible person.' It tends to spiral inward and produce shame rather than action. Remorse is action-focused: 'What I did was terrible, and it has weight, and that weight must be addressed.' In the Buddhist tradition, remorse is not a punishment — it is a form of clear seeing. Milarepa's entire subsequent life begins in remorse: the clear-eyed recognition of what he had done and the weight it carried. That recognition, rather than denial or justification, is what made the next thing possible."*

**Body Moment:** *"Imagine being Milarepa in the moment after. Not in the moment of doing it — but in the moment after. The roof has fallen. The bodies are there. He has done what he set out to do. What is in the body at that moment? Take thirty seconds. Not what you think he should feel — not the correct answer. What do you think the body actually holds, in a person who has done this and is standing in the silence that follows?"*

Wait. Thirty seconds.

*"You don't have to share that. But keep it. It matters for understanding what comes next."*

**Emotional Hook:** The genuine moral discomfort of being asked to enter the perspective of someone who caused mass death — and being told, without apology, that this is where the most important teaching begins.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
*"Tell your partner: do you think what Milarepa did is forgivable — even in principle? And: what would it take? Not what did take — what would need to happen for forgiveness — or transformation — to be possible for someone who has done this? Two minutes."*

Pairs. Teacher listens. No class debrief — take note of the range of positions for C5.

---

#### Activity — 10 minutes
Students write their initial response. Not a polished argument — an honest first position. The prompt:

*"What I think about what Milarepa did. And whether transformation is possible for someone who has done this — and if so, what it would require."*

This is the **before-position** for the unit. It is not graded. It will be returned in C5 so students can compare it to where they arrive. Honesty matters more than eloquence.

Teacher circulates very quietly — no intervention during writing. If a student seems paralysed: "There's no wrong answer here. What do you actually think?"

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
*"The tradition tells this story without apology. Every lineage of the Himalayan Buddhist tradition includes Milarepa among its greatest saints and poets. He is not included despite what he did — the tradition does not pretend the beginning didn't happen. He is included because of the full arc: from where he began to where he ended. Whatever the tradition says next — about transformation, about the capacity of practice, about what a human being is capable of becoming — it is saying about this person."*

*"Remember that."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full Grade 8 prayer set. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer added at close.

Teacher's closing line: *"He was a murderer. He was also Milarepa. We will follow the whole story."*

---

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The moral weight of C1 should not be resolved too quickly — in the debrief, in the next class, or anywhere before the story has been told in full. Some students will want to jump ahead: "But then he became enlightened, right?" Hold them in the beginning. The story matters precisely because the tradition does not begin with Milarepa as a good person who stumbled. It begins with mass murder, and the teaching builds from there. Students who try to minimise or explain away what he did are themselves illustrating Link 9 (clinging — in this case, clinging to a comfortable narrative about what saints are like). You do not need to say this aloud — note it. C2 introduces Marpa, the teacher Milarepa sought out. Students should arrive at C2 carrying the C1 question intact: is transformation possible, and if so, what does it take? The Twelve Links framework from Unit 1 is applicable here — ask yourself before C2: where in the chain did Milarepa's actions originate, and where did remorse interrupt the chain? This connection should surface naturally from students, not be announced by the teacher. Spiral note: the theme of what a human being is capable of has been present since Grade 1 (the circle of kindness that extends to all beings), examined in Grade 4 (the Four Noble Truths — the diagnosis and the possibility of cessation), and is now being tested against the most extreme narrative the curriculum presents. Students have been building toward this story for eight years. Let it land at its full weight.
