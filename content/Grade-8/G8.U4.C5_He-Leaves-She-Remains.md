# G8.U4.C5 — He Leaves; She Remains
## Grade 8 · Unit 4 · Class 5 of 7 · He Leaves; She Remains
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** The walk — Pajapati's three askings and three refusals, the ordination, the intervention of Ananda → the particular quality of asking a third time when you do not know if this time will be different
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Embodied cognition — the body moment in C5 (walking the length of the room slowly, imagining bare feet after hundreds of miles) is not metaphor. It is a small physical echo of an enormous physical act. When students stand and walk, the concept of perseverance moves from the page into the body, where it becomes knowledge of a different kind.
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluate — students evaluate what the passage about the walk and the three askings reveals about Pajapati's character; they evaluate the tradition's implicit claims about why three refusals were necessary; they connect the historical event to the Tara archetype from Grade 6
**Prior Class Summary:** C4 examined the gilded cage — Pajapati's years as queen, her understanding of the Dharma without structural access to full practice, and the concept of structural constraint. Students wrote their third close-reading paragraph and carry the gap between understanding and actualisation.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the specific sequence of events: Pajapati's gathering of women, the walk to find the Buddha, the first refusal, the return, the second refusal, the second return, the third asking, Ananda's intervention ("Are women capable of enlightenment?"), and the ordination
Students will **UNDERSTAND** the distinction between stubbornness and perseverance; the tradition's implicit logic of the three refusals; the connection between Pajapati's act and the Green Tara archetype studied in Grade 6
Students will **BE ABLE TO** write a close-reading paragraph on what the passage reveals about Pajapati's character through the walk and the three askings; they can name the Tara connection and explain it in their own words
Students will **FEEL** the specific combination of exhaustion, conviction, and open-ended hope that characterises asking a third time after two refusals — and the particular quality of not knowing if this time will be different

---

### Materials Checklist
- Reading excerpt prepared: 12 minutes of teacher-read text covering the pivotal episode — the walk, the first refusal, the return and second refusal, the third asking, Ananda's intervention, the ordination. This is the most dramatically rich reading in the unit; do not rush it. The third refusal and the third asking are the heart of the story.
- Student journals and their C2, C3, and C4 close-reading paragraphs
- The question board from C1 (visible — check which questions are now being answered)
- Space in the room to walk — the body moment requires students to walk the length of the room and return. Arrange seating in advance if the room configuration requires it.
- Timer
- **Plan B for the walk:** If the room configuration makes a physical walk impractical, adapt: students stand in place and shift their weight slowly from foot to foot for thirty seconds, imagining the weight of the journey. The body engagement is the essential element; the specific movement can be adapted.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full Grade 8 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Tsog Due pa. Student leader rotates.

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes
Teacher: "In Grade 6 we met Green Tara — the foot extended, ready to step forward immediately, not waiting. Today: Pajapati's Green Tara moment. Before you've heard the story: tell me what you know about what Tara represents, and why I might call this her Tara moment." Take responses — students should recall: Tara as the archetype of immediate compassionate action, of not waiting when something needs doing, of the female principle as dynamically engaged rather than passive. Then: "Hold that. The reading will show you what it looks like in history."

#### Mini Lesson — 14 minutes

**Reading excerpt:** Teacher reads aloud the pivotal episode — approximately 12 minutes. Read it slowly. Do not rush the refusals. The passage:

- Siddhartha has left the palace years ago. He is the Buddha, teaching in the Jeta Grove and elsewhere. Pajapati has received word of where he is.
- She gathers the women — other Sakya women, many of them also widowed, who have been listening to news of the teaching and who want the same thing she wants: to practise, to ordain, to take robes. They are not a small group; some accounts name five hundred women. They cut their hair together. They dress in the orange robes of wandering monastics. They are already practising the form before they have been given permission to.
- The walk — barefoot, in the dry season heat, on roads not designed for comfort. Hundreds of miles. They are walking to where the Buddha is teaching.
- The first arrival — Pajapati appears before the Buddha, her feet swollen, her face dusty, weeping (the tradition notes this detail). She asks: "Lord, may women also receive the going-forth, the higher ordination, in the Dhamma and Vinaya proclaimed by the Tathagata?" He says no. No reason given, or at least none that satisfies. She withdraws.
- The return — she does not go home. She walks back.
- The second asking — no. She withdraws.
- The second return — she walks back again.
- The third asking — Ananda sees her: weeping, swollen-footed, and still there. He asks what is wrong and she tells him. He goes to the Buddha and asks: "Are women, Lord, capable of realising the fruits of practice? Of stream-entry, once-returning, non-returning, arahantship?" The Buddha says: Yes. They are fully capable. Ananda then asks: "Then why not ordain them?" There is a silence in the tradition here — the exact exchange varies by account. The ordination happens.
- The ordination — Pajapati and the five hundred women are ordained. The bhikkhuni sangha — the community of women monastics — is founded. This moment is one of the most consequential in the history of Buddhism.

After the reading, a beat of silence. Then briefly: "Ananda's question was the hinge. He did not argue about rights or justice. He asked about capacity. The answer to the question about capacity was yes. The ordination followed. This is not a tidy resolution — the tradition also records that the Buddha attached additional rules (the eight garudhammas) to women's ordination, rules that placed women's monastics subordinate to men's. That complexity is real, and it is part of the story. But the ordination happened. Pajapati walked back a third time. That is the whole thing."

---

**Vocabulary Moment:** "The word *perseverance* — continued effort in the direction of what one knows to be right, in the face of opposition or repeated difficulty. Not stubbornness: stubbornness maintains a position regardless of evidence. Perseverance maintains action in the direction of what one knows to be right, regardless of opposition. Pajapati's three askings are perseverance. Notice also: she did not change her argument between the first asking and the third. She did not refine her case, find a new angle, lobby through intermediaries (until Ananda). She asked. She was refused. She came back and asked again. The act was the argument."

---

**Body Moment:** "Stand up." Students stand. "Walk the length of the room. Slowly. As you walk: imagine your feet are bare on a road that has taken months to travel. You have been refused twice. You have not gone home. You are walking back to ask a third time. You do not know if this time will be different. You know only that you are going to ask." Walk the full length of the room. Pause at the end. Turn. Walk back. Sit. "That is a fraction of what she carried. What is in your body?"

Take two or three brief responses — one word each if students prefer. Hold the quality in the room before moving on.

---

**Emotional Hook:** The specific quality of the third asking — the particular combination of exhaustion and conviction, hope that has survived two refusals, and the not-knowing of whether this time will be different. This is one of the most specific emotional conditions in the curriculum. Students who have ever persisted at something they were told they couldn't have will recognise it.

#### Turn and Talk — 4 minutes
"Tell your partner: why do you think the Buddha refused the first two times? Not a simple answer — hold the complexity. And why do you think Ananda's question — 'Are women capable of enlightenment?' — was the one that changed it? What did that question do that Pajapati's direct asking hadn't done?" Two minutes, then take two or three responses. The tradition offers multiple interpretations; hold them all without resolving them.

#### Activity — 11 minutes
Students write their fourth close-reading paragraph. Same format.

**Prompt:** "What does the passage about the walk and the three askings show us about Pajapati's character? What specifically in the text — in her actions, her words, the details given — tells you this? You may also note: what does the text *not* show us about her inner state? What are we left to infer?"

Teacher circulates. Key prompts when a student is stuck:
- "What is the difference between the first asking and the third? Does the text treat them differently? How?"
- "What does the detail of Ananda seeing her — weeping, swollen-footed, still there — add to the picture of her character?"
- "What would it mean to not come back after the second refusal? What does coming back tell you?"

Students hold this paragraph alongside the previous three. The stack is at four.

#### Debrief — 3 minutes
Teacher: "She walked back. That is the whole thing. She was refused twice. She walked back. Not because she was certain it would work. Because she knew it was right. The tradition does not tell us what was in her mind between the second refusal and the third asking. It tells us what she did: she walked back. Sometimes that is the entire story."

Look at the question board: this class likely answers several questions from C1. Acknowledge them if students notice.

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Tsog Due pa. Student leader.

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "She was refused. She came back. She was refused. She came back. The ordination happened — and the door she opened has never fully closed, even when it has been nearly shut."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The Tara callback in C5 — Green Tara, foot extended, not waiting — is the curriculum's spiral design at its most intentional and its most satisfying. Students studied Tara in Grade 6 as archetype; they studied Pajapati's name in Grade 2 and the grief touchpoint in Grade 6; now the connection arrives in full: Pajapati IS Green Tara, historically enacted. Tara is the form the tradition found to hold this quality of immediate, undeterred compassionate action. Pajapati is the historical woman in whom that quality lived. This is the curriculum's payoff for the six-year thread. Name it clearly in the PKA and return to it in the debrief if students haven't connected it by then.

The additional rules (the eight garudhammas) placed on women's ordination — making bhikkhuni monastics formally subordinate to monks — are mentioned briefly in the mini lesson and should not be elaborated into a lengthy discussion in C5. They are real and important and the curriculum does not hide them; but C5 is the class of the walk and the ordination, not the class of subsequent complications. C7 (reclaiming the untold) is the space to address what the tradition did and did not do with what Pajapati founded.

C6 follows Pajapati into her monastic life and her enlightenment — the tradition's formal recognition of her as an arahant. It is the quietest class of the unit emotionally, and the most philosophically demanding. After the drama of C5, C6 asks students to think carefully about what the tradition's claim (full enlightenment, in the presence of the Buddha) actually means and implies.

Spiral connections active in C5: Grade 6 Unit 3 (Tara — explicit connection, the payoff of the six-year thread); Grade 6 Unit 2 (perseverance in the face of the Bardo — Pajapati's walk is perseverance at its most literal); Grade 8 Unit 1 (Twelve Links — what Pajapati's ordination makes possible is the full interruption of the chain, not just the craving-watching practice students learned in Unit 1; the ordination gives her the structure to take practice all the way).

The walk body moment is load-bearing in this class. Invest in it. If students feel self-conscious standing and walking in class, acknowledge it briefly: "I know this is a little unusual. Walk anyway. The body knows things the mind is still working out."
