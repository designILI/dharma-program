# G8.U1.C3 — Watching a Craving Arise
## Grade 8 · Unit 1 · Class 3 of 5 · Watching a Craving Arise
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A guided awareness practice → first-person phenomenological observation of the arising of craving in real time, mapped against the Twelve Links
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Experiential learning as evidence — direct first-person observation functions as data in philosophical argument; what students observe in C3 becomes the primary evidence source for C4's argument construction
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluate — students observe and assess their own cognitive and affective processes against the Twelve Links framework, distinguishing what the teaching predicts from what is actually experienced
**Prior Class Summary:** C2 unpacked the Twelve Links in three clusters, introduced the concept of conditionality as both the mechanism of suffering and the ground of liberation, and identified "Link 7.5 — the gap" as the intervention point the practices target. Students annotated their chain drawings with cluster groupings, causal arrows, and their chosen intervention point. They carry the phrase "the gap" and the question of which intervention point gives most leverage.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the term *phenomenology* and what it means to apply a phenomenological method to one's own experience
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that Buddhist meditation practice is a systematic first-person investigation of the arising of mental events — not a relaxation technique but a method for catching the Twelve Links in motion
Students will **BE ABLE TO** write immediate, precise phenomenological observation notes describing at which link they caught the chain, where they lost the thread, and what surprised them — notes usable as philosophical evidence in C4
Students will **FEEL** either the genuine satisfaction of catching a craving arising before it has become an impulse to act, or the genuine frustration of finding the chain faster than attention — both are honest, both are data

---

### Materials Checklist
- Students' annotated chain drawings from C1 and C2 (carried from last class)
- Paper for immediate observation notes after the practice — one sheet per student, already on desks before class begins so there is no delay between practice and writing
- Pencils (not pens — faster for immediate capture)
- Room arranged for practice: if chairs are moveable, arrange them so each student has enough space to sit without touching neighbours; if fixed, students sit upright in their chairs in standard meditation posture
- Clocks or timer visible to the teacher, not to students — so the teacher can track phases without interrupting practice to check time
- **Plan B:** If the room is too loud or unsettled for extended quiet practice, the teacher narrows Phase 2 from 10 to 5 minutes, keeps the instruction sequence the same, and moves directly to individual writing; the observation notes can be written even from a partial or disrupted practice — what students could not catch is itself phenomenological data

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full Grade 8 prayer set, led by the student leader of the day. Sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Tsog Due pa. Student leader conducts.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
No extended PKA today — the class moves quickly toward the practice. Brief orientation:

*"Last class I used the phrase 'Link 7.5 — the gap.' The gap between feeling tone and craving. The gap where practice lives. Today: we go looking for that gap in real time. Not in theory. In this room, right now."*

*"Your chain drawings are in front of you. Before we begin: find Links 6, 7, 8, and 9 on your drawing. Those four links are what we are going to watch today. Contact, feeling tone, craving, clinging. That's the sequence. That's what we're looking for."*

Students locate the four links on their drawings. Thirty seconds.

*"You will not be trying to make the practice go well. You will be trying to observe it accurately. Those are completely different goals."*

**Fallback if students seem unclear on the sequence:** Do one rapid recall — "Link 6 is what? Contact. Link 7 is what? Feeling tone. Link 8 is what? Craving. Link 9 is what? Clinging. Good. That's the territory. Now: let's go inside."

---

#### Mini Lesson — 5 minutes
Brief — this class reserves most of its time for the practice and for writing. The mini-lesson frames the method, not the content.

*"I am going to guide an awareness practice. Your job is not to meditate peacefully — it is to be a careful observer of your own experience. Those goals can feel similar, but they are not. Peaceful meditation asks you to settle. Phenomenological observation asks you to notice — accurately, precisely, without editing — what is actually happening. Including the unsettled parts."*

*"The observation sequence: sensation → feeling tone → craving → impulse. You are trying to catch each transition before the next one has begun. Most people find that the chain moves faster than attention. That is important data. Some people catch one transition clearly, once, in twenty minutes of looking. That is also important data. Both are philosophically usable."*

**Vocabulary Moment:** *"The term* phenomenology *— from the Greek* phainomenon, *'that which appears,' and* logos, *'study of.' The systematic first-person study of conscious experience as it is actually happening — not as it is reported afterward, not as it is theorised to be, but as it appears in direct observation right now. This is not a Western invention. Buddhist meditation is one of the oldest phenomenological practices in human history. The Twelve Links are a phenomenological map — produced by practitioners who watched the arising of their own experience with great precision over long periods. You are entering that method now."*

**Body Moment:** The entire guided practice that follows is the body moment. There is nothing separate.

**Emotional Hook:** Named here for the teacher, not announced to students — the genuine surprise or frustration of trying to catch the arising of craving and finding it faster than attention. Or the satisfaction, if it comes, of catching it once, clearly. Either experience is the hook.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
This occurs *after* the practice and *before* the writing — a brief verbal exchange while the experience is still live.

*"Tell your partner — just one minute each — did you catch a craving arising? At what point did you notice it: before it became an impulse, during the impulse, or only after? What was the feeling tone that preceded it? One minute each, then write."*

Do not debrief aloud to the whole class. Move directly to individual writing.

---

#### Activity — 20 minutes

**Phase 1 — Settling (5 minutes):**
*"Sit upright — spine straight, hands in your lap, eyes closed or softly downcast. Take three slow breaths and let the body settle. You don't need to do anything with your mind yet. Just settle."*

Wait. Long silence. Then: *"If thoughts come, let them come. If sounds come from outside, let them arrive. You are not trying to control what arises. You are setting up to observe it."*

**Phase 2 — The phenomenological walk-through (10 minutes):**
Teacher guides slowly, with long pauses between each instruction. The pauses are not filler — they are the practice.

*"Notice a sensation anywhere in the body. It can be anywhere — hands, face, feet, the contact of clothing on skin. Just find one sensation. Don't choose the most dramatic or the most comfortable. Any sensation."*

[Fifteen-second pause.]

*"Label it — to yourself, silently. Pressure. Warmth. Tingling. Ache. Heaviness. Just one word."*

[Twenty-second pause.]

*"Now: notice whether it has a feeling tone. Pleasant? Unpleasant? Neutral? Don't try to change it. Don't try to intensify or diminish it. Just name its quality."*

[Twenty-second pause.]

*"Now: notice whether any wish arises. Any impulse — however small — to have more of this sensation, or less of it, or for it to be different in some way. You don't have to manufacture a wish. Just notice whether one is there."*

[Thirty-second pause.]

*"If a wish is there: watch it. Don't act on it. Don't suppress it. Don't judge it. Just watch what it does. Does it intensify? Does it move to a different object? Does it fade when you simply observe it?"*

[Thirty-second pause.]

*"Now let that sensation go. Find a different one. Start again. Sensation — label — feeling tone — wish. Move slowly."*

[The teacher repeats this cycle two or three times, with longer pauses each time. The final cycle should include extended silence — one full minute — before the close.]

*"Slowly bring your attention back to the whole body. Take a breath. And when you're ready — eyes open."*

**Phase 3 — Immediate writing (5 minutes):**
Students write immediately — before speaking to anyone, before recalling anything. Fast, uncensored, precise:
- What I observed
- At which link I caught the chain (or couldn't)
- Where I lost the thread
- What surprised me
- One question that arose during the practice

This writing is the primary evidence for C4's argument. Teacher circulates very quietly — no interruption. If a student seems stuck, write on a small slip of paper: "Just describe what happened. Even 'I couldn't catch anything' is a description."

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
*"Every meditation tradition in history — every one — is at some level an attempt to develop this capacity: to see what the mind is doing before it has already done it. To observe the arising of experience with enough precision and enough stillness that the gap between feeling tone and craving becomes visible. Some traditions call this equanimity. Some call it presence. The Buddhist tradition calls it* vipassanā — *clear seeing."*

*"The Twelve Links are not a theory about other people's minds. They are the practitioner's manual for exactly what you just attempted. What you found in that practice — whatever it was — is your data. Next class: you build an argument from it."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full Grade 8 prayer set. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer added at close.

Teacher's closing line: *"What you observed today is philosophy. Keep the notes."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The phenomenological observation notes from C3 are the primary source material for C4's argument-building. Do not collect them — students keep them. Students who observed clearly during the practice have concrete first-person data: "I found the feeling tone but the craving had already become an impulse before I noticed it." Students who found the exercise frustrating also have data: "I couldn't catch the chain before it had already moved to the next link — it was too fast." Both are philosophically usable and both generate legitimate arguments about the nature of the mind. Do not frame one as better than the other in debrief. Note for your own reflection: students who are restless during the practice are themselves illustrating Links 7–9 in real time — the restlessness is a craving arising from a feeling tone (discomfort with stillness). If the energy in the room is strongly unsettled during Phase 2, you can name this aloud after the practice: "Some of you were restless. What was the feeling tone that produced the restlessness? Can you trace it?" Spiral note: this is the first extended period of unstructured practice silence the curriculum has asked of Grade 8. It will feel different at 13–14 than it did in earlier grades. Students from Grade 6 carried a brief grounding practice into the Bardo unit; Grade 7 held healing intention through four Medicine Buddha classes. Grade 8's practice context is self-observation, not healing or devotion — a different relationship with stillness. Some students will find this easier; some will find it harder. Both reactions are worth noting in your reflection.
