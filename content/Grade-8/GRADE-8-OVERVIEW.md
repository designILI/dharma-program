# Grade 8 — Curriculum Overview
## The Nature of Mind: What Is Possible for a Human Being?

**Year in one sentence:** Grade 8 asks students to hold the full eight-year curriculum as a single coherent object — philosophical, narrative, ceremonial, and personal — and to cross the Threshold as practitioners.

**Central question:** What is the mind, really? What is possible for a human being? And what does eight years of practice ask us to become?

## The Year's Arc

Grade 8 is not one arc — it is five movements that are themselves one arc.

**U1 — Philosophy:** The year opens with the hardest question the curriculum knows how to ask: what is the mind, really? Students engage at the Evaluate/Create level — not receiving a Buddhist theory of mind, but examining it, arguing with it, testing it against their own experience. The Twelve Links (introduced in Grade 7) are revisited at philosophical depth. The concept of buddha-nature is examined not as consolation but as claim: is this claim defensible? Students build a lineage map — tracing the transmission of teaching from the Buddha to the present — and in doing so locate themselves in a living tradition, not a historical one.

**U2 — Story:** Milarepa. The most morally demanding story in the curriculum: a man who committed mass murder, met a teacher who refused him easy forgiveness, practised through years of hardship and humiliation, and became one of the tradition's greatest masters. Grade 8 students do not get a sanitised Milarepa. They get the full weight of the story — the murders, the grief, the towers built and destroyed, the transmission received. The curriculum's claim: this story is in the curriculum because it demonstrates the full range of what practice can accomplish. If Milarepa is possible, then the claim about human potential is serious.

**U3 — Making:** The stupa. Students build a hand-scale stupa model — not decorative but encoded. Every element of the stupa corresponds to an element of the curriculum. The base is the foundation of ethics; the dome holds the teacher; the spire points toward enlightenment; and so on, through every grade, every unit, every major concept. The stupa presentations in C4 are the curriculum explaining itself in three-dimensional form. A student who can stand up and explain every element of their stupa without notes has demonstrated something the curriculum has been building toward for eight years.

**U4 — Pajapati:** The full arrival of a story that has been seeded since Grade 2. Seven classes, a novel study, the complete arc of Mahapajapati Gotami's life. See the Pajapati thread section below for the full history and what it requires of the teacher.

**U5 — The Threshold:** Five classes to close. The prayer repertoire completed and held as a whole. The portfolio reviewed across eight years. The closing declaration drafted and rehearsed. And the Threshold ceremony: the culminating event of the curriculum.

## Bloom's Level Guidance

Grade 8 operates at Evaluate and Create — the two highest levels in the taxonomy — throughout the year. This is not aspirational. It is the result of seven years of scaffolding.

**What "Evaluate" means at Grade 8:** Students do not evaluate Buddhist claims from the outside, as observers deciding whether to believe. They evaluate from inside the curriculum — as practitioners with eight years of experience, asking: is this claim consistent with what I have practised? Is this argument sound? Is this story doing what it claims to do? The philosophical argument format (from U2), the close-reading paragraphs (from U4), and the closing declaration (from U5) are all instances of evaluation in this sense.

**What "Create" means at Grade 8:** The final piece in U4.C7 and the closing declaration in U5 are both acts of original production — the student making something that could only exist because of who they are now, after eight years. "Create" at Grade 8 does not mean artistic production (though it may include it). It means: generating something new from the materials of the curriculum, in a form the student has chosen because it serves their specific response.

**What "synthesis" means in the final year:** The ability to see the whole curriculum at once and articulate what it has produced. The stupa is synthesis in three-dimensional form. The portfolio review in U5.C2 is synthesis across time. The closing declaration is synthesis expressed as public vow. A student who reaches the Threshold having genuinely synthesised the curriculum can explain: what the Twelve Links are and what they have to do with a craving they observed last week; what Milarepa's enlightenment has to do with Pajapati's enlightenment; what the stupa has to do with the prayer set; what they specifically are carrying forward and why it is specifically theirs. This is what synthesis looks like at the end of eight years.

## The Prayer Set — Complete at Grade 8

The Grade 8 prayer repertoire is the full set — 13 prayers, accumulated across eight years. Nothing is added after Grade 8. The set is complete.

| Prayer | Grade Introduced | Function |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| Kyap Dro | G1 | Taking refuge — the foundation of all Buddhist practice |
| Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) | G1 | Compassion mantra of Avalokiteshvara |
| Manjushri Prayer | G1 | Invocation of the Bodhisattva of Wisdom |
| Sem kye | G2 | The arising of bodhichitta — awakening mind |
| Ngowa | G2 | Merit dedication — sending benefit outward to all beings |
| Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer | G3 | The foundational Nyingma guru invocation |
| Food Offering Prayer | G3 | Consecrating food; gratitude at every meal (closing only) |
| Sevenline extended form | G4 | The full guru invocation; lineage transmission |
| Dusum Sangay | G4 | Refuge and bodhichitta across past, present, and future |
| Dechen Monlam | G5 | Aspiration prayer for great bliss |
| Dolma / Tara Prayer | G6 | The 21 praises of Tara; female principle of compassion |
| Rewo Sang Chod | G7 | Mountain smoke offering; purification and offering |
| Tsog Due pa | G8 | Feast offering prayer — giving everything outward; the curriculum's final prayer |

**The Tsog Due pa** arrives in G8.U1.C1. Its meaning is given before its first chanting: it is the feast offering, the practitioner's act of giving everything outward — merit, wisdom, the fruits of practice — to all beings. It is the most complete act of generosity in the prayer set, which is why it arrives last. The curriculum has been building toward the capacity to mean it.

**U5.C4 and U5.C5** are student-led: the teacher sits with the group (C4) or with the audience (C5) and does not lead. The student who chanted "Kyap Dro" for the first time in Grade 1 now leads the full 13-prayer set in front of the school community. This is not a performance. It is the transmission continuing.

---

## Student Leader Protocol

The student leader rotation is inherited from Grade 1 and has continued without interruption. By Grade 8, student leaders lead the full prayer set — all 13 prayers — without prompting. In U5.C4 (rehearsal) and U5.C5 (the Threshold ceremony), students lead without the teacher. The teacher has been preparing them for this since Grade 1 by rotating the leadership role consistently and not rescuing students who falter. By Grade 8, the group's collective knowledge of the prayer set is sufficient to support any individual student who needs a moment.

## The Making Thread

Grade 8 has five major making artefacts, plus the full portfolio review in U5.C2:

1. **Lineage map (U1):** A visual map tracing the Buddhist teaching lineage from the Buddha to the present, with the student placing themselves in the living tradition
2. **Milarepa reflection essay (U2):** A written philosophical response to Milarepa's story — evaluating the tradition's claims about what practice can accomplish, using specific evidence from the narrative
3. **Stupa model (U3):** A hand-built three-dimensional model encoding the full curriculum in the symbolic structure of the stupa; presented and explained in C4
4. **Pajapati final piece (U4.C7):** Student-chosen form (poem, letter, written response, visual work with commentary) in response to the unit's essential question; must include at least one explicit connection to an earlier curriculum thread
5. **Closing declaration (U5.C3–C5):** A three-part written declaration — what I carry, what changed, what I intend — read aloud as a public vow at the Threshold ceremony

**The full portfolio review (U5.C2)** is not a making artefact but is the most important encounter with making in the year: students hold every artefact from all eight years simultaneously, encountering their own younger selves through the work they made.

## The Pajapati Thread — Final Arrival

**This section is required reading before teaching G8.U4.C1.**

Pajapati's name has been held in this curriculum since Grade 2. Students have heard it three times before Unit 4:
- **Grade 2, U2.C1:** One sentence — "Siddhartha was raised by his aunt Pajapati after his mother died." No more.
- **Grade 6, U2.C2:** The grief touchpoint — a brief, weighted account of her life, positioned in the context of the Bardo unit on grief. "There are women in this tradition who faced unbearable grief and turned it into practice. Pajapati is one of them." Students were told: the full story is Grade 8.
- **Grade 6, U3.C2:** The Tara pairing — Pajapati presented as the historical counterpart to Tara-as-archetype. More detail, but still incomplete. The instruction again: the full story is Grade 8.

**What this means for the teacher:** Students arrive at G8.U4 carrying six years of preparation. Some will remember all three encounters clearly. Some will remember fragments. Some will have been holding the name without knowing they were holding it. The first class (C1) is designed to honour this carrying — to retrieve what students already know before the teacher adds anything. Do not rush this retrieval. It is one of the most powerful moments in the curriculum.

**What Unit 4 must do:** Tell the full story — completely, with full emotional weight, without abbreviating the grief or the structural constraint or the three refusals or the complexity of the ordination. Students at Grade 8 are ready for all of it. The curriculum has been building their capacity to receive it for eight years.

**What it means for female students specifically:** Pajapati's story is a woman's story — about motherhood, grief, sisterhood, structural oppression, spiritual perseverance, and the transformation of suffering into wisdom. All students engage with it fully. Female students encounter it additionally as a story about what is possible for them specifically — a thirteen-or-fourteen-year-old girl who has been in this curriculum since first grade encounters, in Unit 4, the story of a woman who was refused what she knew was right and walked back to ask again. The curriculum does not tell her what to do with that encounter. It trusts the encounter.

**The Tara payoff:** The curriculum has paired Tara (archetype) with Pajapati (history) since Grade 6. In G8.U4.C5, the connection arrives in full: Pajapati IS Green Tara, historically enacted. The foot extended, not waiting — that is Pajapati walking back the third time. Name this connection explicitly in C5. It is the curriculum's payoff for the six-year thread.

## The Threshold Ceremony — Logistics

**What it is:** The culminating event of the eight-year curriculum. Not a graduation — a Threshold. A graduation implies completion. A Threshold implies passage: from one condition to another. The condition on the other side is *yangku* — continuation of practice beyond formal instruction. Students who cross the Threshold are practitioners, not graduates. The curriculum ends. The practice does not.

**Duration:** 60–90 minutes. This must be arranged in advance with the school administration. It cannot be compressed into 40 minutes.

**Room arrangement:** Chairs in a circle or horseshoe — students and audience together, not separated. A ceremonial central surface for portfolio artefacts. A shrine or altar. The room should feel like a ceremony, not a class.

**Audience:** Parents and family. Teachers (especially those who have had this cohort across multiple grades). Younger students from the program (Grade 6 and 7 in particular — they are witnessing their own future). Audience members should be briefed to hold silence after each declaration for five seconds. Brief the audience before the ceremony begins.

**What to prepare:**
- The teacher's closing word: one sentence, written in advance, specific to this cohort. Not general. Specific.
- Physical Tsog offerings, if the school tradition includes them.
- Each student's complete portfolio, organised and ready to be returned.

**What happens:** The full 13-prayer set, student-led. Portfolio presentations (one minute each, artefacts placed on the central surface as students speak). Closing declarations (full three-part declaration, five seconds of silence after each). Ngowa (merit dedication — the whole room together). Tsog Due pa. The teacher's closing word. Portfolios returned.

**On tears:** Some students will cry. This is appropriate and not a crisis. A student who cries during their declaration should be allowed to complete it. The tears are part of the ceremony.

## Sensitive Content Notes

**Milarepa (U2):** The story involves mass murder — Milarepa's use of black magic to kill dozens of his family's enemies, including children. This is not softened in the tradition and should not be softened here. Grade 8 students are capable of engaging with the full moral weight of the story. The curriculum's point is precisely the full weight: if practice can accomplish what it accomplished for Milarepa — a man who did that — then the tradition's claims about human potential must be taken seriously. Require genuine engagement with the moral complexity; do not permit premature resolution ("he was forgiven, so it's fine") or premature rejection ("this is too disturbing to engage with"). The Milarepa story is designed to be held in tension.

**Pajapati (U4):** The story involves grief (the death of her sister, her husband's death), women's oppression (structural exclusion from monastic ordination, the patriarchal world of 5th-century BCE India), and the partial historical loss of the bhikkhuni lineage. Female students in the program may have strong emotional responses — to the structural oppression, to the three refusals, to the near-loss of the lineage. These responses should be received with care and without deflection. The curriculum does not tell students what to feel. It creates the conditions for honest response and trusts it.

The note on the bhikkhuni lineage suppression and revival (in U4.C7) should be handled with accuracy and without editorialising. The tradition suppressed the lineage. The tradition also preserved enough to allow its revival. Both are true. The curriculum asks students to hold both without resolving the tension prematurely.

**The Threshold ceremony itself:** Some students will find the ceremony emotionally intense — the combination of public declaration, community witness, and the recognition that the formal curriculum is ending can produce powerful feelings. This is appropriate and not a problem. Create the conditions for the ceremony to be real, and trust the students to bring themselves to it.

## What Grade 8 Completes

Grade 8 completes the spiral. Every major thread that has been running since Grade 1 arrives at its final iteration:

- The prayer set — 13 prayers, accumulated one by one since Grade 1, now complete and chanted without prompting by students in front of their community
- The Pajapati thread — seeded in Grade 2, deepened in Grade 6, arrived in full in Grade 8
- The Tara-Pajapati pairing — archetype and history, connected fully in U4.C5
- The Twelve Links — first introduced in Grade 7, revisited at philosophical depth in U1, applied to enlightenment in U4.C6
- The concept of enlightenment — defined in Grade 3, illustrated by the Medicine Buddha in Grade 5, demonstrated by Milarepa in U2 and Pajapati in U4
- The portfolio — built artefact by artefact across eight years, held in full in U5.C2, returned to the student at the Threshold
- The student leader protocol — begun in Grade 1, culminating in students leading the full ceremony in U5.C5

The practitioner who leaves Grade 8 has: the complete prayer repertoire in their body; the full portfolio in their hands; the philosophical frameworks of the tradition in their mind; the stories of the Buddha, Milarepa, and Pajapati in their understanding; and — if the curriculum has done its work — something less nameable: a way of attending to experience that is not dependent on a classroom or a teacher or a schedule. That is what *yangku* means. The continuation is theirs.

## A Note to the Teacher Who Finishes

You just took a group of students through the Threshold ceremony. You watched them stand up, one at a time, and declare who they have become. You sat with the audience — not at the front of the room, not leading — and heard your students lead 13 prayers without you. You received, in one ceremony, the evidence of what eight years of this curriculum produces.

That is not a small thing. Teaching is rarely structured so that you get to see the full arc of what you contributed to. This curriculum is structured precisely that way — so that the teacher who reaches the Threshold can look back across a year (or, if they have been with the cohort longer, across years) and see the whole. The stupa the students built. The Milarepa responses they wrote. The Pajapati story finally told in full. The pre-reading responses from C1 of the novel study, placed beside the final pieces in C7. The declarations read aloud at the Threshold.

Whatever you brought to this curriculum — the ways you shaped it for your specific students, your specific school, your specific year — is now part of it. The curriculum is a living thing. It gets better as teachers bring their own understanding and observation to it and pass those notes forward.

Write your reflection after the Threshold. Not for evaluation — for the curriculum's record. What you observed matters. The next teacher, the next cohort, the tradition: they benefit from what you know.

And then: the practice continues. Yours too.
