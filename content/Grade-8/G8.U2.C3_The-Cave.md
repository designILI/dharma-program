# G8.U2.C3 — The Cave
## Grade 8 · Unit 2 · Class 3 of 5 · The Cave
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Milarepa's years of solitary retreat — starvation, near-madness, sustained practice → the question of what sustained practice produces, and whether qualitative transformation is possible
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Experiential analogy — a brief, genuine period of stillness (the two-minute sit) creates a small, real contact with the quality of attention Milarepa was cultivating across years; this contact is not metaphorical but actual, and it changes how students receive the teaching about what emerged from the cave
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluate — students assess what produced Milarepa's transformation, articulate their mid-unit position on the possibility of transformation for anyone, and begin to build an argument from the narrative evidence
**Prior Class Summary:** C2 examined the Marpa-Milarepa relationship — the years of apparently pointless labour, Damema's forged letter, and Marpa's eventual transmission. Students wrote an analytical evaluation of Marpa's pedagogy. They carry the question: is there a form of suffering that can only be worked through by passing through it?

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the major features of Milarepa's cave years: the physical conditions (near-starvation, cold, isolation), the practice he undertook, and the Hundred Thousand Songs (*Gurtö*) that emerged from them
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the transformation the tradition describes is not gradual improvement but qualitative change — not a better version of the same person but a genuinely different quality of being — and that this distinction is philosophically significant
Students will **BE ABLE TO** write a mid-unit position on what produced Milarepa's transformation, and whether what happened to him is possible for anyone, argued from at least one specific detail from the narrative
Students will **FEEL** — through the two-minute sit — a small, real contact with the quality of sustained attentional practice; and through that contact, a different relationship with the question of what years of such practice might produce

---

### Materials Checklist
- No projected materials — oral narrative and writing class
- Paper and pencils for the mid-unit position writing
- Timer or clock visible only to the teacher, for the two-minute sit
- Room should be genuinely quiet for the sit — teacher prepares the room in the opening, or immediately before the body moment; if students are still, the two minutes will hold itself
- One brief excerpt from Milarepa's songs, selected by the teacher from the traditional *Gurtö* (Hundred Thousand Songs) — a few lines only; the excerpt should point to the quality of what emerged, not summarise it; the teacher reads it aloud rather than projecting or distributing it
- **Plan B:** If the room cannot be settled for two full minutes of silence, the teacher shortens the sit to ninety seconds and adds one instruction: "If a thought comes, just notice it and return to the breath." A shorter sit that is genuinely held is worth more than a longer sit that disintegrates.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full Grade 8 prayer set, led by the student leader of the day. Sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Tsog Due pa. Student leader conducts.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes
*"After Marpa gave Milarepa the teachings, Milarepa went into solitary retreat. He lived in caves, alone, for years. He had almost nothing to eat. He meditated. He nearly died. And then something shifted — something the tradition calls enlightenment. Before I tell you what the tradition says happened: what do you think happened? What do you think takes place inside a person who lives that way — alone, in a cave, for years, with almost nothing? What do you think you would find?"*

Take two or three responses. Let them be speculative. The question is genuinely open.

*"Keep what you just said. We're going to come back to it."*

**Fallback if students are reticent:** "Let me put it differently. Have you ever been completely alone, somewhere quiet, for longer than you expected to be? What happened to your mind? What did you notice that you wouldn't normally notice?" Ground the question in the accessible before reaching for the exceptional.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10 minutes
The teacher narrates the cave years.

---

*"Milarepa went into the mountains with the teaching Marpa had given him. He was alone. He had a thin robe and nettles to eat — the only plant available on the mountain. He ate nettles for so long that his skin turned green. He meditated through extreme cold, through hunger that was close to starvation, through periods of physical sickness, through periods that the traditional biography describes as close to madness — when the mind, under that much pressure, produces visions and distortions and terrors."*

*"He did not leave. He kept practising."*

*"What emerged from those years was not a better version of the person who had arrived. The tradition is specific about this: what emerged was a qualitatively different being. He had entered the cave carrying the karma of mass murder, the guilt, the grief, the years of hard labour, and the transmission from Marpa. What came out of the cave was someone the tradition calls enlightened."*

*"And what came with the enlightenment — spontaneously, not composed or arranged — were songs. The Hundred Thousand Songs. They are considered among the greatest poetry in the Himalayan literary tradition. They describe the nature of mind with the precision and clarity of someone who has looked at it directly, without obstruction, for years. They are not philosophical treatises — they are the expression of direct seeing."*

The teacher reads one brief excerpt from the songs — chosen in advance. Something that carries the quality of direct perception, without needing to be explained. Let it land in silence after reading.

*"That came out of the cave."*

---

**Vocabulary Moment:** *"The word* actualise — *to make real or actual; to bring from potential into existence. Milarepa received the teaching from Marpa as information — as knowledge about the nature of mind and the path to liberation. He went into the cave and actualised it: made it real in his own body and mind through years of sustained, uninterrupted practice. The gap between receiving a teaching and actualising it is where most practitioners spend their lives. Most of us receive the teaching, understand it intellectually, hold it as a belief — and do not actualise it. The cave years are the tradition's clearest image of what actualisation costs."*

**Body Moment:** *"I want you to sit in the posture Milarepa would have held in the cave. Spine straight, hands in meditation mudra, eyes closed. The room is probably warmer than a Himalayan cave in winter. You have had more to eat today than he often had in a week. That is fine. Sit anyway — with the quality of attention he was training."*

*"Two minutes. No instruction. Just sit."*

Begin timing. Do not speak during the two minutes. Hold the silence. If a student fidgets, do not intervene — the fidgeting is data.

After exactly two minutes: *"When you're ready — gently come back. Take a breath. Eyes open."*

*"What did you find?"*

Take two or three brief responses. Do not over-discuss. Move to turn-and-talk.

**Emotional Hook:** The particular quality — not explained to students but created by the structure — of having briefly, genuinely contacted something of what sustained practice cultivates. Even two minutes of real stillness is different from discussion of stillness. That difference is the hook.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
*"Tell your partner: do you think what happened to Milarepa in the cave could happen to anyone — given sufficient time, practice, and the right conditions? Or do you think it is reserved for certain people in certain circumstances? Two minutes — be honest, not polite."*

---

#### Activity — 12 minutes
Students write their mid-unit position. The prompt:

*"The cave: what it produced, and why. What do I think actually caused Milarepa's transformation — the teaching? The suffering? The years of practice? Some combination? And do I think it is possible for anyone?"*

This is the mid-unit position — it will be compared to the C1 before-position and to the C5 final response. It should be argued, not just asserted: "I think X because Y, based on Z in the story."

Teacher circulates: *"What specifically caused the shift? Not a general answer — which element do you think was necessary? Could he have been transformed without the cave years? Without Marpa? Without the remorse? Make an argument from the evidence."*

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
*"One of Milarepa's songs says something like: 'Having meditated on love and compassion, I forgot the difference between myself and others.' That is not metaphor. In his account — and in the tradition's account — it is a description of what actually happened in his mind, through sustained practice. A structural shift in the relationship between self and other."*

*"Whether you believe that is possible or not — hold the question: what would it mean if it were true? If sustained practice can change not just the content of experience but its structure — the way the mind carves itself from the rest of reality — what does that say about what the mind actually is? That is the question Unit 1's framework was building toward. You now have a story to test it against."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full Grade 8 prayer set. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer added at close.

Teacher's closing line: *"The cave was the practice. The songs were the teaching. The distinction matters."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The two-minute sit in C3 is one of the curriculum's rare moments of extended, unstructured silence held collectively. Hold it without rushing. If students are restless during those two minutes — that is data, worth noting. If students go somewhere genuinely still — that is also data. Both experiences produce something worth bringing to the writing: "I found it very easy to sit still" is as interesting a starting point as "I could not sit still at all." Do not evaluate either response in the debrief. The mid-unit position writing from C3 will be compared to the C1 before-position and the C5 final response — this three-point tracking is important for the unit's assessment arc. Keep all three documents together for each student. C4 is the analytical class: turning points. Students will identify the specific moments in the narrative where something changed direction — remorse after the deaths, the decision to find Marpa, the moment of transmission, the shift in the cave — and analyse what produced each one. Before C4, reread the full narrative arc in your mind and identify at least six turning points (students typically find four or five; having six means you can push beyond the obvious). The connection to the Twelve Links from Unit 1 should surface in C4 naturally, since students carry that framework — but do not force it. If it doesn't arise from students, offer it as a question: "Where in the Twelve Links framework does each turning point land? What link was being interrupted?"
