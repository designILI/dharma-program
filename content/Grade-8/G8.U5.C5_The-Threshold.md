# G8.U5.C5 — The Threshold
## Grade 8 · Unit 5 · Class 5 of 5 · The Threshold
*Duration: 60–90 minutes — this is not a standard 40-minute class. Arrange this time in advance with the school administration. An audience is present throughout.*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** The ceremony of completion — the full prayer set led by students, the portfolio presented, the closing declaration read aloud → being witnessed by one's community in the act of declaring who one has become
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Public declaration as consolidation — the act of saying something aloud, in the presence of witnesses, in a ceremonial frame, consolidates what was developed over eight years into a form that can be carried forward. The ceremony does not evaluate learning. It enacts it.
**Bloom's Level:** Create / Synthesise — the ceremony is the final synthesis: eight years of prayers, eight years of artefacts, and a declaration of what the student carries forward, all held together in one event
**Prior Class Summary:** C4 was the full rehearsal — students led the prayer set without the teacher, delivered their portfolio presentations and closing declarations, and received specific feedback. They carry their revised declarations, their selected portfolio artefacts, and the instruction: "Read as if every word matters — because it does."

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the full sequence of the Threshold ceremony and their role in each element; that the ceremony is a threshold, not a graduation — it marks a beginning, not a completion
Students will **UNDERSTAND** what it means to be witnessed by a community in the act of declaring who one has become; why a threshold is different from a graduation; what "yangku" means — the continuation of practice beyond formal instruction
Students will **BE ABLE TO** lead the full prayer set from beginning to end in front of an audience, without prompting; deliver their portfolio presentation and closing declaration with full presence; receive the return of their complete portfolio as practitioners
Students will **FEEL** the specific quality of being witnessed — by community, teachers, family — in the act of declaring who one has become; the Threshold as a lived experience of passage rather than conclusion

---

### Materials Checklist

**Room arrangement (must be completed before the ceremony begins):**
- Chairs arranged in a circle or horseshoe — students and audience together, not separated into performer and spectator rows. The ceremony is held in the round.
- A central surface at the heart of the circle: a low table, a platform, or a clean cloth on the floor. This is where portfolio artefacts will be placed as students present. It should be visually clear and ceremonially prepared before the ceremony.
- A shrine or altar on the central surface or at the front of the room: an image of the Buddha or Padmasambhava (as appropriate to the school's tradition), candles or butter lamps (if permitted), flowers or offerings appropriate to the tradition.
- If physical Tsog offerings are part of the school's ceremony tradition: food, flowers, or other offerings arranged on or near the central surface.

**Materials:**
- Each student's complete portfolio — organised, accessible, and returned to students at the end of the ceremony. This is the ceremony's final act: the portfolio going home.
- Student closing declarations (final versions, revised after C4)
- Selected portfolio artefacts (three to five per student)
- Prayer texts (available but not required — students who have been in the program since Grade 1 know the full set from memory)
- Teacher's closing word — written in advance, one sentence, specific to this cohort

**Audience:**
- Parents and family members of each student — personally invited
- Teachers who have had this cohort, especially across multiple grades
- Younger students from the Dharma program, especially Grade 6 and Grade 7 (they are witnessing their own future)
- Any other members of the school community for whom the invitation is appropriate
- Audience members should be briefed: "This is a ceremony, not a school event. We ask for your full presence for the duration. After each student's closing declaration, we hold silence for five seconds before the next. This is intentional — please hold the silence with us."

**Plan B for attendance gaps:** If some invited audience members cannot attend, the ceremony proceeds. The community present is the community present. Do not delay or abbreviate the ceremony because of absent audience members. The ceremony's quality is not determined by audience size.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — Prayer set (10–15 minutes)
The Grade 8 students lead the full prayer set — all 13 prayers — from beginning to end. The teacher sits with the audience. The teacher does not lead, cue, or prompt. The students who began this curriculum at age six or seven chanting "Kyap Dro" now lead the complete 13-prayer set in front of their community.

The audience participates where they know the prayers. Younger students from the program will know the early prayers. Family members may know some. The teacher chants from the audience position.

No ceremony participant needs to be told what to do. The prayer set leads itself.

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Tsog Due pa · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod

A moment of silence after the final prayer. The students hold it. Then the ceremony continues.

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — None
There is no PKA in C5. The ceremony is the whole class. The prayers are the entry.

#### Mini Lesson — None
There is no mini lesson in C5. The ceremony speaks.

**Vocabulary Moment:** None — the ceremony speaks.

**Body Moment:** The entire ceremony is a body moment. Standing to present. Standing to read the declaration. The posture of declaration. The act of placing an artefact on the central surface. These physical acts are the body's encoding of the whole curriculum. They do not need to be named.

**Emotional Hook:** The specific quality of being witnessed — by the community, by teachers, by family — in the act of declaring who one has become. This is not performance and it is not assessment. It is the lived experience of passage: crossing from one side of eight years to the other, in public, held by the people who love and know you.

#### Activity — The Threshold ceremony (45–60 minutes)

**1. Opening prayers** — completed in the Opening above.

**2. Portfolio presentations**
Each student stands in turn. They hold their selected artefacts (three to five pieces). They speak for approximately one minute — not scripted, but prepared: "This is [name of artefact]. I made it in Grade [number]. It matters to me because [specific reason]. It represents [what it encodes from the curriculum]." As each student finishes speaking about an artefact, they place it on the central surface. The surface fills, piece by piece, with eight years of making. When all students have presented, the central surface is a physical record of the curriculum — visible to everyone in the room.

**3. Closing declarations**
Each student stands in turn and reads their three-part closing declaration in full. No introduction is needed. No preamble. The student stands, finds their posture, and reads.

After each declaration: five seconds of silence. No applause. The quality of the silence is the response — it is not absence of feeling, but the tradition's way of holding something without immediately moving on.

**4. Ngowa — Dedication of merit**
After all declarations have been read, the whole room chants the Ngowa together — students, audience, teacher. The merit of eight years of practice is dedicated outward to all beings. This is not symbolic. It is the tradition's claim about what merit dedication does: the accumulated benefit of years of practice, released in all directions. Students lead. The audience follows as they can.

**5. Tsog Due pa**
Chanted as the closing prayer of the ceremony — the feast offering; giving everything outward. Students lead. The room follows. If physical Tsog offerings are present, they are offered in this moment, as appropriate to the school's tradition.

**6. The teacher's closing word**
One sentence. Prepared in advance. Specific to this cohort. Not a general statement about what the curriculum does — a specific word about who these students are, what they accomplished, and what they carry. The teacher stands to deliver it. Then sits.

**7. Students receive their portfolios**
Each student receives their complete portfolio — every artefact, the prayer reflection from U5.C1, the closing declaration. The portfolio is placed in their hands or set before them. It is theirs. It goes home. It does not stay with the school.

#### Debrief — None (formal)
There is no formal debrief in C5. The ceremony is its own closing. The teacher's word is the final teaching sentence of the curriculum.

If time remains after the portfolios are distributed, the teacher may offer one question to the room — not for answer, not for discussion, but for carrying: "What do you do next — not with the curriculum, not with the prayers, but with yourself?"

Then the room opens. People may speak, embrace, be with each other. The ceremony is complete.

#### Closing — The ceremony is the closing
The teacher's word, delivered in step 6, is the final teaching sentence of the curriculum. There is no additional prayer set after the ceremony. The Tsog Due pa, chanted in step 5, is the ceremony's closing prayer.

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: After the Threshold, there is nothing to carry forward in the formal curriculum sense. The curriculum is complete.

What the teacher carries: the experience of having seen what eight years of this design produces. The students who walked into Grade 1 at age six or seven and are leaving Grade 8 at thirteen or fourteen have been in a living curriculum — one that has been building toward this moment the entire time. The Pajapati thread, held for six years and arriving in full. The prayer set, accumulated prayer by prayer and now chanted without prompting in front of the community. The portfolio, built piece by piece across eight years and now held in both hands. These students are practitioners. Not graduates — practitioners. The curriculum has given them everything it has. The rest is theirs.

**On the concept of the Threshold:** The ceremony is a Threshold, not a graduation, because a graduation implies completion — the thing is finished. A Threshold implies: you are crossing from one condition to another. The condition on the other side of the Threshold is not "done with practice" — it is "practising on your own." *Yangku* — continuation. The curriculum ends. The practice does not.

**On students who cry:** Some students will cry during the Threshold ceremony. This is appropriate. It is not a crisis. It does not need to be managed. A student who cries during their declaration should be allowed to complete their declaration — the ceremony does not pause for tears. The tears are part of the ceremony. If a student needs a moment, offer it quietly and briefly; then return to the declaration.

**On younger students as witnesses:** Grade 6 and Grade 7 students who attend the ceremony are not there as audience in the passive sense. They are there as future participants — witnessing what the program asks of its final-year students, and beginning to understand what they are moving toward. This is one of the curriculum's most powerful implicit teachings. The presence of younger students in the room transmits something that cannot be taught in a class.

**Your written notes after the Threshold ceremony:** Please write them. At whatever length feels right — brief is fine. What you observed in the ceremony. Which students surprised you. Which declarations were the most honest, the most specific, the most unexpected. What you noticed in the room when a particular declaration was read. What the moment of portfolio distribution felt like. These observations are part of the curriculum's ongoing life. They belong in the record that passes to the next teacher, the next cohort, the tradition. This curriculum is itself a living thing. You have just contributed to it.
