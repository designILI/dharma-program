# G8.U3.C1 — Mind Made of Stone
## Grade 8 · Unit 3 · Class 1 of 4 · Mind Made of Stone
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** The physical structure of a stupa → the Buddha's mind and the path to enlightenment encoded in three-dimensional form
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Perceptual reframing — something familiar (a stupa the students have walked past for years) is revealed to carry precise meaning in every proportion and element; the reframe is not from ignorance to knowledge but from unreflective familiarity to informed seeing
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluate / Create — students analyse the stupa's symbolic structure, connect each element to specific teachings, and begin designing their own model
**Prior Class Summary:** C5 of Unit 2 was the culminating "Can anyone change?" response — students wrote and shared their final argued positions on Milarepa's transformation and compared them to their C1 before-positions. They carry the range of positions from the room and the recognition that the rest of Grade 8 tests the claim that human beings are capable of more than they think.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the five primary structural elements of a stupa (base/throne, dome, harmika, spire with thirteen rings, pinnacle) and the meaning assigned to each in the tradition
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that a stupa is not a monument or decorative object — it is the Buddha's mind expressed in form; every element encodes a teaching, and walking around one is itself a practice
Students will **BE ABLE TO** sketch a labelled stupa cross-section with the name and meaning of each element, and indicate which of the eight stupa types they intend to build for the C3 making session
Students will **FEEL** the particular arrival of understanding something they have walked past many times without fully knowing — the sense that familiar things can hold much more than they appeared to

---

### Materials Checklist
- Images of stupas — projected or printed (multiple examples: at minimum, one clear structural diagram showing the named elements, and one or two photographs of actual stupas including, if possible, the stupa on or near the school grounds)
- Reference list or diagram of the eight types of stupa — for students to choose from when designing their model
- Paper and pencils for the stupa sketch (one set per student)
- If there is a stupa on or near the school grounds: confirm access with school administration before this class; the body moment requires circumambulation of a real stupa if one is available
- **Plan B for circumambulation:** If there is no accessible stupa on or near the grounds, or if access cannot be confirmed, students circumambulate the room clockwise in silence, maintaining the quality of attention they would bring to circumambulation of a real stupa — the teacher names the stupa they are circling: "Imagine the stupa at [nearest monastery or known location]. Walk as if it is here." A visualised circumambulation is less powerful but philosophically legitimate.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full Grade 8 prayer set, led by the student leader of the day. Sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Tsog Due pa. Student leader conducts.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
*"When you walk past a stupa — in the school grounds, at the monastery, anywhere you have seen one — what do you notice? What do you do? What do you feel?"*

Take three or four responses. Students may say: I walk around it; I touch it; I don't really notice it; I feel something peaceful; I spin the prayer wheels. All are valid. Receive everything.

*"Today you will understand what you have been walking past. Not as a general spiritual sense — as a precise encoding. Every proportion. Every element. Every layer. You will be able to read it."*

**Fallback if students say they have never seen a stupa:** "You have seen this image in this school. You have seen it in books, in photographs, in the school context. Even if you have never walked past a physical one — you have seen the form. What do you know about it already?" Take whatever comes.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 15 minutes
Display the stupa structural diagram and photographs.

*"A stupa — in Tibetan,* chorten *— is the three-dimensional expression of the Buddha's mind and the path to enlightenment. It is not symbolic in the loose sense of 'representing' something. It is a precise encoding: each structural element corresponds to a specific teaching, and the correspondence is not arbitrary — it reflects the internal logic of the path the teaching describes."*

Walk through the elements from base to pinnacle.

**The base / throne (square, four-tiered):**
*"The foundation. Square — the four directions, the stability of earth. Four-tiered — the four immeasurables: loving-kindness, compassion, empathetic joy, equanimity. The base is the earth element. It is what you stand on before you can go anywhere. In practice terms: you cannot move up the path without these four qualities as your ground."*

**The dome (hemisphere above the base):**
*"The dome is the Buddha's body — and the womb of enlightenment. Its rounded form holds the idea that all beings carry within them the seed of awakening, as seeds enclosed in a pod. The dome corresponds to the water element — fluid, adaptive, holding. This is the teaching of Buddha-nature: the capacity for enlightenment is not something you acquire. It is something you already have, enclosed, waiting."*

**The harmika (rectangular box or platform atop the dome):**
*"The harmika sits above the dome like a throne on the dome's crown. It corresponds to the fire element and to the Buddha's mind — the seat of wisdom, the place from which the teaching radiates. The harmika is what supports the spire."*

**The spire (the rings rising from the harmika):**
*"The spire has thirteen rings — the thirteen levels of the bodhisattva path, from the first awakening of the aspiration to benefit all beings to the complete enlightenment of a Buddha. Each ring is a stage of the path. The spire rises from the harmika toward the pinnacle — from the Buddha's mind toward the culmination of practice."*

**The pinnacle (moon, sun, flame or jewel at the top):**
*"The pinnacle: the space element. Open, luminous, unobstructed. This is the nature of mind as the tradition describes it at its most fundamental level — not the conditioned mind that the Twelve Links describe, but the awareness underneath the conditioning. Clear, open, without centre or edge. This is what the stupa points toward, at its highest point."*

*"And then: the eight types of stupa. Each commemorates a different event in the Buddha's life. The most common types are: the stupa of the Enlightenment (commemorating the moment of awakening under the Bodhi tree); the stupa of the First Turning of the Wheel of Dharma (commemorating the first teaching in Sarnath); and the stupa of Parinirvana (commemorating the Buddha's passing). Each type has slightly different proportions in the base or spire. When you design your model in C2 and build it in C3, you will choose which type to make — and you will know what you are commemorating."*

**Vocabulary Moment:** *"The Tibetan word* chorten *(མཆོད་རྟེན) — literally 'support for offerings' or 'worthy of reverence.' The chorten is simultaneously an object worthy of reverence and a support for the practitioner's own offering. When you walk clockwise around a chorten — circumambulate it — you are offering your movement, your attention, your presence. The walking itself is a practice. You have been doing it, perhaps without knowing why. Now you know."*

**Body Moment:** If a stupa is accessible on or near the school grounds — take the class there now. Walk to it in silence. Circumambulate it once, clockwise, in silence, with attention. The teacher does not narrate during the circumambulation.

After the circumambulation, still at the stupa if possible: *"What were you circumambulating? Not the stone. What does the stone encode? You now know."*

If no stupa is accessible: students stand from their chairs. *"We are going to circumambulate the room. Clockwise. In silence. Walk as if you were walking around the stupa at [nearest known stupa location]. Bring the quality of attention you now know is appropriate."* One full circumambulation of the room. Then return to seats. Same question: "What were you circumambulating?"

**Emotional Hook:** The particular arrival — not explained, but felt — of understanding something you have walked past many times and not fully seen.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
*"Tell your partner: which element of the stupa structure corresponds most directly to something you have studied this year — in Unit 1 or Unit 2? What is the connection? Be specific."*

Students will likely connect: the dome's Buddha-nature teaching to the possibility of transformation studied in Milarepa's story; the pinnacle's open luminous nature to the mind the Twelve Links point toward rather than describe; the base's four immeasurables to Grade 2 and Grade 7 practices. All of these are correct. Take two or three connections in the debrief.

---

#### Activity — 10 minutes
Students sketch a stupa cross-section — a simple line drawing showing each element from base to pinnacle, labelled with: the element's name (English and Tibetan if known), what it corresponds to, and which grade or unit first introduced that teaching.

This is the design draft for the C3 model. It is not detailed — it is a first mapping.

Teacher circulates: *"What type will you make — one of the eight? Which event does it commemorate? Or will you make the standard general form?" "What will the proportions look like — roughly, how tall will the spire be relative to the dome?" "What does the dome mean to you — specifically, from your own study?"*

Students who finish quickly: begin adding detail to the sketch — decoration ideas, texture, any inscriptions they might include on the model.

---

#### Debrief — 3 minutes
*"In C3 you build. In C4 you present. Between now and then: look at every stupa you walk past with the attention you now have. You know what you are looking at. That knowledge changes the walking."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full Grade 8 prayer set. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer added at close.

Teacher's closing line: *"It has been here the whole time. You just learned to read it."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The circumambulation body moment — whether with a real stupa or a visualised one — is the pedagogical centre of C1. If there is a stupa on the school grounds, use it without question. Students who have walked past this stupa for eight years and are now told what they have been walking past — and who then walk around it again with that knowledge — experience a kind of double recognition: "I have done this practice already; now I know what it is." That arrival is significant and should not be rushed or narrated. Confirm stupa access with school administration well before C1 (ideally at the planning stage of the term). If access is unavailable on the day, the visualised circumambulation of the room is the fallback — it is real practice, even if less powerful than the physical encounter. C2 deepens the symbolic analysis by connecting each stupa element to specific content from across the eight-year curriculum. Prepare for C2 by reviewing the spiral connections listed in the C2 mini-lesson section — students may surprise you with connections you have not anticipated, and the class is richer when those emerge spontaneously. Confirm materials for C3 before C2: clay or salt dough (sufficient for one model per student), any armature materials, reference images, shaping tools. Materials need to be available and ready before C3 — do not leave this to the last minute.
