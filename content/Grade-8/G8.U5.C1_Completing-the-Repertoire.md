# G8.U5.C1 — Completing the Repertoire
## Grade 8 · Unit 5 · Class 1 of 5 · Completing the Repertoire
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** All 13 prayers chanted with awareness of their completeness → the recognition that the prayer repertoire is not the school's — it belongs to the student and travels with them
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Spaced practice with holistic consolidation — students have been learning prayers one at a time across eight years. Today, for the first time, they hold all 13 simultaneously with conscious awareness of the whole. This is not review — it is a qualitative shift in how the repertoire is known. Knowing each prayer is one kind of knowledge; knowing the complete set as a whole, with the history of its accumulation, is a different and deeper kind.
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluate / Synthesise — students evaluate which prayer has most changed them and synthesise the prayer set as a complete body of practice, understanding its arc across eight years
**Prior Class Summary:** G8.U4.C7 was the culminating Pajapati class — students produced their final pieces (poem, letter, written response, or visual work), had their pre-reading responses from C1 returned, and heard the debrief: "That is what eight years did." They carry the full arc of the novel study and the particular quality of something arriving at its completion.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** all 13 prayers in the Grade 8 repertoire, organised by the grade in which each was introduced, with the tradition and purpose of each named
Students will **UNDERSTAND** what a prayer repertoire is — not a list but a living body of practice; that the prayers have accumulated over eight years and are now complete; that the repertoire is theirs and travels with them
Students will **BE ABLE TO** chant the full prayer set from beginning to end with conscious awareness of its completeness; identify which prayer has most changed them and explain specifically how and when
Students will **FEEL** the recognition that the repertoire is complete — and the particular quality of holding something whole after years of accumulation, piece by piece

---

### Materials Checklist
- Prayer texts (all 13, in order) — printed or available in the student's own notes; students who have been in the program since Grade 1 should know all prayers from memory, but having the text available is appropriate
- Whiteboard or large paper (for the Grade-by-grade prayer map during the mini lesson)
- Student journals or writing paper (for the prayer reflection activity)
- Timer
- **Plan B:** If the full prayer set chanting runs longer than 5 minutes at the opening (some groups chant more slowly, especially with all 13), do not compress the chanting. Adjust by shortening the mini lesson — the complete prayer map can be given as a written handout rather than walked through orally if needed. The chanting is the core of C1; do not sacrifice it for content coverage.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 8 minutes (extended for this class)
Full Grade 8 prayer set — all 13, chanted with full conscious attention to the completeness of the set. Today, before beginning, the teacher names this explicitly: "We chant the full set today — all 13 prayers, in order. This is the first class in which we chant it with conscious awareness that the set is complete. Nothing is being added after today. This is the whole repertoire." Then: student leaders (ideally one per prayer, or rotating in pairs) lead each prayer in sequence. The teacher chants with the group — fully present, not conducting.

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Tsog Due pa · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod

After the full set, a moment of silence. Then the teacher speaks.

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes
Teacher: "That was the full set — all 13 prayers, accumulated across eight years. Which one entered you most deeply this morning? Not your favourite — not the most impressive or the most difficult. The one that landed most fully today, in this chanting, in this room." Take responses from the room — receive each one without evaluating or ranking. Note them mentally or briefly on the board.

Then: "We are going to map the whole repertoire now — every prayer, in the order it entered your life. Some of you will have been holding the first ones since age six."

#### Mini Lesson — 10 minutes

**The complete repertoire — its history**

Teacher walks through all 13 prayers by grade of introduction. This should be told as a story, not recited as a list. For each prayer: what it is, what it does in the tradition, why it was introduced at that grade, what it was designed to open.

- **Grade 1:** Kyap Dro (the refuge prayer — taking refuge in the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha; the foundation of all practice); Om Mani Padme Hung (the Compassion mantra of Avalokiteshvara; the curriculum's first sonic teaching); Manjushri Prayer (invocation of the Bodhisattva of Wisdom; given to young students to open the mind to learning)
- **Grade 2:** Sem kye (bodhichitta — the arising of the mind of awakening; the aspiration to become awake for the benefit of all beings); Ngowa (the merit dedication — sending the benefit of practice outward to all beings; first act of generosity in the prayer form)
- **Grade 3:** Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer (the foundational prayer of the Nyingma tradition; the guru principle; the Vajrayana lineage entering the curriculum); Food Offering Prayer (the practice of consecrating food before eating; gratitude and awareness at every meal)
- **Grade 4:** Sevenline extended form (the full Guru Rinpoche invocation; the practitioner's relationship with the teacher-lineage deepened); Dusum Sangay (the Refuge and Bodhichitta prayer — past, present, and future; time as practice-ground)
- **Grade 5:** Dechen Monlam (the aspiration prayer for great bliss; the Dharma as aspiration, not only instruction)
- **Grade 6:** Dolma / Tara Prayer (the 21 praises of Tara; the female principle of compassionate action; timed to the Grade 6 unit on Tara)
- **Grade 7:** Rewo Sang Chod (the mountain smoke offering; the purification and offering practice; the practitioner as one who gives to all directions)
- **Grade 8:** Tsog Due pa (the feast offering prayer; the gathering of merit and wisdom; the practitioner offering everything outward; the final prayer of the complete set — given in G8.U1.C1)

---

**Vocabulary Moment:** "The word *repertoire* — a stock of skills, works, or practices that a practitioner can call upon. Your prayer repertoire is now complete. Every prayer in it has been in you for years. It is not the school's repertoire. The school gave it to you — but it is yours now. Wherever you go, it goes. You do not need a building, a teacher, a class schedule. You have the prayers."

---

**Body Moment:** Each student places one hand on their chest — over the sternum, the centre. "All 13 prayers are here. In your throat, in the memory your body holds without needing to be reminded. Can you feel them? Not as words you know — as something that has become part of how you breathe." Thirty seconds of silence. "These will stay. This is what eight years accumulated."

---

**Emotional Hook:** The recognition that the repertoire is complete — and that it belongs to the student, not the school. This is the moment when the curriculum shifts from something that is happening to students, to something that is theirs. The emotional hook is ownership: the prayer set is not assigned reading. It is who they are as practitioners.

#### Turn and Talk — 4 minutes
"Tell your partner: which prayer entered you at the deepest level over these eight years? Not the most impressive, not the one you find most beautiful — the one that actually changed something in you. What did it change? When did you first notice the change?" Two minutes, then take two or three responses to the whole room.

#### Activity — 10 minutes
Students write: "The prayer that changed me — which one, how, and what I understand about why."

Structure (they may follow this loosely or write as they wish):
- Name the prayer and when you first learned it
- What it has done in you — a specific moment when you noticed it working, or a quality that is different in you now because of it
- What you understand about why this prayer, for you

Teacher circulates. Key prompts:
- "Specific. When did you first notice it working? What was different after?"
- "Not just what the prayer says — what it does. What does chanting it do to you?"
- "If someone who had never heard this prayer asked you what it is for, what would you say?"

This reflection is the first artefact of the Unit 5 portfolio — it will be placed alongside the closing declaration from C5 to form the bookends of the final unit.

#### Debrief — 3 minutes
Teacher: "The Threshold ceremony is in four classes. In C4 and C5, you will lead the full prayer set without me — I will sit with the audience. C4 is the rehearsal. C5 is the real ceremony. Today: hold the whole set in your body and know that it is yours. The school gave it to you. What you do with it from here is practice."

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full prayer set — chanted again, from the beginning. All 13. "Once more. The way you will chant it in four classes, for your community." Student leaders. Teacher chants with the group.

Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Tsog Due pa · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Food Offering Prayer.

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "You have been learning these prayers since you were six years old. They are complete, and they are yours."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The prayer reflection from C1 is the first artefact of the Unit 5 portfolio. Hold it carefully — it goes alongside the closing declaration from C5 to form the bookends of the final unit, and is returned to the student along with their full portfolio at the Threshold ceremony.

Coordinate with the school's storage system before C2 to ensure all student portfolios are accessible. The portfolio review in C2 is one of the most logistically demanding classes in the curriculum — artefacts from eight years, some of which may have been stored by the school and some taken home. Begin that coordination now. A lost artefact is not a failure: it becomes an invitation to remember in writing what the making was and what it meant.

The prayer map from the mini lesson — Grade-by-grade introduction of all 13 prayers — can be made into a physical handout for students to keep. It is a useful reference for the Threshold ceremony and beyond. If time allows in the margin of C1, have students annotate the map with their own notes: which prayer they remember most vividly from the grade it was introduced, which surprised them, which they have chanted most.

Note the distinction in the Opening: the prayer set is chanted twice in C1 — once at the opening (with conscious awareness of completeness) and once at the closing (as rehearsal for C4 and C5, when students lead without the teacher). This double chanting is intentional and is specific to C1. Do not replicate it in C2–C3; it would lose its significance.
