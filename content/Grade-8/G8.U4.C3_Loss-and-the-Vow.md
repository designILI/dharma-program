# G8.U4.C3 — Loss and the Vow
## Grade 8 · Unit 4 · Class 3 of 7 · Loss and the Vow
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** The accumulation of Pajapati's losses — sister, foster son, husband → the discovery that the ground of practice is often the ground of grief
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Spiral retrieval with deepening — the Grade 6 grief touchpoint is recalled here not as review but as a new application. Students studied grief as a Bardo in Grade 6; they now apply that framework to a specific life with the depth that eight years of study makes possible. The concept does not repeat — it deepens.
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluate — students evaluate the relationship between Pajapati's losses and her vow, using textual evidence; they evaluate what makes grief transformative rather than merely painful
**Prior Class Summary:** C2 examined Pajapati's early life — the context of women's lives in the Sakya world, the closeness with Maya, the birth of Siddhartha, Maya's death, and Pajapati's choice to raise the child. Students wrote their first close-reading paragraph and hold the question: "What kind of woman raises a child who becomes the Buddha?"

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the accumulation of Pajapati's losses (Maya's death, Siddhartha's departure from the palace, Suddhodana's death), and the specific action she takes in response — cutting her hair, donning robes, gathering women, beginning the walk
Students will **UNDERSTAND** what "ground of practice" means — the specific suffering from which a practitioner's practice grows; the tradition's claim that the greatest practitioners often emerge from the greatest suffering; what distinguishes grief that becomes practice from grief that becomes bitterness
Students will **BE ABLE TO** connect Pajapati's losses to the Bardo teaching from Grade 6 (grief as disorientation, as threshold, as opportunity for transformation) and write a close-reading paragraph on the relationship between loss and vow
Students will **FEEL** the cumulative weight of Pajapati's grief arriving in full — not as distant history but as real, with the depth of the whole curriculum as context

---

### Materials Checklist
- Reading excerpt prepared: 8–10 minutes of teacher-read text covering the accumulation of Pajapati's losses and the arising of her vow — structured as the narrative moving forward from where C2 ended (Maya's death is the first loss; C3's reading focuses on Siddhartha's departure from the palace, and Suddhodana's death). The reading should build slowly — each loss distinct, arriving in order — and arrive at the moment of the vow: the hair-cutting, the robe-donning, the gathering of women, the first steps of the walk.
- Student journals and their C2 close-reading paragraph (students should have it)
- The question board from C1 (visible)
- Timer
- **Plan B:** If the reading excerpt is difficult to keep to 10 minutes, the Suddhodana section can be told in brief narration (2 minutes) rather than read from the text, before returning to the text for the vow. The vow moment must be read in full — it is the heart of C3.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full Grade 8 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Tsog Due pa · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader rotates.

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 6 minutes
Teacher: "In Grade 6, Unit 2, we talked about grief as a Bardo. We said: grief has a Bardo quality — the ground shifts, what was familiar is gone, there is a period of disorientation, and in that disorientation there are openings that didn't exist in ordinary time. We also said, in that class: 'There are women in this tradition who faced unbearable grief and turned it into practice.' That woman was Pajapati. Today: the grief arrives in full."

Ask the class: "What do you remember from the Grade 6 Bardo-grief class? What is the Bardo quality of grief — what does it mean to say grief is Bardo-like?" Take responses. Students may recall: the in-between quality, the dissolution of the familiar, the vulnerability that makes transformation possible. Affirm what they produce. Then: "Hold that frame. We're about to see it in a specific life."

#### Mini Lesson — 12 minutes

**Reading excerpt:** Teacher reads aloud the Chapter 2–3 passage — approximately 10 minutes. This is the emotional core of the unit; read it slowly. The passage covers:

- Maya's death — not as backstory, but in its full emotional reality: Pajapati has carried this loss since Siddhartha's birth. It is the grief beneath all her other griefs, the first crack in the ground.
- Siddhartha's departure from the palace — the night of the Great Renunciation. He leaves without saying goodbye. He leaves the woman who nursed him, raised him, who nursed him at her own breast alongside her own son. He does not wake her. He does not tell her. He is gone in the morning. The tradition tells us this was necessary — that a formal goodbye would have made the renunciation impossible. The tradition does not tell us what it was like to wake and find him gone.
- The years that follow — Pajapati continues as queen. She raises her son Nanda. She waits. Siddhartha is somewhere teaching. He does not come home.
- Suddhodana's death — the king dies. Pajapati is widowed. She is alone in a structural sense that is total: her sister gone, her foster son gone to a world she cannot enter as queen, her husband gone. The three pillars of her world — sister, son, husband — are all gone.
- The vow — from that ground, without ceremony or announcement, she acts. She cuts her hair. She puts on the orange robes of the wandering monastics. She gathers a group of women who want the same thing — other women of the Sakya clan who have also been widowed, who have also been hearing about the teaching, who also want to practice. They are not asking permission. They are acting. And then: the first steps of the walk. She is going to find him.

After the reading, a beat of silence. Then:

---

**Vocabulary Moment:** "The phrase *ground of practice* — the specific suffering or difficulty from which a practitioner's practice grows. Not the practice that exists despite the suffering, but the practice that grows because of it. Pajapati's ground of practice is her grief — the accumulated weight of three irreversible losses. The tradition's repeated claim is not a consolation. It is a description of how transformation works: the same ground that could produce bitterness produces, in certain conditions, practice. C3's question is: what are those conditions?"

---

**Body Moment:** "If you did Grade 6, Unit 2 — recall the body moment from that class. The weight you were asked to hold. Not your specific loss. The quality of weight. Hold that quality now." Pause five seconds. "Pajapati, having lost her sister, her foster son without farewell, and her husband — takes that weight and walks. She does not sit with it and wait for it to lift. She walks toward the teaching. Not away from her grief — *through* it, *with* it, toward the thing that might transmute it. What does it feel like, in the body, to walk toward something when the weight is that heavy?" Thirty seconds of silence. Then: "Open."

---

**Emotional Hook:** The cumulative weight of three losses, arriving now in full — not in the abbreviated form of the Grade 6 touchpoints, but as a life's progression, loss by loss. The emotional hook is not pity. It is recognition: this is what grief in its complete form looks like, and this is what the tradition says is possible within it.

#### Turn and Talk — 4 minutes
"Tell your partner: what is the difference between grief that becomes bitterness and grief that becomes practice? Is it temperament? Circumstances? A choice? What do you think made the difference for Pajapati — and what in the reading supports your answer?" Two minutes, then take two or three responses to the whole room. Hold complexity — this is a genuine question the tradition wrestles with.

#### Activity — 12 minutes
Students write their second close-reading paragraph. Same format as C2: one focused paragraph, specific evidence from the reading.

**Prompt:** "What does this passage tell us about the relationship between Pajapati's losses and her vow? How does the text show us that the grief is what makes the vow possible — rather than something that must be overcome before the vow can be made?"

Teacher circulates. Key prompts when a student is stuck:
- "What is the order of the losses in the reading? Why does that order matter?"
- "What is the first thing Pajapati does after Suddhodana's death? What does that tell you about where the vow comes from?"
- "Is the vow a sudden decision or something that has been forming? What in the text tells you?"

Students keep this paragraph alongside their C2 paragraph — the stack is building.

#### Debrief — 4 minutes
Teacher: "She cut her hair, put on robes, and walked. She had nothing — no husband, no palace status, no formal permission. She had everything she needed: the grief that had hollowed her out, and the teaching that filled what was left. The vow was not despite the losses. It was made of them."

Note for the teacher: look at the question board. Are there any questions from C1 that C3 begins to answer? Acknowledge them briefly if so.

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Tsog Due pa · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod · Food Offering Prayer. Student leader.

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "The ground that could produce bitterness produced, instead, a walk of hundreds of miles — and that distinction is the whole story."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The Grade 6 callback in C3 — grief as Bardo — is the curriculum's spiral design working at its deepest. Students who have been in the program since Grade 6 will recognise the reference not as review but as arrival: the concept they held abstractly in Grade 6 is now applied to a specific life, with the full weight of the narrative behind it. This is what spiral curriculum is for. Name the spiral explicitly in the PKA — do not assume students will make the connection without the prompt.

C4 addresses the gilded cage — the period before Pajapati's losses, when she was queen, materially comfortable, and spiritually alive but structurally constrained. This is a deliberate counterpoint to C3: C3 shows grief as ground of practice; C4 shows comfort as a different kind of constraint. The two together form the full picture of Pajapati's situation — there was no easy path. Loss made the vow possible; but even without the loss, the gilded cage was already a problem. The vow would have had to come somehow.

Watch the room in C3. This class can be genuinely moving — the accumulation of losses in the reading is heavy. If the room holds it with real weight, that is appropriate and not a problem. If a student needs a moment, offer it without making it a disruption. The body moment is designed to create access to the weight without overwhelming; adjust the timing if the room needs more or less.

Spiral connections active in C3: Grade 6 Unit 2 (grief as Bardo — explicit callback in PKA); Grade 6 Unit 3 (Pajapati as Tara's historical counterpart — the walk she is about to take is Green Tara's energy; this will be made explicit in C5, not yet); Grade 8 Unit 1 (the Twelve Links — what Pajapati's vow interrupts is the chain of conditioned arising; her walk is the first step out of the chain's orbit).
