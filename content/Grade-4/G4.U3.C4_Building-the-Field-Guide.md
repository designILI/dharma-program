# G4.U3.C4 — Building the Field Guide
## Grade 4 · Unit 3 · Class 4 of 5 · Building the Field Guide
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Completing and curating all Field Guide entries → understanding as something you can hold, return to, and give to someone else
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Consolidation through curation — students are not learning new content in this class; they are organising, refining, and making coherent what they have already encountered. This process of curation is itself a form of deeper understanding: deciding what to include, how to describe it accurately, and whether an entry is genuinely complete requires the student to test their own knowledge.
**Bloom's Level:** Apply / Analyze — students apply their knowledge to the completion of Field Guide entries (Apply), and evaluate the coherence and accuracy of their own and a partner's work (Analyze). The peer review moment is a structured analytic exercise.
**Prior Class Summary:** C3 was the mala session — students practised Om Mani Padme Hung with a mala for an extended period, began Field Guide entry #3, and carried the phrase "the three gates" (body, speech, mind engaged together). The class had an unusually quiet, inward quality that students may carry as a felt memory.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the minimum complete set of Field Guide entries: bell and dorje (C1), incense and shrine arrangement (C2), mala (C3) — and what makes each entry complete (illustration, name, function, symbolic meaning, handling instructions).
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that a field guide is a reference — a document made for future use, including by one's future self who may have forgotten details. The standard is: could someone who was not in the class use this to understand the object?
Students will **BE ABLE TO** complete all three minimum entries and, if time allows, add entries for other ritual objects they know from this year's study (prayer flags, water bowls, tormas) or from their own experience.
Students will **FEEL** the particular satisfaction of making something whole — the Field Guide as a coherent document, not a collection of unconnected pages.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Field Guide booklets — returned from storage; all entries so far included
- Reference images of all objects studied (bell, dorje, incense setup, shrine arrangement, mala) — available for students who want to check their illustrations against the actual objects
- The actual objects if available (bell, dorje, mala) — on a central table for students to look at while illustrating
- Pencils, coloured pencils, and fine-tipped markers
- A model completed entry — prepared by the teacher in advance, showing what a "complete" entry looks like vs. an "incomplete" one (can be on the board or a printed handout)
- **Plan B (if some students have significant gaps across multiple entries — more than one entry largely missing):** Prioritise structural completion over aesthetic quality. A complete, simply illustrated entry is more useful than a beautifully drawn incomplete one. Help students identify: what is the minimum information needed? Write that first. Then illustrate if time allows.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
The student leader for this class leads the full Grade 4 prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay**
*(Food Offering Prayer is reserved for the closing.)*

After the Om Mani Padme Hung (7×), the teacher pauses briefly and leads 21 additional repetitions with the mala — hands moving, pace steady, brief and deliberate. This is a transition into the work of the class: hands engaged before desk work begins. Students may count along on their own malas if they have them, or simply chant.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Field Guide booklets are distributed. Students open them and look at what they have.

*"You have Field Guide entries for: bell and dorje, incense and shrine arrangement, mala. Today we complete the guide and make it whole. Before we begin — look at what you have. What's missing? What's incomplete? What would a reader of this guide need to know that isn't there yet?"*

Students scan their entries. Take 2–3 responses — students may identify gaps in their own work: an illustration not finished, a symbolic meaning not written, a traditional name missing. The teacher notes any systematic gaps (entries that most students are missing the same element from) and addresses those directly in the Mini Lesson.

Then: *"A field guide is a reference. Someone who was not in these classes should be able to pick this up and understand each object. That is the standard. Not beautiful — complete. Today you make it complete."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Brief. Show the model entry on the board (or distribute the example sheet).

"A complete Field Guide entry has five elements: (1) an illustration that accurately shows the object — not symbolic, not impressionistic, but recognisable; (2) the name, including traditional name if known; (3) the function — what is this object used for, in practice; (4) the symbolic meaning — what does it represent in Buddhist teaching; (5) how to use or handle it correctly, where applicable."

"An incomplete entry might have the illustration and the name, but no symbolic meaning. Or it might have the meaning but no illustration. Use the model to check your entries — which of the five elements are present, which are absent?"

**Vocabulary Moment:** Write *reference* on the board.

"A reference is a document you return to when you need information. You made it so that you — or someone else — can use it later. A dictionary is a reference. A map is a reference. Your Field Guide is a reference. The test of a reference is not how good it looks; it is whether it is useful when needed. Five years from now, if you open this guide, can you remember what a dorje is? Can you show it to someone and explain it? That is the test."

**Body Moment:** Students hold their Field Guide with one hand, and with the other, hold the corresponding object or look at the reference image. Looking from object to page and back — checking: does the illustration capture the real thing? Is this accurate enough to be a reference?

This back-and-forth — object, page, object, page — is the body engaging in the evaluation process. It is different from simply looking at the page.

**Emotional Hook:** The satisfaction of making something complete — the Field Guide as a whole, coherent document. This is a quieter satisfaction than the making of the torma, but it is real, and students feel it when they turn through the pages of a finished guide.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

*"Trade Field Guides with your partner. Read their entries. You have five minutes. When you are done: tell them one thing they captured well, and one thing that could be clearer or more complete. Be specific — not 'this is good,' but 'I notice you wrote that the dorje represents compassion, and I understand what that means.' Or: 'I can't tell from the illustration which end of the dorje is which — could you add a label?' Specific observations. Then switch."*

Students exchange guides. The teacher circulates — modelling the register of specific, useful feedback rather than general praise or critique. If a student's feedback is too vague ("it's nice"), the teacher prompts: "What specifically is clear? What would you want to know that isn't there?"

This is peer analysis — students are practising the same analytic skill they will use in the presentations tomorrow.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

Complete and refine all Field Guide entries.

The minimum three entries must be complete by the end of this class. Students work through their gaps systematically: first add missing written elements (most important), then refine illustrations, then add any additional entries.

Extension: students who complete the three minimum entries can add entries for ritual objects from earlier in the year:
- Prayer flags (from Grade 1) — what they look like, what they carry, how they are hung
- Water bowls (from Grade 4, Unit 1) — the seven bowls, the substances, what they represent (the *Argham* sequence)
- Tormas (from Grade 4, Unit 2) — what they are made of, what they represent, the fact that they are given away

These extension entries connect the Field Guide outward from Unit 3 to the year as a whole, making it a broader document of practice.

Teacher circulates with specific feedback on accuracy and completeness. When a student's entry is complete: "Done. What could you add?" Extension is always available for students who finish early.

At the end of the activity period: Field Guide booklets are collected. They are not returned until C5, when students will need them for their presentations.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

*"You have been learning to read a language this year — the language of ritual. The field guide is your dictionary. What would you add to it next year, if you kept going? If you moved into Grade 5 with this guide, what would you want to be in it?"*

Take 2–3 responses. Students may name objects they have seen or heard of but haven't studied: thangkas, butter lamps, cymbals, the large horns used in monastery ceremonies. Receive these — they are evidence of genuine curiosity, and they name objects that will indeed appear in later grades. The teacher can say: "That is coming. Not next year — but it will come. Keep that question."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
The student leader leads the full closing prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay**

Teacher's closing line, specific to today:
*"Tomorrow you present. Come knowing one entry well enough to teach it to someone who has never seen the object."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: Field Guides are collected at the end of C4 and returned at the start of C5. Students may need two minutes at the opening of C5 to review their chosen entry before presentations begin — build this in. C5 format: each student presents one entry to the class, explaining the object as if to someone who has never encountered it. Presentations are 1–2 minutes each. Students choose their entry at the start of C5 (not pre-assigned, not pre-rehearsed at home — the spontaneity is part of the assessment). The teacher's role in C5 is to listen, make one genuine observation per student (not a grade, a recognition), and hold the space for the class to be witnessed by each other. After C5, Field Guides are returned to students as their Unit 3 portfolio artefact. Together with the Unit 1 seven-bowl diagram and the Unit 2 design sketch and portfolio paragraph, the Field Guide completes the Grade 4 portfolio. Note for the next teacher or the student's Grade 5 file: the Field Guide and the making experience (torma) are the two primary artefacts of this year. They speak to each other — one made and given away, one built and kept. That contrast is intentional and worth naming in any transition document.
