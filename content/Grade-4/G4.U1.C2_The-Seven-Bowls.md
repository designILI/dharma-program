# G4.U1.C2 — The Seven Bowls
## Grade 4 · Unit 1 · Class 2 of 6 · The Seven Bowls
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Seven bowls of water → seven qualities offered to the awakened mind
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Dual coding — students encounter each offering through its name, its Sanskrit term, its symbolic meaning, and a body gesture simultaneously. Three channels (verbal, visual, kinesthetic) encoding the same information increases retention and gives students multiple retrieval routes on Demonstration Day.
**Bloom's Level:** Understand — students are asked to grasp what each bowl represents, not merely to memorise names. The target is comprehension: being able to say not just "this is Argham" but "this bowl stands for the craving that the mind releases when it drinks deeply." The diagram activity asks them to record this understanding in their own words.
**Prior Class Summary:** C1 established why human beings make offerings — not as transaction but as training of the mind. Students wrote a personal reflection on this question, now the opening page of their diagram journals. They carry the open-palms gesture and the word *puja*.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the names, Sanskrit terms, and order of all seven water bowl offerings: Argham, Padyam, Pushpe, Dhupe, Aloke, Gandhe, Naividya/Shapta.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that each bowl is a symbol — that the water stands for a quality of mind or a teaching about transformation, and that the seven together form a complete offering rather than a random collection.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** draw seven bowls in the correct order, label each with its name and Sanskrit term, and write a note on what each represents — the beginning of their annotated diagram.
Students will **FEEL** the moment of recognition when they realise the bowls are not about the water — that they are standing in front of a teaching encoded in objects. This is the affective centre of the class.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Student diagram journals (collected and returned from C1)
- Pencils — one per student
- Seven bowls, ideally the school's offering bowls, set out on the demonstration table or shrine before class, unlabelled — students will name them as they are introduced
- Optional: seven small cards with the Sanskrit names written on them, placed face-down beside each bowl to be turned over as each offering is introduced
- Coloured pencils or pens for the diagram activity — one set per student or shared sets
- Teacher reference: the seven offerings with full names, Sanskrit terms, and meanings (printed or visible to the teacher only — not shown to students yet)
- **Plan B — if bowls are unavailable:** Draw seven circles on the board in a straight line before class. Use these as the demonstration surface. Students imagine the water; the meanings hold without the physical objects. Note this is a significant loss — if possible, borrow bowls from the monastery or kitchen.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 4 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline Prayer (extended form) · Dusum Sangay.

Student leader leads.

Before the student leader begins, the teacher says briefly: "Today we're learning what each of the seven bowls represents — the offerings made to the awakened mind. As we chant the Dusum Sangay prayer, hold this: offerings are made to awakened awareness across all of time — past, present, future. The prayer and the bowls are connected."

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

"Last class you wrote about why humans offer. What did you write? What was your answer?" Take 3–4 responses — include students who offered something unexpected or personal. Do not correct; receive.

Then: "In Himalayan Buddhist practice, the seven-bowl water offering is one of the most common daily practices in the world. Monks and practitioners set up these seven bowls every morning on shrines from Tibet to Nepal to Bhutan to Ladakh. Each bowl represents something specific — one of seven things traditionally offered to an honoured guest."

Pause. "What seven things might you offer a very important visitor to your home? If the most important person in the world arrived at your door, what would you give them?" Let students brainstorm freely for 1–2 minutes.

Collect responses: water, food, a place to sit, a gift, music, flowers. Whatever comes.

"Here is what the tradition says. And when you hear it, notice how it matches what you already thought — and where it surprises you."

**Fallback if brainstorm is thin:** "Think about a guest arriving at your house after a long journey. They are tired, dusty from the road. What do they need first? And then?" Walk through the arrival sequence: water to drink, water to wash, something beautiful to see, something pleasant to smell, light if it is evening, something comforting on the skin, food or music for the enjoyment of it all. The seven offerings follow exactly this sequence.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Present each offering in order. Stand beside the bowls and point to each one as it is named. Take enough time with each that students can absorb the symbol — this is a teaching, not a list.

**The seven offerings:**

"The first bowl: **Drinking water — Argham**. The first thing you offer any guest is water to drink. In the practice, this represents the offering that meets the deepest thirst — the thirst of the grasping mind. When the mind drinks deeply and settles, craving quiets. We offer this first because it goes to the root."

"The second bowl: **Washing water — Padyam**. Water to wash the feet — the traveller arrives dusty from the road. We wash the dust away. In the practice, this represents purification — the washing away of confusion. The dust is not dirt on a body; it is the confusion in the mind."

"The third bowl: **Flowers — Pushpe**. We offer beauty to the eye. Flowers are placed here not because the awakened mind needs to look at flowers, but because the offering is about the gesture — the willingness to offer beauty, to say: what is lovely in the world belongs to you. This represents the flowering of virtue — the way goodness blooms when it is cultivated."

"The fourth bowl: **Incense — Dhupe**. Incense is offered to the sense of smell. Watch incense smoke: it travels in every direction without preference, without choosing who receives it. It goes to the corners of the room, out the window, into the air. Dhupe represents virtue that pervades all directions — goodness that does not stay contained, that reaches everyone without choosing."

"The fifth bowl: **Light — Aloke**. A lamp. Of all seven offerings, this one carries perhaps the most obvious teaching: light dispels darkness. In the practice, the lamp represents wisdom — the understanding that dispels ignorance. We offer light because ignorance is the deepest darkness, and wisdom is what the awakened mind embodies. We are offering back what the Buddha represents."

"The sixth bowl: **Perfume or scented water — Gandhe**. Offered to the sense of touch — perfume applied to the skin. This represents purity of conduct. The way a fragrance clings to what it touches, the way pure conduct leaves its mark on everything we do."

"The seventh bowl: **Food or music — Naividya or Shapta**. The seventh offering gathers all the senses together — the enjoyment of a full and beautiful meal, or of music that moves through the whole body. This represents the complete offering of oneself — not one quality, not one sense, but everything, all at once."

Pause. Look at the seven bowls. Then:

"Seven offerings for seven senses of welcome. Together they make something complete. And together they represent a teaching: the mind can be cleansed, purified, made beautiful, opened, illuminated, refined, and fully given. These bowls are a map of what transformation looks like."

**Vocabulary Moment:** "The word *symbol* — something that represents something else. The bowl of water is not just water. It is a symbol — it stands for a teaching, a quality, a transformation. Ritual language works entirely through symbols. A symbol says: this thing points to something beyond itself. The whole language of ritual is a language of pointing. This whole year, we are learning to read that language — to look at an object and ask: what is it pointing to?"

**Body Moment:** "As I name each offering again, do this with me — one gesture for each one. You don't need to remember the exact gesture; just let your hands respond to the meaning."

Move through the seven:
- Argham (drinking water): Hands cupped together, as if drinking.
- Padyam (washing water): Hands passing slowly over each other, as if washing.
- Pushpe (flowers): Hands beginning closed, then opening like a bloom opening.
- Dhupe (incense): One hand rises slowly, fingers trailing upward like smoke traveling.
- Aloke (light): Both hands frame a small flame — cupped slightly, a held brightness.
- Gandhe (perfume): One hand touches the opposite wrist — as if applying perfume to the pulse point.
- Naividya/Shapta (food/music): Both arms open wide — everything, offered at once.

Do this together once. Then: "That sequence — in your hands now. That is the teaching. Hold it there."

**Emotional Hook:** The moment students realise the bowls are not about water. Watch for it — the slight shift in posture or expression when the understanding lands: *these bowls are a teaching encoded in objects*. Do not name this moment for students. Let it arrive.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: which of the seven offerings feels most meaningful to you right now? Which one would you choose if you could only make one? And — why? What is it about that offering that speaks to you?"

Give 2 minutes for pairs to talk. Then take 2–3 responses. Listen not just for which bowl they choose but for the reasoning — it will reveal what the student values, what they are drawn toward, and sometimes what they need.

**What to listen for:** Students who choose Aloke (light/wisdom) are often reaching toward understanding. Students who choose Argham (drinking water/cleansing craving) may be holding something heavy. Students who choose Naividya/Shapta (full offering) are often generous by nature. None of this is diagnostic — just worth noticing and keeping in mind.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

"Open your diagram journals. Find a fresh double page — you'll need the space."

"You are going to draw your seven-bowl diagram. Draw seven bowls in a straight line, evenly spaced — this is how they are arranged on the shrine. Inside or below each bowl, you will write three things: the name, the Sanskrit term, and what it represents."

"This is a reference diagram — careful and precise. Think of it as something a stranger could use to understand the practice. Not decoration. A teaching on paper."

Write the three required elements on the board:
1. Name (e.g., Drinking water)
2. Sanskrit term (e.g., Argham)
3. What it represents (e.g., Cleansing the craving mind)

Teacher circulates. Check that Sanskrit terms are being recorded accurately — write all seven terms on the board for reference:
Argham · Padyam · Pushpe · Dhupe · Aloke · Gandhe · Naividya / Shapta

Encourage care with the drawing — the bowls should be recognisable as bowls, roughly the same size, in a clear line. This is not an art class but the diagram should be legible and made with attention.

**Extension:** Students who complete the three required elements can add a fourth annotation below each bowl: *"What this teaches me about my own mind."* This is the deeper interpretive layer — connecting the symbolic meaning to the student's inner experience. Do not require this; invite it for students who are ready.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

"Look at your diagram. Seven bowls — water to drink, water to wash, flowers, incense, light, perfume, food and music. Together they represent a complete offering. Everything a guest would need: to arrive, to be refreshed, to be welcomed in beauty, to be surrounded by fragrance, to have light, to be comforted, and to be given joy."

"What do you think the tradition is teaching by making the offering complete? Why not just one bowl?" Pause for responses.

"The completeness is the teaching. The practice doesn't offer one quality and leave the rest out. It offers everything — the whole range of what the mind can give. That wholeness is what we practise. Next class, we find out how — the correct way to set up, fill, and remove the bowls. You'll find out that the 'how' is also part of the teaching."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline Prayer (extended form) · Dusum Sangay.

Student leader leads.

Teacher's closing line: "Seven bowls. Seven qualities. One complete offering. You now know what they are — next class you will learn how to make them, and you will discover that the how is inseparable from the what."

Collect diagram journals at the end of class.

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The seven-bowl diagram begun today is the primary making artefact for Unit 1. Students will annotate and refine it across C2–C5; it is collected at the end of each class and returned at the start of the next. Keep it safely — this is the portfolio piece for the unit assessment. If any student has not labelled all seven bowls by the end of C2, note this and give them 5 minutes at the start of C3 to complete it.

The body moment — one gesture per offering, in order — is worth returning to in C3 and C4 as a quick retrieval warmup before practical work. By C4, students should be able to mime the sequence from memory without being prompted. If you do it each class, it will settle in without feeling like memorisation.

Spiral connections: The Sanskrit vocabulary (Argham, Padyam, etc.) is new but the concept of symbolic language is not. In Grade 3, students encountered symbols through the Tsethar practice — the physical act of releasing an animal was understood to represent the release of a being from suffering. Grade 4 takes that symbolism further: each object in the seven-bowl offering is a precisely encoded symbol within a system of seven. The density and precision of the symbolic language is new and worth naming explicitly: "In Grade 3 we saw that a single act — releasing an animal — could carry a large meaning. Here we have seven objects, each with a distinct meaning. The language is more complex now."
