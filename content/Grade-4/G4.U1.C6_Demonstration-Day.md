# G4.U1.C6 — Demonstration Day
## Grade 4 · Unit 1 · Class 6 of 6 · Demonstration Day
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Setting up and narrating the seven-bowl offering → knowledge become transmission; knowing something well enough to give it to someone else
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Transfer through teaching — the act of narrating what you know to an audience consolidates understanding in a way that private practice cannot. Students who can teach a practice, accurately and in their own words, have understood it at a qualitatively different level from students who can only execute it or describe it when prompted. The demonstration is both assessment and deepening.
**Bloom's Level:** Analyze — students are not merely reproducing the practice (Apply). By narrating while doing — explaining what each bowl represents and why, connecting the physical precision to the training of the mind — they demonstrate the ability to interpret and relate parts to a whole. The Demonstration Day format surfaces whether the understanding is genuine or surface.
**Prior Class Summary:** C5 was diagram completion — students annotated the seven-bowl practice with everything they know. They carry completed diagrams and growing fluency in the physical practice. Today: they demonstrate both.

**Format note:** This class is structured as a ceremony and demonstration event. The schema is honoured throughout. The Activity section describes the demonstration format in full. The pacing of this class depends on class size and available demonstration spaces; the teacher should plan the rotation before class.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** that Demonstration Day is the culminating assessment for Unit 1 — not a test of perfection but of genuine understanding expressed through teaching.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the ability to narrate a practice to someone who does not know it is the deepest form of knowing — that transmission is both the proof of learning and its continuation.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** set up the seven-bowl offering correctly and narrate what they are doing and why — in their own words, in order, with sufficient clarity that the audience understands both the what and the why.
Students will **FEEL** the experience of knowing something well enough to teach it — which is different from performance, different from recitation, and different from completing an exercise. This is the affective target of the entire unit.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Seven offering bowls — fully set up and ready at the demonstration table or shrine before students arrive; the teacher's own set available for the closing ceremony
- Water vessel — one per demonstration station if multiple stations are used
- Clean cloth
- Plant or soil vessel for the final water emptying
- Student diagram journals — returned at the start of class (held by teacher since end of C5)
- External audience if arranged: another class, resident teacher, school staff member, or senior students. Confirm before class; send a brief invitation the day before if possible. Audience members should know they are welcome to ask the demonstrating student one question after the demonstration.
- A small bell or chime if available — used to mark the opening and close of the ceremony
- **Plan B — if no external audience is available:** Demonstrations proceed in pairs — each student demonstrates to a partner while the teacher circulates and observes. Pairs then swap. The teacher's role shifts to close, attentive observation of each pair rather than witnessing group demonstrations. This still works; the authentic quality of transmission is present even between two students, as long as the framing is right: "Your partner has never done this before. Teach them."
- **Plan B — if a student is significantly unready (identified in C4/C5 notes):** That student demonstrates with the teacher as a quiet supportive presence — not prompting, but standing nearby. The goal is that every student completes a demonstration, even if the sequence is imperfect. What the teacher observes and assesses is the understanding in the narration, not technical perfection.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 4 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline Prayer (extended form) · Dusum Sangay.

Teacher leads — more slowly than usual. This is intentional: the opening sets the quality of attention for the entire class.

Say before beginning the prayer set: "Today we demonstrate what we have learned. Before we begin: one full set of prayers, with full attention. This is how every offering begins — not with doing, but with settling. We settle first. Then we act."

There is no student leader for the opening today — the teacher leads, and the quality of the teacher's own attention and voice during the prayer set is itself part of the ceremony. If a student has been designated as leader for this class, they may lead the prayers but the teacher opens with the settling instruction.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 3 minutes

(Not retrieval — settling.)

After prayers: "Stand beside your demonstration space." *(Or: Stand at your place if demonstrations are not yet arranged.)*

"Take one breath."

Pause. Let the breath happen — do not rush past it.

"Feel what you know. It is in your hands. You have done this many times. The practice is not in what you can remember to say — it is in what your hands already know. If you lose the words, return to the bowls. They will show you what comes next."

Return to seated. Return diagram journals. "You may keep your diagram open beside you during your demonstration if you need it — but your goal is to narrate from knowledge, not from reading."

---

#### Mini Lesson — 2 minutes

Brief. Frame the demonstration clearly.

"When you demonstrate today, you are teaching. That is the word: teaching. Not performing, not reciting, not showing what you remember. Teaching someone who does not know this practice what they would be doing and why."

"Narrate what you are doing as you do it. Take your time. The practice will carry you — let it."

**Vocabulary Moment:** "The word *transmission* — in Buddhist tradition, teachings are transmitted from teacher to student, person to person, across generations. A teaching that is not transmitted disappears. What you are doing today is part of that chain. You received this practice from someone, in this room, across five classes. Today you give it forward. You are part of the transmission."

**Body Moment:** The demonstration itself — full setup, filling, narration, removal.

**Emotional Hook:** The experience of knowing something well enough to teach it. This is different from knowing well enough to be tested. It has a different texture — a generosity in it, a direction outward. Students will feel this difference even if they cannot name it. Do not name it before the demonstration; let them feel it during.

---

#### Activity — 20–25 minutes

**The demonstrations.**

Structure according to class size and available demonstration spaces. Pairs or individual demonstrations, depending on time and setup. If an external audience is present, seat them before demonstrations begin, with a brief introduction from the teacher: "This class has been studying the seven-bowl water offering — one of the most common daily practices in Himalayan Buddhist tradition. Each student will demonstrate the practice and explain what each bowl represents. You are welcome to watch, and if you like, to ask one question at the end."

**Each demonstration:**
The student (or pair) approaches the demonstration table. Sets up the seven bowls in order, narrating as they go — what each bowl is, what it represents, why the practice is done this way. Fills each bowl slowly, narrating the significance of the pour, the spacing, the attention. Pauses after the bowls are filled. Then removes in reverse order, narrating the removal.

The whole demonstration need not be perfectly fluent or verbatim. What the teacher is listening for:
- Is the order correct?
- Are the key meanings present (even if not perfectly phrased)?
- Is the narration in the student's own words rather than a memorised script?
- Is there evidence that the student understands the connection between the physical practice and the training of the mind — trenpa, precision, the offering mind?

After each demonstration: brief, specific acknowledgment from the teacher. Not a grade — an observation. One specific thing noticed: "What I heard in that narration — when you explained the light bowl, you connected wisdom to the darkness that precedes it. That was your own understanding, not something I said. That matters."

Do not correct publicly during Demonstration Day. If a student makes a significant error in sequence or meaning, note it privately and address it after class or at the start of Unit 2 — briefly, specifically, without shame.

**If an external audience member asks a question:** Let the student answer. Do not step in unless the question genuinely cannot be addressed. The discomfort of not immediately knowing the answer is part of the transmission experience — students learn that a real teacher can say "I don't know yet" and that this is not failure.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

Bring the class together after all demonstrations are complete.

"You know the seven bowls now. Their names. Their order. What each one represents. The correct way to set them up and take them down. What the practice is doing to the mind of the person who makes it."

"This is a practice you can do for the rest of your life. It will always mean what it means. That is what ritual is — a container that holds its meaning across time. The bowls that were set up this morning in monasteries across the Himalayas mean the same thing they meant a thousand years ago. You are inside that continuity now."

Pause. Then: "One question before we close. When you wrote 'Why do we offer?' on the first day — what did you write then? And what would you write now?" Do not require an answer from every student — invite one or two to share if they are willing. This is the before-and-after of the unit.

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline Prayer (extended form) · Dusum Sangay.

**The class closes with a real offering.** After or during the final chanting of Om Mani Padme Hung, the teacher (and students who wish to join) performs or observes one final seven-bowl setup at the shrine — the bowls placed, filled, held in attention. Students chant Om Mani Padme Hung softly through this. Then Ngowa — the dedication of merit — closes the unit.

Return diagram journals to students. They take them home or add them to their portfolios.

Teacher's closing line: "In Unit 1, you learned what the seven bowls represent. In Unit 2, you will make something to place on the shrine — a torma. The practice is the same: precision, attention, care. The medium changes. Well done."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: Diagrams are returned to students after Demonstration Day — they take them home or add them to their school portfolios. The C1 written reflection ("Why do we offer?") and the completed diagram together form the Unit 1 portfolio artefact.

Unit 2 begins: Making Tormas. Name this transition clearly to students, as suggested in the closing line above. The thread is explicit: Unit 1 was about what we offer — the water and the symbolic qualities it represents. Unit 2 is about making something to offer — the physical craft of creating a torma. The act of making is itself a form of practice: patience, precision, care, non-attachment to the outcome (the torma will be offered, not kept). The skills carried from Unit 1 — trenpa, the offering mind, precision as mindfulness — are the exact same skills Unit 2 will require. Students will recognise them.

For your assessment records: note for each student — (1) sequence accuracy (did they set up the bowls in the correct order?), (2) meaning accuracy (did they correctly identify what each bowl represents?), (3) quality of narration (were they speaking from their own understanding or reciting?), (4) evidence of the deeper understanding — did they connect the physical precision to the training of the mind? This final indicator, present in even one moment of the narration, is the strongest evidence that the unit's essential question ("Why do we offer, and what does the act of offering teach us about the mind that makes it?") was genuinely engaged.

There is no grade. The assessment is for the teacher's understanding of where each student is, so that Unit 2 can meet them there.

Spiral connections: The transmission theme of C6 connects forward to the full arc of the Dharma Program — by Grade 9, students will be supporting younger grades in practice. Every Demonstration Day is planting that seed. Backward: the first time students encountered "teaching what you know" was in Grade 2's retelling activities. Grade 4 lifts this to a new level — not storytelling but practical transmission, embodied knowledge moving person to person.

The closing ceremony — the real offering, the Om Mani Padme Hung chanted softly as the bowls are set — is not pedagogical decoration. It is the unit arriving at its own conclusion: the practice, done together, dedicated outward. This moment is worth protecting even if time is short.
