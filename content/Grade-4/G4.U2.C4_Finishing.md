# G4.U2.C4 — Finishing
## Grade 4 · Unit 2 · Class 4 of 5 · Finishing
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Completing the torma in one final session → finishing as a practice in itself — the quiet recognition that work is whole enough to offer
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Closure and transfer preparation — this lesson is structured as a deliberate closing of the making arc, so that students arrive at C5 having already made a psychological transition from maker to giver. The body gesture (holding the torma in both hands, feeling its weight) is the physical bridge between making and offering.
**Bloom's Level:** Apply / early Analyze — the final making is still application, but the debrief and portfolio writing push into analysis: what did this cost? What am I about to give away? What do those questions reveal?
**Prior Class Summary:** C3 was the shaping session — students refined their forms and began adding detail. They carry the distinction between imposing a plan and listening to the material, and the feeling of a form beginning to become particularly theirs. They were warned at the end of C3 that today is the last making session.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** that today is the final making session — the torma will be completed and stored until the offering in C5.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that *complete* does not mean perfect; it means whole enough to offer. The standard is wholeness, not flawlessness.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** finish their torma in this session and, in writing, articulate what went into making it and what it means to be about to give it away.
Students will **FEEL** the specific quiet of completion — not triumph or relief, but the particular stillness of something that is done.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Stored tormas from C3 (returned to each student, exactly as stored)
- Reserve material — a small amount per student for final finishing or repair
- Work surface protection (wax paper or cloth squares)
- Small bowls of water for smoothing
- Blunt wooden shaping tools (for any final fine work)
- Sketchbooks or portfolio paper and pencils (for the portfolio writing at end of session)
- Airtight container or wrapping — for final careful storage of completed tormas until C5
- Labels or small cards — for students to write their name on, to be placed with their torma in storage
- **Plan B (if a student's torma is significantly incomplete and cannot be finished in this session):** Do not allow this to become distressing. Simplify the form — ask the student to bring the torma to a complete state at a reduced level of ambition. A simple, stable cone that is whole is a complete torma. A complex, unfinished form is not. The standard is wholeness, and wholeness is achievable in every case if the form is simplified. Make this clear before the student becomes discouraged.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
The student leader for this class leads the full Grade 4 prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay**
*(Food Offering Prayer is reserved for the closing.)*

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
Tormas are returned — one per student, placed on the work surface.

*"Last class you refined your torma. Before we begin today — what does 'finished' mean? How do you know when something is done?"*

Take 2–3 responses. Students may say: "when it looks right," "when you can't think of anything more to add," "when it feels ready." Do not evaluate — listen. Then the teacher offers the standard for today:

*"In this unit, the standard for finished is: whole enough to offer. Not perfect — complete. A torma that is complete and offered is worth more than a torma that is still being perfected and never reaches the shrine. Today: finish it. Whatever state your torma is in at the end of this class — that is your torma."*

This sets a clear frame. Students need to know that the work ends today, and that "ends today" is a liberating constraint, not a harsh one.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Brief — three minutes maximum before moving to the Body Moment.

"There is a Zen saying: before enlightenment, chop wood, carry water. After enlightenment, chop wood, carry water. Finishing is not dramatic. It is the quiet recognition that the work is done — not because it is flawless, but because it is whole. The work that was required has been done. What comes next is offering, not more making."

"When you know something is finished, you will feel it. Not as a fanfare — as a small stillness. A moment when the hands stop moving not because you are tired, but because the form is telling you it is complete."

**Vocabulary Moment:** Write *complete* on the board.

"Complete — not the same as perfect. Perfect is an ideal that is never fully reached. Complete is a condition: whole, sufficient, ready to be given. A complete torma can be offered. That is the test."

**Body Moment:** "Pick up your torma in both hands — like this." Teacher demonstrates: two hands, cupped beneath, torma resting in the palms, presented. Not gripped. "Feel its weight. Feel what went into it — three sessions of work. Now: is it ready to be held like this — offered, presented, given? If yes: today you make it so. If you are not sure: that is fine. That is what this session is for."

Students hold their tormas briefly in the offering gesture — then set them down and begin. No further instruction needed.

**Emotional Hook:** The particular quiet of completing something — not triumph, not relief, but the specific stillness that follows sustained effort. Students will feel this at different times during the session. The teacher should not name it in advance — let it arrive.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

This happens midway through the making period, when students have been working for ten minutes.

*"Tell your partner — keep your hands moving if you can: when do you know something is finished? Have you ever worked on something until it felt exactly done? What did that feel like?"*

Students talk. The teacher listens for descriptions of completion — the quiet, the stillness, the sense of rightness. These descriptions will be different for everyone, and all are valid. The question is not answered definitively — it is held.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

Final making session. The rule: finish today. The teacher circulates and supports completion:
- For students who are overwhelmed by remaining work: help them identify what is essential and what is optional. "What does this torma need to be complete? Not what would make it better — what does it need?"
- For students who are adding and adding: "What would you need to stop adding? Is it there already?"
- For students who have finished before the others: set the torma on the work surface and do not touch it. Then take out the sketchbook and write: a paragraph for the portfolio. The prompt (written on the board):

*"What I put into this — what it cost me to make it — and what I am about to give away."*

This is not a public piece of writing. It is honest private reflection. The teacher does not read it unless the student offers. It goes into the portfolio alongside the design sketch from C1.

When each student finishes, they hold their torma in the offering gesture one more time — both hands, cupped — then set it down carefully, write their name on a label, and bring it to the storage area. They do this themselves. The act of placing it in storage is the first release — a small one, a rehearsal.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

The teacher does not rush this.

*"Transfer question — hold this until next class, but hear it now: 'You made this over three classes. You are about to give it away. What does that feel like?'"*

Students hold the question. Take 1–2 responses if students want to speak — but do not press. This question is not meant to be answered fully in this moment. It is meant to be carried into C5, where the offering actually happens. Students who speak now are beginning a conversation they will continue next class.

*"Come to class next time ready to offer it. Ready — not without feeling. Just ready."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
The student leader leads the full closing prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay**

Teacher's closing line, specific to today — holding the class in readiness for C5:
*"It's complete. Next class, we offer it — and find out what we know about letting go."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: All tormas should be complete after this class. Store them in the designated space — carefully, labelled — until C5. Do not allow students to work on their tormas between C4 and C5; the making is done. C5 is the offering: tormas are brought to the shrine, a simple ceremony is conducted, and the tormas are dedicated and — eventually — dissolved or burned. This must be arranged before C5 begins. Confirm with school administration: if dissolution in water is chosen, have a clean vessel ready and know where the water will be released (flowing water is traditional; a garden is acceptable). If burning is chosen, arrange an outdoor space with adult supervision; the students do not need to be present for the dissolution or burning, and it is better that they are not — what matters is that it happens, and that the teacher can report truthfully to them in the following unit: "They have been returned." If a student's torma cannot be offered due to damage, prepare a simple substitute (a fresh small cone of dough shaped by the teacher) and explain: "The practice of offering continues — the object that carries it can change." Spiral connection: the transfer question ("you made this over three classes — you are about to give it away — what does that feel like?") is the culminating assessment prompt for Unit 2. It threads forward to the Grade 7 curriculum's engagement with impermanence (*anicca*) and the formal Panglhabsol ceremony, where students encounter non-attachment in a much larger ritual frame.
