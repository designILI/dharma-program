# G4.U1.C4 — Practising Together
## Grade 4 · Unit 1 · Class 4 of 6 · Practising Together
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Repeated physical practice without narration → knowledge passing from the mind into the hands; embodied transmission
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Spaced and varied practice — students repeat the full sequence multiple times within a single session, with brief pauses between rounds. Repetition with variation (rotating pairs, adding narration as extension) deepens procedural memory and allows students to discover their own gaps rather than having gaps pointed out by the teacher.
**Bloom's Level:** Apply — this class sits squarely in application. Students are not learning new content; they are building fluency with what they already know. The Analyze thread enters subtly: in the debrief and the Turn and Talk, students are asked to observe their own learning process — what has changed, what is still difficult, and why.
**Prior Class Summary:** C3 was the full practical demonstration and first hands-on practice session. Students learned the correct sequence — placement, filling, removal — and practised the mindful pour. They carry the concept of *trenpa* (དྲན་པ — mindfulness as remembering) and the felt difference between rushing through a task and moving through it with precision.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the full sequence of the seven-bowl offering practice — placement, filling, and removal — without requiring teacher narration to guide them through it.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that fluency is not speed but ease born from practice; that the goal is for the practice to settle into the body so that the mind is freed to bring quality of attention rather than spending itself on remembering what comes next.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** complete the full sequence at least twice in this session without narration, with increasing precision in spacing, pour, and clean stop.
Students will **FEEL** the quality of sustained quiet attention — the settling that happens when nine-year-old hands are doing something real, carefully, together. This is the affective centre of this class.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Seven offering bowls — the school's bowls; the same set used in C3
- Additional bowl sets if available (for simultaneous pair/group practice)
- Water vessel — one per pair or group
- Clean cloth for spills
- Demonstration table or shrine surface
- Plant or soil vessel for receiving emptied water
- Diagram journals (collected and held by teacher — not needed this class; returned in C5)
- **Plan B — if only one set of bowls is available:** Structure the session as rotating demonstration pairs — one pair practices while the rest of the class observes and counts silently (this focuses the observers' attention and makes observation active). Rotate every round so each student has at least two full practice turns. Avoid turning observation into a competition; frame it as: "When you are watching, practise trenpa — watching carefully is also part of the training."
- **Plan B — if bowls are unavailable this session:** The teacher sets up and removes while the full class follows in gesture and quiet narration (speaking just below a whisper). This is significantly less effective than physical practice; if bowls were available in C3, make every effort to secure them for C4. Note in My Reflection if this Plan B was needed.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 4 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline Prayer (extended form) · Dusum Sangay.

Student leader leads.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Mime warmup — standing, together, no prompt needed: "Mime the seven offerings with me. In order. From memory."

Move through all seven gestures: cupped hands (Argham) → hands washing (Padyam) → hands blooming (Pushpe) → smoke rising (Dhupe) → framing a flame (Aloke) → perfume at the wrist (Gandhe) → arms open wide (Naividya/Shapta).

Do it twice through, the second time without any teacher cue — just movement.

Then, without notes: "Without looking at your diagrams — what is the third offering? What does it represent?" Wait. Take responses. "The sixth?" Wait. Take responses.

Keep this quick and informal. The point is not to quiz but to check where understanding lives — in the body (gesture warmup) and in retrieval (naming without visual support). No student should feel embarrassed if they cannot recall immediately; just say: "Good — that's why we keep practising."

**Fallback if retrieval is weak:** Don't extend this section. Move to the Mini Lesson — the practice itself will teach what verbal retrieval could not. The goal of this prior knowledge check is to calibrate, not to correct.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 5 minutes

Brief. Do not let this run longer than 5 minutes — the teaching is in the activity, not in the words.

"Today we practice the full sequence without narration. I will not tell you what comes next. I will not remind you to slow down, or check your spacing, or stop cleanly. That responsibility moves to you — and to your partner."

"Before we begin: watch."

Teacher then performs the full sequence — silently, in real time. Placement of seven bowls, one finger-width apart. Filling, left to right, slow pour, clean stop at two-thirds. Pause. Then removal, right to left, water to earth. No words throughout.

Students watch. The silence during the demonstration is instructive in itself — it models what the practice looks and feels like when it is settled.

**Vocabulary Moment:** "The word *fluency* — when something moves through you without effort because it lives in your body. Not speed. Ease. You are aiming for fluency in this practice by Demonstration Day. Fluency means your hands know what to do, and your mind is free to be present — not occupied with remembering what comes next. Fluency is what you are building today."

**Body Moment:** The entire session from this point forward is a body moment — hands, bowls, water, attention.

**Emotional Hook:** The quality of silence in a room full of nine-year-olds who are actually doing something with full attention. This does not happen by accident. It happens because the task is real, the standard is clear, and they have been trusted to do it without constant direction. Notice it. You do not need to name it — but it is worth holding.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

After the first full round of practice (placement, filling, pause, removal), everyone stops. One shared breath together before talking.

"Tell your partner: what was easier than last class? What is still hard? Be specific — not 'the whole thing felt better' but what exactly changed, and what exactly is still catching you."

Give 2 minutes. Then take 2–3 responses from the class. The specificity is important — it builds metacognitive awareness (what is my actual challenge?) and also helps the teacher identify where more support is needed before C5.

**What to listen for:** Students who say the spacing is still uneven are often still placing bowls quickly without checking. Students who say the pour is easy but the clean stop is hard have good physical control but something rushes at the finish. Both are worth noting.

---

#### Activity — 20–25 minutes

Multiple rounds of the full sequence. The structure:

**Round 1:** Each pair or group works through the sequence completely — placement, filling (slow pour, clean stop, two-thirds), one held breath, removal (right to left, water to earth). Teacher circulates, observing quietly.

Between rounds: everyone stops. One shared breath. "Begin again from placement."

**Round 2:** Same sequence. This time the teacher circulates with more targeted corrections — specific, quiet, non-judgmental:
- "The spacing between those two bowls — one finger, check it."
- "Slow right down at the stop. You're anticipating the next bowl."
- "That pour — good. That was genuinely careful."

**Round 3 (if time allows):** Same sequence. Introduce the extension option: students who have reached ease with the physical sequence can try narrating quietly to their partner as they work — naming each bowl as they place it, and what it represents. This is not required; invite it for students who are visibly comfortable with the physical practice.

Rotate pairs if this aids the learning — students who are more fluent should be paired at some point with students who are still finding the sequence, so that peer teaching occurs naturally.

The teacher does not take over when a student struggles. Specific corrections are offered once; then the student is left to work with it. Trust the practice to teach.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

"Practising something physical until it lives in your body — this is how the tradition passes down. Before books, before printing, before photographs of the shrine arrangement — every teaching was transmitted body to body. Teacher shows. Student watches. Student does. Student does again. Student does many times, until the hands know without the mind having to remember."

"What you did today is that transmission. You received it from someone who received it, who received it from someone else — back through time. That chain does not break. Each generation practises until it lives in the body, and then transmits it to the next."

"What you know now, you can teach. That is the test of understanding — not whether you can say it, but whether you can do it and show someone else how."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline Prayer (extended form) · Dusum Sangay.

Student leader leads.

Teacher's closing line: "Next class, we put what is in your hands onto paper — your diagram, completed and annotated with everything you now know. Then: Demonstration Day."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: After C4, most students should be approaching fluency in the sequence — able to move through placement, filling, and removal without narration, with reasonable accuracy in spacing and pour. C5 is the diagram completion class — quieter, more individual, less physical. C6 is Demonstration Day.

Identify before C5 which students still need additional practice. Do not wait for C6 to discover this. If two or three students are consistently losing the sequence or rushing without noticing, offer a brief practice review at the very start of C5 — five minutes with the bowls before journals open — specifically for those students. This is not remediation; it is the practice asking for more time, which is its nature.

Note also which students showed genuine ease today — not just competence, but the beginnings of fluency. These students can serve as demonstration partners on Demonstration Day (C6) for an audience member who wants to try a bowl placement.

Spiral connections: The body-to-body transmission teaching in the debrief is worth naming explicitly as it connects to both the opening prayer set (the prayers are transmitted in exactly the same way — generation to generation, teacher to student, mouth to ear) and to the Tsethar practice in Grade 3, where the form of the practice was demonstrated, not explained, and students absorbed it through watching and doing. Grade 4 adds the metacognitive dimension: students are now old enough to understand that they are inside a transmission, not just receiving a lesson.
