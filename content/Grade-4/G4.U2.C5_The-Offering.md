# G4.U2.C5 — The Offering
## Grade 4 · Unit 2 · Class 5 of 5 · The Offering
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Placing the completed torma on the shrine → relinquishment as the completion of a practice, not the end of a project
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Experiential transfer — this class is the moment when accumulated understanding (three sessions of making) meets its intended action (offering and release). The transfer cannot be simulated or rehearsed; it happens in real time. The teacher's role is to hold the space clearly so that the experience can be what it is.
**Bloom's Level:** Analyze / Evaluate — students are not learning new content; they are encountering what the content means when enacted. The debrief question ("what do you know now about non-attachment that you didn't know before you made something and gave it away?") asks for genuine synthesis.
**Prior Class Summary:** C4 was the finishing session — students completed their tormas and held the question "you made this over three classes — you are about to give it away — what does that feel like?" They carry completed objects and that unresolved question. The tormas have been in storage since C4.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the word *relinquish* — to give up something one has been holding — and the word *Ngowa* as the dedication of merit.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the value of a torma is not in the object but in the making and the giving — and that this is not a comfortable idea but a true one.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** place their torma on the shrine with the offering gesture, receive the chanting of the class as they do so, and speak briefly about what they made and why it matters.
Students will **FEEL** what actually happens in the body at the moment something you made is set down somewhere you cannot take it back from. This may be relief, grief, lightness, strangeness, or some combination. Whatever it is, it is real, and it is the teaching.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Completed tormas, stored from C4 — retrieved before class and arranged where the teacher can distribute them at the right moment (not yet visible to students when they arrive)
- The classroom shrine or demonstration table — set up completely before students arrive: shrine cloth, water bowls from Unit 1, incense if used, central space cleared to receive the tormas
- Bell (for the teacher's use at the close)
- A clean vessel of water (for dissolution after class — if dissolution is the chosen method)
- Students' portfolio materials — sketch from C1, portfolio paragraph from C4 — available to return after the offering
- **Plan B (if the shrine space is unavailable on the day):** Designate a clean table or desk, covered with a cloth, in the centre or front of the room. The offering can happen here. What matters is that the space is treated with the same deliberateness as a shrine — prepared in advance, clean, intentional. If a student's torma has been damaged in storage: have a small handmade substitute ready (a fresh cone shaped by the teacher). Explain: "The practice of offering continues — the form changes but the intention holds."

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
The teacher sets up the shrine before students arrive. The room is quiet when students enter.

The student leader leads the full Grade 4 prayer set — but the teacher leads this opening more slowly than usual, with more space between prayers. This is a ceremony, not a standard class opening. The pace communicates that.

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay**
*(Food Offering Prayer at the closing.)*

After prayers: a brief settling silence. Thirty seconds. Then the teacher begins.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

This is not a recall exercise. It is a settling.

*"We asked last class: you made this over three classes — you are about to give it away. Hold that question now. Not in your head — in your body. Notice what is actually happening right now."*

Silence. Twenty to thirty seconds.

*"Has anyone thought about this during the week? Does anyone want to say what's happening for them right now — before we begin?"*

Take 1–2 responses if offered. Do not solicit further responses if students are quiet. The quiet is appropriate. The teacher does not fill it.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 2–3 minutes

This is deliberately brief. The lesson today is the offering, not the talk.

*"Today we offer. The torma was made to be placed on the shrine, offered to the Three Jewels and to all beings, and then released — dissolved, or burned, or returned to the earth. You are not keeping it. That is the whole point. The value was in the making and in the giving. Not in the having."*

Pause.

**Vocabulary Moment:** Write *relinquish* on the board.

"To relinquish — to give up something you have been holding. To release. This is the moment of relinquishment. It is not comfortable. It is not supposed to be. It is the practice."

**Body Moment:** "When it is your turn, you will bring your torma forward. Hold it in both hands, like this." Teacher demonstrates the offering gesture: two hands, cupped beneath the torma, palms up, the object resting in the hands and presented forward. "Walk to the shrine. Place it in the space that is there for it. Step back. Return to your seat. That is the offering."

**Emotional Hook:** What actually happens in the body when something you made is taken from your hands and placed somewhere you cannot take it back. Do not describe it. Simply say: *"Notice what happens when your hands are empty."*

---

#### Activity — The Offering — 20 minutes

The teacher distributes the completed tormas — one to each student. Students hold them in their laps or on the desk in front of them.

*"We will go one at a time. As each person brings their torma to the shrine, the rest of the class chants Om Mani Padme Hung — softly, continuously, until the person has placed the torma and returned to their seat. The chanting is support. It is the community holding the moment."*

The student leader begins the chanting. One student comes forward — carrying their torma in the offering gesture — walks to the shrine, places it carefully in the designated space, steps back, returns to their seat. The chanting continues.

The next student rises. And the next. The teacher maintains a steady, unhurried pace — not so slow as to make the waiting agonising, not so fast as to rush what is happening. The chanting continues throughout.

When all tormas have been placed: a full stop. The chanting ends. The shrine now holds all the tormas. A moment of silence — the class sees what they made, together, on the shrine.

Then: the teacher leads the full prayer set together, in the following order:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa**

The Ngowa — the dedication of merit — is given particular weight. This is the formal dedication of everything that went into the making and the offering: the patience, the difficulty, the completing, the releasing. The merit of all of it is offered for the benefit of all beings.

After the Ngowa: *"These tormas will be dissolved / returned to the earth after class. The form goes. The merit stays. That is the teaching."*

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

*"What did it feel like — the moment you let it go? The moment your hands were empty?"*

Take 3–4 responses. Do not rush this. Do not summarise or interpret — let students speak. The range of responses will be wide: some students will feel relief, some a kind of sadness, some something they cannot name. All of these are correct.

Then: *"What do you know now about non-attachment that you didn't know before you made something and gave it away? Not what you were told — what do you know?"*

This is the culminating question. Take 2–3 responses. Do not resolve it with a neat conclusion. The knowledge is theirs. The teacher's role here is to receive it.

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
The student leader leads the full closing prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay**

After the final prayer: the teacher rings the bell once. Lets the sound fully decay. Silence.

Teacher's closing line — one sentence for the arc of the unit:
*"You made something with care, you let it go with honesty, and everything from this unit forward has been prepared by what your hands learned in these five classes."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: After students leave, carry out the dissolution or disposal as arranged. If dissolving in water: pour the water and the dissolved material into flowing water or into the earth of a garden. If burning: do so with a brief moment of intention — a few words of dedication. The students do not need to witness this; it is enough that it happens, and that the teacher can report it honestly if asked. Return students' portfolio materials (design sketch from C1, portfolio paragraph from C4) before or during the next unit — they form the Unit 2 artefact in the Grade 4 portfolio. Unit 3 begins next class — Instruments & Ritual Objects. The thread of the year is: Unit 1 was about understanding ritual objects (the water bowls); Unit 2 was about making and offering (the tormas); Unit 3 is about instruments and the objects that accompany practice — bell, dorje, mala, incense. These are not made in class; they are studied, held, and read. The shift for students is from doing and making to understanding and reading. They bring to Unit 3 their bodies of experience: the precision of the bowls, the patience of the making. Name this shift at the opening of G4.U3.C1. Spiral connection forward: the Ngowa prayer (dedication of merit) was formally introduced in Grade 3 and is here used in its most significant context yet — the dedication of a sustained creative and devotional act. In Grade 7, the Panglhabsol ceremony will use the same structure of dedication on a much larger scale. The seed planted here matters.
