# G4.U3.C1 — The Sound of the Shrine
## Grade 4 · Unit 3 · Class 1 of 5 · The Sound of the Shrine
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** The bell and dorje — their form, their sound, the way they are held → the union of wisdom and compassion as a relationship that requires both to be present
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Sensory priming before conceptual introduction — students experience the bell through sound before any vocabulary or symbolic meaning is offered. This reverses the usual instruction sequence and creates genuine curiosity: students have been changed by the experience before they receive the explanation.
**Bloom's Level:** Understand — this is the entry class for Unit 3. Students are receiving new objects, new vocabulary, and new symbolic relationships. The Field Guide entry at the end of the class moves toward Apply (encoding what they received into an organized, usable form).
**Prior Class Summary:** C5 of Unit 2 was the offering — students placed their completed tormas on the shrine, chanted together as each student offered, and dedicated the merit of the making and the giving. They carry the experience of non-attachment in the body, and the phrase "the value was in the making and in the giving."

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the names *drilbu* (bell, དྲིལ་བུ) and *dorje* (vajra, རྡོ་རྗེ), what each represents (wisdom and compassion; emptiness and method; left hand and right hand), and why they are always used together.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that a bell is not merely a sound-making instrument — it is a teaching about the voice of the Dharma and the nature of wisdom; and that the silence after a bell ring is itself part of what the bell teaches.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** complete Field Guide entry #1 — an illustrated, annotated record of the bell and dorje with their symbolic meanings and correct handling.
Students will **FEEL** the particular quality of attention that a bell ringing in a quiet room creates — the way sound organises stillness.

---

### Materials Checklist
- A bell (drilbu) and dorje — real objects if available. If a traditional ritual bell is unavailable, any small bell with a clear, sustained tone will allow the opening sound moment; note this to students.
- Images of a ritual drilbu and dorje for reference (printout or screen display), including labelled diagrams showing: bell (handle, skirt, clapper, inscriptions) and dorje (central sphere, prongs, lotus base)
- Sketchbooks or Field Guide booklets — one per student (the Field Guide is a small booklet, stapled or bound, that students will build across C1–C4; prepare these before class)
- Pencils and coloured pencils or fine-tipped markers for illustration
- **Plan B (if no bell is available):** Use a recording of a ritual bell (available online — search "Himalayan singing bowl" or "drilbu sound"). Play it through speakers, let the sound fully decay. The experience of listening is the same; the holding of the object is adapted — students hold a smooth stone or cup their hands, weight and form approximated. This works. Name the substitution honestly.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
The student leader for this class leads the full Grade 4 prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay**
*(Food Offering Prayer is reserved for the closing.)*

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Before any introduction of this unit's content:

*"Close your eyes. I am going to make a sound. Just listen."*

The teacher rings the bell once — a clean, clear ring. Let the sound fully decay. Complete silence. Wait until the sound is genuinely gone before speaking.

*"Open your eyes. What did you notice? What happened in your body when the sound began? What happened as it faded?"*

Take responses. Students may describe: a feeling of attention, a kind of stillness, something in the chest or throat, an instinct to be quiet, a memory of a shrine or a ceremony. Do not evaluate or redirect. Receive all of it.

Then: *"You have spent two units working with ritual objects — water bowls, tormas. You have held them, filled them, made them. Today we start differently. Today we start with what a ritual object sounds like."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

"What you just heard is a drilbu — a bell. In Dharma term: དྲིལ་བུ. It is used in Himalayan Buddhist practice alongside the dorje — རྡོ་རྗེ — and these two objects are never separated in ceremonial use. Let me show you both."

Show the bell and dorje — hold each up separately first so students can see the form clearly. Then hold them in their correct hands: bell in the left, dorje in the right.

"The bell represents wisdom — specifically, the wisdom of emptiness. Emptiness in Buddhist teaching does not mean nothingness. It means that things do not exist independently, permanently, in the way they appear to. The bell's sound fills the room and then disappears completely. Where did it go? That is the bell asking about emptiness. Its sound is called the voice of the Dharma — the teaching itself, travelling without a body."

"The dorje represents compassion — skilful means, the capacity to act in ways that relieve suffering. Where the bell is the understanding of reality, the dorje is the action that flows from that understanding. One without the other is incomplete."

"Bell in the left hand — wisdom. Dorje in the right hand — compassion. Together: the union of wisdom and compassion. In Himalayan Buddhist thought, this is not a metaphor. It is the structure of a fully awakened mind. Every time a practitioner picks up these two objects, they are rehearsing that structure — holding it in their bodies."

Ring the bell again — this time students watch as well as hear.

**Vocabulary Moment:** Write *dorje* on the board, and beneath it: *vajra* (Sanskrit).

"Dorje — from the Sanskrit *vajra*, meaning thunderbolt or diamond. A thunderbolt is powerful, sudden, clarifying. A diamond is indestructible — nothing can cut it except itself. The dorje represents the mind's capacity to cut through confusion and delusion. It is made of the same substance as the thing it clarifies. Remember that."

**Body Moment:** If bell and dorje are available: the teacher demonstrates how each is held — bell in the left hand at the level of the heart, dorje in the right hand at the same level, the tips of the dorje pointing upward. Students who are able come forward in small groups of three or four and hold the objects (with teacher guidance and care — these are ritual objects; they are held with two hands when being passed). Those who do not hold the actual objects: mirror the grip with their own hands, feeling the imagined weight and position.

"Feel the weight. Notice what the grip does to your posture — the way holding both objects at heart level pulls the spine upright, the way the arms settle. This is not accidental."

**Emotional Hook:** Before moving on, the teacher rings the bell one more time — and this time, says nothing. Let the sound decay completely. Then:

*"What is the sound after the bell stops? Is there silence — or is there something else?"*

This question is not answered. It is placed in the room and left there.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

*"Tell your partner: what does the sound of a bell make you think of, or feel? Has that sound ever meant something to you — at a shrine, a ceremony, a school assembly, a meal, anywhere? Take two minutes each."*

Students talk in pairs. The teacher circulates and listens — not to correct, but to gather the range of associations. Bell sounds carry different meanings for different students depending on their family practices, their school experience, their previous Dharma classes. All associations are valid; the lesson will not overwrite them but extend them.

What to listen for: students for whom the bell has no prior association (this is fine — the class is building one), and students for whom the bell has a rich prior context (these students may have the most to contribute to the debrief).

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

Students begin Field Guide entry #1 — the bell and dorje.

The entry should contain:
1. An illustration of the bell (labelled: handle, skirt, clapper, face/inscriptions if visible)
2. An illustration of the dorje (labelled: central sphere, prongs, lotus base at each end)
3. Written answers to:
   - What does the bell represent?
   - What does the dorje represent?
   - How is each held, and why?
   - What does the teacher observe when they are used correctly?

The teacher circulates — quality of illustration matters here. This is not a quick sketch. Students should take time with the drawing, looking at the actual objects or the reference images. The illustration is not decorative; it is part of the learning. An accurate illustration requires careful looking.

For students who finish early: write one sentence at the bottom of the entry — "The thing I did not expect about this object was ___." This becomes a personal note in the Field Guide.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

*"Every time you hear a bell — in a shrine, in a corridor, anywhere — it is now possible to let that sound be what it is: a moment of clarity. A small pause in whatever is happening. The bell is teaching even when no one is deliberately practising. That is what it means for an object to carry meaning — it carries it whether or not anyone is paying attention. But the teaching only reaches you when you are."*

Brief pause. Then: *"What is the sound after the bell stops?"* — returned as a closing question, still unanswered, still open.

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
The student leader leads the full closing prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay**

After the final prayer: the teacher rings the bell once. Lets the sound fully decay.

Teacher's closing line, specific to today:
*"You began Unit 3 the same way a shrine begins every ceremony — with a sound. That was not accidental."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The Field Guide is a booklet students will build across C1–C4 and present in C5. Collect all Field Guide booklets at the end of each class and keep them safely — this prevents damage, loss, and between-class additions that fall outside the taught entries. Return them at the start of each class. Plan ahead: C3 is the mala session — the most practical class of Unit 3, requiring one mala per pair at minimum. Confirm availability with the school now, before C2. If the school does not have enough malas, investigate borrowing from the temple or monastery, or purchasing simple wooden malas (inexpensive; available at any Himalayan religious-supply shop). C2 covers incense and the shrine arrangement — confirm with school administration whether incense can be burned in the classroom. If ventilation is poor or there is a concern about fire safety, substitute a scented sachet or an essential oil diffuser (sandalwood is appropriate to context) and explain to students why. The substitution is not a failure of practice — it is a demonstration of the distinction between the symbol (purification, outward-going intention) and the specific material vehicle. Spiral connection: the question "what is the sound after the bell stops?" connects directly to G3.U2 (where students studied *sunyata* through the concept of space — what remains when something is absent). The bell question is the same inquiry from a different angle. Name this to students if and when it becomes relevant — probably in C2 or C3, not necessarily today.
