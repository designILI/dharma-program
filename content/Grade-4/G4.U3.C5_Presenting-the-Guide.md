# G4.U3.C5 — Presenting the Guide
## Grade 4 · Unit 3 · Class 5 of 5 · Presenting the Guide
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Each student presents one Field Guide entry to the class → being witnessed as a form of completion; the difference between knowing something privately and being able to teach it
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Retrieval and articulation as consolidation — presenting knowledge to an audience requires a different quality of understanding than writing it down privately. Students must hold their knowledge clearly enough to speak it, choose what to foreground, and respond to the room. This is the deepest consolidation of the year's learning.
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluate / Create — in presenting, students must evaluate which information is most important to convey, select for clarity, and produce a spontaneous explanatory performance. This is the highest Bloom's level reached in the Grade 4 curriculum.
**Prior Class Summary:** C4 was the Field Guide completion class — students refined all entries, gave and received specific peer feedback, and the guides were collected. Students carry complete Field Guides and were told: "Come knowing one entry well enough to teach it."

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the vocabulary of the year — and be able to deploy it naturally in their presentation: *puja, trenpa, japa, zopa, dorje, drilbu, mala, torma, Argham, symbol, transmission, non-attachment*.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the Field Guide is evidence of a year of learning — not a performance, but a genuine record of what they encountered and how they encountered it.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** present one Field Guide entry clearly to the class, explaining the object as if to someone who has never encountered it, including what it looks like, what it is for, what it represents, and one thing that surprised them.
Students will **FEEL** what it is to be witnessed — to share something worked for, in a room that is paying full attention — and what it is to witness others with that quality of attention.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Field Guide booklets — returned to students at the opening of class (distribute before prayer, or immediately after)
- The actual ritual objects if available — bell, dorje, mala — on a central table for students to pick up and use as props during their presentation if they choose
- Reference images available as backup
- The shrine (or demonstration table), set up as in earlier classes — visible to all students during presentations
- The teacher's notes page for the class — a simple grid or list where the teacher can record one genuine observation per student (not a grade; a recognition)
- Bell — for the teacher to ring at the very end
- **Plan B (if class time is short):** Presentations of 1–2 minutes each are the target. If the class has more students than can all present in 40 minutes, group students by entry topic — all those presenting on the mala go consecutively, then the bell-and-dorje group, etc. This is less ideal than individual presentations but preserves the essential experience of being witnessed. Alternatively, extend the lesson by one class if the school calendar allows. Do not rush the closing — the year's closing deserves its full time.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
The teacher leads the opening — not the student leader, for this one class. This is the closing of the year, and the teacher holds it.

The full Grade 4 prayer set, led slowly, with more space than usual:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay**
*(Food Offering Prayer at the closing.)*

After prayers: "Today we close the year. Grade 4 is about the language of ritual. By the end of this class, you will have demonstrated that you can read, speak, and teach that language. Let's begin."

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 2–3 minutes

"Choose one entry from your Field Guide to present. Not necessarily the one you know best — the one you find most interesting. You have two minutes to decide and prepare. Review your entry, choose what you will say, decide if you want to use the actual object."

Students open their guides, choose, and prepare brief notes. The teacher circulates but does not guide the choice — the choice itself is part of the assessment. What a student finds most interesting is evidence of genuine engagement with the material.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 1 minute

Keep this minimal. The lesson today is the presenting.

"Your presentation has three parts: first, show the object or your illustration. Second, explain what it is and what it represents. Third, tell us one thing you noticed about it that surprised you, or that you did not expect. That is all. One to two minutes."

**Vocabulary Moment:** No new vocabulary. This is a synthesis class. The teacher invites students to use any vocabulary from the year — and lists them on the board as a reference: *puja, trenpa, japa, zopa, dorje, drilbu, mala, torma, Argham, symbol, transmission, non-attachment, Three Jewels, Könchok Sum, paramita*. Students are not required to use all of them; the list is a reminder of what they have.

**Body Moment:** "Before we begin — stand up. Take one breath. This is how we begin every practice — with a breath and with the body upright." Students stand. One breath together. "Sit."

Then: the student leader leads three repetitions of Om Mani Padme Hung — not seven, not 108, but three: one for each unit of the year. A small, deliberate form.

**Emotional Hook:** Being witnessed — sharing something you worked for, in a room that is paying full attention. This is not performance anxiety (though some students will feel that); it is the experience of a community receiving what a member has learned. Both sides of that exchange — giving and receiving — are the emotional content of this class.

---

#### Activity — Presentations — 20 minutes

One by one, students present. The order is not pre-assigned; the student leader calls on volunteers, or the teacher moves through the room in a natural order.

Each student:
1. Shows the object (actual or illustrated)
2. Explains what it is and what it represents
3. Names one thing that surprised them or that they did not expect

The teacher listens to every presentation with full attention and no visible note-taking during the presentation itself (notes can be made briefly between presentations). After each presentation: the teacher names one specific, genuine observation. Not a grade. Not "good job." Something specific:

- "I noticed you explained the guru bead rule in a way that I haven't heard before — that the reversal is an act of honour, not just a rule. That is a precise understanding."
- "You showed us the bell and explained why it is in the left hand. That is not obvious — it requires knowing what the left hand represents. You know that."
- "The thing that surprised you — that the torma is given away — I notice that surprised you even though you made one and gave one away. That is interesting. Hold that."

After all presentations: a brief whole-class reflection. The teacher asks: "What did you hear from your classmates that you want to remember? What did someone else notice that you hadn't noticed yourself?"

Take 3–4 responses.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

"You have spent a year learning the language of ritual. You know what the seven bowls mean, how to make something and give it away, how to read a shrine, how to count with a mala. That is not small. Most people who walk into a Tibetan Buddhist shrine room do not know what they are looking at. You do."

"Next year — Grade 5 — you will use this knowledge to understand something much larger: the Wheel of Life. The Sipa Khorlo. It is a single image — painted on a wall or on thangka cloth — that contains more symbolic meaning than anything you have studied this year. Everything you learned here is preparation for that. You will see why when you get there."

"Before you go: one question to carry into the summer. A wheel has no beginning and no end — it just turns. What do you think that means?"

Do not answer the question. Leave it open.

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
The student leader leads the full closing prayer set — complete, unhurried:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay**

After the final prayer: the teacher rings the bell once. Lets the sound fully decay. Complete silence. The room holds it.

Teacher's closing line — one sentence for the whole year:
*"You came in not knowing the language, and you leave able to read it — that is what a year of practice does."*

Dismissal. Field Guide booklets are returned to each student as they leave — to keep, to use, to return to.

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: Field Guides are returned to students as their portfolio artefact for Unit 3. Together with the Unit 1 diagram (seven-bowl offering setup, drawn and labelled) and the Unit 2 torma portfolio pages (design sketch from C1, paragraph from C4), the Field Guide completes the Grade 4 portfolio. These three artefacts are worth noting in any transition to Grade 5: the diagram shows understanding of ritual objects (Unit 1), the torma pages show engagement with making and non-attachment (Unit 2), and the Field Guide shows the capacity to read and explain ritual objects (Unit 3). Grade 4 is complete. Thread into Grade 5: the Wheel of Life (Sipa Khorlo) is the most symbol-dense object in the curriculum — a single image that encodes the entire Buddhist understanding of conditioned existence, the causes of suffering, and the path of liberation. Students who understand how ritual objects encode meaning (Grade 4) will approach the Wheel as readers rather than merely viewers. They will already know: a pointed top means something. A placement on the right side means something. A counting tool means something. The Wheel of Life takes all of those skills and scales them enormously. Tell them this in the debrief of this class — not as a preview of content, but as a naming of skill: "You will read the Wheel the same way you read a shrine. But the Wheel has more rooms." One practical note: the bell ringing at the very end of this class — after the final prayer, before dismissal — is intentional and should not be skipped. It echoes the opening of Unit 3 (where the first thing students heard was the bell, before any content was taught), and it closes the year with the same sound that opened the unit. Students who notice this are demonstrating exactly the kind of pattern recognition that Grade 4 was designed to develop.
