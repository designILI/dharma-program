# G1.U3.C3 — Drawing the Lotus Lake
## Grade 1 · Unit 3 · Class 3 of 4 · Drawing the Lotus Lake
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A drawing made by hand → the mind's second encounter with the sacred image — attention as devotion

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Dual coding through generative drawing. When students redraw the birth scene from memory and reference, they are not copying — they are reconstructing the image in their own visual language. This reconstruction deepens retention and moves the image from something told to something owned. The quietness of the studio is itself a learning condition.

**Bloom's Level:** Remember / Understand / Apply. Students retrieve both the story (Remember) and its meaning (Understand), then apply this through the physical act of constructing the image (Apply — lowest level: replication with personal interpretation).

**Prior Class Summary:** Last class students learned who Guru Rinpoche is — the one who brought the Dharma to Tibet, the lamp that lit a thousand lamps. They added to their listening sketches with words and symbols. They now know the story and its significance. Today they begin the full artwork that will be presented in C4.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the key visual elements of the birth scene: the lake (water), the lotus (flower and stem), and Guru Rinpoche (seated child, radiant, calm) — enough to construct them intentionally in their own drawing.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that making a drawing of something sacred is itself a form of attention and respect — that how carefully you look and work is part of what the artwork means.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** produce a drawing that includes the water, the lotus, and the figure of Guru Rinpoche on the lotus — even in simple form — and begin to add colour.

Students will **FEEL** the particular quality of focused, quiet attention that good artwork requires — what it is like when the room is still and the hand is moving and the mind is settled. This is a near-meditative state, and six-year-olds can enter it if the conditions are right.

---

### Materials Checklist
- one sheet of white cardstock per student or heavy drawing paper (A4 or A3 — A3 is better if available; larger scale allows more expressive drawing)
- Pencils (one per student, well-sharpened — check in advance)
- Coloured pencils or washable watercolour sets (one set per student or shared between two; if watercolour, you need small water cups and newspaper under each workspace)
- Crayons as a backup/supplement for strong colour coverage
- The large lotus image (from C1 and C2) posted at the front of the room throughout the class
- The illustrated Guru Rinpoche biography open to the birth scene image, placed on a table where students can walk up and look — not passed around (reduces distraction)
- Students' listening sketches from C1 returned to them (these are references, not final drafts)
- Blackboard or chart paper: simple step-by-step drawing guide drawn by the teacher at the start of the activity (see Activity section for what to draw)
- Optional: soft instrumental music or drone — only if the teacher knows this helps this particular group settle; silence is equally valid

*Advance prep: Set up one workspace per student before students arrive — paper centred, pencil to the right, colours ready. This signals to students that something considered is about to happen. Walk through the drawing steps yourself the night before so you know where children are likely to get stuck (usually the figure of Guru Rinpoche — how to draw a seated person simply).*

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Children come in to find the workspaces already set up. They sit — in the semicircle first, away from the workspaces, for prayer.

Student leader of the day comes forward.

**Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer.** Together, unhurried.

Student leader returns to seat. Teacher stands.

*"Today is a working day. Almost the whole class is going to be drawing. I want to tell you something before we start: when you make a drawing of something sacred — something like the birth of Guru Rinpoche — the drawing itself becomes an offering. That means: work carefully. Work quietly. Look at the image often. Don't rush. What you make today will be on the wall next class. So let's begin right."*

No long preamble. Set the tone, then move.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Students still seated in the semicircle.

*"Before you pick up a pencil — let's remember what we're drawing. Close your eyes. I'll describe it and you picture it.*

*There is a lake. Wide. Still. Blue-green water.*

*In the middle of the lake — a stem rising up from the water.*

*At the top of the stem — a lotus flower. Pink petals, open.*

*Sitting in the middle of the lotus — a child. Small. Very still. Eyes open, but peaceful. A glow of light around him.*

*That is what we are drawing today."*

Eyes open. *"What do you remember? What details do you want to make sure you include?"*

Take three or four responses. Accept: the colour of the lotus, the light around his body, the water ripples, the colour of the sky. Write three or four words on the board as students say them — WATER, LOTUS, GURU RINPOCHE, LIGHT — these are the four elements every drawing should include.

*"Good. And if you forget while you're drawing — look up at the picture."* Point to the large image. *"It is there the whole time. And your sketch from last week is also there for you."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

This is the drawing demonstration. Keep it brief and practical — the bulk of the class is the activity itself.

On the board or large paper, the teacher draws a simple four-step sequence:

**Step 1 — The Water**
A horizontal line across the lower third of the page. Then wavy lines above and below to suggest water. *"Your lake. It doesn't have to be realistic — just so we know it's water."*

**Step 2 — The Lotus Stem and Flower**
From the centre of the water line, draw a vertical stem rising up. Then at the top: a simple lotus — draw a central petal, then add petals around it like a crown or a star shape. *"Lotus petals go up and outward — like a cup opening to the sun."* Demonstrate the petal shape slowly: a rounded top, slightly pointed base. *"Do your petals first in pencil, lightly, then colour them in."*

**Step 3 — The Figure**
*"This is the part some of you might find tricky — drawing Guru Rinpoche himself. Let's keep it simple."* Draw a small circle for the head above the lotus centre. Then a triangular or rounded shape below for the seated body — like a small mountain shape, for a person seated cross-legged. Two small hands visible at the sides. *"He is seated in meditation. His body is calm, his hands are in his lap. That is all you need — a round head, a mountain-shaped body, two hands."*

**Step 4 — Light and Sky**
Rays of light around the figure — simple lines going outward, or a glow drawn with yellow or gold. The sky above — blue, or whatever the student feels is right. *"In some paintings there are clouds. In some, a clear sky. In some, rainbow light. Look at the picture — or follow what's in your own mind."*

**Vocabulary Moment:** *"The glow of light around a sacred figure — in art, it's sometimes called a halo, or in Himalayan Buddhist art, an aura of light. It tells us: this person is radiant. What does radiant mean? Giving off light from the inside. When you draw his light, you are saying: he is radiant."*

**Body Moment:** *"Before you go to your seats — let's make the lotus one more time with our hands."* (Hands together, palms open upward, fingers spreading.) *"Feel the shape. Now go to your workspaces and put that shape on the page."*

**Emotional Hook:** Do not narrate this. Once students are at their workspaces, lower the ambient energy of the room. If there has been noise or restlessness, say quietly: *"Take one breath before you start. Then begin."* The transition from talking to working quietly — held by the teacher's own stillness — is the hook.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

This Turn and Talk happens before the full activity, during the mini lesson transition — it is brief and preparatory rather than reflective.

As students move toward their workspaces:

*"Before you sit — turn to the person next to you. Tell them: what is the one part of this drawing you're most looking forward to making?"*

Thirty seconds. Then: *"Okay — sit down and begin with that part if you want. Or start with the water, which is always easiest."*

The purpose here is not deep reflection — it is activating enthusiasm and agency before a longer quiet activity.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

Students work on their drawings at their individual workspaces. This is the heart of the class.

**First five minutes — pencil sketch only.** Encourage students to draw lightly so they can adjust. Circulate and check that everyone has the four elements started: water, lotus, figure, light. If a student has only drawn water and stopped, prompt gently: *"What grows up from the water?"*

**After five minutes — add colour.** Students can work in pencil and colour simultaneously, or finish the pencil sketch and then colour. Either is fine.

**Teacher's role during the activity:**
- Move around the room slowly.
- Crouch beside individual students to look — do not lean over from behind.
- Make comments that are specific and observational, not evaluative: *"Your lotus petals are very open,"* not *"That looks great."*
- Ask questions that deepen attention: *"What colour is the water in your picture? Why did you choose that colour?"*
- If the room gets noisy, lower your own voice rather than raising it. Model the quiet you want.

*Scaffold for a stuck student:* If a student is frozen and cannot begin: *"Start with the water. Draw a long, slow line right across the middle of your page."* Stay beside them for sixty seconds while they start. Then move on — return after a minute to check.

If a student has drawn something entirely unrelated: *"Tell me about your picture."* Listen. Then: *"Can we find a way to put the lotus somewhere on this page too? Even just a small lotus, anywhere?"*

*Extension for an early finisher:* *"You've got all four things — water, lotus, Guru Rinpoche, light. Can you add one more detail? What was around the lake? Were there mountains? Trees? Other people coming to look? What do you imagine?"*

At approximately the 25-minute mark from the start of class (15 minutes into the activity), give a two-minute warning: *"Two more minutes. Start finishing your main shapes. We will continue colouring next class if you need."*

Collect drawings. Write each student's name on the back if not already there.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

Students back in the semicircle, artworks collected.

*"You just spent fifteen minutes working quietly on one image. That's not easy — to look carefully, to draw carefully, to stay with one thing. That's a skill. And it's also a kind of practice.*

*I want to ask you: while you were drawing — was there a moment when you were thinking very hard about Guru Rinpoche? About the lake, or the lotus? What was in your mind?"*

Take two or three responses. Accept any genuine answer — *"I was thinking about the colour of the water,"* *"I was thinking about whether he was small or big,"* *"I was thinking about the mud at the bottom."* All of these show that the drawing produced thinking about the subject.

*"When you look carefully at something — when you draw it — you understand it a little more. That's why people have made images of sacred things for thousands of years. Not to decorate walls. To look. To understand. Next class, you'll finish your drawings and share them with the class. So tomorrow night — or whenever you have a quiet moment — picture the lake in your mind one more time."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader to the front.

**Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer.** Together. Teacher joins fully.

Teacher's closing sentence, specific to today:

*"You made something today. That is already an offering. I'll see you next class to finish it."*

Dismiss per school custom.

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
The studio silence is a learning condition, not a classroom management strategy. There is a difference between a room that is quiet because students are anxious and a room that is quiet because students are absorbed. The teacher's own stillness — moving slowly, speaking quietly, crouching to look rather than leaning from above — is what produces the second kind. If the room becomes noisy, lower your voice rather than raising it. Move to one child, speak softly, let the quiet travel outward from there. Do not call out from the front.

Carry forward: drawings are collected at the end of this class and returned in C4 (G1.U3.C4 — Our Lotus Gallery). If any drawings are unfinished, schedule the first five minutes of C4 for finishing touches before the gallery walk begins. Tell students this at the end of class: "Some of you may want to add one more detail next time, and there will be time for that." This removes anxiety about incompleteness and keeps the making open.

This is the third class in Grade 1 in which students have made something with spiritual intention — after the prayer flags (G1.U1.C4–C5) and the leaf crown (G1.U2.C3). The explicit statement in this lesson — "when you make a drawing of something sacred, the drawing is an offering" — is the curriculum's first direct naming of this principle. Students will not fully understand it now; it is a seed. The same principle deepens through G4.U2 (tormas made as offering), G6.U4.C4 (masks made for ceremony), and G8.U3.C3 (stupa building as collective devotional act). You are planting, not harvesting. Say the sentence once, clearly, without elaboration, and move on.

The four-step drawing demonstration is scaffolding for students who freeze at a blank page, not a constraint on those who don't. Students who draw in a different sequence, or who add elements not in the demonstration, are doing exactly what should happen. The scaffold is permission to begin; it is not a template to follow exactly.

Watch for the moment the room goes genuinely quiet and students are deeply absorbed — this is the near-meditative state described in the Bloom's note, and it can last 5–10 minutes in a well-held class. Do not interrupt it with check-in questions or time announcements until necessary. The child who draws Guru Rinpoche very small may be feeling the vastness of the world around him; the child who draws him very large may feel his importance most powerfully. Neither is wrong. Observe without correcting. Watch also for the student who spends the entire activity on water ripples and never reaches the figure — redirect early: "What comes up from the water? Put that in next."
