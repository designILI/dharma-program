# G1.U1.C5 — Finishing & Sharing
## Grade 1 · Unit 1 · Class 5 of 6 · Finishing & Sharing
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Completing a painted flag → the courage to name who you made something for, and why that naming matters

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Retrieval Practice with Emotional Salience — when students name their wish aloud (the person their flag is for), they are not merely recalling information. They are performing the wish. Research on emotional encoding confirms that information and experiences connected to strong feeling are retained longer and more deeply. The act of saying "This flag is for my grandmother" is simultaneously a memory event, a speech act, and an affective one. All three reinforce each other.

**Bloom's Level:** Apply and Understand — students complete a creative task (Apply) and then articulate the meaning of what they made (Understand). The articulation is the assessment: if a child can say who the flag is for and why they painted what they painted, they have understood the unit's central idea.

**Prior Class Summary:** In C4, students painted background colours and mantra syllables on their cloth flags, working slowly and with a specific person in mind. Flags were left to dry. Some students completed their flags; others need more time today. the teacher reviewed all flags between C4 and C5 and knows which students need completion time and which are ready to share.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** that each flag in this class was made for a specific person, by a specific child, with a specific wish.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that putting a wish into words — naming it, saying it aloud — is a part of sending it.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** complete their flag (if needed) and share one sentence: who the flag is for, and what they wish for that person.

Students will **FEEL** the warmth and slight vulnerability of sharing something they made with care, and the safety of being received well by their class.

---

### Materials Checklist

- Completed and nearly-completed flags from C4 — laid out at tables before class, sorted by completion status:
  - Table A: 3–4 students who need 10–15 minutes of additional painting
  - Table B: remaining students whose flags are complete
- Fabric paint (remaining colours from C4), brushes, water cups, paper towels — set up at Table A only
- Fine black fabric marker or thin brush with black paint — for adding detail to syllables, available to all students who want it
- Six syllable cards (propped at front)
- Teacher's completed sample flag
- Completed flags already dry — pinned or clipped to a length of string or cord across one area of the room, so students can see them displayed as they arrive (teacher's flag hangs first, as a model)
  - **Advance prep:** String a cord across one wall or between two points before class. Hang teacher's flag and any student flags that are fully dry and complete. Do not hang flags that need more work — these stay at the table.
- Wooden clothespins (one per student, plus several extra) — for hanging flags on the cord in C6. Today students see the cord and can imagine their flag on it.
- Singing bowl
- Sharing prompt written clearly on a piece of card and set on the teacher's chair, visible from the circle: *"This flag is for ___. My wish for them is ___."*

**Room setup:** Circle chairs near the door; tables set for Table A painting in one area; Table B students will have a different activity (see below). The cord with hanging flags is visible as students enter — this is deliberate. Let it create anticipation.

**Student leader:** Brief before class. Today's sharing will be gentle and not pressured — make sure the leader knows this is a calm, reflective class.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Students enter. They see the cord with flags. Many will immediately want to look at them — allow a moment of natural looking and commenting. Then settle into the circle.

Student leader steps forward.

**Kyap Dro (Refuge):** Leader recites, the teacher alongside.

**Om Mani Padme Hung:** Seven times. Today the teacher invites students to chant with eyes closed if they like. *"Just the sound. Just the sound going out."*

**Manjushri Prayer:** Leader recites.

Strike the bowl once.

*"Look at the flags on the cord. One of those spots is waiting for yours. Next class, they all go up together."*

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

*"Before we do anything today — I want to ask you to think back to when you were painting. What were you thinking about? Was it easy to hold your person in mind while you painted, or did your mind go somewhere else?"*

This is an honest question, not a test. Six-year-olds are genuinely varied in their capacity for sustained intentional focus, and the teacher knows this.

**Strong-response example:** *"I kept thinking about my aama and then I'd forget and then I'd think about lunch."* — Receive with warmth: *"That's exactly how it goes, isn't it? The mind wanders. But you kept coming back. That's already the practice."*

**Surface response:** *"I was thinking about making it look good."* — *"And who were you making it look good for?"* Draw the thread back to the recipient.

**If a student says they weren't thinking about anyone:** *"That's honest. Today during sharing, you might find the person comes to you. Or maybe you'll find you were painting for yourself — and that's a real wish too."*

*"Today we finish, and then we share. Here's the prompt you'll use:"*

Hold up the card: **"This flag is for ___. My wish for them is ___."**

Read it aloud twice. *"That's all you need to say. Two sentences — or even just one. When you're ready, you'll share it with the class."*

*"You don't have to share anything private if you don't want to. But I hope you will, because hearing each other's wishes is part of the practice."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

*"Before we paint and share — I want to do something first. I'll show you mine."*

the teacher takes their own flag from the cord.

*"This is the flag I painted. I chose the syllable Mani — because Mani points to compassion, and the person I made this for needs some. I'm not going to say their name, because it's private. But I'll say my wish:"*

*"This flag is for someone I love who is far away. My wish for them is: may they feel less alone."*

Hold the flag up. Let the sentence settle.

*"That's it. That's the whole sharing. It doesn't have to be long. It just has to be true."*

**Vocabulary Moment:** *"Dedication — when we do something good and then say 'I'm sending this to you,' that's called a dedication. We'll do it together in the next class with all our flags. But you can practise now: when you name your person and your wish, that's your dedication."*

**Body Moment:** Guide one slow breath — in, out. Then: *"Think of your person. Just hold their face or name or feeling for a moment. Now pick up your flag."*

**Emotional Hook:** the teacher's own sharing is the emotional hook. A teacher who is genuinely present, genuinely wishing, genuinely willing to be seen doing it — this gives students permission to do the same. Do not over-script your own sharing. Let it be real. If it moves you, let that be visible — not performed, but not hidden either. The class will feel the difference. What you are creating is the conditions for honest sharing: safety, example, and time.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

This Turn and Talk prepares students for the whole-class sharing, reducing performance anxiety by letting them practice with one trusted partner first.

*"Turn to your partner. Practise your two sentences. 'This flag is for ___. My wish for them is ___.' Your partner's job is just to listen — not to respond, not to comment. Just listen."*

Both partners share in turn. Total time: approximately three minutes.

Signal with the bowl.

*"How did it feel to say it out loud?"*

Let two or three responses emerge briefly. Some students may report that it felt strange, or that they almost cried, or that it felt right. All are valid.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

**Part 1 — Finishing time (10–12 minutes):**

Students who need more painting time go to Table A. the teacher circulates, giving brief, specific encouragement: *"Your syllable is there — I can read it. That's what matters."* Do not ask students to keep painting if they are genuinely finished. A sparse flag hung with intention is worth more than a crowded one painted to please the teacher.

Students whose flags are complete go to Table B and receive this instruction: *"Your flag is done. Use this time to practise your two sentences quietly to yourself. Or: look at the flags on the cord and write (or draw) in your mind what you notice about them. What's different? What's the same?"*

At the twelve-minute mark, all painting stops. Hands washed.

**Part 2 — Whole-class sharing (8–10 minutes):**

Students return to the circle. Flags — dry or nearly dry — are in their laps or set gently on the floor in front of them.

*"We're going to go around the circle. When it's your turn, hold up your flag and say your two sentences. Or just one. Or say the person's name and nothing else — even that is enough."*

*"I'll start."* the teacher holds up their flag and says their two sentences again — slightly different from the mini lesson version, because this is live and not scripted. Let it be natural.

Go around the circle. the teacher's role during sharing:
- Receive each sharing with one word or a nod: *"Thank you."*
- Do not elaborate, evaluate, or compare. Every flag is equal.
- If a student says something that is clearly difficult — a sick parent, a person who has died — receive it with extra stillness. *"Thank you for sharing that."* Then move on. Do not make it into a moment. The student who shared will be grateful that it was held without being amplified.
- If a student passes: *"That's fine. Your wish is yours."* Move on without comment.
- If a student shares something unexpected (wishes for an animal, for a fictional character) — receive it as real. The wish is real.

**Scaffold:** Any student who freezes — whisper: *"Just the name. Just say their name."* This is enough.

**Extension:** A student who shares fluently and wants more: *"After class, would you write your two sentences on a small paper to attach to the back of your flag?"* This becomes a record, a dedication note.

After all students have shared, hold the moment:

*"Fourteen wishes. Fourteen people — or animals, or places — who just received something. They may not know it. But you know it. And now we all know it."*

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

*"Tomorrow — well, next class — we hang them. All of them, on a line, in the wind. And then the Lung-ta takes over."*

*"Here is something I want to leave you with: you painted something for someone else. You said their name, or held them in your heart. That is already kindness. That has already happened. The hanging is the celebration of something that is already real."*

Do not ask the year's essential question today. Let the sharing be the whole thing.

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Flags are gathered carefully. Student leader steps forward.

**Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung (seven times), Manjushri Prayer.**

Strike the bowl once.

*"Next class, we hoist. Come ready."*
