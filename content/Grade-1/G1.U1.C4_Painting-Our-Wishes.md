# G1.U1.C4 — Painting Our Wishes
## Grade 1 · Unit 1 · Class 4 of 6 · Painting Our Wishes
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Brush on cloth → a wish becoming a physical thing that will outlast this moment

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Embodied Cognition — when the body is engaged in slow, careful making, the mind enters a different quality of attention. Painting a mantra syllable is not illustration; it is the mantra passing through the hand. The action of painting and the act of wishing are not separated in this lesson — they are the same event. Students are not making a craft. They are performing a wish.

**Bloom's Level:** Apply — students take the knowledge they have built (the mantra, its syllables, its meaning, the tradition of prayer flags) and enact it in a new form: painted cloth. This is the lesson where understanding becomes making.

**Prior Class Summary:** In C3, students looked at real prayer flags, learned the five colours and the Lung-ta (Wind Horse), and held their own plain cloth flag pieces. They traced details from photographs onto cloth with crayon. Each student privately identified a person they want to send their wish to. They left knowing that next class they would paint. The anticipation is already doing its work.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** which syllable of Om Mani Padme Hung they have chosen to paint, and why they chose it.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that making something carefully, with a person in mind, is itself an act of wishing — the making and the wish are not separate.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** paint at least one mantra syllable onto their cloth flag, with attention and care.

Students will **FEEL** the meditative quality of slow, focused making — and the weight of doing something for someone else.

---

### Materials Checklist

- Students' cloth flag pieces from C3, stored flat with names on the back (one piece per student, retrieved before class)
- Fabric paint — available colours: blue, white, red, green, yellow (the five traditional flag colours), plus black for outlines if desired. **At minimum**, provide the five traditional colours. Additional colours are welcome but not required.
  - Advance prep: Pour small amounts into individual containers (egg-carton cups work well). Each table gets its own set. Do not put communal brushes in paint pots — too messy and leads to colour contamination.
- Soft, medium-sized paintbrushes — 2 per student (one for colour, one for outlining). **Advance prep:** Test brushes on scrap cloth the day before. Stiff brushes will frustrate six-year-olds.
- Water cups — 1 per student, half-filled. Label with each child's name or position to reduce confusion.
- Newspaper or plastic sheeting to cover tables
- Paper towels — at least 10 per table
- Six syllable cards from C2 (retrieved from storage) — mounted or propped at the front of the room where all students can see them from their seats
- One completed sample flag (teacher-made) — showing at least one syllable painted clearly, with the Wind Horse as background. **Advance prep:** the teacher makes this the evening before. It does not need to be beautiful — it needs to be honest. A teacher's genuine attempt is more useful to students than a polished example.
- Smooth stones from C3 (or alternatives: small wooden blocks, clothespins) to hold flags flat during painting
- Soft music (optional): if the school has access to a recording of Om Mani Padme Hung being chanted, play it quietly in the background during the painting time. Not required — if music is unavailable, comfortable quiet is ideal. Do not play anything distracting.
- Drying rack or clean flat surface — flags need to dry undisturbed after class

**Room setup:** Tables covered with newspaper before students arrive. Paint, water, brushes, and paper towels already placed. Cloth pieces at each student's place, name-side down. Six syllable cards visible at the front. Teacher's sample flag hung or propped where students can see it. Stones/weights at each place.

**Student leader:** Brief as usual. Note that today's Opening leads into a slightly longer transition as students settle at tables — the leader should be seated before the activity begins so they are not holding paint-covered hands during closing prayers.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Students enter the circle first — do not begin at tables. This is intentional: the circle grounds them before the making begins.

Student leader steps forward.

**Kyap Dro (Refuge):** Leader recites. the teacher alongside, hands folded, still.

**Om Mani Padme Hung:** Seven times. Today the chanting should feel purposeful — students know they are about to paint this. Let that awareness come without commentary.

**Manjushri Prayer:** Leader recites.

Strike the bowl once.

*"Before we sit down to paint — I want to ask you to carry something with you to your table. The person you thought about last time. The one you'd send your wish to. Hold them in mind while you paint. You don't have to say their name yet — just keep them with you."*

Pause one full breath. Then: *"Let's go to our seats."*

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Students are at their tables but have not yet touched materials.

*"Look at the syllable cards at the front. Can anyone name them — all six — in order?"*

Invite the group to try together. They may need help. OM — MA — NI — PAD — ME — HUNG. If students stumble, support without correcting: *"What comes after MA? Anyone?"*

*"Today you're going to choose one of these six to paint on your flag. Just one. You can paint more than one if you have time, but start with one — the one that feels most yours."*

Exact retrieval prompt: *"Before you choose — can you remember what each syllable points toward? Let's go through them."* Point to each card and invite a student to give the pointer. Use students who were particularly engaged in C2, but open it to anyone.

**Strong-response example (OM):** *"Body, speech, and mind — like the kindest person."* — Receive: *"Yes. When we say Om, we're aiming toward that."*

**Surface response (HUNG):** A child says "I forget what Hung means." the teacher: *"Hung is the sending. Spread your arms — remember? That's Hung. Sending it to everyone."*

**Fallback if no one recalls:** Go through the cards yourself, one pointer each, very briefly. This takes two minutes maximum. Students are recalling — not being taught again.

*"Now — which one do you want to paint? Think about it. The one that spoke to you, or the one for the person you're thinking about. Take thirty seconds."*

Silence for thirty seconds. Then: *"Have you chosen?"*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Hold up the teacher-made sample flag.

*"This is what I made last night, so you can see what we're working toward. It doesn't have to look exactly like mine — actually, I hope it doesn't. Each one should look like you."*

Point out: the syllable in the centre, large and clear; the background colour; the Wind Horse, even roughly drawn.

*"Here's how we paint today. Three steps."*

**Step 1 — Background colour:** *"Choose your colour first — one of the five. Wet your brush, dip it in paint, and fill the whole cloth. Light layer, not soaking wet. This is your flag's sky."*

Demonstrate on a scrap piece of cloth, visibly and slowly. Show students how much paint is too much (dripping), and how to get an even stroke. Show how to rinse the brush after.

**Step 2 — Your syllable in the centre:** *"While the background is still a little wet, or after it dries — you'll paint your syllable in the middle. You can look at the card at the front. You can paint it however you like — big, small, in a different colour so it shows up. If you want to paint letters, look carefully at the card. If you want to make the shape in your own way, that is also fine."*

Note: Grade 1 students painting Tibetan script will produce approximations, not accurate script. This is correct and appropriate. The intention matters more than the accuracy.

**Step 3 — Wind Horse or decoration (if time):** *"If you have time after your syllable, you can add the Wind Horse — or a lotus, or any image that feels right for your flag. Look at the photographs from last class if you need help."*

**Body Moment:** Before anyone picks up a brush, guide thirty seconds of slow breathing together.

*"Put your hands flat on the table in front of you. Close your eyes if you want. Take a breath in — slow — and let it out. One more. Now open your eyes. Pick up your brush. Paint for the person you're thinking about."*

No further instruction. Let the painting begin.

**Vocabulary Moment:** While students work, the teacher moves between tables. At a natural pause, speak quietly to a small group: *"What you're doing right now has a word in our tradition — it's called making merit. When you do something good — something kind, like painting a wish for someone — it creates goodness. Not just for you. For them too. Even if they never see this flag."*

**Emotional Hook:** The condition you are creating is concentrated intention — the child who is doing something not for themselves, but for someone they love. This is unusual for a six-year-old, who is normally the centre of their own universe. Watch for the child who paints with unusual slowness and care. Watch for the child who is very quiet. Watch for the child who, without prompting, says the person's name quietly while they paint. These are children who have entered the practice. Notice them. Say nothing. Stay near.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

This Turn and Talk happens during the painting activity, not before it. At approximately the fifteen-minute mark (when most students have background colour down and are working on their syllable):

*"Keep painting — you can keep going while you talk. Turn to the person next to you and tell them: what syllable are you painting? Why did you choose it?"*

Students can talk while their hands keep moving. This is intentional — the dual engagement keeps both the body and the conversation grounded in the making.

Circulate. Listen for students who articulate their choice clearly — these are the students you will invite to speak briefly during the C5 sharing. Make a mental note.

Signal to continue working (no bowl strike — the painting continues).

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

The painting activity runs from the end of the mini lesson to the debrief — approximately 20 minutes total. The structure above covers most of it. Here are additional teacher-circulation notes:

**Table 1 student seems stuck on the syllable shape:** Sit beside them. Put your finger on the card. *"See this stroke at the top? Start there. One stroke at a time."*

**Student wants to paint all six syllables:** *"That's a wonderful idea — but give each one the attention it deserves. One, really well. Then we'll see if time allows."*

**Student finishes early:** *"Your background and syllable are done. Now — what does your person look like? Can you add something on the flag that makes you think of them? Not their face — just something. A colour they love. A shape that reminds you of them."* This is a genuine extension: transforming the flag from a religious object into a personal one.

**Student whose paint is running and distressed:** *"Wet paint runs a little — that's the paint doing its thing. Wait one minute and it will settle. The drips become part of it."* This is also true.

At the five-minute warning: *"Start finishing what you have. It doesn't have to be complete today — we have next class too. What matters is that you worked carefully."*

Collect painted flags — hold by corners, do not stack. Lay flat on the drying surface with name tags beside each one. Note which students need more time in C5.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

Students wash hands and return to the circle.

*"While you were painting — were you thinking about your person?"*

Let students answer briefly. Do not require a response.

*"Here's what I noticed: it was quiet in here. You were working carefully. When you do something carefully — slowly, with someone in mind — that care goes into what you're making. It's not magic. But it's also not nothing."*

Let that sit.

*"Next class, we finish. And then every person will have a chance to say something — just one sentence — about who their flag is for. You don't have to say much. But think about what you want to say."*

Do not answer the year question today. Let the making be the debrief.

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Students reconvene in the circle, hands clean.

Student leader leads: Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer.

Strike the bowl once.

*"Your flags are drying. The paint is settling. And somewhere, the person you were thinking about — they don't know it yet, but something good was sent their way today."*
