# G1.U2.C1 — A Tree Who Loved a Boy
## Grade 1 · Unit 2 · Class 1 of 4 · A Tree Who Loved a Boy
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A tree that gives everything it has → love that keeps giving with nothing asked in return

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Narrative transport — when children are fully inside a story, the emotional truth of its ideas enters through felt experience rather than instruction. In this lesson, *The Giving Tree* is not used to teach a concept; it *is* the concept. The story does the work. The teacher's job is to protect the story's landing.

**Bloom's Level:** Remember and Understand. Students receive the whole story and sit with it. No analysis yet — that comes in C2. Today the only task is to let the story in. Understanding operates here at the level of emotional recognition: *I know this feeling. I have been given to.*

**Prior Class Summary:** In Unit 1, Class 6 (Hoisting Day), students hung their prayer flags together in a small ceremony and each said aloud who their wish was for. They carry a sense of completion and warmth — their flags are moving in the wind somewhere in the school, carrying their intentions outward. This class begins something new, but the thread of giving and wishing well for others continues invisibly.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** the story of *The Giving Tree* by Shel Silverstein — its sequence, its characters, and its ending.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the tree gave everything it had, kept giving even as it had less and less, and was happy doing so.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** sit quietly with the ending before being asked to speak — holding an idea in the body before putting it into words.

Students will **FEEL** moved, perhaps a little quiet, perhaps with a recognition of someone in their own life who gives to them the way the tree gives.

---

### Materials Checklist

- *The Giving Tree* by Shel Silverstein — 1 copy, teacher's. Know the book well enough to read it without pausing to locate your place. Practice the pacing once before class: the ending should feel unhurried. The last two spreads — the old man and the stump — need space.
- Reading rug or circle mat — enough room for all the children to sit close together, facing the teacher. Proximity matters for read-alouds with this age.
- Room setup: chairs and desks pushed to the edges if possible. Sit on the floor for this reading. The informality signals: *we are doing something special*.
- Optional: one small green leaf (paper or real) held by the teacher during the opening transition — a quiet signal that something new is beginning. Not required; trust your instinct.
- No worksheets. No art materials today. Nothing on the desks.

**Advance prep:** Read the book aloud to yourself at least once. Time it — it runs 8–10 minutes at the right pace. Mark (lightly in pencil) two places: (1) when the boy first takes the apples to sell — this is where the tone shifts; (2) the final page. Practice the pause after the last line. It should last at least 10 full seconds — longer if the room holds it.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Designate one child as student leader before class begins (rotate this role each class). The student leader comes to the front of the circle. Teacher chants along, unhurried, present — not conducting, but participating.

**Kyap Dro (Refuge Prayer)** — recited together. Teacher models tone: steady, soft, not performative.

**Om Mani Padme Hung** — seven rounds minimum, or more if the room is still settling. Let the sound do the work of gathering.

**Manjushri Prayer** — together. Teacher closes eyes briefly at the end.

After prayers, student leader returns to their spot. Teacher says, simply:

*"Thank you, [name]. Our flags are still flying from last week — our wishes are out there. Today we begin something new. I want to read you a story."*

Hold the book up so they can see the cover. Don't explain it. Don't preview it. Just begin.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

This happens *before* the read-aloud, woven into the transition from the opening.

Prompt (exact words): *"Before I read — has anyone ever gotten a gift from someone? Not a birthday present. I mean — has someone ever done something for you, or given you something, just because they wanted to? What was it?"*

Give children time. This age needs 5–7 seconds of silence before hands rise. Wait.

**Strong response example:** "My amala always makes my tea in the morning and brings it to my bed." — Receive this warmly. *"She just does that for you. That's a gift."*

**Surface response + deepening:** If a child says "My friend gave me a toy" — receive it, then: *"And did you ask for it, or did they just give it?" (Child responds.) "So they just wanted to give it to you — without you asking."* Let that land. Don't push further.

**Fallback if nothing comes:** *"I'll tell you what made me think of this. I know someone who used to make my lunch every single day, every single morning, without me ever asking. I didn't think about it much then. I think about it now."* Pause. Then: *"Ready?"* Open the book.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

The read-aloud *is* the mini lesson. Protect it.

Seat children in a close arc so all students can see the illustrations. Hold the book facing out so images are visible, glancing at text — you should be able to read without dropping your head. If you need to, practice holding it angled slightly toward you.

**Pacing notes:**

- Early pages (the boy swings, climbs, eats apples): Move at a warm, easy pace. Let children feel the happiness of the relationship. This is where they bond with the tree and the boy.

- *"And the tree was happy."* — Read this line the same way each time it appears. Don't perform it. Say it plainly. It will begin to accumulate weight on its own.

- When the boy grows up and starts asking for things (money, a house, a boat): Slow down slightly. Not dramatically — the shift is in the story, not in your voice. Children will feel it if you don't telegraph it.

- The final pages — the old man, hunched, and the stump: Read slowly. The last line is: *"And the tree was happy. But not really."* (Note: Some editions vary. Know your edition's exact ending.) Read it once. Close the book quietly. Set it in your lap.

**Then: silence.**

Count to ten in your head. Keep your face open and soft — not somber, not smiling. Present. If a child starts to speak, hold up one gentle finger and shake your head slightly. *Not yet.* Ten seconds at minimum. More if the room holds.

After the silence, say quietly: *"I just want to sit with that for a moment."* Another few seconds. Then: *"What did you notice?"*

**Vocabulary Moment:** The word *gave* appears throughout the book. Don't define it. After the reading, when a child uses it, reflect it back: *"The tree gave. Yes. What did it give?"* Let the list build from the children — apples, branches, trunk, stump. The vocabulary deepens through the story's own accumulation.

**Body Moment:** Before the read-aloud begins, ask children to place both hands open in their laps, palms up. *"These are giving hands. Hands that are ready to give something."* This sets the body posture for the listening. You may return to it at the end: *"Look at your hands. When the tree had nothing left — what did it still have?"* (Possible child answer: itself, the stump, love.)

**Emotional Hook:** You are creating the condition of *felt recognition* — the quiet, almost wordless knowing that says *someone has loved me this way.* Don't name this. Don't explain it. The silence after the book is the container for it. Your job is to hold that container steady.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Wait until after the initial "What did you notice?" responses settle — usually 3–4 children will share something. Then:

Prompt (exact words): *"Turn to the person next to you. Tell them: who is a giving tree in your life? Who just keeps giving to you?"*

Fixed pairs: assign these in advance or use established seating pairs from Unit 1. Pairs stay consistent across the unit.

Give 2–3 minutes. Move through the circle, crouching to listen — not to assess, but to gather what is alive in the room. Note one or two things you hear to bring back to the group.

Reconvene: *"Eyes back to me."* Wait for full attention. Then: *"I heard some beautiful things. I heard someone say their amala. I heard someone say their grandfather. I heard someone say their teacher."* (Use what you actually heard.) *"All of those people — they're still giving, aren't they? Even right now."*

**What to listen for:** Are children staying in the concrete and specific (a person, an action), or going abstract? Keep pulling them toward the concrete. *"Who? What do they give you?"* Abstract answers ("love," "kindness") are fine to receive — just ask: *"What does that look like when they do it? Can you show me with your hands?"*

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

Today's activity is a *reflection walk*, not a worksheet or art project — that comes in C3.

**Step 1 — "Thank you drawing" (10 minutes):** Give each child one sheet of plain paper and something to draw with (crayons, coloured pencils). Instruction:

*"Draw the giving tree in your life. It doesn't have to look like a tree. Draw the person — or if you want, draw what they give you. Just one person. Take your time."*

Teacher's role: Move slowly through the room. Crouch at eye level. Look genuinely at what children are making. Don't comment on technique. Ask: *"Who is this?"* and *"What are they giving?"* These questions are for the child, not for you. They don't need to share the drawing with anyone.

**Scaffold for a stuck child:** *"Close your eyes for three seconds. Think about this morning. Who did something for you before you got to school?"* Almost always something surfaces — a parent, a grandparent, a sibling, the cook at the hostel. *"Draw that person."*

**Extension for an early finisher:** *"On the back, can you draw what the tree felt like inside when it gave everything away? What does happy look like on the inside?"* This is not a requirement — offer it gently as an invitation.

**Step 2 — Gallery pause (3 minutes):** Children leave their drawings face-up on the floor (or on their desks) and walk quietly through the "gallery" to look at each other's. No talking during the walk. One minute of quiet looking. Then return to the circle.

This models something important: that other people's givers are worth seeing and respecting.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

Children return to the circle. Leave the drawings where they are for now.

Opening question (exact words): *"After hearing the story, and thinking about your person — what is something the tree had at the very end that the boy didn't know about?"*

Let children respond. You are listening for something like: the tree was still happy. The tree still had its love. The tree wasn't empty in the way the boy might have thought.

If the room is quiet, try: *"When you drew your person — the one who keeps giving to you — do you think they feel empty? Or do they feel something else?"*

**The "so what" moment — say something like this (not exactly, but in this spirit):**

*"I wonder if some kinds of giving don't run out. Like — the tree gave its apples, its branches, its trunk. At the end it had almost nothing left that you could hold in your hands. But something was still there. Something that couldn't be given away."*

Pause. Don't label it. Don't say the word "love" unless a child has already used it — in which case, simply: *"Yes. That's what I think too."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Return the student leader role (can be the same child, or the one who will lead tomorrow — your call).

**Kyap Dro** — together. Teacher chants along, present.

**Om Mani Padme Hung** — seven rounds.

**Manjushri Prayer** — together.

After prayers, teacher says — one sentence, specific to today:

*"Today you held a story in your hands and in your hearts. Carry it home carefully."*

Children fold their drawings and take them with them. No instructions about what to do with them. They are theirs.

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Unit 2 begins here, and the transition from Unit 1 matters. Students arrive carrying the warmth of Hoisting Day (G1.U1.C6) — their flags are moving in the wind, their wishes are out. Do not make much of this connection explicitly; it works better as an invisible thread than as a teaching point. What carries forward into C2 is logistical: did every student make a drawing and take it home? Check this in C2's opening, lightly — not as assessment, but as a thread. If a child lost their drawing, let them make a new one quietly at the start of C2's activity; the drawing itself is not the lesson.

The silence after the read-aloud is the class's pedagogical non-negotiable. Everything else — the prior knowledge activation, the gallery pause, the debrief — can be adjusted in emphasis. The silence cannot. A teacher who fills that silence with a question, however warm, has cut the lesson off at its peak. Practice holding it before class: count to ten in your head, keep your face open, stay present. If a child speaks into the silence within three seconds, hold up one gentle finger — not harshly, just: not yet. The silence is the container for felt recognition, and felt recognition is what this lesson exists to produce.

This is Class 1 of a four-class thread (G1.U2.C1–C4) built around unconditional love and gratitude. The arc: receive the story (C1) → understand the distinction between giving freely and giving for return (C2) → make the gratitude visible in a wearable object (C3) → offer it outward in a ceremonial circle (C4). C1 plants the emotional seed; everything that follows grows from the quality of this reading. This thread builds directly forward into the metta/compassion circles of G2.U1, where love for a specific giver becomes love extended outward to all beings. It also connects to G7.U1, where students return to this same book and reckon with the full cost of the tree's giving without limit — the transactional versus unconditional distinction examined at a depth appropriate to adolescents. For now, let the story be only what it is.

Watch for the child who goes very quiet at the ending — quieter than the others, perhaps looking at their hands or lap. That is the lesson landing. Do not call attention to it. The child who draws someone unexpected in the activity — not a parent, but a cook at the hostel or an older student who always saves them a seat — has already begun generalising the concept beyond the obvious. That is understanding in motion, and it is the most important thing you will see today.
