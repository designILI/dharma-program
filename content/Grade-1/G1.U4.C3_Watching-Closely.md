# G1.U4.C3 — Watching Closely
## Grade 1 · Unit 4 · Class 3 of 4 · Watching Closely
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A real insect, observed carefully → the practice of sustained attention as a form of respect and care

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Observation as slow encoding. Children's default relationship with small creatures is brief: a glance, a reaction (delight or disgust), and then movement on. This lesson disrupts that pattern by requiring sustained, silent attention — five to eight full minutes of looking at one creature. What students encode in that sustained looking is not merely data; it is a relationship. You cannot look at something for that long without beginning to care about it.

**Bloom's Level:** Apply. Students are not hearing a story or discussing a concept — they are applying patient attention to a real creature, in real time. The drawing is evidence of that attention. This is the most physical and applied class of the unit.

**Prior Class Summary:** Last class students asked whether bugs feel, reasoned toward the idea that all living beings want to live, and learned the word ahimsa. They drew a bug and imagined its inner thoughts. They carry curiosity and a new moral framework. Today they apply that framework to a real, living creature in front of them.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** at least three observable features of the specific insect they observed — body parts, movement patterns, colour, behaviour — by drawing and describing them.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that careful observation — really looking — is a form of attention and respect, and that the ability to be still and watch is a skill that can be practised.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** sit in sustained, quiet observation for five or more minutes — without touching, disturbing, or losing focus on the creature — and produce a drawing that reflects what they actually saw.

Students will **FEEL** the quality of attention that is distinct from ordinary looking: the slowing-down, the noticing of things they would have missed in a glance, the sense of having been present with another living thing.

---

### Materials Checklist
- one clipboard per student (one per student), or a hard surface each student can carry outdoors — a thin board, a hardback book
- one sheet of white drawing paper per student, pre-clipped to clipboards
- one pencil per student (well-sharpened and attached to clipboards with a rubber band so they don't drop in the grass)
- Small hand lenses / magnifying glasses — one per pair of students if available; these transform the experience
- "Bug Rescue Observation Journal" cover page (a simple header sheet that will be attached to the front of students' observation drawings to make a two-page unit portfolio document) — prepared by teacher; can be handwritten: "My Bug Rescue Journal — Name: ___ — Class: ___"
- Bug Thoughts drawings from C2 (returned to students at start of class — these go into the journal)
- Container with a live insect if possible — OR students find insects in the schoolyard (see Activity). If bringing an insect into class first to introduce: use a transparent container, same as C2.
- Standard prayer card (opening and closing done indoors)
- Plan B (in case of rain, no insects findable, or outdoor conditions not possible): three or four insects in clear containers, placed at stations around the room; students rotate. Less ideal but functional.

*Advance prep: Walk the schoolyard the morning of class — find where insects are. Under stones, near soil, around plant stems, under leaf litter. Know two or three spots you can take students to. If the class has magnifying glasses, test them. Set up clipboards the night before. This class depends on preparation — if you arrive without knowing where insects are, you will spend your activity time looking rather than observing.*

*Note on silence: this class requires a quality of quiet that six-year-olds can manage but need to be set up for. The opening indoors — grounded in prayer and a clear explanation of what silence means today — is what makes the outdoor quiet possible. Take time to prepare it.*

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Class opens indoors. Children in the semicircle as usual.

Student leader of the day comes forward.

**Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer.** Together, unhurried. Teacher joins with particular attention today — the quality of the prayer opening will set the quality of the observation.

Student leader returns. Teacher stands.

*"Today we are going outside. But before we go, I want to tell you what kind of outside trip this is.*

*This is not a play break. This is not free time. We are going outside to observe — to really look — at a living creature. That means: quiet feet, quiet voices, quiet hands. Your job is to look, and to draw what you see. Nothing else.*

*Can you do that?"*

Pause for an affirmation from the group — a nod, a yes, a raised hand.

*"Good. Collect your clipboard. We go in one line."*

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Done indoors before going out. Quick and purposeful.

Return Bug Thoughts drawings from C2. Students hold them.

*"Look at the bug you drew last class — the one you imagined the inner life of. Remember what you said it was thinking? What it wanted?"*

Thirty seconds to look. Then:

*"Today you're going to meet a real one — or something like it. And here's the challenge: the real bug won't talk. It won't tell you anything. You have to figure out what it's doing just by watching. That's called observation. Ob-ser-va-tion."* (Write on board.) *"Careful watching. Paying attention on purpose.*

*Here are the questions you're going to try to answer with your drawing and your eyes — not your words:"*

Write on board:
- How does it move?
- What does it look like up close?
- What is it doing right now?
- What does it seem to want?

*"You don't have to write those questions — they're just in your head as you watch. Alright — let's go."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

The mini lesson happens in transition and in the first moments outside — it is brief and practical.

As class moves outdoors (single file, quietly), teacher says:

*"When we get there — I'll show you a spot where there might be insects. When you find one, sit down near it. Don't touch it. Don't get too close at first — watch from a little distance, then slowly move closer if the creature is calm.*

*Start with the outline of the bug on your page. Then add details — legs, wings, markings. Draw slowly. Look up at the bug, then look down at your page. Look up. Look down. That back-and-forth is how you learn to really see.*

*If your bug disappears or flies away — find another one nearby. Or stay where you are and watch the same spot — another one might come.*

*No talking to your neighbour while you're watching. If you need to ask me something, wait until I come to you."*

At the site (near soil, under a stone, along a wall, in grass), teacher gently demonstrates finding an insect — crouching low, moving slowly, not disturbing:

*"Look — right here."* (Point to an ant, beetle, or other creature.) *"Watch how I sit — slowly, don't block its light. And now I just look."* Teacher models five seconds of genuine, quiet looking. *"See? It keeps going. It doesn't even know I'm here."*

**Vocabulary Moment (outdoors):** *"What we're doing right now is called natural observation. Scientists do this. Monks do this. Artists do this. Anyone who wants to really know something has to be willing to sit still and pay attention. That's our practice today: natural observation."*

**Body Moment:** The whole class is a body moment. But specifically: *"Feel your feet on the ground. Feel your breath slow down a little. Your body knows how to be still — let it."* This is said once, quietly, before students spread out to observe.

**Emotional Hook:** The insect itself is the hook. If even one child says, quietly, without prompting: *"It's so small,"* or *"Look at its legs,"* or *"It's carrying something,"* the hook has worked. Teacher receives these observations with a nod — not excitement, just recognition.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Conducted quietly, in the observation space.

After five to six minutes of independent observation, teacher moves through the group and says in a low voice to pairs:

*"In thirty seconds — just thirty — whisper to the person next to you: what is the most interesting thing you've noticed so far? Then back to drawing."*

Pairs whisper briefly. Teacher listens. This is a brief, contained exchange — not a disruption to the quiet. It also serves as a check: if a student has noticed nothing, the partner's observation might re-focus their attention.

Reconvene: no whole-group debrief yet — this happens indoors. Keep the observation going.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

**The Observation Drawing**

Students spread out in the observation area. Each student locates an insect and begins drawing.

The activity has two phases:

**Phase 1 (8–10 minutes): Silent observation and drawing**
Students work independently. Teacher moves through the group slowly, crouching beside each student for fifteen to twenty seconds. Teacher does not speak — just looks at what the child is drawing, looks at the insect, and nods. Presence without intervention.

If a child is drawing without looking at the insect (drawing from imagination rather than observation), teacher points gently from child's drawing to the real insect: an invitation to look again.

If magnifying glasses are available: hand them to students one at a time. The first time a child looks through a magnifying glass at a living insect, it often produces an audible reaction. This is welcome. Let it be brief, then redirect to drawing.

**Phase 2 (5 minutes): Adding labels and words**
*"Now — somewhere on your page, add at least two words or labels. What body part are you drawing? What colour is it? What is it doing? Write what you see."*

Students add words. Teacher assists with spelling on request — come, write the word beside the student's page lightly in pencil so they can copy it.

Two-minute warning: *"Two more minutes. Finish your last detail."*

Return indoors for debrief and closing. Students carry their clipboards. Line up quietly — the quality of quiet returning inside should mirror the quality that was maintained outside.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

Students seated indoors in the semicircle, clipboards in hand.

*"Let me see your drawings."* Students hold them up together. Teacher looks across the room. Receive what is there — the variety of insects, the different levels of detail, the genuine observation marks. Say one specific thing: *"I can see that this student's bug has six legs drawn very carefully — and here, the wings folded across the back."*

*"Now tell me — what was the hardest part of that? What was hardest about just sitting and watching?"*

Take three or four responses: "staying still," "it kept moving," "I wanted to touch it," "my leg hurt from sitting."

*"Yes. All of those. Sitting still and paying attention — that's a practice. It's the same practice as the parrot, actually. The parrot didn't do one big dramatic thing. It did the same small thing over and over, patiently. You just did the same thing — you looked patiently, over and over, at one small creature.*

*Next class is the last class of this unit. You're going to do something with a real bug — a real action of care. What might that be?"*

Let children guess. Don't confirm or deny. Let the anticipation sit overnight.

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader to the front.

**Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer.** Together. The class is quiet and grounded from the observation — this prayer may feel particularly settled today.

Teacher's closing sentence, specific to today:

*"You looked at something very small for a long time. That is not nothing. That is attention. And attention is a form of care."*

Dismiss per school custom.

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
**Spiral connections:** Backward: G1.U4.C1 (the parrot's patient back-and-forth — now students practice patience themselves through sustained observation rather than hearing about it in a story); G1.U4.C2 (ahimsa framework — C3 is the first embodied enactment: observing without disturbing is ahimsa in practice, not just as principle). Forward within unit: G1.U4.C4 (The Rescue — the capacity for observing without touching, built in C3, prepares students for the gentle physical care required in C4). Forward across curriculum: G3.U1.C2 (observation as the first step before any intervention — the same careful-looking skill, applied in Tsethar/life-release context, where students must assess whether a creature needs rescue before acting); G3.U1 (Tsethar practice requires exactly this quality of patient, non-disturbing attention, applied to creatures students will handle); G8.U3.C3 (stupa building — the quality of sustained, patient attention cultivated here recurs in a different medium and at a different scale).

**Pedagogical framing:** Advance prep is not optional — walk the schoolyard the morning of class and locate insects. If you arrive without knowing where to take students, you will spend the observation window searching rather than observing, and the lesson collapses. The drawing is not an art activity: it is evidence of attention. What students draw is precisely what they actually saw, not what they remember or imagine a bug looks like. Gently distinguish these when circulating — if a child is drawing from memory, point from their paper to the living creature: "Look again." The quality of silence established indoors (during the opening prayer and the prior knowledge activation) is what makes outdoor silence possible. Do not underinvest in the indoor preparation.

**What to watch for:** A student going genuinely still and quiet for the full observation window is doing the deepest work in the unit. The child who makes an audible discovery mid-observation ("it's carrying something!" / "its legs are so thin!") is showing that the quality of looking has changed — and their discovery will often pull other students' attention in the same direction. If magnifying lenses are available, watch the first moment a child looks through one at a living insect: that reaction (often audible) is the emotional hook for this class, unreplicable by any other means. A student drawing from imagination rather than observation needs a gentle redirect, not a correction: "Look at the bug again — look at its legs. How many? Are they like what you drew?"

The observation drawing from this class combines with the Bug Thoughts drawing from G1.U4.C2 and the journal cover page to form the Bug Rescue Observation Journal — a two-page portfolio document. Return the Bug Thoughts drawings at the start of class as noted in the plan. Do not rush the debrief's closing question ("what might you do with a real bug next class?") — let the anticipation sit overnight. It carries C4's opening energy.
