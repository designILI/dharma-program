# G1.U5.C4 — Reading Our Book
## Grade 1 · Unit 5 · Class 4 of 4 · Reading Our Book
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Reading the class-made book and offering flowers → A story belongs to those who tell it; offering is a way of saying: this matters.

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Retrieval + Ceremony — this class pairs a final retrieval event (each child reads their page aloud) with a ceremonial act (flower offering). Ceremony encodes meaning in the body and in the social fabric of the class: this story is not just information, it is something we hold together. The book becomes a class artefact that outlasts the unit.

**Bloom's Level:** Remember / Understand / Apply

**Prior Class Summary:** In C3, each student made one illustrated and captioned page for the class book, choosing their favourite moment from the birth story. the teacher assembled the pages into a single bound book before today's class. Students come ready to "read" their page aloud — which for many means pointing to their picture and speaking their caption from memory or reading it if they can.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** the birth story in the sequence that the class book presents it (through hearing all the pages read in order).

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the flower offering is a way of expressing gratitude and marking something as precious.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** present their own page aloud to the class — a complete, confident act of authorship, however brief.

Students will **FEEL** pride, belonging, and something like reverence — the particular stillness that comes with a ritual that the class has prepared for together.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

The garden cloth is spread at the centre before students arrive. The bowl of flowers sits at the edge of the cloth. The class book is placed face-up at the centre.

Students come in and sit in the circle. The sight of the cloth, the flowers, and the assembled book will land differently today — let them notice without narrating.

Designated student leader comes forward.

*"[Student name], please lead us."*

**Kyap Dro (Refuge Prayer)** — leader recites, class follows.
**Om Mani Padme Hung** — three rounds together, hands at heart.
**Manjushri Prayer** — leader recites, class follows.

After the prayers, a genuine pause. The circle, the cloth, the flowers — let it sit.

*"Today we are going to do two things: we are going to read our book, and we are going to offer flowers. Both of these things together make this class a little different. Pay attention."*

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

*"We've been living with this story for four classes now. Before we open the book — eyes closed. I'm going to say a word, and you say the first thing you see in your mind."*

Call out slowly, leaving 5 seconds between each:

- *"Garden…"*
- *"White elephant…"*
- *"Seven steps…"*
- *"Lotus…"*
- *"Asita…"*
- *"Siddhartha…"*

After the last one, let the silence sit for a moment, then:

*"Open your eyes. What did you see?"*

Take 3–4 responses — not a full class discussion, just a brief surfacing. This is a settling ritual as much as retrieval.

*"Good. All of that is inside you now. The story lives in you. That's what we're going to share today."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 5 minutes

(This class has no formal Mini Lesson in the usual sense — the reading IS the teaching. Keep this section brief and purposeful.)

Hold up the book.

*"This is our book. Every page was made by one of you. There are fourteen pages — one for each person in this class. In a moment, we're going to go around the circle and each person will take the book when it comes to them, find their page, and read it to us — or show it and tell us what it says. You don't have to read every word perfectly. Just tell us what you made."*

**Vocabulary Moment:** *"We're going to do something called a reading — where someone reads aloud so others can hear. But we're also going to do something called an offering. Does anyone know what an offering is?"*

Take 1–2 responses. Most six-year-olds will have seen an offering at home or at a temple.

*"An offering is when you give something beautiful as a way of saying: this matters. We are going to offer flowers today — one each — to say: the story of the Buddha's birth matters to us. We'll do that together at the end."*

**Emotional Hook:** Hold one of the flowers in your hand briefly — look at it. Put it back. Don't explain. The gesture communicates that something is about to become ceremonial. Conditions: the visible bowl of flowers, the garden cloth, the quiet in the circle, your own unhurried quality. These create the container. Students feel it without needing it named.

---

#### Activity: The Reading — 20 minutes

Pass the book to the first student in the circle (traditionally the student who is furthest from the door, or begin with a confident reader to model).

*"Find your page. When you find it, hold the book so everyone can see your picture. Then read or tell us what it says."*

**Practicalities:**
- The book passes around the circle; each student takes it when it reaches them.
- Students who can read their caption independently: read it aloud.
- Students who struggle to read: you as teacher read the caption in a murmur alongside them, just quietly enough that they hear support but the class hears them.
- Students who cannot read yet: *"Just show us your picture and tell us what's happening in it."* This is equal participation.

As each page is read, make a brief appreciative remark — not performative, but real:

- *"You drew the lotus flowers. Look at all the colours."*
- *"You chose the wise man Asita. That was the moment that surprised me too."*
- *"Queen Maya — look how tall she is in your picture."*

Keep the book moving — the reading should take 15–18 minutes for the class. If the class is very engaged and each sharing runs long, let it — this is the culminating event. If energy dips mid-way, pick up your own pace slightly.

When the book has made the full circle, hold it up.

*"Look what we made. Fourteen pages. One story. Every one of us is in this book."*

Set the book back in the centre of the cloth.

---

#### Flower Offering — 5 minutes

*"Now we're going to offer our flowers."*

Pass the bowl around the circle. Each child takes one flower.

*"Hold it in both hands."*

Wait until every student holds a flower.

*"In a moment, we are going to place our flowers on the cloth — around the book — as an offering. This is our way of saying: the story of the Buddha's birth matters to us. When you place your flower, you don't need to say anything. Just place it carefully."*

Begin with yourself — walk to the cloth and place your flower first. Then, one by one, students come forward and place theirs. Do not rush. Allow the accumulation — flowers building around the book — to be slow and beautiful.

When all flowers are placed, stand or sit together and look at what you've made.

One full, shared breath.

---

#### Debrief — 3–5 minutes

*"The Buddha was born in a garden. We've just made a little garden here, haven't we?"*

Pause.

*"Here is a question I want to leave with you. You don't need to answer it right now — you can think about it for days, for weeks. Maybe even years."*

(If you have a printed card, hold it up.)

*"The Buddha was born a baby, just like you. And he grew up to be known for something. He was known for his kindness, his wisdom, his care for every living being. Now: you were a baby once too. What kind person do you want to grow into?"*

Allow 2–3 students to respond if they want to. Do not push. Hold the question open.

*"You don't have to know yet. But it's worth asking."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader comes forward.

**Kyap Dro (Refuge Prayer)** — leader recites, class follows.
**Om Mani Padme Hung** — three rounds together, hands at heart.
**Manjushri Prayer** — leader recites, class follows.

After closing:

*"The book will live in our classroom. You can read it any time. It's ours."*

Place the class book in a visible, accessible location in the classroom — a bookshelf at student height, a classroom library corner, or near the small shrine.
