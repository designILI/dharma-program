# G1.U6.C3 — Jataka: The Hunter and the Quail
## Grade 1 · Unit 6 · Jataka Library Session 3 of 5 · The Hunter and the Quail
*Duration: 40 minutes*
*Note: Standalone rotating session — can be taught at any point in the year.*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A quail outwits a hunter by coordinating her flock — together they lift the net and carry it away, escaping every trap — until one day a quarrel breaks their unity and the hunter catches them → together, even the small can protect each other; but unity only holds when care holds it.
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Narrative structure — the story has a two-part arc: success (unity works) and failure (disunity undoes it). The structural contrast makes the lesson double-sided and memorable. Students feel both the triumph and the loss, which gives the concept of solidarity more texture than a simple happy-ending story would.
**Bloom's Level:** Understand / Apply
**Prior Class Summary:** "This session may occur at any point in the year. Students carry cumulative year experience. The settle→story→discuss→draw ritual structure is familiar."

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the story of the quail flock, the hunter's net, and what happened when the birds worked together — and what happened when they didn't.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that solidarity (sticking together, caring for each other) is a form of protection that small beings can create even when they cannot fight.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** name a time they worked with others toward something none of them could do alone — or a time a group fell apart because of a quarrel.
Students will **FEEL** the strength that comes from togetherness — and carry the memory of that feeling as a resource.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Source: Traditional Jataka tale (Jataka No. 33 / oral tradition); teacher's oral telling from this plan
- Drawing supplies: crayons, colored pencils, paper squares for the wisdom wall
- Optional prop: a piece of loosely woven fabric or a net-like bag (a mesh produce bag works well) — to show students what a hunter's net looks like
- Wisdom wall: space and tape ready for today's drawings

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
The student leader stands at the front. Class settles — feet flat, hands in lap, one breath together.

Student leader leads:
- **Kyap Dro** (Refuge prayer) — recited together
- **Om Mani Padme Hung** — seven times, steady and slow
- **Manjushri Prayer** — recited together

the teacher: "Thank you. One breath — in... and out. Today's Jataka story is about birds — very small birds — and a hunter with a very big net. And it's about what happens when small creatures stand together. And what happens when they don't."

---

#### Story Preparation — 2 minutes
"Quail are birds about this size." *(Hold up a fist.)* "Very small. Very quick. They live on the ground and in low grass, and they travel in flocks — groups. A hunter who caught quail could sell them at the market, so hunters were always setting traps. Today's story is about one hunter, one flock, and one very wise quail. Let's go."

---

#### The Story — 8–10 minutes
*Tell the first half (the successful escapes) with growing energy and delight. Tell the quarrel section with a quiet, somber shift. Let the change in tone carry the lesson.*

---

Long ago, near the edge of a great forest, a hunter made his living trapping quail. He was skilled — he knew where the birds fed, and he would scatter grain on the ground and hide his net in the grass nearby. When the quail came to eat and were all gathered together, he would pull the net over them, trap them, and take them to market.

He did this day after day, and for a long time, he was very successful.

But then something changed. Every time he spread his net, the quail would get caught — and then, somehow, they would escape. He couldn't understand it.

Here is what was happening.

In this particular flock of quail, there was a leader. A small, brown quail with sharp eyes and a calm mind — the bodhisattva in the form of a bird. When the flock was first caught in the net, this quail leader said: "Listen. When we are all caught together, every one of us must lift at the same moment. All together, we can lift the net. Then we carry it to a thorn bush, hang it there, and fly free."

The other quail were doubtful. "We are so small. The net is so heavy."

"Together," said the leader. "On my signal."

They tried it. And it worked. Every quail beat its wings together, the net rose from the ground, they carried it to a thorn bush, set it down, slipped through the gaps, and flew away.

The hunter came back to find his net caught in a thornbush — and no quail.

He set his net again the next day. The quail were caught again. Again they lifted together. Again they escaped.

This went on for many days. The hunter returned home every night empty-handed. His wife began to complain: "Why can't you catch any quail? Are you losing your skill?"

"No," he said. "These quail have learned something. But it won't last. Sooner or later, they will quarrel. And when they quarrel, they won't be able to lift. Wait."

He was patient. He kept setting his net.

And sure enough, one morning, a quail accidentally scratched another while they were feeding. The scratched quail turned and snapped: "Watch where you're putting your feet!" The other quail snapped back: "Why should I? You were standing in my way!"

A small quarrel. The kind that happens in every flock, in every family, in every classroom.

But that day, when the hunter's net fell over them, and the leader called "Lift! All together — lift!" — some of the quail were still angry. "I'm not flying next to *her*," muttered one. "I won't lift on *his* signal," muttered another.

They flapped — but not together. This one pulled left. That one pulled right. This one lifted a moment late.

The net did not rise.

The hunter came and gathered his net full of quail.

The leader, watching from outside the net — she had already flown free before it fell — sat on a branch and watched with grief. Not anger. Not "I told you so." Grief. Because she understood: it was not the hunter's skill that had caught them. It was the small, ordinary quarrel that had.

---

#### The Discussion — 8 minutes
the teacher: "Stay there for a moment."

*(Brief silence.)*

"What is the first thing that comes into your mind from that story?"

*(Take 3–4 responses without comment.)*

"The hunter said something interesting. He said: 'Sooner or later, they will quarrel.' He was very sure about that. Why do you think he was so sure?"

*(Guide toward: quarrels happen. They are normal. The hunter wasn't evil — he was just patient, waiting for what always happens eventually.)*

"Was the quail who scratched the other one bad? Was the one who got angry bad?"

*(Important: no. They were ordinary. The lesson is not about blaming anyone. The lesson is about what ordinary quarrels can do if they happen at the wrong moment.)*

"So what protected the quail — what was their power against the hunter? What was it made of?"

*(Guide toward: agreement. Caring about each other enough to lift at the same time. Not strength — coordination, unity, togetherness.)*

"What broke that protection?"

*(The quarrel — but specifically: the choice to stay angry right at the moment when they needed each other.)*

**Vocabulary Moment:** Arrived at through discussion — "You've been describing something that has a name. When people — or birds — choose to stay together and act as one, that's called *solidarity*. Solidarity is choosing each other, even when you're a little bit annoyed, because you know you need each other. Solidarity is the quail's net — it's what they had instead of claws or size or speed."

**Body Moment:** "Stand up. We're going to try the lift. Every person puts both hands out, palms up — like you're holding the edge of a net. Now: on the count of three, everyone lifts at exactly the same time. One... two... three — LIFT." *(Arms go up together.)* "Now let's try it out of sync — some people go early, some go late." *(Let it be messy and uncoordinated.)* "See the difference? Same bodies. Same strength. But timing changes everything. Sit down."

**Emotional Hook:** Don't explain — just ask: "When the leader sat on the branch and watched the hunter carry away her flock — what do you think was in her chest? What did that feel like?" *(Let students name it: sad, guilty, helpless, heartbroken. Receive all of it.)* "That kind of sadness — when something breaks that didn't have to break — that is the feeling this story is about."

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
Assign fixed pairs.

Prompt — read slowly, pause before they start talking:

*"Tell your partner about a time you did something together with other people that you couldn't have done alone. Or — tell them about a time a group you were part of fell apart because of a fight or a misunderstanding. What happened? What did it feel like?"*

One person talks, one listens, then switch.

What to listen for: students describing real group experiences — a game, a project, a family task, a moment on the playground. When they stop talking about the quail and start talking about themselves, the session is working.

Bring the class back. Invite one or two pairs to share.

---

#### Adding to the Wisdom Wall — 8–10 minutes
"Now we add to our wisdom wall. Today you'll draw one moment from the quail's story that stayed with you. It might be the moment all the birds lifted the net and flew free. It might be the quarrel. It might be the leader sitting on the branch at the end."

"Choose the moment that means most to you."

Distribute paper squares and supplies. Students draw in quiet or with soft conversation. the teacher circulates: "What moment did you choose? What's happening in your picture?"

After 6–7 minutes, students bring drawings to the wisdom wall under the label: **"The Hunter and the Quail — Solidarity."**

---

#### Debrief — 3 minutes
Gather at the wisdom wall.

"One last question. If you could ask the quail leader — sitting on that branch, watching — one question, what would you ask?"

*(Take 2–3 responses. Examples: "Did you feel like it was your fault?" / "What would you do differently?" / "Did the flock stay together after that?" Receive each one.)*

the teacher — one closing sentence: "The quail's protection wasn't something the hunter could take away — it was something they could only lose themselves, from the inside. That's what makes solidarity so precious and so fragile at once."

---

#### Closing — 2 minutes
Student leader returns to the front.

- **Kyap Dro** — together
- **Om Mani Padme Hung** — seven times
- **Manjushri Prayer** — together

the teacher connects the story to the mantra: "When we say Om Mani Padme Hung together — at the same time, same words — we are practicing exactly what the quail learned. Many voices, one sound. That is solidarity in the form of prayer."
