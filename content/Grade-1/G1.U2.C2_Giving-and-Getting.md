# G1.U2.C2 — Giving and Getting
## Grade 1 · Unit 2 · Class 2 of 4 · Giving and Getting
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Something kind you did that nobody thanked you for → giving without expecting anything back

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Elaborative interrogation — asking "why?" and "how did that feel?" about familiar experiences forces children to construct understanding rather than receive it. Today, rather than being told that giving without reward is meaningful, students locate that meaning in their own memories. The concept arrives through their own language, from the inside out.

**Bloom's Level:** Understand. Students move from remembering the story (C1) to interpreting the idea the story carries. They are being asked to recognise something about giving in their own lives — not to define it, but to notice it, name it, and begin to hold it as an idea they own.

**Prior Class Summary:** In C1, students heard *The Giving Tree* read aloud all the way through and sat in silence at the ending. Each child drew one person who gives to them — a "giving tree" in their life — and took the drawing home. They carry the story and a felt sense of what it means to be given to generously and without condition.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** that the tree in the story gave without asking for anything in return, and that this is what made the giving feel different from an exchange.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that giving something — time, help, kindness — without wanting a reward is a different kind of giving, and that it can feel good even when no one says thank you.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** name one time they gave something (help, kindness, an action) and describe how it felt — whether or not they were thanked.

Students will **FEEL** a quiet pride in their own capacity to give, and curiosity about why giving can feel good even when it costs something.

---

### Materials Checklist

- *The Giving Tree* by Shel Silverstein — 1 copy, teacher's. You will NOT re-read the whole book. You will read one or two pages as anchors. Know which pages: (1) the page where the tree says *"I am too old and sad to play"* — no, return to simpler anchor — use the page where the boy takes the apples and sells them, and the line *"And the tree was happy. But not really."* (2) The final page with the stump and the old man, and the last two lines. Bookmark both.
- A simple talking object for the discussion circle — a small smooth stone, a pinecone, or a wooden object. Whatever is natural to the context. Whoever holds it speaks; everyone else listens. Introduce this today if it isn't already established.
- No art materials today — this class is conversation and reflection.
- Room setup: full circle on the floor or in chairs arranged in a circle. Teacher sits in the circle as a participant, not at the front.
- Optional: write the question *"Has someone ever given you something without being asked?"* on a small card or the board as a visual anchor for the discussion.

**Advance prep:** Think through your own answer to the activation prompt. You may share it briefly. Think of a time you did something kind and weren't thanked. Know what you'll say in one or two sentences — children need to see that the teacher is also inside this question, not above it.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Student leader (rotate from C1) comes to the front. Teacher chants along, present and unhurried — not managing, not watching the clock. Just in it.

**Kyap Dro (Refuge Prayer)** — together. Let the room settle fully before beginning.

**Om Mani Padme Hung** — seven rounds. If the room is restless today, do ten. Let the chanting do the work of gathering.

**Manjushri Prayer** — together. Teacher closes eyes at the end, holds one breath of quiet.

Student leader returns to their spot. Teacher waits a beat — two seconds — before speaking.

*"Before we go anywhere today — who still has their drawing from last class? Who remembers who they drew?"*

Brief show of hands or quick go-around. Don't dwell. This is just a thread back to C1. Then:

*"Today we're going to think about the giving part. Not the receiving — the giving."*

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Move directly from the opening into the activation. No preamble.

Prompt (exact words): *"I want to ask you something. Has anyone ever done something kind — helped someone, shared something, done a job that needed doing — and nobody said thank you? Nobody even noticed? What happened? How did it feel?"*

Give the room silence. This question reaches into a slightly vulnerable place — the unacknowledged effort. Wait 8–10 seconds. If nothing comes:

**Deepening prompt:** *"Or — has anyone ever held a door, or picked something up that fell, or helped someone who was sad — and they just walked away without saying anything? What did that feel like inside?"*

**Strong response example:** A child says: *"I cleaned up the classroom and the teacher didn't see."* — Receive this: *"You did it anyway. You cleaned it up even though no one was going to know."* Pause. *"How did that feel?"* Whatever the child says — complicated, a bit sad, okay, fine — receive it fully. Don't correct or redirect toward the "right" feeling.

**Surface response + deepening:** If a child says *"I felt bad"* — *"Yes. What kind of bad? Like you were punished? Or more like... it didn't feel complete?"* You're helping them notice the texture of the feeling, not change it.

**Teacher's own example (brief, if the room needs a model):** *"I remember once I stayed late to prepare something special for a class. No one really noticed. I felt — I'm not sure. A little invisible. But I also felt something else. I did it because I wanted to. That part still felt okay."* Don't over-explain. Plant it and move.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Open the book. Say: *"Remember when we read this? I want to go back to two places."*

**First anchor — any page where the tree gives and then says "and the tree was happy":** Read that line. Then look up: *"Every time the boy takes something — apples, branches, the trunk — the tree says it was happy. Why do you think it kept saying that?"*

Let 2–3 children respond. You're not looking for the "right" answer. You're inviting them to sit with a puzzle: the tree keeps losing things and keeps being happy. That's strange. Let it be strange.

**Second anchor — final page, the stump and the old man:** Read the last two lines. Close the book. *"It says 'the tree was happy.' But then it says 'but not really.' What was different at the end?"*

Again, 2–3 responses. Listen for: the tree had nothing left to give. Or: the boy was too old to play. Or: something was missing. Receive all of these. Then:

*"I wonder — was the tree happy when it gave things? And was that different from this last giving? What do you think the difference was?"*

You're circling toward the idea that there is a kind of giving that comes from fullness and love, and a kind that comes from being used up. Don't land this as a lesson. Let it hover.

**Vocabulary Moment:** The word *expect*. Use it in context: *"When you do something kind and wait to see if someone will say thank you — that's called expecting something back. The tree — did it expect anything back?"* Pause. *"What would 'not expecting anything' feel like?"* You're not defining the word; you're using it where it lives.

**Body Moment:** Ask children to cross their arms over their chest — a self-hug. *"This is what keeping something for yourself feels like — holding it in."* Then ask them to open their arms wide, palms out. *"And this is what giving looks like — opening up."* Return to the crossed arms: *"Which one do you think the tree was doing?"* Children will immediately say "open." *"Even at the very end, when it had almost nothing — what do you think its arms were doing?"* Let them feel the answer in their bodies before they say it.

**Emotional Hook:** You are working with the feeling of *unrecognised effort* — something almost every 6-year-old has felt, often recently. This is a safe and generative emotion to be with in this context. It opens rather than closes. Hold it without rushing to resolve it. The resolution — that giving can feel whole even without recognition — should arrive from the children themselves, not from you.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Prompt (exact words): *"Turn to your partner. Each of you tell one time you gave something — helped, shared, did something kind — and what it felt like after. Even if nobody thanked you."*

Fixed pairs — same as C1. Two to three minutes. Move through the room listening.

Reconvene with the talking object. Pass it to one child: *"What did your partner say? Tell me one thing they shared — not yours, theirs."* This shifts from self-report to listening-report: children must have actually listened to their partner. Do this with two or three pairs. Brief; don't over-process.

**What to listen for:** Specificity. *"I helped my brother"* is a beginning. *"I helped my brother find his shoe when he was going to be late"* is specific and alive. Gently press for specificity when you hear the general. Also listen for children who frame giving as a transaction — *"I helped and then he helped me back"* — not to correct this, but to note it: today's question is about the giving that goes one way.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

**"The Tree's Side of the Story" — paired role-play and reflection (12 minutes)**

This is a light, playful body activity — not performance, not theatre. No audience. Every pair does it simultaneously.

**Step 1 — Set up (2 minutes):** One child in each pair is "the tree." The other is "the boy" (gender is irrelevant to the story role — all children can play any character). Teacher demonstrates with one willing child:

*"The boy is going to ask the tree for something — apples, branches, a boat, a quiet place to sit. The tree is going to give it. Trees don't talk in the story, so the tree uses only their hands and face — no words. The boy says: 'I need something.' The tree gives it. That's it."*

Do one round as demo. Keep it simple and warm. Laugh if it's funny — it will be.

**Step 2 — Play (5 minutes):** Pairs play three or four rounds. Boys (or "askers") name something they need. Trees give it silently with their hands. Teacher moves through, crouching, watching. Don't direct. Only intervene if children are stuck or if it's become rough.

After a few rounds, say: *"Switch."* Now the other partner is the tree.

**Step 3 — Sit and reflect (5 minutes):** Everyone sits. Teacher asks:

*"What did it feel like to be the tree? When you were the one giving — what happened in your body?"*

Pass the talking object. Three or four children share. Don't rush this — this is the moment the body experience converts into language.

Then: *"What did it feel like to be the one asking and receiving?"*

You're not steering toward a conclusion. You're simply letting both experiences exist and be named.

**Scaffold for a stuck child (during role-play):** If a child as "the tree" freezes and doesn't know what to give, crouch next to them and whisper: *"What does a tree have? Shade? A branch to sit on? Its roots to lean against?"* Physical, concrete options unlock the body faster than abstract prompting.

**Extension for an early finisher:** *"On a piece of scrap paper — just for yourself — write or draw one word that describes what the tree felt like inside when it was giving. Not a sentence. Just one word."*

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

Return to the full circle with the talking object.

Opening question (exact words): *"Is there a difference between giving something because you want to, and giving something because you have to or because you want something back?"*

Let two or three children respond. You are listening for the distinction to emerge from them — even imperfectly phrased. If a child says *"It feels different"* — *"Say more. What feels different?"*

**The "so what" moment — say something like this:**

*"The tree never sent the boy a letter saying 'you forgot to say thank you.' It never stopped giving. It just kept giving — not because it would get something back, but because giving was what made it feel like itself. I think that's a kind of love. Not a love that keeps score. A love that just keeps going."*

Pause. Don't expand. Let it sit.

Then, quieter: *"Does anyone have that kind of person in their life? Someone who just keeps giving — who never seems to keep score?"*

If children name people — and they will — receive each name with simple warmth. *"Yes. That's a giving tree."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader comes forward.

**Kyap Dro** — together.

**Om Mani Padme Hung** — seven rounds.

**Manjushri Prayer** — together.

Teacher's closing sentence, specific to today:

*"Next class we are going to make something. We are going to make leaves — leaves that hold the names of all the people who give to us. Start thinking about your list."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward from C1: did every student take their drawing home? Confirm this at the opening — briefly, not as a check. If drawings are missing, it's fine; the drawing isn't needed for today. What today needs is the story. The two anchor pages you will read are enough to return students to the story's emotional register, so trust the structure. Do not re-read the whole book.

The role-play in this class is the lesson, not an activity that accompanies the lesson. Six-year-olds understand with their bodies before they understand with language. A child who plays the tree role and discovers they feel pleasure — not emptiness — in giving has learned what no explanation could reach. The activity is designed to produce this discovery from the inside out. If role-play feels unfamiliar in your classroom, the body moment (crossing arms versus opening arms wide) can serve as a more contained version of the same insight — but lean toward the full role-play if the group can hold it. Simultaneous, unpresented pair-play is less socially risky than performance for an audience; make sure that is how you run it.

This lesson draws the distinction between transactional and unconditional giving for the first time in the curriculum. The distinction works with the feeling of unrecognised effort — something every child has felt recently. This emotional territory is generative, not damaging; it opens rather than closes. Resist the urge to resolve it toward a tidy conclusion before students arrive there themselves. The debrief's "so what" moment should feel like the children landing on something they discovered, not something they were told. The same distinction is revisited six years later in G7.U1, where students wrestle with the full cost of the tree's unconditional giving — what the boy loses by never giving back. That depth is not available yet; for now, hold the idea lightly.

Watch for students who frame all giving as transaction — "I helped him and then he helped me back." They are not wrong; they are at the beginning of a longer journey. Note them for C3's crown-making: they may need more time with the activity before the warmth of gratitude settles into something non-transactional. Also watch for the child who, during the tree role-play, clearly stops performing and just gives — quietly, steadily, with their hands. That child is inside the lesson.
