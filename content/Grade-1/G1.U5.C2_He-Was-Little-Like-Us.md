# G1.U5.C2 — He Was Little Like Us
## Grade 1 · Unit 5 · Class 2 of 4 · He Was Little Like Us
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Baby photographs and first memories → Every great person was once small; we are connected to the Buddha through our shared beginning.

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Self-Referential Encoding — the brain retains information significantly better when it is linked to the self. Here, the Buddha's babyhood becomes real not through repetition of facts but by connecting it to each child's own experience of being small. "He was a baby just like you were" is not a sentimental aside — it is the primary cognitive move of this lesson.

**Bloom's Level:** Remember / Understand

**Prior Class Summary:** In C1, students heard the full birth story of Siddhartha — the Lumbini garden, Queen Maya, the white elephant dream, the seven steps, the lotus flowers, and Asita's prophecy. They drew their own "garden maps" of the scene. Many went home with the question: "What did someone hope for you when you were born?" C2 now turns inward: we use their own babyhood to make the Buddha's beginnings feel real and close.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** that the Buddha had a childhood — that before he became a great teacher, he was a baby, then a small child, then a young person.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that great kindness and wisdom do not appear fully formed — they grow, the way every person grows from a helpless baby.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** name one way the Buddha's early life was like their own, and one thing they hope to grow into.

Students will **FEEL** a sense of genuine closeness to the Buddha — not distance or formality, but a warm, surprised recognition: *he was like me*.

---

### Materials Checklist

- [ ] A baby photograph of the teacher (if willing to share — this is optional but powerful; even a photograph from a magazine of a baby works as a stand-in, framed with: "this is what all babies look like")
- [ ] Several baby photographs brought in by students who were given advance notice (the parent connection from C1; note: some children may not have photographs — have alternates ready, see below)
- [ ] The same illustrated birth-story book from C1 (open to the image of baby Siddhartha, if one exists)
- [ ] Chart paper or whiteboard with two columns drawn: **"Baby Siddhartha"** | **"Baby [child's name]"**
- [ ] Sticky notes (small) and pencils/crayons — one per student
- [ ] Settle bell or singing bowl
- [ ] Alternates for students without baby photos: a piece of paper where they draw themselves as a baby, OR a photo of any animal baby (each child has a "baby-self")

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Students come in and settle. If any brought baby photographs, they hold them in their laps (not passing around yet — anticipation is useful).

One designated student leader comes to the front.

*"Let's begin. [Student name], would you lead us?"*

**Kyap Dro (Refuge Prayer)** — leader recites, class follows.
**Om Mani Padme Hung** — three rounds together, hands at heart.
**Manjushri Prayer** — leader recites, class follows.

Pause briefly after closing. Let the room be quiet for one breath.

*"Thank you. Sit down softly."*

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

*"Before we go on — last time we heard a big story. Let me see what you remember. I'll start it, you keep going."*

Use a retrieval chain — one child adds one detail, the next child adds the next:

*"There was a queen. Her name was…?"* → [Queen Maya]
*"And she had a dream. What was in the dream?"* → [a white elephant]
*"Where was the baby born?"* → [Lumbini garden]
*"And what happened when he took his first steps?"* → [lotus flowers bloomed]
*"And the wise man who came to see him — he cried. What did he say?"* → [the baby would be a great king or a great teacher]
*"And the baby's name was…?"* → [Siddhartha]

Keep it brisk and warm — this is not a quiz, it's a retrieval game. Affirm every response. If a student gets something wrong, say *"Almost — let me give you a hint"* and nudge rather than correct flatly.

If students seem shaky on details: *"That's okay — the story is long. Today we're going to think about one part of it differently: the baby part."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Open the book to the baby Siddhartha image if it exists. Or hold up the picture of a baby generally.

*"Look at this. Baby Siddhartha. What do you notice about him?"*

Take 4–5 responses. Students will say things like "he's small" or "he looks happy." Accept all.

*"He's small. He can't walk yet — well, actually the story says he took seven steps, but that was special magic. Most of the time, he was just a baby. He needed someone to feed him. Someone to hold him. Someone to keep him warm at night."*

Now — if you brought your own baby photo — hold it up.

*"Here is a picture of me when I was very small."*

Allow the room to react. Students love this. Let them be delighted for a moment.

*"I could not walk. I could not talk. I didn't know anything about Buddhism or Dharma or teaching. I was just a baby."*

Then:

*"Now — who brought a picture of themselves as a baby?"*

Invite those students to hold up their photos briefly. Warm, appreciative responses from the class — keep it gentle, not performative.

For students who didn't bring a photo: *"That's okay — not everyone has photos at home. But you were still a baby. Everyone was. Close your eyes for a moment and try to imagine yourself — very, very small. Smaller than you are now. You had to be carried everywhere."*

**Vocabulary Moment:** Write *infancy* on the board — or simply *baby time*. *"Every person has a time when they were completely new to the world. Even the Buddha. We call it infancy — baby time. The Buddha had one too."*

Now draw the two-column chart:

**Baby Siddhartha** | **Baby you**

*"Let's think together. What do we know about when Siddhartha was a baby? What was true for him?"*

Fill the left column together: needed care, had parents, was born somewhere, couldn't speak, was small, grew slowly. Let students suggest; add a few yourself if needed.

*"Now — what's true for baby you?"*

Fill the right column. It will look remarkably similar. Let the students notice this.

*"Look at these two columns. What do you see?"*

**Emotional Hook:** The moment students themselves say "they're the same" or "we were the same" is the key emotional moment. Do not pre-empt it. Ask the question and wait. If they don't get there, gesture between the columns slowly: *"They look… similar. Don't they?"* Conditions for recognition: slow pace, the chart visible, genuine curiosity in your voice — not a leading question. Students who feel surprised by their own insight hold onto it.

**Body Moment:** *"Everyone, hold up your hands. Look at them. These were once very, very tiny. Baby hands. Can you make them small — scrunch them into tiny fists, like a newborn? That's how small they were."*

Students scrunch their fists. Pause.

*"The Buddha had tiny hands like that too."*

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

*"Turn and face your partner. Here is the question:"*

*"When you were a baby — before you could talk, before you could walk — someone took care of you. Who was it? And what do you think they did to take care of you?"*

Fixed pairs. 2–3 minutes. Circulate and listen warmly.

**What to listen for:** Children who describe a specific act of care — "my amala cooked for me" or "my dad carried me on his back." These are the most important responses. Also listen for children who are uncertain — "I don't know, maybe my grandma?" — because not every child has a stable family picture. Receive uncertainty with the same warmth: *"That makes sense — someone did take care of you. Someone always does for babies."*

Bring back together. Invite 2–3 sharings.

*"That's exactly what someone did for baby Siddhartha, too. Queen Maya held him. His father the king watched over him. People cared for him. And from all that care — he grew."*

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

**Activity: "When I Was a Baby" Sticky Note + Drawing**

Give each student one sticky note and a small piece of paper.

Step 1 — Sticky note (2 minutes): *"On your sticky note, write or draw one thing that is true about baby you — something you know or something your family told you. Maybe your weight when you were born, or something you liked, or who held you first. If you don't know, you can draw: 'Someone held me.'"*

Step 2 — Drawing (10 minutes): *"Now on your paper, draw two things side by side: on the left, baby Siddhartha in the Lumbini garden. On the right, baby you — wherever you were born, with whoever was there."*

Prompt as they work:
- *"Who is in your picture on the right?"*
- *"Does your baby-self look happy? Surprised? Asleep?"*
- *"What is the same about the two pictures?"*

Move around the room. Engage every student. The conversations are informal oral rehearsal for the culminating question.

**Scaffold:** For students who don't know anything about their infancy, reframe: *"That's okay — draw what you imagine. You were somewhere warm. Someone was near."*

**Extension:** *"Can you write a sentence under each picture? 'When the Buddha was born…' and 'When I was born…'"*

At the end of work time, invite 3–4 students to share what they drew. Ask them: *"What's the same in your two pictures?"*

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

Gather the class.

*"I want to ask you one question before we close. If you could grow up to be known for one thing — the way the Buddha is known for his kindness and his teaching — what would you want it to be?"*

Take 4–5 responses. Do not evaluate or rank them. Each one is received warmly.

*"These things you said — kind, brave, helpful, good — these are things that can grow. They don't appear fully formed. They grow, slowly, from when you are a baby. That's what today's lesson is about. Even the Buddha started from zero."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader comes back to the front.

**Kyap Dro (Refuge Prayer)** — leader recites, class follows.
**Om Mani Padme Hung** — three rounds together.
**Manjushri Prayer** — leader recites, class follows.

*"Today you found something important: the Buddha was a baby just like you. Next time, you're going to make a page for our class book — your own picture of 'When the Buddha was born.' Keep your drawings from today safe."*

Collect work or store in portfolio folders.
