# G1.U3.C2 — The Precious Teacher
## Grade 1 · Unit 3 · Class 2 of 4 · The Precious Teacher
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A person with a name and a face → the idea of a lineage holder who protects a whole tradition

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Elaborative interrogation — building meaning by asking "who" and "why" rather than "what." Students already hold the birth story. Now they attach significance to the character: who is this person to us? This moves content from episodic memory (the story) to semantic memory (the concept of a precious teacher).

**Bloom's Level:** Remember / Understand. Students retrieve the story from last class (Remember) and begin constructing understanding of who Guru Rinpoche is — why he matters, what relationship Himalayan Buddhists have with him (Understand). They are not yet evaluating or applying; they are building a framework of meaning.

**Prior Class Summary:** Last class students heard the birth story — Padmasambhava appearing on a lotus in Lake Dhanakosha, already wise, already compassionate. They made a listening sketch and heard the two names: Padmasambhava and Guru Rinpoche. They carry a sense of wonder and a mental image of the lotus with a radiant child.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** that Guru Rinpoche (Padmasambhava) is considered one of the most important teachers of the Himalayan Buddhist tradition, that he is connected to the school and to the teacher's own lineage, and that he brought the Dharma to Tibet.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that a teacher can matter not just to one person but to a whole tradition — like a lamp that lights other lamps — and that Guru Rinpoche's influence is still felt today.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** say at least one reason why Guru Rinpoche matters ("He brought the teachings to Tibet," "He is our protector," "Our school is connected to him") in response to the question "Why do we care about Guru Rinpoche?"

Students will **FEEL** a sense of personal connection — that Guru Rinpoche is not a distant historical figure but someone related to their own school, their own teacher, their own tradition. The emotional register: recognition, closeness, a small but real sense of belonging to something larger.

---

### Materials Checklist
- The illustrated Guru Rinpoche biography (same copy as C1, bookmarked now to any image showing Guru Rinpoche in a teaching posture or surrounded by students/disciples — distinct from the birth scene)
- A second image: any thangka print or illustrated image of Guru Rinpoche that can be posted at the front of the room. Ideally one that shows his face clearly — his expression (peaceful, slightly wrathful, radiant — depending on the image style) is useful for discussion.
- One image of the school itself if available (photograph on the wall, or a simple hand-drawn map showing "our school" and "Tibet" — not required but useful for the lineage moment)
- Whiteboard or chart paper and marker — to draw a simple "lamp lighting lamp" diagram during the mini lesson
- Students' listening sketches from C1 (returned at the start of activity)
- Standard prayer card

*Advance prep: Think carefully about what you want to say about your own relationship to Guru Rinpoche. One personal sentence — "In my training, I learned to recite his prayer every morning" or "When I am unsure, I think of Guru Rinpoche" — is worth more than five sentences of biography. Have it ready.*

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Children settle in the semicircle. The thangka or illustrated image of Guru Rinpoche is posted at the front — visible throughout the class.

Student leader of the day comes forward.

**Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer.** Together. Unhurried. Teacher joins fully — not supervising, but practicing.

Student leader returns to place. Teacher takes one breath.

*"Today we're going to keep learning about the person in that picture. We met him last week. Do you remember his two names?"*

Pause — invite a hand or two before moving on. This is a gentle warm-up, not a quiz.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

*"Let's start with what you remember from last week. Close your eyes for a moment — just ten seconds. Picture the lake. Picture the lotus. Picture the child. What do you see?"*

Ten seconds of silence. Then: *"Open your eyes. Who would like to tell me one thing they remember?"*

Take three to four responses. Accept everything — the lake, the mud, the light, the name. Write the two names on the board as a child says them: PADMASAMBHAVA and GURU RINPOCHE.

Strong response: *"He was born on a lotus and he was already wise."* → *"Yes. And when the king asked him 'Who are your parents?' — what did he say? Does anyone remember?"* (The child in the story said: "My father is wisdom, my mother is compassion.") If no one remembers, teacher supplies it gently.

*"So we know the story of how he came into the world. Today's question is different. Today we ask: why do we care? Who is he to us?"*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

the teacher sits. This section is a conversation, not a lecture — but the teacher leads it with intention.

**Part 1: What Guru Rinpoche did**

*"After he grew up — remember, the king raised him — Guru Rinpoche became a great teacher. He learned everything. He mastered all the wisdom teachings. And then, a king in Tibet — very far away — invited him to come.*

*Tibet was a difficult place. There were forces working against the Dharma — against the teachings of the Buddha. The king needed help. So Guru Rinpoche came.*

*And wherever he went, he tamed the wild forces. He taught the Dharma. He blessed the land. He hid special teachings in rocks and lakes — called terma — for future teachers to discover when people needed them most.*

*Because of Guru Rinpoche, the Dharma took root in Tibet. Because of that, our tradition survived. And because our tradition survived — we are sitting here today."*

Pause. Let that chain settle.

**Part 2: The lamp that lights other lamps**

On the board, draw a simple candle. Then draw a second candle being lit from it. Then a third. Then a fourth.

*"When a lamp lights another lamp — does the first lamp lose its light? No. The first lamp still burns just as brightly. And now there are two lamps. Then three. Then many.*

*A teacher is like that. Guru Rinpoche's wisdom lit the lamp of his students. His students lit their students. And so on, and so on, for more than a thousand years — all the way to the teachers who taught me, and to me, and I am here with you."*

Draw a final candle labeled "us" at the end of the chain.

*"That is what we mean when we say lineage. It means: the light kept being passed on."*

**Part 3: Personal connection (teacher's own)**

*"I want to tell you something personal. In my own training, [the teacher inserts their own one or two sentences here — for example: "Every morning I recite Guru Rinpoche's prayer. When I feel confused or when something is difficult, his prayer steadies me." Or: "When I was young, my own teacher taught me to think of Guru Rinpoche as a very great friend and protector — not far away, but close."] I am telling you this because Guru Rinpoche is not only history. He is still here for us, in the prayers, in the lineage, in this school."*

**Vocabulary Moment:** *"The word for Guru Rinpoche in our tradition — one of the names people use — is 'Precious Teacher.' Rinpoche means precious. Like a jewel. Something rare and valuable and irreplaceable. That is what he is. Can everyone say Rinpoche?"* (Class repeats.) *"It is also a title we give to great living teachers. But for now — Guru Rinpoche, the Precious Teacher."*

**Body Moment:** *"Let's do something. Sit up straight — like someone who is already a little bit wise. Put your hands on your knees. Now take one breath that is slow and full, like a lamp burning steadily."* Students breathe together once. *"That's the posture of someone paying attention. That's the posture Guru Rinpoche is often shown in."*

**Emotional Hook:** After the lamp-and-lineage moment, before moving on — look at the image at the front of the room. *"Take a look at his face."* Silence for five seconds. Do not describe the face; let each student see it themselves. The act of looking, unhurried, is the hook.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Fixed pairs. Students turn to face their partner.

*"Here is my question — and I really want you to think about it, not just say the first thing. The question is: Have you ever had a teacher — or a person — who made you feel like you could do something you couldn't do before? A teacher, a parent, a big cousin, anyone. Tell your partner about that person."*

Two minutes. Teacher circulates quietly. Listen for the texture of what children say — they will name real people in their lives, and this grounds the concept of "a teacher who matters."

Reconvene: *"I heard some beautiful things. People named their dads, their mums, older siblings, their teachers. That feeling — of someone helping you become more than you were — that is what a great teacher does. Guru Rinpoche did that for a whole tradition. For all of us."*

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

**"Who Is He to Me?" — Adding to the Listening Sketch**

Return students' listening sketches from C1.

*"Look at your picture from last week. You drew the lake, the lotus, maybe the child. Today we're going to add something — a sentence, or a few words, or a picture — that answers this question: 'Who is Guru Rinpoche?'"*

For students who are not yet writing fluently, the teacher scribes their words directly on the page, or students draw a symbol. Options:
- A lamp (lineage)
- A heart (he cares for our tradition)
- The mountains of Tibet (he came to Tibet)
- Their own face next to his (he is connected to me)

*"Write or draw your answer right on the page, anywhere there is space. This is your page — you can decorate it, add colour, make it yours."*

Teacher circulates. Ask individual students: *"What are you adding?"* and *"Why did you choose that?"* — not to assess, but to help them articulate what they know.

*Scaffold for a stuck student:* *"Tell me one thing you remember about him."* When they say something, respond: *"Can you draw that?"* — and stay nearby while they start.

*Extension for an early finisher:* *"Can you write one question you still have about Guru Rinpoche? Something you'd like to know?"* These questions go on a separate slip and are collected by the teacher — they become the starting point for C3 and C4.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

Students bring their pages and sit in the semicircle.

*"Let me ask you: if you had to tell a friend who doesn't know anything about Guru Rinpoche — one sentence, just one — what would you say? Who is he?"*

Take four or five responses. Hear the variety. Then teacher offers one more framing, quietly:

*"There is a Himalayan Buddhist saying — people say that Guru Rinpoche is like a second Buddha for Tibet. Not because he is the same as the Buddha, but because of what he did: he brought the light of the teachings to a place where it was dark. And that light is still burning. Here. In this room."*

Pause. Then: *"In two more classes, you're going to make a drawing of him — of the moment we talked about in our first class. And in the last class of this unit, you'll share it. So keep that image clear in your mind."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader to the front.

**Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer.** Together.

Teacher's closing sentence, specific to today:

*"You now know two things: the story of how Guru Rinpoche came into the world, and why he matters to us. That is a real thing you carry."*

Dismiss per school custom.

---

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward from C1: the listening sketches are returned at the start of the Activity — ensure they are stored and ready. Also carry any questions that emerged in C1's debrief or Turn and Talk ("But how did he get there?" "What are terma?"). These questions are generative; today's lesson answers "Why does he matter to us?" rather than "What else happened in his life?", so hold unanswered biographical questions for C3's debrief and C4's gallery discussion. Do not try to answer everything now.

The lamp-and-lineage diagram is the lesson's visual centre. Draw it live on the board, lamp by lamp, in front of students. Do not present it as a pre-made chart. The act of constructing it in real time — one flame lit from another — models exactly what the concept means: understanding built step by step, from teacher to student. Draw it slowly. When you write "us" at the end of the chain, pause before saying anything. Let students register that the chain ends here, in this room, with them. That pause is the lesson's peak.

The teacher's personal sentence in Part 3 is not optional. Without it, the lineage remains abstract history. With it, it becomes real — present in this room, embodied in the teacher standing in front of them. One genuine sentence about your own relationship to Guru Rinpoche is worth more than five biographical sentences. Prepare it before class, not while you are teaching. This is the conceptual pivot of the unit: from story (C1) to significance (C2). The shift from episodic memory to semantic memory — from "what happened" to "who is this to us" — is deliberate. Do not add more story detail at the expense of the pivot.

This lesson connects backward to G1.U1.C2, where "Mani" pointed toward compassion and students first held the mantra as a wish for all beings. Guru Rinpoche's origin — "my mother is compassion" — lands differently for students who already carry that syllable in their bodies. The lesson also connects forward to G2.U3 (Guru Rinpoche's character deepens) and G5.U3 (the Dharma-to-Tibet story is told in full historical depth). Neither of those is this lesson's job; stay here.

Watch for the child who makes the lamp-lineage connection before you draw it ("So your teacher taught you, and you teach us — and we'll teach someone?"). Give them the word for it: lineage. Watch for the student who collects extension questions about the terma — note their name; they are primed for G5.U3. And watch for a student who says "But is he real?" or "That's just a story." Receive it without argument. Ask: "What would make someone real to you?" — then move on. Revisit privately after class.
