# G1.U4.C4 — The Rescue
## Grade 1 · Unit 4 · Class 4 of 4 · The Rescue
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Carrying a living creature to safety with one's own hands → the experience of ethical action made real; ahimsa enacted, not merely discussed

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Enactment and ritual encoding. When students perform a real, small, ceremonious act — finding an insect, holding it gently, carrying it outside, releasing it — they are not practising ethics. They are doing ethics. The action encodes the value more deeply than any lesson, because the body remembers what it has done. The brief ceremony around the release (a moment of intention, a closing dedication) gives the act structure and significance.

**Bloom's Level:** Apply. This is the most applied class of the unit. Students are not discussing or planning — they are acting. They find a living creature, handle it with care, make a choice to protect it, and release it. The word ahimsa, encountered abstractly in C2, becomes physical in C4.

**Prior Class Summary:** Last class students went outside to observe insects — sitting quietly, drawing what they saw with patience and attention. They returned with observation drawings and, in some cases, a genuinely new relationship with insects: curiosity rather than aversion. They carry the question "what will we do with a real bug next class?" Today is the answer.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** how to safely and gently handle a small insect — scoop rather than grab, let it walk onto a flat surface, carry it to the door, release it in the grass.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that choosing to protect a small creature is a real act of ahimsa — that ethics is not a feeling, it is a choice followed by an action.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** find or receive a live insect, carry it gently and safely to the outdoors, and release it — completing the full arc of the unit from story to concept to action.

Students will **FEEL** the small but real satisfaction of having done something good: not for praise, not for a grade, but because a living thing needed help and they helped it. This is the beginning of intrinsic ethical motivation.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Three to four transparent containers with gentle ventilation (jars with punctured lids, or clear takeaway containers with holes), each containing one small insect — collected by the teacher the morning of class from the schoolyard. Ideal species: ants, small beetles, moths, earwigs — any insect found locally that can be handled without danger. Avoid bees, wasps, or any stinging/biting species.
- Small pieces of leaf or fruit in each container (one tiny piece — for the insect's comfort during the class period)
- Flat cardstock pieces (one per student) — used as a surface to encourage the insect to walk onto for carrying. Cut to approximately A5 size.
- Student observation journals: assemble the two-page journal now if not already done — Bug Thoughts drawing (C2) + Observation Drawing (C3) + a final page (today): one sentence the student writes or dictates about what they did and why. Teacher prepares a simple final journal page: "Today I ___. I did this because ___." Students complete this page during the debrief.
- A pen or pencil per student for the final journal page
- Tape or stapler to assemble the journals at the end of class
- Camera (teacher's phone or school camera) — to photograph the release moment for portfolio documentation
- Standard prayer card

*Advance prep: Collect the insects yourself, with care, before students arrive. Check each container — the insect must be alive, active, and unharmed.*

**Plan B**

1. **If outdoor time is not possible today** (weather, logistics): Stay inside. Teacher brings a single insect in a clear container (prepared in advance). The class observes it together at close range, then releases it from the classroom window or door. The activity becomes a collective observation rather than individual handling.

2. **If no insects can be found at all**: Run a guided visualization instead. Teacher leads: "Close your eyes. You are very, very small — the size of a grain of rice. You are a beetle, sitting on a stone in the morning sun." Walk students through the beetle's full sensory experience for 5 minutes (warmth, texture, smell, sound from below). Then the drawing activity proceeds as planned — but students draw their imagined creature, not an observed one.

3. **Admin/supervision note**: This activity requires students to leave the classroom. Confirm in advance with school leadership that this is permitted and that supervision is adequate for the outdoor space. Do not proceed outdoors without this confirmation.

*Ceremonial note: The closing prayer today is offered as a dedication to the creature(s) released. This is a real Dharma practice — dedicating merit to all beings. Mention this to students simply: "We're going to dedicate our prayers today to all the small creatures, including the one we just released." This is the thread that connects the whole unit — from the parrot (who called the gods to send rain) to the child sitting in this room who releases a beetle into the grass.*

*Note for Unit 5: After this class, students move to Unit 5 — Buddha's Birth. The thread to carry forward: all lives are precious from the beginning. The parrot's life was precious. The bug's life was precious. The baby who became the Buddha — his life was precious from its first moment. This is the connective tissue between units.*

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Students enter to find the insect containers on the teacher's desk, visible. They will notice immediately. Do not address this yet.

Settle in the semicircle. Student leader of the day comes forward.

**Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer.** Together, unhurried. Teacher joins fully.

Student leader returns. Teacher stands.

*"Welcome. Today is the last class of Unit 4. You have learned a lot this unit — about a parrot, about whether bugs feel, about observing carefully. Today you are going to do something. Not learn about something. Do something.*

*Look at the desk."*

Pause. Let the containers register.

*"Each of those has a living creature inside. Today, each of you is going to carry one to safety outside. That's the class."*

One breath. Let it be that simple.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

*"Before we begin — let me ask: what do you remember about ahimsa?"*

Take three to four quick responses. Students will remember the word, the definition, the hand movement from C2.

*"And what did the parrot do — in that story from our first class?"*

Two or three responses: it carried drops of water, it kept going, it saved the birds.

*"Today you are the parrot. Small action. Real creature. Let's see what happens.*

*But first — I need to tell you how to do this safely. Not for you — for the bug. Let me show you."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

**The technique** — modelled by the teacher before students touch anything.

Take one container. Open it slowly, lid tilted away from the insect. Place the flat cardstock piece on the opening.

*"I'm not going to grab it. I never grab. I'm going to let it walk onto this card by itself — or I'm going to very gently slide the card under it. Watch."*

Model this carefully. If the insect walks onto the card on its own: *"See — it chose to step on. That means I haven't frightened it."*

If it doesn't immediately: *"I'll wait. I'm not in a hurry. The bug is not in danger. I just wait."* (Wait. Model patience.) Then gently nudge with a finger tip — not touching the insect, but encouraging it toward the card.

Once the insect is on the card:

*"Now I carry it. Flat. Like I'm carrying a tray. Both hands under the card. I walk slowly. I don't tilt. I don't run. The bug doesn't know it's going somewhere safe — it just knows this is a flat surface and it's moving. So I make the moving as smooth as I can."*

Walk a few steps, demonstrating the carrying posture.

*"When we get outside — I find a patch of grass or soil. I put the card down near the ground. I tilt it very slightly so the bug can step off. And then — I wait. I watch it walk away. I don't celebrate loudly. I just watch it go."*

Demonstrate the release: place the card down, tilt, wait for the imaginary bug to walk away. *"There. Off it goes. That's it. That's the whole thing."*

*"Now. Any questions about the technique? Not about the bug — about how to carry it."*

Answer any technique questions. Keep it brief.

**Vocabulary Moment:** *"What you're about to do has a name in the Dharma tradition. It's called 'life release' — in Tibetan, tshe thar. Buddhist practitioners have been doing this for a very long time. It means: giving life back. You find a creature that is trapped or in danger, and you give it its freedom back. Tshe thar. You are doing that today — a real Dharma practice."*

**Body Moment:** *"Before you take your container — everyone hold your hands out flat, like a tray. Flat. Steady. Still."* Students hold hands out. *"That's the posture. Keep your hands like that. Remember what it feels like to hold something precious."*

**Emotional Hook:** Say nothing. Open one container slightly so the sound of the insect moving inside is just barely audible. Let that sound — tiny, alive, real — do the work.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Brief, before the activity begins.

*"Turn to your partner. Tell them: are you nervous? Excited? Unsure? And what do you think it will feel like when the bug walks onto your card?"*

One minute only. Then: *"Okay. Let's go."*

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

**The Bug Release**

This activity happens in three stages.

**Stage 1: Receiving and preparing (indoors, 3–4 minutes)**

the teacher distributes containers — one per student, or one per pair if insects are limited. Students do not open them yet.

*"Look at your container. Look at your creature. One moment — just look at it."*

Thirty seconds of quiet looking.

*"What do you want to say to it? You don't have to say it out loud — just think it. Or say it very quietly."*

Fifteen seconds.

*"Alright. When you're ready — take your card. Open your container slowly. Let it come to you."*

Teacher moves through the room — one at a time — assisting where a student is unsure. Stay calm. There is no emergency. There is no wrong move as long as the insect is not harmed.

**Stage 2: The procession (moving from classroom to outdoors, 2–3 minutes)**

Students carry their cards and insects in a loose, single-file procession to the schoolyard. Teacher leads, moving slowly.

*"Walk slowly. No rushing. Watch your card."*

The procession itself is the ceremony. Teacher does not speak during it beyond gentle reminders. If a student is moving too fast: a hand on the shoulder, a slower pace modelled.

**Stage 3: The release (outdoors, 5–8 minutes)**

Students find their own spot in the grass or soil — spread out, not crowding.

*"Find your spot. Put your card down near the ground. Tilt it slowly. Wait."*

Teacher circulates. Photograph the release moments if possible (silently, without disrupting the children's attention). This is not a photo opportunity — it is documentation.

As each child's insect walks away, the child watches. Some will be quiet. Some will grin. Some will feel a small sadness. All of these are real and right.

After all insects have been released — or after eight minutes, whichever comes first — gather students in a loose group in the schoolyard.

*"Is everyone's creature gone?"*

Pause. Look at the grass.

*"Good. They're out there now. Going where they need to go."*

Return indoors for the debrief and journal completion.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes (with journal completion woven in)

Students seated in the semicircle. Final journal pages distributed.

*"You just did something. I want you to write one sentence — or draw, if you prefer — that finishes these two sentences:"*

**On the page:** *"Today I ___. I did this because ___."*

Students write or draw. Teacher scribes for students who need it. Three to four minutes.

Then collect all pages. Assemble the journals quickly (Bug Thoughts drawing + Observation Drawing + Final Page) — staple or bind while students watch.

*"Your journal goes into your portfolio. It's a record of this unit: you thought about whether bugs feel, you observed one carefully, and you released one. That's a real thing you did."*

One final question, posed to the whole group:

*"Last question. Transfer prompt — that means: a question you take with you outside this classroom.*

*Next time you see a bug indoors — at home, in your room, anywhere — what might you do, instead of squashing it?"*

Take four or five responses. Let them be specific and real: "carry it to the window," "put a cup over it and take it outside," "let it walk on a piece of paper." These are real, practical acts of ahimsa.

*"Any of those. All of those. Choose the one you can do."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader to the front.

**Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer.** Together.

Before the student leader begins, teacher says:

*"Today we're going to dedicate our prayers to all the small creatures — the ones in our schoolyard, the ones we can't see, the ones we might step on without knowing. Including the one you just released. Can we do that?"*

Student leader leads the prayers. The class prays with that dedication held quietly.

Teacher's closing sentence, specific to today:

*"You gave something its life back today. Next class, we begin a new unit — about another life that was precious from its very first moment."*

*(This last sentence opens the door to Unit 5 — Buddha's Birth — without closing the door of this unit abruptly. The thread: all lives are precious from the beginning. Hold it lightly.)*

Dismiss per school custom.
