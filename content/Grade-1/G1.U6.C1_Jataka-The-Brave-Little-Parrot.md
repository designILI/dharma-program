# G1.U6.C1 — Jataka: The Brave Little Parrot
## Grade 1 · Unit 6 · Jataka Library Session 1 of 5 · The Brave Little Parrot
*Duration: 40 minutes*
*Note: Standalone rotating session — can be taught at any point in the year.*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A small parrot carries water drop by drop through fire and smoke to save trapped birds → patient, persistent caring is real bravery.
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Narrative structure — the story builds tension through repetition (drop after drop, trip after trip) so that students feel the parrot's effort accumulating before the emotional release. The repetitive structure mirrors how persistence itself works: nothing dramatic happens each time, but the total changes everything.
**Bloom's Level:** Understand / Apply
**Prior Class Summary:** "This session may occur at any point in the year. Students carry cumulative year experience. The settle→story→discuss→draw ritual structure is familiar."

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the story of the Brave Little Parrot and what she did in the face of a forest fire.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that bravery is not the absence of fear — it is choosing to keep going, one small action at a time, because others need you.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** name a moment in their own life when they kept trying even though something was hard or scary.
Students will **FEEL** the quiet, determined warmth of someone who does not give up — and recognise that feeling as something they have carried themselves.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Source: Traditional Jataka tale (Jatakamala / oral tradition); teacher's oral telling from this plan
- Drawing supplies: crayons, colored pencils, paper squares for the wisdom wall
- Optional prop: a small cup of water and a feather (or leaf) to dip and hold up — demonstrates how little a drop is
- Wisdom wall: space and tape ready for today's drawings

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
The student leader for today stands at the front. The class settles — feet flat, hands in lap, one slow breath together.

Student leader leads:
- **Kyap Dro** (Refuge prayer) — recited together
- **Om Mani Padme Hung** — seven times, soft and steady
- **Manjushri Prayer** — recited together

the teacher: "Thank you. Let's take one more breath — in slowly... and out. Today we have another Jataka story. These stories come from the Buddha's past lives, when he was still learning. Today's story is about a very small bird who did something very, very brave."

---

#### Story Preparation — 2 minutes
"Before I tell you the story, I want you to think about something. Have you ever tried to do something — carry water, build something, move something heavy — that needed many, many trips? You had to go back and forth, back and forth? Hold that feeling. Now listen."

---

#### The Story — 8–10 minutes
*Tell with a steady, unhurried voice. Let the repetitive sequences slow down and breathe.*

---

Long ago, in a great forest, there lived a small parrot with bright green feathers and quick dark eyes. She had lived in that forest all her life. She knew every tree, every branch, every birdsong. The forest was her home, and every creature in it was her family.

One dry season, lightning struck a dead tree at the edge of the forest. A spark caught. Then another. And soon — fire. It spread fast, jumping from branch to branch, tree to tree. Animals ran. Deer crashed through the undergrowth. Snakes slipped into the earth. Monkeys leaped for the highest canopy.

But not all the birds could fly away in time. Some were too young. Some were injured. Some were simply too frightened and huddled together in a hollow tree, frozen.

The little parrot flew out of the smoke and looked back. She saw the fire coming. She saw her friends trapped. And she felt something rising in her chest — not bravery yet. Fear first. Her wings wanted to keep flying away, far away, until the smoke was far behind her.

But she turned around.

She flew to the river. She dipped her two small wings into the water — just the tips of her feathers — and she flew back toward the fire. She shook her wings over the flames. Drops fell. Tiny drops. Almost nothing.

She flew back to the river.

She dipped her wings.

She flew back to the fire.

She shook her wings. More drops.

Back and forth. Back and forth. The smoke burned her eyes. The heat dried her throat. Her wings grew heavy. Other birds, watching from a safe distance, called out to her: "Stop! You're so small! It's too much fire! Those drops can't help!" 

She heard them. She kept flying.

The gods in the heavens looked down. One of them — some say it was Indra, king of the gods — was watching. He had seen many acts of courage. Soldiers. Warriors. Great beings of power. But this small bird, going back and forth with her tiny drops, her eyes red from smoke, refusing to stop — something about her broke his heart open.

He took the form of an eagle and flew down beside her. "Little parrot," he said, "what are you doing? Your drops are nothing against this fire. You cannot save them."

She looked at him, eyes stinging. "Maybe not," she said. "But they are my family. And I am not done yet."

The eagle — the god — felt something shift inside him. He had been watching from a comfortable distance for too long. He called the rain. 

The clouds gathered. Water fell — not in drops, but in sheets. The fire hissed and shrank and went out. The trapped birds stumbled free into the cool air.

The little parrot landed, exhausted, wings dripping. And she looked at her friends, alive and breathing.

That was enough.

---

#### The Discussion — 8 minutes
the teacher: "Sit quietly for just a moment. Stay in the story."

*(Ten seconds of silence.)*

"Here is my first question: What did the parrot do that you remember most? Not the ending — what moment do you keep seeing?"

*(Take 3–4 responses. Listen for: the back-and-forth trips, the moment she turned around, the moment she talked to the eagle.)*

"The other birds told her to stop. They said her drops couldn't help. Has anyone ever had someone tell you that what you were doing wasn't enough — or wasn't going to work?"

*(Take responses. Validate: "Yes. That's a real feeling.")*

"And yet she kept going. Why do you think she kept going, even when it was hard, even when someone told her to stop?"

*(Surface responses. Students may say: she loved them / she didn't want to give up / she believed / she was brave.)*

"Someone just said brave. Let's look at that word carefully. Was she scared?"

*(Yes — she was scared. The story shows that. Her wings wanted to fly away.)*

"So if she was scared, how was she brave?"

*(Guide toward: bravery isn't not being scared. Bravery is choosing to keep going even when you're scared.)*

**Vocabulary Moment:** Once students have arrived at it through discussion — "You've named it: that word is *perseverance*. It's a big word for something you already know how to do. It means: you keep going, step by step, even when it's hard. The parrot didn't save the forest in one big act. She did it in tiny drops, again and again. That's perseverance."

**Body Moment:** "Stand up for just a second. Put your arms out like wings. Now dip them — like you're dipping into water. Now fly. Dip. Fly. Dip. Fly." *(Three or four rounds, light and playful.)* "That's what she did. Hundreds of times. Sit back down."

**Emotional Hook:** Don't explain this — just let it land. Ask: "When the eagle came down and the rain fell — and the parrot landed, wings dripping, and saw her friends were safe — what do you think she felt?" *(Let students answer. Relief. Happiness. Tiredness. Pride. Let every answer be right.)* "All of that at once. That's what perseverance feels like when it works."

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
Assign fixed pairs (pairs students already know from earlier in the year).

Prompt — read it slowly, give them a moment to think before they start talking:

*"When is a time you kept going, even though something was hard or scary or someone said you couldn't? It doesn't have to be big. Even something small at home or at school. Tell your partner: what was it, and why did you keep going?"*

Tell students to take turns — one person talks, one listens, then switch.

What to listen for: students stop describing the parrot and start describing their own lives. When a student says "I kept practicing even though I kept falling" or "I didn't want to do it but I did it anyway" — that's the transfer. That is the goal.

Bring the class back together. Ask one or two pairs to share what they talked about (not what their partner shared — what they themselves shared).

---

#### Adding to the Wisdom Wall — 8–10 minutes
"Now we add to our story wisdom wall. Today you're going to draw one moment from the parrot's story — the moment that stayed with you most. It could be the parrot dipping her wings. It could be the fire. It could be the moment the rain came. It could be the parrot landing at the end."

"There's no wrong moment to choose. The moment you choose tells us what the story meant to you."

Distribute paper squares and drawing supplies. Students draw in quiet or with soft, low conversation. the teacher circulates, asking quietly: "What moment did you pick?" Not evaluating — witnessing.

After 6–7 minutes, students bring their drawings to the wisdom wall and place them under the label: **"The Brave Little Parrot — Perseverance."**

---

#### Debrief — 3 minutes
Once drawings are on the wall, gather the class.

"One last question. If you could ask the parrot one question — not about what happened, but about how she felt or what she was thinking — what would you ask her?"

*(Take 2–3 responses. Examples students might give: "Were you scared?" / "Did you think it was going to work?" / "How did you keep going?" Receive each one warmly.)*

the teacher — one closing sentence, said simply: "She kept going because her love for her friends was bigger than her fear of the fire. That's what perseverance looks like when it comes from the heart."

---

#### Closing — 2 minutes
Student leader returns to the front.

- **Kyap Dro** — together
- **Om Mani Padme Hung** — seven times
- **Manjushri Prayer** — together

the teacher connects the story to the mantra: "When we say Om Mani Padme Hung, we are calling on the compassion that never gives up — like the parrot's wings, going back again and again. The mantra is our own small drops."
