# G1.U3.C4 — Our Lotus Gallery
## Grade 1 · Unit 3 · Class 4 of 4 · Our Lotus Gallery
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A drawing held up and shared → the meaning of bearing witness to someone else's sacred image

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Retrieval and transfer through presentation. When students present their work and describe it to others, they are performing the highest retrieval task — articulating meaning publicly. The 7-line prayer, heard for the first time as a listening practice, provides a closing sensory experience that re-anchors the whole unit in something beyond words.

**Bloom's Level:** Remember / Understand / Apply. Students retrieve the story and their understanding of it (Remember / Understand), apply it through presenting their artwork (Apply), and the prayer listening at the end is a brief encounter with something beyond Bloom's categories — pure receptive attention.

**Prior Class Summary:** Last class students spent most of the 40 minutes drawing — building the lotus-lake scene in colour on cardstock. They worked quietly. They have unfinished work or nearly-finished work to complete. They carry the particular satisfaction of having made something. They are ready to share it.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the birth story of Guru Rinpoche well enough to describe their drawing to a peer or to the class — using the elements they drew: water, lotus, figure, light.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that describing what you made and why you made it is a form of teaching — that sharing an image carries the story forward.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** hold up their drawing and say at least two sentences about what they drew and what it means — in their own words.

Students will **FEEL** pride in their own work and genuine attention toward their classmates' work. And in the final prayer moment: stillness, perhaps a little awe — the quiet that comes from hearing something beautiful without being asked to understand it.

---

### Materials Checklist
- All student drawings from C3 (stored by teacher; returned at the start of the activity)
- Pencils and coloured pencils for anyone who needs finishing time
- Tape or adhesive for posting drawings on the wall (designate a "gallery wall" in the room before class — clear the space, perhaps drape a piece of fabric or hang a simple border to frame it as special)
- Camera or phone (teacher's) — optional, to photograph the gallery before it comes down; good for portfolio documentation
- The illustrated Guru Rinpoche biography nearby but closed — available for reference during share, not the focus
- Standard prayer card
- Quiet and readiness: this is the culminating class; the room setup should feel a little more considered than usual

*Advance prep: Post a simple sign on the gallery wall — handwritten is fine — saying "Our Lotus Gallery." If you can hang a small thangka or image of Guru Rinpoche above the gallery space, do so. The gallery should be visible when students enter so they see immediately what the class will be about. Do not hang the student drawings yet — they go up during class.*

*Note: The 7-line Guru Rinpoche Prayer is chanted once by the teacher at the close of this class, while students listen with eyes closed. This is a listening practice — students do not memorise or recite it. the teacher should be comfortable chanting it from memory or from a text. Practice once before class. The sound of the chant is the offering; fluency and reverence matter more than volume.*

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Students enter and see the gallery wall — empty, labelled, waiting. Teacher does not comment on it at the start.

Students settle in the semicircle. Student leader of the day comes forward.

**Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer.** Together. Teacher joins fully and with particular care today — this is the culminating class.

Student leader returns. Teacher stands.

*"Today is a special class. You've been working on something for three weeks — the story of Guru Rinpoche, who he is, and a drawing of the moment he came into the world. Today you finish, and today you share."*

One breath. *"Let's begin."*

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

*"Before you get your drawings, I want to ask: if a friend from another class came in right now and asked you — 'Who is Guru Rinpoche, and what happened when he was born?' — what would you say?"*

Take three or four volunteers. Let each student say something. Do not correct or add — just receive. After a few responses:

*"We've come a long way from the first class when I told you the story and you'd never heard it before. Now it's yours. You can tell it."*

Then: *"In a few minutes, you'll each share your drawing with us. Your job is to say: what is in your picture, and what does it mean to you. You don't have to be perfect — just honest."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

This is the finishing time plus a brief framing of the gallery.

Return all drawings. Students go to their workspaces.

*"You have about eight minutes to finish. Look at your drawing. Is there anything missing? Anything you want to add or complete? Do that now."*

Teacher circulates. This is not a teaching moment — it is support time. Sit beside any student who looks uncertain. *"What does it still need?"* is the only useful prompt.

At the six-minute mark: *"Two more minutes."*

At eight minutes: *"Pencils and colours down. Look at your drawing. That's the one you're going to share."*

**Vocabulary Moment** (woven into the transition): *"In a moment we are going to do something called a gallery share. A gallery is a place where art is shown and seen. A gallery share means: we each hold our work up and say a few words about it. Then we all look. Then we listen. You've seen galleries — in temples, in books, on the walls of important places. This is our gallery. It's just as real."*

**Body Moment:** *"Before we begin the gallery, hold your drawing in front of you — not to your face, but out in front so we can see it. Like this."* Teacher demonstrates holding a page out at arm's length, centred. *"Practice that. Good. When it's your turn, that's how you hold it."*

**Emotional Hook:** Do not narrate this. As students hold up their drawings to practice the gesture, look across the room at the drawings. There is real colour and variety and effort there. Let yourself be moved by it — that response will be visible to children and it is the truest form of invitation.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

Fixed pairs. Students turn to their partner, both holding their drawings.

*"Before we share with the whole class — share with your partner. Show them your drawing and tell them two things: what you drew, and one thing you want people to notice. One minute each. Partner A goes first."*

Two to three minutes total. Teacher circulates and listens for what students say — to help quiet students find their words, and to hear anything particularly vivid that might be lifted into the whole-group share.

Reconvene: *"Good. Now we're ready for the gallery."*

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

**The Gallery Share**

Students remain seated in the semicircle with their drawings.

the teacher establishes the format:

*"Here is how we do it. I will call your name. You stand up, hold your drawing so everyone can see it, and say two sentences: 'In my drawing I see ...' and 'One thing I want you to notice is ...' Then we all look — quietly — for five seconds. Then you sit. Then the next person. We go around the whole circle."*

Model once: *"I'll do it first."* Take a piece of paper or sketch and demonstrate: stand, hold it up, say the two sentences, allow the silence, sit.

Then begin with the student leader of the day and continue around the circle.

**Teacher's role during the share:**
- After each student presents: hold the five-second looking silence. Count it internally. Do not fill it.
- Offer one reflective response after every third or fourth student — not a compliment, but an observation: *"I notice that Pema drew the rays of light going all the way to the corners of the page."* or *"Three people chose blue for the water and two chose green — isn't that interesting."*
- For a student who freezes: *"Just tell me — what's the most important thing in your picture?"* Then: *"Say that."*

After all students have shared:

*"Now let's put them on the wall."*

Students come up one by one (or two at a time) and tape their drawing to the gallery wall. Teacher places each drawing with care — not hastily, not haphazardly. Arrange them so they fill the wall evenly.

Step back. Class looks together at the gallery. Ten seconds of silence.

*"That is the birth of Guru Rinpoche — in fourteen different ways. Fourteen minds, fourteen hands, one story."*

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

Students seated back in the semicircle, gallery visible behind.

*"Before we close — one more question. Think about the whole unit. We heard the story. We learned who he is. We drew him. Now we're looking at our drawings together. Is there anything — even small — that you understand now that you didn't understand three classes ago?"*

Take three or four responses. Accept everything. Then:

*"I want to tell you something about what you just did. You brought the image of Guru Rinpoche's birth — a story from a long time ago, from a far away place — into this room, with your own hands. That image now lives in this room for a little while. And it will live in your mind for a long time. That's what pictures do.*

*There is one more thing I want to give you today. A prayer connected to Guru Rinpoche — the 7-line prayer. You're not going to memorise it today. You're just going to listen. Close your eyes, and listen."*

---

#### The 7-Line Prayer — Listening Practice (within the Debrief / Closing transition)

Students close their eyes. Teacher instructs:

*"Sit comfortably. Hands in your lap. Eyes closed. You don't have to understand the words — just listen to the sound. The prayer is asking Guru Rinpoche to be present with us, to bless us, to send his wisdom. Just receive it."*

the teacher chants the 7-line Guru Rinpoche Prayer once, in full, from memory. He chants it as prayer — not as performance, not as demonstration. The room holds this.

After the final line, silence for ten seconds.

*"Open your eyes."*

One breath together.

*"That prayer has been recited for more than a thousand years. You just heard it. It belongs to you now too."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader to the front.

**Kyap Dro, Om Mani Padme Hung, Manjushri Prayer.** Together, with particular care. The class carries a quality of having touched something real.

Teacher's closing sentence, specific to today:

*"Three weeks ago you heard a story. Today you shared what that story became in your hands. Well done. Next class — something completely different. We're going to look very, very small."*

*(This last sentence is the bridge to Unit 4 — do not elaborate. Let the curiosity sit.)*

Dismiss per school custom.

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: Students have just spent three classes in awe of a great being — extraordinary, radiant, born on a lotus. Unit 4 pivots to wonder at the smallest beings: insects, bugs, creatures easily overlooked. Thread the needle: all beings, great and tiny, are worthy of attention and care. The 7-line prayer's reception — how students sat with it — is worth noting. Some may ask about it. Hold those questions warmly.
