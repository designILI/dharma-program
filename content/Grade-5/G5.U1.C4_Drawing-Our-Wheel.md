# G5.U1.C4 — Drawing Our Wheel

## Grade 5 · Unit 1 · Class 4 of 5 · Drawing Our Wheel

*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Putting pencil to paper and drawing the Wheel of Life from understanding → discovering, through the act of making, what one knows and what one does not yet know

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Generation effect — when students produce an image from their own understanding rather than copying a model, the act of generation itself consolidates and tests comprehension simultaneously. Errors made in drawing reveal gaps in understanding with far greater precision than any verbal test could; and the effort of production deepens retention more than passive review. The drawing is assessment and learning at once.

**Bloom's Level:** Apply / Analyze — students apply their accumulated understanding from C1–C3 to produce a coherent personal rendering of the complete Wheel. The act of drawing demands analysis: where does this element sit in relation to that one? What does this part mean — and how do I show that meaning visually? Students who hit a section they cannot draw are analysing their own knowledge boundary in real time.

**Prior Class Summary:** C3 surveyed all six realms — their names, characteristic experiences, and the dual reading (cosmological and psychological). Students labeled all six realms on their developing Wheel drawing and carry the six-realm gestures. They hold the question: "Which realm do I visit most often as a state of mind?"

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** the complete structural inventory of the Wheel of Life — all required elements: the outer figure (Yama), the inner ring (light and dark), the six realms, the hub (three animals), the figure outside (Buddha pointing) — and can locate each in the image.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that drawing is not copying — it is translation, and that the act of translating understanding into image is itself a form of analysis: what you can draw, you know; what you cannot draw reveals what still needs understanding.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** produce a personally rendered, complete Wheel of Life drawing that includes all required elements, labeled, in a style that is their own rather than a direct copy of the thangka.

Students will **FEEL** the experience of discovering — through the resistance of the pencil and the blankness of the page — exactly where their understanding is solid and where it has gaps; and the quiet satisfaction of making something that contains a complete teaching.

---

### Materials Checklist

- Large, clear image of the Wheel of Life — displayed throughout Unit 1 (already up)

  - **Plan B (if working drawings are lost or unavailable):** Distribute A3 blank paper. Students begin their personal Wheel drawing fresh in C4, with the goal of completing it in C5. They will work from the displayed image and their own memory/notes. The C1 from-memory sketch remains filed separately for the C5 comparison; this situation only affects the working drawing.
- A3 blank white paper (one per student) — if students need a fresh sheet or want to start the final version on a clean page
- Pencils, coloured pencils or thin markers (optional, for realm differentiation — not required)

- Chalk or whiteboard markers — for the Mini Lesson checklist on the board
- The Teacher's Checklist written on the board before class (see Mini Lesson below)

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 5 prayer set, led by the student leader for this class:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam**

*(Food Offering Prayer at closing only.)*

**Warmup retrieval — full body vocabulary sequence** (before handing to student leader):

Teacher: *"Before we open — the whole map in your body."*

Realm gestures, called by the teacher:

*"God realm. Demi-god realm. Human realm. Animal realm. Hungry ghost realm. Hell realms."*

Students gesture each. Then the three poisons:

*"Ignorance. Desire. Aversion."*

Students gesture each. This takes under ninety seconds and brings the full framework of C1–C3 into working memory before the drawing session begins. Student leader then leads the prayer set.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

The teacher does not display the Wheel yet — or turns the projection off, covers the printed image briefly — for the first thirty seconds of this beat.

*"Close your eyes. See the Wheel of Life in your mind. Start from the outside: who is holding it? Now move inward: what is the next layer? Keep going inward. What do you reach? What is at the very center? And — outside the Wheel entirely — what is there?"*

Give thirty full seconds of eyes-closed visualization.

*"Open your eyes. Tell the class: what did you see most clearly? What was sharp? And where did your mental image go blank — where couldn't you quite see?"*

Take four or five responses. Make note — on the board or in your own memory — of which elements students can visualize clearly (hub, usually, after C2; realms, after C3) and which remain vague (the inner ring of light and dark; the outer rim of twelve nidanas; Yama's exact posture). This informs where to direct attention during the drawing session.

Then:

*"Today you draw. Not from looking — from knowing. But the image is there on the wall, and you may look at it whenever you need. The goal is not to draw without looking. The goal is to draw from understanding — to know why each element is where it is, not just to copy its position."*

**Fallback (if students are visibly anxious about drawing):** *"Some of you may be thinking: I'm not a good drawer. Here is the thing: the Wheel of Life in this room has been painted by monks with decades of training. Yours does not need to match theirs. It needs to show that you understand what is in it. A circle with three animals labeled in the middle and six sections labeled correctly is a complete Wheel of Life. Start with what you know, and build from there."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 5–8 minutes

Brief. The teacher has written the checklist on the board before class — students will refer to it throughout the drawing session.

*"Before we draw — the checklist. Your personal Wheel of Life needs to include all of these elements. Style is yours. But all of these must be present and labeled."*

**The Wheel of Life Drawing Checklist** (written on the board):

1. **The outer holding figure** — Yama, Lord of Death (at least suggested; can be a simplified figure or an enclosing border with his face/hands visible)
2. **The inner ring of light and dark** — a half-white, half-black ring between the hub and the six realms, showing beings rising (light) and falling (dark)
3. **The six realms** — all six, in their approximate correct positions, each labeled with its name
4. **The hub** — the three animals (pig, snake, rooster), correctly labeled with their poisons
5. **The figure outside the Wheel** — the Buddha, pointing; clearly outside and separate from Yama's grasp
6. **Labels** — every section identified

*"The inner ring of light and dark is the one most students forget — it's the ring between the hub and the six realms. One half is light, one half is dark. Beings on the light side are moving upward — doing virtuous deeds. Beings on the dark side are moving downward — pulled by non-virtue. Look at the image now. Can you find it?"*

Give students fifteen seconds to find the inner ring in the displayed image. Confirm: *"Yes — that ring. Add it to your drawing today."*

**Vocabulary Moment:**

*"The word* mandala *— from Sanskrit, मण्डल. In Dharma term:* དཀྱིལ་འཁོར*, kyilkhor — literally 'center-circle.' A mandala is a sacred diagram — often circular — that represents a complete cosmos or a complete teaching. The Wheel of Life is one of the great mandalas of the Himalayan Buddhist tradition. Your drawing is a personal mandala — not a decoration, but a map of your understanding of a complete system. When you draw it with attention and care, the drawing itself becomes a practice."*

Write *mandala* / *kyilkhor* / དཀྱིལ་འཁོར on the board.

**Body Moment:**

Before students put pencil to paper:

*"Before you draw the first line — feel the pencil in your hand. The weight of it. The point of it. In Grade 4 you filled the water bowls with that same quality of attention. In Grade 3 you shaped the torma with that quality. This drawing deserves the same. Not speed — presence. Take one breath, and then begin."*

One breath. Then: drawing.

**Emotional Hook:**

The experience of translation — of discovering, through the resistance of the blank page, what one knows and what one does not. This often arrives mid-drawing, when a student reaches an element they cannot quite render and realises: I don't fully understand this yet. That moment is not failure — it is the most precise form of assessment available.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

After approximately 10 minutes of drawing — not at the start, not at the end, but mid-process when students are in the drawing:

*"Without stopping — keep your pencil moving if you can — tell the person beside you: what is the hardest part of this to draw? What is the element you are least sure of? Where did your hand slow down?"*

Pairs talk briefly — sixty to ninety seconds is enough. Then return to drawing.

**What to listen for:** Students who cannot draw the inner ring of light and dark have not yet internalized the moral causation layer of the Wheel. Students who cannot place the Buddha outside correctly may be conflating liberation with the six realms. Students who discover mid-drawing that they mislabeled a realm are engaging in genuine self-assessment. Note all of these — they are formative information for C5 and for Unit 2.

---

#### Activity — 20+ minutes

**Drawing session — the full main block of this class.**

Students draw their personal Wheel of Life from the checklist, with the Wheel image available for reference. Teacher circulates continuously, offering specific prompts — not corrections of style, but observations that deepen understanding:

- *"Your hungry ghost realm — what makes it look hungry? What would you add to show the characteristic experience of that realm, not just label it?"*
- *"Where is the inner ring in your drawing? The light half and the dark half — can I find them?"*
- *"Your Buddha outside the Wheel — is he clearly outside Yama's grip? Look at the image: there is space between Yama and the Buddha. Does your drawing show that?"*
- *"The three animals in the hub — are they biting each other's tails? What does that mean in terms of how they relate to each other?"*
- *"Which realm is at the top of your Wheel? Why is the god realm at the top and not the human realm?"*

Teacher does not tell students what to draw or correct their visual choices. The teacher asks questions that lead students back to the image and their own understanding.

**Scaffold for students who are overwhelmed:** Begin with the simplest frame — a large circle. Inside it, a smaller circle. Inside that, a hub. Label the outer circle "Yama." Divide the area between the hub and the outer circle into six sections. Now fill in. Building the armature first prevents the paralysis of facing a completely blank page.

**Extension (for students who complete the checklist quickly):** Add small figures inside each realm that convey — through posture, expression, or symbolic detail — the characteristic experience of beings in that realm. No correct answer for what these figures look like — the teacher asks: *"If I didn't know the Wheel of Life at all, could I look at your drawing and tell what each realm feels like? What would you add to make that clearer?"*

A second extension: students may add a small symbol of their own devising — something from their own life or imagination — that represents each realm's characteristic experience. This is explicitly not part of the traditional thangka; it is the student's personal layer, placed alongside the traditional elements.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

*"What did you discover about your understanding while drawing? What did you think you knew — until your hand tried to draw it?"*

Take three or four responses. The most common discovery: students thought they understood the inner ring of light and dark but couldn't render it; or they knew all six realms but forgot their correct positions relative to each other; or they found that labeling forced them to be more precise than they had been.

*"This is what drawing does that looking cannot. When you look, you can glide over the parts you don't fully understand. Your eye skips and fills in. When you draw, you cannot skip anything. Your hand stops. And where your hand stops — that is what you still need to learn.*

*"Next class: we finish. And then you hold your drawing alongside the sketch you made on day one — two minutes of looking — and you see the distance between them. That distance is what this unit has done."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

**Collect the in-progress drawings** before closing prayers — they will be returned at the start of C5. Label each with the student's name if not already labeled.

Full prayer set, student leader:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam**

Teacher's closing line:

*"Your drawing is not finished yet — but it already holds more than you knew two weeks ago. Come back next class knowing what you still need to add. The Wheel is almost yours."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*

Carry forward: Wheels-in-progress are collected by the teacher at the end of C4 and returned at the start of C5. Keeping them between classes protects them from loss or damage and ensures the C5 comparison (in-progress drawing returned alongside the C1 from-memory sketch) is clean. Before C5, the teacher should review each in-progress drawing and note — for each student — which checklist elements are present and which are missing or incomplete. Write a brief note per student (a word or two per element) to guide the C5 circulating conversations. Do not mark or grade the drawings; note only what is present and what is still needed. C5 is a quieter, more reflective class — finishing, comparison, and the three-part written reflection. Students who have not completed the checklist by the end of C4 need targeted prompting at the start of C5's activity block, not at the expense of the comparison and reflection time, which is the pedagogical heart of C5. Spiral connection: this drawing session mirrors Grade 4's construction of the Field Guide to ritual instruments — in both cases, students translate understanding into a produced artefact. In Grade 4, the artefact was a reference document; in Grade 5, it is a personal mandala. The same quality of attention is required. If time allows, name this connection to students during the drawing session: "You brought this same care to your Field Guide. This is the same practice."
