# G5.U2.C4 — Drawing the Realms
## Grade 5 · Unit 2 · Class 4 of 5 · Drawing the Realms
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Completing the three detailed realm drawings → the experience of holding something honestly examined in one's own hands
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Generative retrieval through production — annotating a drawing requires the student to reconstruct understanding from the inside rather than recognize it from the outside. Each annotation decision (what does the klesha look like for this realm? what is my personal example?) is an act of retrieval and consolidation, not copy-down.
**Bloom's Level:** Apply and Analyze — students apply the psychological reading of each realm to their own drawn image (Apply) and analyze what they have chosen to depict and why, articulating the characteristic experience and the ruling klesha with sufficient precision to annotate it legibly (Analyze).
**Prior Class Summary:** C3 deepened the human realm, introduced the three antidotes — wisdom, generosity, compassion — and led students through the three-poison / three-antidote gesture sequence. Students added a "Way Out" page to their realm drawings. They carry the recognition that the curriculum has been practising the antidotes since Grade 1.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the four required elements of a complete realm drawing: image of the beings, characteristic experience, ruling klesha, personal example.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that a drawing that holds real personal reflection is qualitatively different from a drawing that depicts external information only — and that the difference is visible in the annotation.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** complete their three realm drawings with all four elements present, at a level of specificity that makes each realm's psychological quality legible to a reader.
Students will **FEEL** the particular satisfaction of a nearly-complete piece of work that holds something genuine — and the slight edge of vulnerability that comes with it.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Student portfolios with realm drawings (pages from C1, C2, C3)
- Drawing paper (one or two additional sheets per student, in case of additions or restarts)
- Pencils, coloured pencils or crayons
- The Wheel of Life image displayed at the front of the room
- Board or chart paper — teacher writes the four required elements at the start of the activity

**Plan B:** If a student's portfolio has been lost or damaged, have blank three-page sets (plus the "Way Out" page) ready so they can produce a fresh version this class. Four pages in one class is achievable if the student has the knowledge and is given the full activity time. If drawing materials are limited, pencil annotation is sufficient — the written reflection matters as much as the visual.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Student leader opens the full prayer set. Teacher chants with the class.

Full Grade 5 prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam**

*(Food Offering Prayer is closing only.)*

Immediately after Dechen Monlam, the teacher leads the three-poison → three-antidote gesture sequence as a warmup — without lengthy explanation. "Let's begin with the gestures." Do the full sequence once through, slowly: ignorance → wisdom, desire → generosity, aversion → compassion. No commentary required; the body carries it.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
**"You've been working on three realm drawings across C1, C2, and C3. Today you finish them. Before you open your portfolios — which three realms did you choose? What drew you to those three specifically?"**

Take a quick round — each student names their three (or as many as are willing to share). The teacher listens for patterns and notes them without comment. If someone chose the hungry ghost realm, notice it. If someone didn't include the human realm, note it — they may have added it as a "Way Out" page, which counts.

Then: "Before we open the portfolios, let's check: what makes a realm drawing complete?" Students should be able to produce the four elements from memory. If they can't recall all four, that's information — the teacher writes them on the board now.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 5 minutes
Brief — this is a working class, not a teaching class. The mini lesson is a checklist review.

Teacher writes on the board:

**A complete realm drawing has:**
1. **Image of the beings** — what they look like, what surrounds them
2. **Characteristic experience** — what it feels like from inside this realm
3. **Ruling klesha** — which poison drives it (ignorance / desire / aversion)
4. **Personal example** — one specific moment from your own life

"Read your drawings against this list. What's missing? That's where you work today."

**Vocabulary Moment:** "No new vocabulary today. Today the vocabulary is in your drawings. The words you've been building across Unit 2 — *yidag*, *moment-to-moment arising*, *antidote*, *klesha* — they belong in the annotations."

**Body Moment:** Students open their portfolios and hold their drawings — both hands, looking at the work they've already done. The teacher does not narrate this; simply says: "Hold them for a moment. Look at what you've made." Ten seconds of quiet. This is the same quality of attention brought to the torma offering and the water bowl filling — not casual handling, but care.

**Emotional Hook:** The experience of a nearly-complete drawing that holds something real — and the awareness that it is about to be seen by one other person, and then by the whole class in C5.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
**Prompt:** "Trade your drawings with a partner for two minutes. Read their annotations — all of them, not just the image. Then tell them one specific thing that made the realm real for you: something they wrote or drew that revealed the characteristic experience of that realm. Not 'it's good' — something specific."

Structure: two minutes reading in silence, then one minute of feedback each direction. The teacher circulates and listens for feedback that is genuinely specific — and gently prompts more specificity where it isn't: "What exactly showed the craving? Which word or image?"

What to listen for: students whose annotations describe something generic vs. students whose annotations describe something particular and personal. This information helps the teacher guide the activity period: who needs to go deeper, and who is ready for the extension.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes
Students complete their three realm drawings — all four elements present in each. Teacher writes the four-element checklist on the board and leaves it there for the duration.

The teacher circulates with genuine, specific attention. Observations are offered as observations, not evaluations:
- "I notice you've drawn the hungry ghost surrounded by water but unable to drink — that's a detail from the traditional accounts. What made you include that?"
- "Your annotation for the animal realm says 'only the immediate need' — can you be more specific? What immediate need? What did it look like the last time you felt that?"
- "The klesha for the demi-god realm — you've written 'jealousy.' The traditional term is *envy* or *pride*, depending on the variant. Which feels more accurate for what you drew?"

These are not corrections — they are invitations to go one layer deeper.

**Extension:** Students who finish all four elements in all three realm drawings (including the "Way Out" page) can write a short paragraph on a separate sheet: "What do these three realms have in common? Why did I choose them?" This is a genuine inquiry question, not a summary task — the teacher should frame it that way.

Before the debrief: teacher asks students to look at all their drawings together and choose one to present in C5. They should write the name of that realm in the top corner of the drawing they've chosen, or mark it with a small star.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
**"Next class: you'll present one realm to the whole class — the one that speaks to you most. Your presentation is two minutes: what the realm is, what it feels like from inside, which klesha rules it, and one example — personal or observed. Then: why did you choose this one? That's the most important question."**

Brief pause. "Tonight, think about why you chose the realm you chose. Not what you'll say — why it called to you. That's where the honest answer lives."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Student leader closes the full prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam**

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "These drawings are yours. They hold something about what it's been like to be a mind moving through the world. Next class, you share one. Come ready."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: Collect the completed drawings at the end of this class and hold them until C5 — students should not take them home before presenting. This ensures they arrive in C5 with their work in hand and are not scrambling to find it. C5 is the culminating class: presentations, reflection, and the closing of the Wheel of Life study. The transition into Unit 3 is named in the C5 debrief — students are about to meet Padmasambhava in the historical sense. They have been encountering him since Grade 1 (the birth story in G1.U3), and have had his prayer since Grade 2. Grade 5 is the full introduction to his role as the teacher who provided the map — the Dharma in Tibet — so that beings have access to the way out of the Wheel. Having spent Units 1–2 inside the map (what samsara is and how beings turn), they now meet the mapmaker. This transition is explicit in the C5 debrief, but seed it here: "Next unit, we meet the teacher who made the way out accessible to an entire culture."
