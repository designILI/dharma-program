# G5.U3.C4 — Our Map
## Grade 5 · Unit 3 · Class 4 of 4 · Our Map
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Presenting the completed lineage map → the experience of being the current end of something ancient, and the weight of that
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Elaborative retrieval through public articulation — presenting a personal artefact that holds historical knowledge, personal reflection, and an explicit intention integrates all three levels of learning simultaneously. The audience's attention and the teacher's specific observation complete the learning loop.
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluate and Synthesize — students evaluate one link in the chain (why does it matter? what would be lost without it?) and articulate a personal intention as the current end of the chain (Evaluate). The culminating question — "What keeps beings turning, and what is the way out?" — asks for a synthesis of the entire year's study (Synthesize).
**Prior Class Summary:** C3 completed the lineage maps — all the way from the Buddha to the student's own name. Students carry the question "What does that ask of you?" and a written sentence about what they intend to carry forward.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the structure of the lineage map presentation: one link, why it matters, what would be lost without it, and what they intend to carry forward.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that being at the end of a lineage is not a passive position — it is a custodial one: for the duration of their life, they hold the chain and can pass it forward.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** present their lineage map clearly, explain one link in the chain with specificity, and articulate a personal intention as the chain's current end.
Students will **FEEL** the experience of being the current end of something ancient — not as burden but as a specific form of dignity and responsibility — and the particular quality of an ending that is also a beginning.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Completed lineage maps (collected from students after C3 — return them at the start of this class)
- Wall space or display surface for gallery format (if using gallery rather than share circle)
- Bell
- Board or chart paper: presentation structure written out (for reference, not as a script)

**Plan B:** If the room cannot accommodate a gallery format, a share circle works equally well — students hold their maps in their hands and present from their seat or a designated spot at the front. If a student is presenting a map that is less complete than others, the teacher should not draw attention to the difference — the presentation reflects what the student has done, and that is what matters. If a student was absent for C3, they can present whatever they have and describe the part of the chain they understand best.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Student leader opens the full prayer set. Teacher chants with the class — slowly, with care. This is the last class of the year.

Full Grade 5 prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam**

*(Food Offering Prayer is closing only.)*

Before the opening, the teacher says only: "Today we close Grade 5. This is the last class of the year. Let's begin with the full set, with full attention." No further preamble.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 3–5 minutes
Teacher returns the lineage maps. Students receive them and hold them.

**"Your name is at the end of a chain that goes back to the Buddha. Look at your map. Find the one link you find most interesting — the one you want to present today. The one that, when you look at the map, calls to you. Give yourself one minute."**

One minute of quiet looking. No talking. The teacher looks at their own imagined map alongside the students.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 2 minutes
Brief. Presentation structure, said once clearly:

**"Tell us: (1) what the link is — who the teacher is and what they did. (2) Why this link matters — what would be lost if this teacher had not held the chain. (3) What you intend to carry forward as the current end of the chain."**

"Two minutes each. One specific observation from me per presentation. We witness each other."

**Vocabulary Moment:** Write on the board: *custodian.*

"A custodian: a keeper, a guardian — someone who holds something carefully and passes it on. Every person in this lineage was a custodian of the teachings. For the duration of their life, they held the chain and passed it forward. That is what you are now. Small, new, at the end — but a custodian."

**Body Moment:** Before presentations begin — students hold their completed maps in both hands, at heart level. The teacher says: "Hold them. Feel the weight of this." Thirty seconds of quiet. No instruction — just the weight.

**Emotional Hook:** The experience of being the current end of something that stretches back fourteen centuries. The recognition that the chain did not break, that it reached this room, and that it now rests — briefly, temporarily — in these hands.

---

#### Turn and Talk — not applicable this class
The presentations are the structured sharing. No separate Turn and Talk.

---

#### Activity — 20–25 minutes
**Map presentations — gallery or share circle format.**

Each student presents for approximately two minutes. The teacher listens with full attention and offers one specific, genuine observation per student — not evaluation, not praise, but recognition. The observation names something particular:

- "You chose Vairocana as your link — and you said that without him, the prayers would have stayed in a language no one here could speak. That's the right reason. That's why it matters."
- "Your map goes all the way from the Buddha to your own name, and you drew a small gap between the last teacher and yourself — with a question mark. That question mark is honest. Not every link is fully documented. The chain is real even where we can't see every connection."
- "You said you intend to carry forward the practice of noticing which realm you're in. That's specific. That's the right level of specificity for an intention."

If a student is hesitant during their presentation, the teacher can ask a gentle prompt: "Tell us about the link you chose — why did that one call to you?" Not to rescue them, but to re-open the door.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
After the last presentation, the teacher asks:

**"Grade 5 asked one question all year: what keeps beings turning in the Wheel of Life, and what is the way out? Looking back — what is your answer? Not the official Buddhist answer. Your answer, now, having been through the year."**

Take 3–4 responses. The teacher receives them fully, without correcting or supplementing. Hold the last response with a genuine pause.

Then: "Grade 6 takes you deeper: into impermanence, into grief, into the Bardo — the space between one life and the next. And into Tara, the female bodhisattva who moves toward suffering rather than away from it. The chain you're in goes into that. The map you've spent this year learning continues there."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
**Student leader leads the full closing prayer set.** Teacher chants with the class — not ahead of it.

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam**

After the standard closing set, the teacher chants the Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer once more — alone, without announcement, without explanation. The class listens, or joins. Either is right.

Then: bell, once. Silence. The teacher allows the silence to be complete before speaking.

Teacher's closing line — the whole year in one sentence: "You came into Grade 5 with a drawing of the Wheel. You leave with a map of the chain that offers the way out of it. That is enough for one year."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: Lineage maps are returned to students as a portfolio artefact — or retained for year-end portfolios per the school's practice. Together with the Wheel of Life drawing (Unit 1) and the three detailed realm drawings (Unit 2), these constitute the Grade 5 portfolio: three artefacts at three scales — cosmic map, interior map, historical chain. Grade 5 is complete. THREAD INTO GRADE 6: Grade 6 is the year of impermanence, grief, and the feminine in Buddhism. Three units: Losar and the Wheel of the Year (seasonal impermanence), the Bardo (what happens at death — extending the Wheel's framework), and Tara (the bodhisattva who does not turn away from suffering). Students who have spent Grade 5 inside the Wheel of Life — understanding samsara, the six realms, the three antidotes, and the lineage — are ready for the Bardo teaching and for Tara. Students carry the lineage framing into Grade 6: the Bardo teaching they will receive in Grade 6 also came through this lineage, through Padmasambhava himself (the Bardo Thodol is attributed to Padmasambhava's terma tradition). PAJAPATI NOTE: Grade 6, Unit 3 (Tara) includes a partial return to the name Pajapati — first planted in G2.U2.C1. Students will encounter a fuller version of her story in Grade 6 and the complete telling in Grade 8. The name has been held for four years by this point. Do not explain this thread to students proactively — let it surface through familiarity when they encounter the name again.
