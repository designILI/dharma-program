# G5.U2.C3 — Realms Inside Us
## Grade 5 · Unit 2 · Class 3 of 5 · Realms Inside Us
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** The human realm and the three antidotes → the realization that the entire curriculum has been practising the antidotes by other names
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Schema activation and integration — students carry five years of practice (prayers, offerings, tsethar, torma, kind-act inquiries) as lived experience. This class provides the conceptual framework — the three antidotes — that names what those practices were doing. Prior learning is not supplemented; it is re-organized under a structure that was always implicit.
**Bloom's Level:** Analyze and Apply — students analyze the three poisons and their antidotes as a structure (Analyze), then apply that structure by identifying which antidote they have practised most and which is hardest (Apply), and by encoding the transformation bodily (gesture sequence).
**Prior Class Summary:** C2 went deep into the hungry ghost realm — insatiable craving, the impossibility of satisfaction through more-of-the-same. Students carry the yidag image, the Tibetan word *yidag*, and the private recognition of what they crave beyond satisfaction.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the three antidotes: wisdom (for ignorance), generosity (for desire), compassion (for aversion).
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the human realm is precious because it uniquely contains both the suffering that motivates practice and the reflective capacity that enables it — and that this makes it the only realm in which liberation is accessible.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** perform the three-poison gesture sequence and its corresponding antidote sequence, and identify which antidote they feel most and least cultivated in.
Students will **FEEL** the genuine surprise of recognizing that what they have been doing in Dharma class since Grade 1 — chanting, offering, releasing fish, asking why kindness matters — has been practising the three antidotes all along.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Student leader opens the full prayer set. Teacher chants with the class.

Full Grade 5 prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam**

*(Food Offering Prayer is closing only.)*

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
**"Last class was about the hungry ghost realm — one of the hardest to look at. Today we move to the human realm — the one the Buddha is standing outside the Wheel, pointing toward. Before I explain what makes it special: what do you think makes it special? What does the human realm offer that the others don't?"**

Take responses. Students may say: humans can think, humans can choose, humans can practise. All of these are right. Affirm what arrives and hold it loosely: "We're going to look at this more precisely. But you already have most of it."

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes
Two threads — taught in sequence, not simultaneously.

**Thread 1: Precious human birth.**

Write on the board: *precious human birth.*

"The traditional teaching holds that a human birth is rare and precious — not because humans are the best beings, but because the human realm is the only one where two conditions are present simultaneously: enough suffering to motivate change, and enough intelligence to pursue practice. Think about what that means."

"In the god realm, there is not enough suffering — the gods are comfortable, and comfort makes practice feel unnecessary. Why change when everything is fine? In the animal realm, there is not enough reflective capacity — the animal acts on instinct, cannot sit with a question, cannot ask 'why am I like this?' The hell realm has overwhelming suffering but no space to think. The hungry ghost realm has craving so insistent it leaves no room for wisdom."

"The human realm has both: enough difficulty to make you want to change, and enough reflective capacity to actually do something about it. That's the teaching. It's not that humans are superior — it's that the conditions happen to align here."

"And it's brief. This is the impermanence teaching from Grade 4, returning here. We don't have unlimited time in the human realm. This is not meant to frighten — it's meant to orient. Use this life. That's the whole instruction."

**Thread 2: The three antidotes.**

Write on the board in two columns:

| Poison | Antidote |
|---|---|
| Ignorance | Wisdom — seeing clearly |
| Desire | Generosity — releasing the grip |
| Aversion | Compassion — turning toward |

"Each of the three poisons that drive the Wheel has a direct antidote. Not suppression — replacement. You cannot force yourself to stop craving by willpower alone. But you can cultivate generosity — and generosity, genuinely practised, loosens the grip of craving. You cannot force yourself to stop pushing things away. But you can cultivate compassion — turning toward what you want to avoid — and that changes the quality of mind."

"These three antidotes are not additions to the curriculum. They are the curriculum. They have been since Grade 1."

**Vocabulary Moment:** Write on the board: *antidote.*

"In medicine: a substance that counteracts a poison. In Buddhist teaching: a quality of mind that counteracts a destructive quality of mind — not by force, but by replacing one condition with another. The antidote to ignorance is not the suppression of confusion — it's the cultivation of clear seeing. The antidote to desire is not the suppression of wanting — it's the cultivation of the open hand."

**Body Moment:** The three-poison / three-antidote gesture sequence. This is the most important physical encoding in Unit 2 — return to it in C4 and C5.

Do this slowly. Feel the transformation in each pair.

- **Ignorance → Wisdom:** Hands cover eyes (blind, not seeing). Then: hands draw away from the face, eyes open wide, gaze clear and steady. The gesture opens.
- **Desire → Generosity:** Hands reach forward, grasping, pulling toward the body. Then: hands open, palms up, extending forward — the gesture of giving away.
- **Aversion → Compassion:** Hands press outward, pushing away, rejecting. Then: hands draw inward and then open forward — the gesture of receiving, moving toward, drawing near.

Do the full sequence twice: poison, then antidote. Slow enough that students feel the difference in their bodies.

**Emotional Hook:** The realization. The teacher names it if students don't arrive there themselves — but give them the chance first:

"Look at these three antidotes. Where have you practised wisdom? Where have you practised generosity? Where have you practised compassion? Think across this whole program — not just this year."

Then, if it hasn't emerged: "Om Mani Padme Hung — the compassion mantra, since Grade 1. The tsethar in Grade 3 — releasing the fish. The torma offering in Grade 4. The water bowls. The kind-act inquiry in Grade 3 — asking whether you were doing it for the thanks. These were not random. They were practising the three antidotes. You have been building the antidotes for five years."

Receive this from students warmly. Do not rush past it.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
**Prompt:** "Look at the three antidotes — wisdom, generosity, compassion. Which one do you feel you've practised most in this program? And which one is hardest for you — not impossible, just harder? Tell your partner, and be specific: give an example of when you practised it, or an example of when it was hard."

Structure: each partner speaks for 90 seconds. Prompt students to be specific, not general: not "compassion is hard" but "compassion is hard when someone is being unkind to someone I care about, because I want to push back, not draw toward."

What to listen for: honest differentiation. Students who say all three are equal are probably not thinking yet — gently press: "Is there really no difference? Think about a specific moment."

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes
Students add a final page to their six-realm drawings: a page titled **"The Way Out."**

The structure of this page:
- The human realm in the center — drawn or labelled
- The three antidotes arranged around it — one in each direction if students want a spatial arrangement, or listed and annotated
- The Buddha outside the Wheel, pointing — drawn simply, or indicated
- Annotations for each antidote: **What does wisdom look like?** (Not in abstract — in a specific act or quality of attention.) **What does generosity look like?** **What does compassion look like?**

Extension for students who finish: at the bottom of the page, write one sentence connecting the three antidotes to something they have actually done in the Dharma program — a specific practice, prayer, or act from any year.

Teacher circulates with genuine attention. Ask: "What does wisdom look like for you — specifically? What does it look like in the body, in the face, in the quality of attention?" These questions are not rhetorical — the teacher wants to know.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
**"The Wheel of Life is not hopeless — it has a door. The door is the human realm, held open by the three antidotes. The curriculum you've been in since Grade 1 has been walking you toward that door, one practice at a time. What do you notice, looking back?"**

Take 2–3 responses. Hold the last one briefly before moving to closing.

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Student leader closes the full prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam**

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "Five years of practice. You've been building the antidotes since you were six years old. That's not nothing — that's actually quite a lot."
