# G5.U2.C2 — The Hungry Ghosts
## Grade 5 · Unit 2 · Class 2 of 5 · The Hungry Ghosts
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** The image of the hungry ghost (huge stomach, tiny mouth) → the structure of craving that cannot be resolved by getting more
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Concrete-abstract bridging — the yidag image is a precise visual metaphor. Students hold the concrete image (enormous stomach, mouth too small to consume) and then reason from it to the abstract psychological insight: the problem is not the absence of what is craved, but the shape of the craving itself. The image does the thinking.
**Bloom's Level:** Analyze — students move beyond identifying the hungry ghost realm (Remember/Understand) to analyzing the structure of a craving: what kind of hunger is it, why doesn't satisfaction resolve it, and what in their own experience operates by this logic.
**Prior Class Summary:** C1 gave all six realms through a psychological lens — specific, mundane examples of each state of mind, from god-realm satisfaction to hell-realm engulfment. Students began their three-realm detail drawings and carry the phrase *moment-to-moment arising* and the recognition that these realms are familiar from ordinary experience.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the name *yidag* (ཡི་དགས), the visual image of the hungry ghost, and the defining characteristic: craving that cannot be resolved by obtaining more of what is craved.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the hungry ghost realm describes a structure of mind — not a moral failing — and that this structure is present in many ordinary forms of craving, including the desire for approval, stimulation, and comfort.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** identify at least one recurring pattern in their own experience (or in life more broadly) that operates by hungry ghost logic — and annotate it in their drawing.
Students will **FEEL** the quiet, uncomfortable, and honest recognition that they know this realm personally — and the relief that comes from being able to name it without shame.

---

- Student portfolios with realm drawings begun in C1

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Student leader opens the full prayer set. The teacher chants with the class.

Full Grade 5 prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam**

*(Food Offering Prayer is closing only.)*

Dechen Monlam: let the class carry it. The teacher notices who is joining from memory and who is still finding the words.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
Teacher does not offer any information yet. Simply asks:

**"Last class I said we'd spend a full class on the hungry ghost realm. Before I explain anything: what do you already know about it? What do you remember?"**

Take responses. Students will offer: huge stomach, tiny mouth, always hungry, can't eat. Write key phrases on the board as they come.

Then hold the central image and ask one further question: **"Why a huge stomach and a tiny mouth? What does that image actually say? What is it trying to teach?"**

Do not answer immediately. Let students think. The image is precise — it teaches without words if students look at it carefully. Whatever they generate here becomes the foundation for the mini lesson.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes
**The hungry ghost realm — full depth.**

Teacher begins with the image itself, before any interpretation:

"The yidag — the hungry ghost — is depicted in Buddhist art as a being with an enormous, distended stomach and a mouth so small it cannot take in what surrounds it. They are not depicted in a desert — they are depicted surrounded by what they want: food, water, comfort. And they cannot consume it. Some accounts say the food turns to fire in their mouths. The thing they crave burns them when they reach it."

Then: the psychological reading.

"This is the realm of addiction in the broadest sense of that word. Not only substances — anything you keep reaching for that doesn't satisfy, that leaves you wanting more immediately after you get it. Think about the things you check repeatedly: certain apps, certain kinds of attention, certain foods, a particular kind of praise. You get it. It helps a little. And within minutes — sometimes seconds — the craving is back. That is the hungry ghost pattern."

Then: the central insight — this is the load-bearing moment.

"The hungry ghost doesn't suffer because there isn't enough. There is enough. The beings are surrounded by what they want. The hungry ghost suffers because the craving itself is the problem. The mouth is too small — that's the teaching. The hunger isn't caused by the food being absent. It's caused by the shape of the ghost. More food, more approval, more stimulation — none of it resolves the hunger, because the hunger is structural, not circumstantial."

Pause here. Let this land.

"This is why the image has lasted two thousand years. It describes something universal: the mind that craves beyond what any satisfaction can provide. It is not a moral judgment. The hungry ghost is not bad — it is suffering. The image is not a rebuke; it is a precise diagnosis."

**Spiral connection — name it explicitly:** "In Grade 3, we talked about why we do kind acts — and we examined the question of reciprocity. Are you doing a kind thing for the thanks? Acting for recognition is a kind of hungry ghost state: the craving for return, for the acknowledgment that validates the act. Even generosity can be caught in the hungry ghost pattern if it is done for the feeling of being thanked. That was Grade 3. This is what it looks like from the inside."

**Vocabulary Moment:** Write on the board: *yidag* (ཡི་དགས).

"The Dharma term for the hungry ghost — *yidag* — means something like 'the tormented departed' — beings caught between states, unable to find peace or sustenance. The image is at least two thousand years old. It has survived because it describes something that has not changed."

**Body Moment:** Quietly, without drama: "Think of one thing you crave regularly — something you reach for that gives you some relief but leaves you wanting more soon after. You don't have to name it out loud. Just hold it in mind." Five seconds. "Now notice: what happens in your body when you picture not having access to it — not permanently, just for the next hour? What does your stomach do? Your chest?" Twenty seconds of genuine interior attention. No sharing required. The teacher does this too — not as performance, but as practice.

**Emotional Hook:** The uncomfortable and honest recognition — which most students will feel privately — that they know this state from the inside. The relief of having a precise name for something familiar. The absence of shame in that naming.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
**Prompt:** "Tell your partner: what is one thing in your life — or in life more broadly, if you'd prefer not to speak personally — that operates like the hungry ghost realm? Where getting more of it doesn't actually satisfy, at least not for long. You don't have to name something private. You can describe a pattern you've observed in yourself, in others, or in the world."

Structure: each partner speaks for 90 seconds. The teacher listens without intervening. This conversation can be quiet — this is a more interior class than most, and some pairs may sit with the question rather than rush to answer it. That is fine.

What to listen for: students who identify the pattern with recognition rather than performance — genuine examples rather than cited ones. Also note any student who goes very quiet or who names something that suggests real personal weight. Follow up individually after class if needed.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes
Students work on their hungry ghost detail drawing — completing or expanding the drawing begun in C1, or beginning it fresh if the hungry ghost was not one of their three chosen realms in C1.

*(If the hungry ghost was not one of a student's three chosen realms: invite them to add it as a fourth page. This realm warrants its own drawing for everyone.)*

The drawing should include all four elements:
1. **The image of the beings** — what the hungry ghost looks like; what surrounds them; what they are reaching for
2. **The characteristic experience** — what it feels like from inside: the reaching, the brief relief, the immediate renewal of the craving
3. **The ruling klesha** — desire (*dö chak*, འདོད་ཆགས) — name it in the annotation
4. **A personal example** — this annotation is **private**: it goes on the drawing but is not shared. Students can fold or cover this section if they wish. Teacher does not ask to see it.

Teacher circulates quietly. The tone of this class is more interior than usual — lower energy, more reflective. The teacher models this with a quieter presence and a slower pace.

If a student wants to talk while drawing, let them. If a student is working in silence, honor the silence.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
**"The Buddhist answer to the hungry ghost realm is not willpower — that's worth saying directly. Willpower alone cannot resolve a structural craving. If it could, the craving wouldn't be structural. The Buddhist response is understanding: if you see clearly why the craving doesn't satisfy — really see it, not just think it — something loosens. The image exists for that reason. Not to shame you for craving. To illuminate the shape of the craving, so you can start to work with it differently."**

Pause. Then: "Next class, we move to the human realm — the one the Buddha is pointing toward in the Wheel of Life. There's a thread between today and next class: if the hungry ghost realm is insatiable craving, the human realm is the one place where craving can actually be examined, understood, and worked with. That's not an accident."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Student leader closes the full prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam**

Dechen Monlam: "May all beings be free from suffering. May all beings find great happiness." Let this prayer arrive after the hungry ghost class without announcement or framing. It lands differently here. The teacher chants it with full attention — that is the only instruction.

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "The hungry ghost is not a monster — it's a mirror. You've looked at it honestly today. That took something."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: Handle this class with genuine care. The hungry ghost realm is the most psychologically acute in Unit 2, and for some students it will be more than intellectually interesting — it will describe something real in their family or home life. Experiences of addiction, scarcity, compulsive behavior, or the constant pursuit of approval may arise. The private annotation and the body moment are intentional safety valves: they create a protected interior space where a student can make a connection without being asked to disclose it. Do not push students to name what their craving is. "You don't have to name it out loud" is not a social nicety — it is a pedagogical decision. If a student shares something that warrants pastoral follow-up, note it in Your Reflection and follow the school's care process. This class is not a therapy session — it is a precise examination of a state of mind using a two-thousand-year-old image that has proven accurate. The distinction between those two things matters. Spiral connection named explicitly in this class: Grade 3, Unit 2, Class 3 (acts of kindness and the question of reciprocity). C3 deepens the human realm — the antidote direction. The thread: if the hungry ghost realm is insatiable craving, the human realm is the one place where craving can be examined and worked with. Hold that thread across the turn to C3.
