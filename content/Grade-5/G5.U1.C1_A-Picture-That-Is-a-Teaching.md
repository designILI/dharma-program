# G5.U1.C1 — A Picture That Is a Teaching

## Grade 5 · Unit 1 · Class 1 of 5 · A Picture That Is a Teaching

*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A large circular image painted at the entrance of temples → a complete map of conditioned existence and the possibility of liberation

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Observation before interpretation — when students look before they are told what to see, their attention is genuinely active and their subsequent understanding is personally grounded rather than received. First encounter without scaffolding activates curiosity as a learning state.

**Bloom's Level:** Understand (initial) — students are asked to observe, orient, and begin forming their own interpretive categories before the tradition's vocabulary is offered. The from-memory sketch externalises what the image has given them and creates the before-marker against which all future learning in this unit will be measured.

**Prior Class Summary:** This is the first class of Grade 5. Students have completed Grade 4's full arc — the seven-bowl water offering, the torma, the Field Guide to ritual instruments, the bell. They have been told that Grade 5 will ask them to read an entire image, not a single object. Today that image appears for the first time.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** the name Sipa Khorlo (Wheel of Life / Wheel of Existence), that it is found at the entrance of virtually every Himalayan Buddhist temple, and that it depicts the cycle of conditioned existence called samsara.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that this image is a complete teaching — not a decoration — and that it has been placed at the threshold of temples for hundreds of years as both a warning and an invitation to every person who enters.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** articulate what their eye went to first in the image, make a from-memory sketch of what the two minutes of silent looking gave them, and identify at least one detail they noticed but cannot yet explain.

Students will **FEEL** the particular alertness of encountering a complex, ancient thing that has not yet been explained to them — the productive tension of not-yet-knowing held alongside genuine curiosity.

---

### Materials Checklist

- Large, clear image of the Wheel of Life (Sipa Khorlo) — printed at A2 or larger, OR projected clearly on a wall or screen. This image should remain displayed in the classroom throughout all of Unit 1.
  - **Plan B (if projector fails):** Use the printed version. If no large print is available, a good-quality A3 print is workable — seat students in a close arc so all can see clearly. Do not proceed with a small image; this lesson depends on looking.
- One sheet of blank white paper per student (not lined — for the from-memory sketch)
- Pencils (one per student — not pens; students will want to revise their Wheel drawings across C1–C5)
- A folder or envelope per student for safe-keeping of C1 sketch and all subsequent Wheel drawings (clearly labeled with each student's name)
- Chalk or whiteboard markers for the board

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 5 prayer set, led by the student leader for this class:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam**

*(Food Offering Prayer is closing only — not opening.)*

**Dechen Monlam — first introduction (teacher leads this section before handing to student leader):**

The teacher chants the Dechen Monlam once through alone, then pauses and speaks:

*"This is the Dechen Monlam — the Prayer of Great Bliss. བདེ་ཆེན་སྨོན་ལམ. 'Monlam' means aspiration, a wish. 'Dechen' means great bliss, great happiness. This prayer is an aspiration that all beings may be free from suffering and find true happiness. We are introducing it today for a reason: what we are about to study — the Wheel of Life — is a map of suffering, of beings turning endlessly through cycle after cycle. The Dechen Monlam is our response to that map. We study the Wheel so that we understand what we are wishing freedom from. From today, we chant it together."*

The teacher chants it once more, inviting students to join where they can. Student leader then leads the remaining prayers.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

The teacher does not display the Wheel yet. First, this question:

*"Last year you learned to read ritual objects. You learned that a bell is not just a bell — it encodes a teaching about the nature of sound, impermanence, and wisdom. You made a torma and gave it away. You built a Field Guide. Every object you studied taught you something that could not be said in plain words. Today I am going to show you an image. Don't ask me what it means yet. Don't try to explain it. Just look."*

Now display — or project — the Wheel of Life clearly. Allow two full minutes of silence. No prompting. Let students look.

After the two minutes:

*"What do you see?"*

Accept observations only — not interpretations. If a student says "it's a wheel of life," gently redirect: *"What do you actually see in the image? What are the shapes, the figures, the colours?"* Strong responses might include: "There's a circle," "There are animals in the very middle," "A big creature is holding the whole thing," "There are sections — like a pie," "There's a small figure standing outside the circle," "There are people in each section."

**Fallback (if silence):** Point to a single feature — the three animals at the hub — and ask: *"What are these three animals doing?"* One specific question almost always unlocks observation in the whole class.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

After the observation round, the teacher begins the first orientation — not an explanation of everything, but a placing of the image in context.

*"This is called the Sipa Khorlo — སྲིད་པའི་འཁོར་ལོ. The Wheel of Life. It is painted at the entrance of almost every Himalayan Buddhist temple. Before a person walks in to pray, to make offerings, to meditate — they pass this image. Every day. For hundreds of years. What do you think it is for? Why would you put this at the entrance?"*

Take two or three responses. Then:

*"The tradition says: so that before you enter the sacred space, you remember what you are trying to be free from. The Wheel is a map. Today we begin to learn to read it."*

**First orientation — three anchoring points only (do not teach all parts today):**

1. *"The Wheel is held by a large, powerful figure. This is Yama — the Lord of Death. Yama holds the Wheel because impermanence holds everything in existence. No being inside the Wheel escapes its turning. Death — the fact that nothing lasts — is what holds the whole cycle together."*

2. *"The Wheel has layers. You can see sections, rings, a center. Each layer is a different teaching. We will study each layer in this unit. But today — just know that there are layers."*

3. *"Look at the figure outside the Wheel. Small, standing apart. Not being held by Yama. Not inside the turning."* (Pause.) *"Who do you think that is? What do you think that figure is doing?"* Take responses. *"That figure is the Buddha. And he is pointing."* (Pause.) *"What is he pointing at? We will spend the whole year finding out. But here is what the image has been telling people for centuries: there is a way out. The Wheel is not a prison with no door."*

**Vocabulary Moment:**

*"The word* samsara *— Sanskrit. In Dharma term:* འཁོར་བ *, khorwa — 'the cycling,' 'the turning.' When Buddhists speak of samsara, they mean the state of being driven by forces we don't fully control — by desire, by ignorance, by aversion — turning endlessly through birth, life, death, and rebirth. The Wheel of Life is a map of samsara. It is a map of where we are before we find the door."*

Write *samsara* and *khorwa* on the board.

**Body Moment:**

*"Stand up. Find a little space."*

Students stand.

*"Turn in a slow circle — one full rotation. Slowly."*

Students turn once.

*"Now stop."*

Ten seconds of stillness.

*"How does it feel to stop? What happened in your body when the turning stopped?"*

Take two or three brief responses.

*"That is what the Buddha is pointing to. The stopping of the turning. But before we can stop, we have to understand what the turning is, and what is driving it. That is what this unit is for."*

**Emotional Hook:**

*"This image has been looking at people for centuries. At the entrance of temples, in monastery courtyards, in prayer halls across the Himalayan world. Monks, farmers, pilgrims, children — all passed this image. Many of them could not read words. But they could read this. The image was teaching even when no one was there to explain it. That is what a great symbol does."*

Allow a moment of stillness with that.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

*"Turn to the person beside you. Tell them: what is the most striking thing you noticed in the image — the thing your eye went to first? And what do you think it might mean? You are not being asked to be right. You are being asked to look carefully and say what you saw."*

Pairs talk for two to three minutes. Teacher circulates and listens — note which students are already making interpretive moves ("I think the animals in the middle are connected to each other") and which are observational ("I saw a lot of people in different sections"). Both are valuable. Do not correct either mode.

**What to listen for:** Students who notice the figure outside the Wheel before being directed to it are already sensing the structure of the teaching. Students who are drawn to the three animals at the center are often the ones who will engage most with C2's lesson on the three poisons.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

**From-memory sketch:**

*"Now: close your eyes for five seconds and see the Wheel in your mind."*

(Five seconds.)

*"Open your eyes. On your blank sheet of paper — draw what you remember. Not what you see now — what you saw. Draw from what the two minutes gave you. Do not look at the image while you draw."*

Teacher turns off the projector or angles the print away.

Students draw for approximately 10–12 minutes. The sketch will be incomplete, asymmetrical, partial — this is entirely correct. The point is not accuracy but what was retained. Teacher circulates and says nothing corrective. If a student is stuck: *"Draw what you are most sure about first. Even one circle and three animals in the middle is a beginning."*

After drawing:

*"At the top of your paper, write today's date and one sentence: 'This is what I saw in two minutes.' That's all."*

Teacher collects the sketches and places them in each student's labeled folder. These will be returned in C5 alongside the finished drawing.

**Extension (for students who finish quickly):** On the back of their sketch, write two questions — things they noticed in the image but cannot yet explain. These questions will be collected (the teacher notes them for future classes) and returned to the student in C5.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

*"What you drew from memory is what the image gave you in two minutes of looking. It is not wrong — it is where you are right now with this image. By the end of this unit, you will draw this image from understanding — and explain every element. That is the difference between seeing and reading. We have been teaching you to read. Last year you read a bell, a torma, a water bowl. This year you are reading a library. The whole Wheel of Life is one complete teaching. We will spend the year inside it."*

*"Here is what I want you to hold until next class: the Wheel has a center. And the center is what drives everything. What is at the center? What do you think drives the whole Wheel?"*

(This question is held — not answered. It is the bridge to C2.)

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set, student leader:

**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam**

Teacher's closing line (spoken after the prayers, before students leave):

*"The Wheel of Life has been waiting at the temple door for five hundred years. It waited for you to be ready to read it. Today you took your first look. Next class, we go to the center."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*

Carry forward: The from-memory sketch is the essential before-marker for this unit — it is not assessed, graded, or corrected. Its only purpose is comparison: at the end of C5, students will hold this sketch alongside their finished drawing and see the arc of their own learning. Keep all sketches in labeled folders between C1 and C5. Do not return them to students between classes; the comparison in C5 depends on the contrast being fresh. The Wheel of Life image should remain visibly displayed in the classroom throughout all of Unit 1 — on the wall, board, or a stable surface where students can look at it before and after class. Students will study it incidentally between classes; this is exactly right. The Dechen Monlam is introduced today — chant it at every opening and closing from this point forward, through the remainder of Grade 5. Some students will learn it quickly; others will find the sounds before they find the meaning. Both are fine. Spiral connection: Grade 4 trained students to read individual symbolic objects (the bell, the water bowl, the torma). Today's lesson names that training explicitly and scales it: "You learned to read a bell. Now you're reading a whole library." This framing is important — it honours Grade 4's work while marking the step-change in Grade 5's ambition. Name this to students. The question held at the end of the Debrief ("What is at the center? What drives the whole Wheel?") is an intentional bridge to C2. Do not answer it. Let it sit overnight.
