# G5.U3.C1 — The Second Buddha
## Grade 5 · Unit 3 · Class 1 of 4 · The Second Buddha
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** The historical and legendary figure of Padmasambhava → the recognition that students are not at the beginning of something but inside something that has been going on for a very long time
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Prior knowledge activation through structured recall — students have accumulated Padmasambhava material across four years (birth story, taming narrative, Seven-Line prayer). This class organizes that existing knowledge into a coherent picture, adding the historical frame that gives it weight. The PKA phase makes the prior learning visible before adding to it.
**Bloom's Level:** Understand and Analyze — students consolidate existing knowledge about Padmasambhava (Understand) and analyze the concept of lineage: what it means to be inside a chain of transmission, what that asks of a person, and why it would be worth the effort to establish (Analyze).
**Prior Class Summary:** C5 of Unit 2 was the realm presentation class — students presented their detailed realm drawings and reflected on what Units 1 and 2 had taught them about the Wheel of Life. The unit closed with the thread: "You've been encountering Padmasambhava since Grade 1. Next unit, we meet the mapmaker."

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the epithet "Second Buddha," the country of Oddiyana (named in the Seven-Line prayer), the historical context — 8th-century Tibet, King Trisong Detsen, the project of building Samye monastery — and the key function Padmasambhava served: making the awakening mind accessible to an entire culture.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** the concept of lineage — a chain of transmission, teacher to student, from the Buddha to the present — and that every teaching in this school reaches them through that chain.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** sketch the beginning of a lineage map — the structural diagram from the Buddha through Padmasambhava toward the present — and reflect on what it means to be inside that chain.
Students will **FEEL** the quiet, grounding recognition of being inside something ancient and ongoing — not isolated in one's own experience of practice, but connected to a very long conversation.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Drawing paper (one large sheet per student — for the lineage map, which continues in C2 and C3)
- Pencils and fine-line markers (for the map structure)
- Board or chart paper for key names and dates during the mini lesson
- The Wheel of Life image (may remain displayed or can be set aside — teacher's discretion)

**Plan B:** If large-format paper is unavailable, standard A4 paper works — students use both sides or attach sheets later. If the teacher is uncertain about specific lineage names, write what is known on the board with space: "We'll fill in the rest as we learn more." The structural exercise — drawing the bones of a lineage map — can begin with the names the teacher knows confidently: the Buddha, Padmasambhava, Samye, the school's tradition.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Student leader opens the full prayer set. Teacher chants with the class.

Full Grade 5 prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam**

*(Food Offering Prayer is closing only.)*

The Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer: chant with particular care today. After the full set, the teacher says — simply, without drama: "This is the prayer we've been building since Grade 2. Today we meet its subject more fully."

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
**"You've been encountering Guru Rinpoche — Padmasambhava — since Grade 1. What do you know about him already? Let's put together everything we remember from the program."**

Teacher writes on the board as students contribute. Receive everything: born on a lotus, came from a lake, faced a frightening spirit with compassion (G2.U3), the Seven-Line prayer is addressed to him (G2–G3), the prayer has seven lines and asks him to grant blessings.

When the board is filled: "Look at how much you already know. This has been building for four years. Today we add the historical frame — when, where, and why he came to Tibet. Because there was a specific moment in history when Tibet needed him, and he went."

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes
The historical and legendary context. The teacher speaks as a storyteller who knows the material, not as a presenter reading from notes.

"Tibet in the 8th century: Buddhism had reached the edges of the country — monks had come, teachings had arrived at the borders — but it hadn't taken root. The country was full of powerful spirits and forces that the early missionaries couldn't handle. The spirits of the mountains, the valleys, the rivers — they were not placid. They were active, and they were hostile to what was coming."

"King Trisong Detsen wanted to build the first Buddhist monastery in Tibet — Samye. Imagine: a whole monastery, the first of its kind, a place where monks could be ordained, where texts could be translated, where the Dharma could live. Every time construction began, the spirits tore it down at night. The workers would arrive in the morning and find the previous day's work undone."

"So the king sent for Padmasambhava. He came from Oddiyana — you know that name from the Seven-Line prayer: 'In the northwest of the land of Uddiyana...' He made a long and difficult journey to Tibet. The spirits felt him coming."

"What did he do when he arrived? He did not fight the spirits. He met them. One by one, he encountered each powerful force in Tibet — the mountain spirits, the lake spirits, the local protectors — and he saw their nature. He didn't destroy them. He turned them. Each spirit that had been an obstacle became a Dharma protector — a guardian of the teachings. Once the spirits were turned, Samye could be built. It was completed in 779 CE. The first Tibetan monks were ordained there."

"Why is he called the Second Buddha? Not because he was equal to the historical Shakyamuni Buddha in the doctrinal sense — but because he performed the same function for Tibet that the Buddha performed for India. He made the awakening mind accessible to an entire culture. The teachings didn't just arrive in Tibet — they were made to fit. That's the work of a Second Buddha."

**Vocabulary Moment:** Write on the board: *lineage.*

"A lineage is a chain of transmission — teacher to student, from the Buddha to the present. Every teaching we have in this school comes through a lineage. Someone taught it to someone who taught it to someone, all the way back. Padmasambhava is one of the most important links in the Tibetan Buddhist lineage — because without him, the chain in Tibet might have broken. He held it and passed it forward."

**Body Moment:** Students physically trace the chain — slowly, with attention.

"Point to yourself." They point to themselves.

"Your teacher had a teacher." Students gesture outward — toward where the teacher stands.

"And that teacher had a teacher." Another gesture outward, further.

"And that teacher had a teacher." Further again.

"And so on — back through centuries. Back through the translators of the 8th century. Back through Padmasambhava. Back to the historical Buddha."

Let that settle for a moment. Then: "You are not at the beginning of something. You are inside something that has been going on for a very long time."

**Emotional Hook:** The quiet, grounding recognition of being part of a very long conversation — not isolated in one's own practice, but connected backward through history. The slight weight of that connection, which is not burden but belonging.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
**Prompt:** "Tell your partner: what does it mean to you to be inside a lineage — to be a link in a chain that goes back centuries? What does that feel like? And what does it ask of you — if anything?"

Structure: each partner speaks for 90 seconds. The teacher listens. This is a question that may not have a quick answer — if pairs go quiet after the first question, prompt the second: "And what does it ask of you?"

What to listen for: students who feel the weight of the connection — the sense of responsibility or seriousness that comes with being part of something ongoing. Also students who feel inspired by it — the sense of not being alone in the practice.

---

#### Activity — 15 minutes
Students begin their lineage map — the making artefact for Unit 3.

The lineage map is a visual diagram of the chain from the Buddha through Padmasambhava to the present. Today: sketch the structure.

Teacher provides the key structural nodes on the board:
- **Shakyamuni Buddha** — the historical Buddha, 5th century BCE, India
- **The early lineage teachers** — the teacher names the relevant figures for this school's tradition (2–3 names, briefly)
- **Padmasambhava** — 8th century CE, from Oddiyana
- **Samye Monastery** — the first Tibetan monastery, 779 CE
- **[The school's lineage figures]** — teacher provides names relevant to this tradition
- **The school's founding teachers**
- *(Students' own teacher — to be added in C3)*

Students draw the bones of the map: boxes or circles for each figure, lines connecting them. Names written clearly. Dates where known. The visual structure matters — this is not a list; it is a web or chain that should be visible as a connected form on the page.

Teacher circulates: "Who comes between Padmasambhava and the next name you've written? What do you know about that link?" If students don't know — that's fine: they write a box with a question mark. "We'll fill it in next class."

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
**"Padmasambhava didn't bring the Dharma to Tibet because it was convenient. It was extremely difficult — the journey, the resistance, the work of turning each spirit one by one. The tradition asks: why? Why does it matter enough to go to that trouble?"**

Take responses. The answer the tradition gives: because beings are turning in the Wheel of Life, suffering in states they cannot see their way out of. The map — the Dharma — is available. To have it and not share it would be unconscionable. That is the bodhisattva logic: not because it's easy, but because it's needed.

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Student leader closes the full prayer set:
**Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam**

Teacher's closing line, specific to today: "The Seven-Line prayer you've been chanting since Grade 2 is addressed to this person, at this historical moment, because he chose to come to Tibet when he didn't have to. Every time you chant it, you are responding to that choice."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The lineage map begun today is the making artefact for Unit 3 — students will add to it in C2, complete it in C3, and present it in C4. Store maps in the classroom (portfolios or a dedicated folder) after each class. Before C2, gather the specific lineage information relevant to this school's tradition — the chain of named teachers from Padmasambhava to the school's founders and resident teacher. The more specific and real this chain is (actual names, approximate dates if known), the more powerfully the exercise lands. A lineage drawn with real names is a different object from a generic diagram. If the teacher is uncertain about specific lineage details, consult the school's senior teacher or founding documentation before C2. Spiral connections relevant to this class: G1.U3 (Padmasambhava's birth story — born on a lotus), G2.U3 (the taming narrative — Shelging Karmo), G2.U3.C3 (the Seven-Line prayer meaning), G3 (prayer memorisation). The student body gesture (pointing to self, then outward through the chain) is this class's most important physical encoding — it should be slow and felt, not gestural and quick.
