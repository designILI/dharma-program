# G6.U4.C5 — Our Performance
## Grade 6 · Unit 4 · Class 5 of 5 · Our Performance
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A mask worn, movements enacted → the body as a vehicle for a year of learning arriving in a single ceremony
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Performance as integration — when the culmination of a unit requires the body to synthesise what the mind has learned, understanding is encoded at a qualitatively deeper level. The performance does not assess knowledge; it completes it.
**Bloom's Level:** Apply / Create — students enact what they have studied, embodying it in real time for an audience.
**Prior Class Summary:** C4 was the mask-making session — each student completed a mask of their chosen Cham figure. They carry the mask, the three movements learned in C3, and the instruction to practise between classes. Today: the full performance. The final class of Grade 6.

*Format note: This class is structured as a ceremony. The schema is present throughout; the Activity section describes the performance format in detail.*

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the full Cham sequence they are performing and the meaning of each element — figure, movement, direction, and dedication.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the performance is not a product to be evaluated but an act of invocation — and that its value comes from the quality of presence brought to it, not from technical perfection.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** perform the Cham sequence masked, name their figure and its quality in the share circle, and receive the Ngowa dedication as performers.
Students will **FEEL** the specific quality that comes when a year's study arrives in the body — not elation, not relief, but a quiet fullness. Something completed.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Completed masks — one per student (stored safely from C4, checked before class)
- Performance space prepared in advance: seating for any audience (another class, parents, school staff — arranged and confirmed), simple shrine or altar at the front (an image of the Buddha or relevant figure; a butter lamp or candle if the school permits; bell and dorje if available)
- Full prayer set reference — student leader has the set memorised or has a printed copy to lead from
- The teacher's bell, if used in the school's practice
- **Plan B for masks:** If any mask is damaged or incomplete from C4, the student works with what they have — the embodiment and the naming matter more than the mask's condition. A damaged mask that a student made and wears is more significant than a replacement.
- **Plan B for space:** If the performance space cannot be arranged as described, the performance can be conducted within the classroom — desks to the walls, prayer set at the front, students performing in whatever space is available. The ceremony is in the attention, not the architecture.
- **Plan B for audience:** If no external audience is available, the class performs for each other — half the class performs while the other half observes, then they switch for the debrief circle. The share circle follows as normal.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 7 minutes (ceremony begins before the performance)

The performance space is already arranged. The audience, if present, is already seated. Students enter the performance space and stand in a line or arc — masks not yet on.

Teacher stands at the front. Not a stage front — facing the same direction as the students, or at the side of the space. The teacher does not introduce the performance to the audience. The opening is the prayer.

Full Grade 6 prayer set including Dolma:
Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · *Food Offering Prayer (closing only — held for the actual close)*

Student leader leads — standing, facing the same direction as the class, or facing the class from the front. The prayer set is chanted at full pace, with full attention. The audience is invited into the silence of it.

After the prayer set (before the performance begins): "Before we begin: look at your mask. Look at the movements in your hands and feet. Look at where you are. You have spent five classes getting here. Take one breath."

One full breath. Shared.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 2 minutes (arrival, not retrieval)
Teacher (quietly): *"When you put on the mask and begin to move, you are not performing. You are invoking — bringing into this space a quality that the tradition has held for centuries. The audience will see what you embody. Be present. Move deliberately. Hold the figure."*

No questions. No retrieval. This is the moment before a ceremony, not a classroom check-in.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 2 minutes

Brief framing only.

"Everything you need to know — you already know. The figure: you made it. The movements: they are in your body. The meaning: you wrote it, you spoke it, you held it in the body moment in C2. What you do now is let all of that arrive at once."

**Vocabulary Moment:** Write on the board (done before students enter, visible throughout): *invocation* — calling something into presence. "Every movement you make today is an invocation. Your mask is not a disguise — it is a door."

**Body Moment:** Masks go on. Students put on their masks — or hold them to their faces if elastic is absent. "Feel the mask on your face. You can still see. You can still breathe. But something has shifted. Hold that." Ten seconds of stillness. Masked.

**Emotional Hook:** The moment of putting on the mask. The body shifts when the face is covered and the figure is invoked. This is not metaphor — it is a physiological and attentional fact. The teacher does not comment on it; they let it happen.

---

#### Activity (the performance) — 25 minutes

**1. Setting the space:**
The student leader (today's class leader) steps forward. They lead Om Mani Padme Hung — chanted three times by everyone in the space, including the audience. Three rounds, slow, full. This sets the space for the performance. No announcement before it — the student leader simply begins.

**2. The performance:**
Students perform the Cham sequence — the combined walk, turn, and mudra — in their figure's quality. The sequence runs as rehearsed:
- One full circuit of the performance space, moving deliberately in the Cham walk
- A quarter-turn sequence (facing the four directions, holding the mudra at each direction — briefly)
- A final return to the starting position

The teacher has rehearsed the specific sequence with students in C3 and refined it based on available space. If the performance space is small: students move in a circle rather than a circuit, turning at each quarter to face outward. Whatever the arrangement, each student moves through the full sequence once.

The movement is silent — no teacher narration, no music unless the school has traditional Cham music available and the resident teacher recommends its use.

**3. The removal:**
Students return to their starting positions. They remove their masks — deliberately, not hurriedly. They hold the masks at their sides or place them on the floor in front of them.

**4. The naming circle:**
Students stand in a circle (or an arc if space is narrow). In turn — the student leader begins — each student says:

"I embodied [figure name]. They represent [quality]. What I felt in the body was [one word or phrase]."

No more than three sentences. No performance notes or commentary from the teacher during the circle. The audience receives it in silence.

If a student cannot find words: "You don't need to say all three. Say one thing." The naming is important; the completeness of the sentence is not.

**5. The dedication:**
The student leader leads Ngowa — the dedication of merit — immediately after the last naming. This is the last formal element of the performance. The merit of the practice — the movement, the attention, the invocation — is sent outward to all beings.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
Teacher: *"Grade 6 has moved from philosophy to the body — from asking 'what is death?' in Unit 1 to wearing a figure who represents impermanence and moving in ceremony. That is the full arc of this year. What is different about knowledge that lives in the body versus knowledge that lives in the mind? Take a moment — don't answer immediately."*

Brief silence.

Two or three students respond. Receive everything.

Then: "This is the end of Grade 6. The prayer set continues. Pajapati continues. The Bardo continues — because you are living beings, which means impermanence is still with you. What this year has given you is a way to be with it. You have it in your body now."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full prayer set including Dolma and Food Offering Prayer. Student leader. This is the final closing of Grade 6 — the teacher chants with full presence alongside the class.

Teacher's closing line: one sentence for the whole year. The teacher writes this themselves — it is personal to the class, to what has moved through the room across the year. The instruction: be specific and true. Do not use a formula.

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: Masks are taken home after the performance as the Unit 4 portfolio artefact. Together with the Unit 1 philosophical reflection on rebirth, the Unit 2 Bardo concept map, the Unit 3 "Our Taras" drawing, and the Unit 3 C2 Pajapati journal entry, these constitute the Grade 6 portfolio. Grade 6 is complete. Thread into Grade 7: Grade 7 is "Teaching as Practice" — the highest level before Grade 8. Students will study bullying and empathy at genuine depth (not as a lesson unit but as a lived inquiry), design and teach a lesson to younger students, study the Medicine Buddha, and participate in Panglhabsol (the practice of purifying broken commitments). The Rewo Sang Chod (Smoke Offering Prayer) joins the prayer set in Grade 7. The thread from Grade 6: students have been inside the teachings — as students, as practitioners, as performers. In Grade 7, they become teachers. That reversal — from receiving to transmitting — is the Grade 7 threshold. It is a significant step, and it is prepared for by everything Grade 6 has done: the philosophical depth, the Bardo territory, the historical and archetypal figures, and the embodied performance. Pajapati note: her story has now been partially told across Grade 6 (two touchpoints: G6.U2.C2 and G6.U3.C2). Her full story — teaching, dying, implications for the tradition — is the Grade 8 novel study. Hold the thread carefully. Do not elaborate it further in Grades 6 or 7 beyond what the curriculum provides. Grade 8 needs students to arrive having held the name for a long time without having received the full account. Spiral connections: the Ngowa dedication (used to close the performance) connects directly to the bodhisattva aspiration from Grade 5 and the prayer-set practice from Grade 3. The performance itself — in its quality of ceremony — connects to the offering practices from Grades 3 and 4, brought now to full embodied scale.
