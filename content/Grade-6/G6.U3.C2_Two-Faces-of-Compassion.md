# G6.U3.C2 — Two Faces of Compassion
## Grade 6 · Unit 3 · Class 2 of 4 · Two Faces of Compassion
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A walk across the room → what it costs a human being to keep going when everything says stop
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Narrative as conceptual anchor — the story of Pajapati, told with enough historical detail that it becomes specific and real, anchors the abstract distinction between archetype and history in a lived example. Students remember particular people more durably than general principles.
**Bloom's Level:** Analyze — students examine the relationship between archetype (Tara) and history (Pajapati), asking what each one offers that the other cannot. They are not being asked to prefer one; they are being asked to see why both are necessary.
**Prior Class Summary:** C1 introduced Tara — her origin story, the two forms (White and Green), the vow to be present at every threshold of difficulty in female form. Students took both postures in their bodies and sketched both figures. They carry the question: what is the vow you are building?

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** who Pajapati was — her relationship to the Buddha, what she did, what happened, and why it matters — with enough historical weight to hold the name fully.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** the distinction between archetype and history: that Tara makes the quality of swift compassion graspable and portable, while Pajapati demonstrates that a human being actually embodied that quality in historical time.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** write a journal entry that holds both Tara and Pajapati, naming what each one offers that the other does not.
Students will **FEEL** the specific weight of a name they have carried since Grade 2 finally arriving with its full historical significance.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Portfolio notebooks or lined writing paper (one per student)
- Pencils
- Clear floor space for the walking body moment (desks arranged so students can walk the length of the room — rearrange before class begins)
- Written reference on the board: *Pajapati Gotami* and *ordained*
- **Plan B:** If floor space cannot be cleared, the walking moment can be adapted — students walk in place, slowly and deliberately, for ninety seconds, with the same instruction and reflection. This preserves the embodied quality, though with less spatial impact.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full Grade 6 prayer set (Dolma not yet introduced — that arrives in C3):
Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · *Food Offering Prayer (closing only)*

Student leader leads the set. The teacher holds the opening with full attention.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes
Teacher: *"Last class, Tara made a vow — to attain enlightenment in female form and to be present at every threshold of difficulty, forever, until samsara is emptied. Today: what does that vow look like when a human being actually carries it out? Not a bodhisattva on a lotus — a woman, in history, on foot."*

Take two or three responses — students may name historical figures they know, or say they are not sure. Receive all of it.

Then: "There is one woman in the tradition whose story the curriculum has been holding for you since Grade 2. Today it arrives. Her name is Pajapati Gotami."

---

#### Mini Lesson — 15 minutes

**Part 1 — Brief Tara review (3 minutes):**
Tara is an archetype. The word archetype means a symbolic figure that makes a quality graspable — it gives a face, a posture, a name to something that would otherwise remain abstract. The Tara archetype makes the quality of swift, active compassion portable across centuries and cultures. When you invoke Tara, you are not calling on an external power — you are calling the quality into presence. Archetypes are not less real than historical figures; they operate at a different level. They hold a truth that does not depend on a single time or place.

**Part 2 — Pajapati as historical Tara-figure (12 minutes):**
Now: what does it look like when a human being actually embodies that quality — not through divine birth, but through human life, with grief and refusal and walking?

The teacher tells the story, slowly, with full presence. Do not rush it.

---

Pajapati Gotami was the younger sister of Queen Maya. Queen Maya was the mother of Siddhartha — the man who would become the Buddha. Maya died shortly after giving birth. Pajapati took the newborn child and raised him as her own, nursing him alongside her own biological son. She loved him completely. She did not make a distinction between Siddhartha and her biological child; he was hers.

She watched him grow up in the palace. She watched him leave — the night he walked out of the gates and into the forest to seek awakening. She stayed. She raised her children. She waited.

When he became the Buddha and began to teach, the teaching reached her. She became a lay practitioner of growing seriousness. She meditated. She studied. She practised deeply.

Then her husband — the king — died. Everything she had organised her life around was gone. Her son the prince had left years ago. Her sister was dead. Now her husband was dead. She was a widow, in a society where widows had very limited roles. She had a question that would not leave her.

She went to the Buddha. She asked to be ordained as a nun — to enter the monastic community and practise fully, not as a householder but as someone who had given her whole life to the path.

He said no.

She asked again. No.

She went away. She came back. She cut her hair. She put on the ochre robes of a renunciant, even though she had not been formally ordained. She walked — barefoot, with a group of women who wanted the same thing — hundreds of miles to find him again.

He said no a third time.

Pause.

His attendant — Ananda, who had great compassion and was known as the one who remembered everything — went to the Buddha privately. He asked him a single question: "Are women capable of attaining enlightenment?" The Buddha said yes. Ananda said: "Then why won't you ordain them?"

There was a silence.

The Buddha ordained Pajapati — and the women who had walked with her — as the first Buddhist nuns. He gave them the full ordination, with vows.

She practised. She taught others. She became fully enlightened — not almost, not partly: fully, completely awakened, in that very life. When she was dying, the Buddha came to her. She died in his presence, having completed the path she had walked toward for decades.

---

After the story — do not immediately move on. Let it sit for a few seconds.

Then: "Tara shows what is possible — she is the symbol of swift compassion in female form, present at every threshold. Pajapati shows that it has actually been done — by a human woman, in historical time, through grief and refusal and barefoot walking and love."

**Vocabulary Moment:** Write on the board: *ordained*. "Ordained — formally admitted into the monastic community through a ceremony of taking vows. When Pajapati was ordained, it was not only her own liberation that changed. It changed what was possible for every woman in the Buddhist tradition from that moment forward — anywhere, in any century. This is one of the most consequential single acts in the history of the Dharma. Without that ordination, the lineage of Buddhist nuns does not exist."

**Body Moment:**
"Stand up. Space yourselves so you can walk to the back of the room."

Students stand. The teacher does not perform energy or urgency here — the instruction is quiet, deliberate.

"Walk to the back of the room and return. Do not rush. As you walk — even this short distance — hold this: Pajapati walked hundreds of miles, barefoot. She was refused three times. She kept walking. What is it like to decide to keep going when everything says stop?"

Students walk the length of the room and return. They sit.

"What did you feel?"

Take three or four responses. Receive them without sorting.

**Emotional Hook:** The cumulative weight of the name Pajapati — held since Grade 2, given its first real content in G6.U2.C2, now arriving with full historical gravity. For students who have carried this name for four years without knowing her story, today is the moment of arrival. The teacher does not point at this — it will happen on its own. For female students in particular, both the story of Pajapati's refusal to stop and her eventual full attainment may carry specific weight. Receive what comes.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 4 minutes
*"Tell your partner: what do you think Pajapati felt when the Buddha said no the third time — and what do you think made her keep going anyway?"*

Pairs work. This is an interpretive question; there is no single correct answer. What to listen for: students who distinguish between external motivation (wanting ordination) and internal motivation (love, conviction, something she could not abandon); students who express anger or surprise at the refusals and what they mean about the tradition; students who connect Pajapati's persistence to something in their own experience.

One or two pairs share briefly.

---

#### Activity — 10 minutes
Students write a journal entry in their portfolios titled: *"Tara and Pajapati — archetype and history."*

Prompt: *What does each one offer that the other doesn't? Why do we need both?*

This is a portfolio piece — it will be collected. The teacher circulates, asking: "Which one do you find more powerful right now? Why do you think that is?" These questions are not rhetorical — the answers are data.

Students who finish early: "Add one more paragraph: which quality of Pajapati do you think was hardest — the walking, the refusal, or the enlightenment — and why?"

---

#### Debrief — 4 minutes
Teacher: *"Pajapati is not a historical footnote. She is the reason there are Buddhist nuns — anywhere, ever, from that moment to now. And this curriculum has been holding her name since you were seven years old. You have been ready for longer than you knew. Her full story — her teaching, her dying, what it means — that comes in Grade 8. When it arrives, you will understand it differently because you have been carrying her name for so long."*

Do not give more of the story now. If students ask: "Her full story is Grade 8. I am not holding it back because it is too difficult — I am holding it because you will meet it better when you have had more time. That is how the curriculum works."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full prayer set — with Food Offering Prayer at close. Student leader.

Teacher's closing line: "Tara is the archetype — she shows us what is possible. Pajapati is the history — she shows us it has been done. Both of these are true at the same time."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The Pajapati journal entry (Tara + Pajapati, archetype + history) is a portfolio piece — collect it before or after class, or ensure students know it will be held. This is one of four Grade 6 portfolio artefacts. The instruction about not giving the full story is not optional — Grade 8 is built on students not having received it yet. If a student knows more of the story from family or personal knowledge, receive it warmly and say: "Yes — you already know some of her story. Hold that. It will matter in Grade 8." Female students note: This class is particularly significant for female students. Observe during the body moment, the journal activity, and the debrief — note in Your Reflection which students are most moved by the Pajapati material. This is not sentiment; it is important pedagogical data for the teachers who will receive these students in Grades 7 and 8. C3 introduces the Dolma prayer — it should feel like an arrival after the weight of C1 and C2. The floor must be clear for the walking body moment; arrange the room before students arrive. Spiral connections: Pajapati's name was first introduced in Grade 2 and held across the curriculum. The theme of perseverance under refusal connects to the bodhisattva path material from Grade 5. The ordination consequence — a single act that changes the entire tradition — connects to the discussion of karmic momentum from G6.U2.
