# G6.U3.C1 — The Swift Protectress
## Grade 6 · Unit 3 · Class 1 of 4 · The Swift Protectress
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Two postures (lotus and extended foot) → the two qualities of awakened compassion — sustaining and swift
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Embodied cognition — when students physically take the two Tara postures, their bodies encode the distinction between White and Green Tara more durably than description alone. The posture precedes the concept.
**Bloom's Level:** Analyze — students examine the origin story and the two forms, considering what each one represents and which quality is more present in themselves right now.
**Prior Class Summary:** The final class of Unit 2 was a sustained philosophical discussion — students defended their positions on the Bardo teaching with reasons. They carry the discussion's energy and the Bardo unit's closing understanding that practice matters regardless of belief. Now: from the territory of death, to the figure who stands at its threshold.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** who Tara is, where she comes from (the origin story of the princess who refused to change her form), and the two main forms — White Tara and Green Tara — with their distinctive iconographic features.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that both forms are expressions of the same awakened compassion — one sustaining, one swift — and that the tradition holds these as complementary, not in tension.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** take the two Tara postures and name the quality each one embodies; sketch both forms with their distinguishing features labelled.
Students will **FEEL** the quiet arrival that comes after intense philosophical territory — Tara as a figure who answers something the Bardo raised.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Drawing paper or portfolio notebooks (one per student)
- Pencils, basic coloring materials
- Images of White Tara and Green Tara (printed or displayed — at least one clear image of each)
- Printed or written reference: the Dharma term *Drolma* with its meaning
- **Plan B:** If images are unavailable, the teacher draws both figures on the board, narrating the iconographic features as they draw (this is itself a strong teaching practice — students often remember a drawing made in front of them more vividly than a printed image)

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full Grade 6 prayer set (Dolma not yet introduced — that arrives in C3):
Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam

Student leader leads the set. The teacher does not prompt or correct during the prayers — the student leader holds the space.

After the prayers, the teacher speaks: "Six classes in the Bardo. We went into the territory of death — the three Bardos, the ground luminosity, the moment of transition. Now: we meet the figure who stands at the threshold of that territory and says: I will not leave you there alone. Her name is Tara."

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes
Teacher: *"Has anyone heard of Tara before — in any context? What do you know?"*

Take every response. Some students will know her from home practice. Some will have seen her image in the school's shrines or prayer halls. Some will know the name from older siblings or parents. Some will know nothing — that is also fine.

Receive everything without correcting or sorting. After the responses settle: "Let me tell you who she is."

**Fallback (if no responses come):** "Look around — she is probably in this building right now. Have you passed her image today? What do you notice about how she looks?" This redirects to the visual environment without requiring prior knowledge.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 15 minutes

**The origin story:**
A long time ago, there was a princess who had devoted her life to practice and had given rise to Bodhichitta — the wish to attain enlightenment for the benefit of all beings. She was serious and committed. Some of the monks and teachers around her said: "You should pray to be reborn as a man. The path to full enlightenment is better accomplished in a male body."

She refused.

She said: "There is no male or female in the dharmadhātu — in the nature of reality. Those who cling to gender and say 'only in a male form can this be done' are blind. I will attain enlightenment in a female form. And in every future life — until samsara itself is emptied — I will continue to be born female." She continued her practice. She attained enlightenment. She became Tara — *she who saves*.

Pause. Let the story land.

**White Tara:**
Seated in the full lotus posture — completely still, completely encompassing. She has seven eyes: one in the center of her forehead, one in each palm, one in each sole of her foot. Seven eyes of all-seeing compassion. She looks in every direction at once, she misses nothing, she sustains everything. She is associated with long life, with healing, with protection from illness. Her quality is gentleness and endurance — she holds beings in safety the way still water holds light.

**Green Tara:**
Seated, but with one foot extended — slightly lowered toward the ground, ready to step forward. She does not wait. One hand is in the boon-granting mudra, palm outward and down — offering, opening, releasing. Her quality is immediacy. The tradition says that when you call on Green Tara, she responds before you finish the syllable. She is associated with rescue from the eight great fears — and with swift, active protection from whatever threatens beings. She is ready. She has been ready.

**The relationship between them:**
These are not two separate beings. They are two qualities of the same awakened compassion — sustaining and swift, encompassing and immediate. "Think of it this way: White Tara holds you when you need to be held. Green Tara moves when you need someone to move. A mother who sits with a sick child through the night — that is White Tara. A mother who runs to her child when they fall — that is Green Tara. Same love. Different expression."

**Vocabulary Moment:** Write on the board: *Drolma (སྒྲོལ་མ)*. "The traditional name for Tara is Drolma — which means 'she who liberates' or 'she who saves.' The prayer we will learn in Class 3 is called the Dolma prayer — named for her. The Sanskrit name Tara also means 'she who saves,' or 'she who carries across.' The name itself is a teaching: she brings beings across difficulty."

**Body Moment:**
Clear space. "We are going to try both postures."

(1) White Tara: Sit in full lotus or cross-legged, hands resting in the lap in the meditation mudra, eyes slightly open and downcast — soft, encompassing. "Feel what it is to be completely still and completely present. Seven eyes — seeing in every direction without moving. Nothing escapes your awareness. You do not need to reach out; your presence is the protection." Hold for thirty seconds.

(2) Green Tara: Shift — one foot extends forward slightly, off the cross-legged position, lowered toward the floor. One hand moves to the boon-granting mudra, palm out and downward. "Feel what it is to be ready to move. You are not tense — you are available. One step and you could be there." Hold for thirty seconds.

"Return to ordinary sitting. Which posture felt more natural to you right now, today? Remember that answer — it might tell you something."

**Emotional Hook:** The origin story — the princess who refused. She was told that she needed to change her form in order to progress. She said no. She said: I will do this as I am, and I will keep doing this as I am, in every life, until it is done. For any student who has been told — in any register, explicit or implicit — that they are not enough as they are, this story has specific weight. The teacher does not point at anyone. The story points itself.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 4 minutes
*"Tell your partner: what quality did you feel in the White Tara posture versus the Green Tara posture? Which one would you want beside you if you were afraid?"*

Pairs work. Teacher circulates and listens. What to listen for: students who are specific about what the posture felt like in the body; students who resist one posture or the other; students who say something unexpected about fear and what they need when they feel it.

Brief whole-group share: two or three pairs. No correction — receive everything.

---

#### Activity — 10 minutes
Students draw both forms of Tara in their portfolios or on drawing paper — a quick sketch of each form, labelling the distinguishing features:
- White Tara: seven eyes (forehead, palms, soles of feet), lotus posture, hands in meditation mudra
- Green Tara: extended foot, boon-granting mudra (one hand down and out), ready posture

These are working sketches, not finished art. Speed and attention matter more than beauty. Teacher circulates: "What is the most important thing about this figure? Does your sketch show it?"

These sketches go into the student's portfolio alongside the "Our Taras" drawing that comes in C4.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
Teacher: *"Tara made a vow — not to attain enlightenment and then rest in peace, but to be present at every threshold of difficulty, in female form, forever, until samsara is emptied. That vow is the green foot extended, ready to step forward. She is still keeping that vow. What is the vow you are building, through this program? What are you choosing to practice, not just believe?"*

Do not require an answer aloud. Let the question settle. If one or two students respond, receive it. Then: "Next class: what does this vow look like in a human life — in a real historical woman who walked barefoot across hundreds of miles because she would not stop. We will meet her."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full prayer set — same as opening, with Food Offering Prayer added at close. Student leader.

Teacher's closing line: "White Tara holds. Green Tara moves. Both of these are already inside you — the capacity to hold someone, and the capacity to step forward. This is what the tradition is pointing at."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The origin story — the princess who refused to change her form — is one of the most important stories in the curriculum for female students. Receive any strong responses to it with full attention; do not deflect, redirect, or soften it. This story is meant to land hard, especially at age 11–12. C2 pairs Tara-as-archetype with Pajapati-as-history — read the C2 notes carefully before teaching C1 so the full arc is in view. The Dolma prayer enters in C3 — confirm the school's authorised version with the resident teacher before C3, not after. The two postures (White and Green Tara) should be returned to in C4 when students name their personal Tara figure. Spiral connections: the "vow" concept connects to the bodhisattva vow students encountered in Grade 5 (Bodhichitta unit). The seven eyes of White Tara connect to the theme of all-pervading compassion from Grade 4. Grade 2 introduced Tara by name; students have held that name for four years and may respond with recognition.
