# G6.U4.C2 — The Figures and Their Meanings
## Grade 6 · Unit 4 · Class 2 of 5 · The Figures and Their Meanings
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Five Cham figures (appearance, posture, costume) → five distinct qualities that the tradition has embodied and danced for centuries
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Category formation through contrast — students learn the Cham figures not as a list but as a set of distinct types, each with a specific quality. By comparing them (protective vs. instructive, wrathful vs. skeletal, cosmic vs. historical), students build categories they can use to make a mask-design decision.
**Bloom's Level:** Analyze — students examine each figure's appearance, meaning, and connection to prior learning, then confirm their mask choice with reasoning.
**Prior Class Summary:** C1 introduced Cham — what it is, its traditional context, the basic Cham step. Students wrote a portfolio entry naming their preferred figure. They carry the body-change of the first deliberate Cham movement and the opening question of what it would feel like to wear a figure from the tradition.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the five key Cham figures — Yama, Mahakala, Citipati, Guru Rinpoche, and the Dharma protectors — with their appearance, quality, and function in the dance.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** the concept of wrathful compassion — that fierce, powerful, even frightening presence can be an expression of love and protection, not anger.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** briefly embody one figure's posture; confirm their mask choice with a written meaning profile.
Students will **FEEL** the strangeness and power of inhabiting a figure from the tradition — even briefly, without a costume — and finding that the body responds.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Images of each key Cham figure (Yama, Mahakala, Citipati, Guru Rinpoche, one Dharma protector) — printed or displayed; one image per figure
- Portfolio notebooks (one per student)
- Pencils
- Clear floor space for the body moment (desks arranged so students can stand in place with room to gesture)
- Board space to write the five figures with their qualities
- **Plan B:** If images are limited, the teacher describes each figure in vivid physical detail while students draw a quick impression from the description. This is itself effective — students who actively construct a visual remember it better than those who passively receive one. Begin sketching the figures on the board as they are described if this aids the class.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full Grade 6 prayer set including Dolma:
Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer

Student leader leads. After prayers: brief Cham movement warm-up — repeat the deliberate step from C1, one circuit or one minute. "Before we sit: let's wake up the body that knows this movement now."

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes
Teacher: *"Last class, each of you named the figure you most want to embody. Let's hear a few — who are they and why?"*

Take three or four responses. Listen for specificity: not just the name but the reason. Receive unusual choices with interest rather than redirection. Then: "Today we go deeper. You named a figure you feel drawn to — now we learn what that figure carries. The mask you make will represent this meaning, not just this appearance."

---

#### Mini Lesson — 15 minutes

The teacher presents the five key figures using images and brief, vivid descriptions. Write each name and its core quality on the board as it is introduced.

**Yama — Lord of Death (quality: the honesty of impermanence)**
A terrifying figure by appearance: bull-headed, dark-skinned, wearing a crown of skulls and elaborate robes. He holds the Wheel of Life in his arms and in his teeth. His face is fierce. In Cham, the Yama dancer does not become frightening — they embody the inescapable fact of impermanence, made visible. Yama's presence in the dance says: death is real. This moment is real. Your practice is real. The dancer does not threaten the audience; they remind it. Students encountered Yama holding the Wheel in the Unit 2 Bardo study. Now they can see what it would look like to wear him.

**Mahakala — the Great Black One (quality: fierce protective compassion)**
A wrathful protector figure: very dark, often depicted with many arms, wearing a crown of skulls, dancing in a wide, grounded posture. His expression is ferocious. He protects the Dharma and the ceremony. But his ferocity is not anger — it is the energy of a protector who will not let harm enter the space he guards. In Cham, the Mahakala dancer creates a perimeter of protection around the performance. This is expressed through fierce movement, strong gestures, and absolute presence.

**Citipati — the Skeleton Dancers (quality: the lightness of impermanence)**
Two figures in full skeleton costume — head to toe, bone-white, dancing together. They are a specific pair from a particular story: two monks who were so deep in meditation that they did not notice when a thief killed them, and continued in that meditative state until they found their own skeletons. In Cham, they dance. Their presence is not morbid — they move with a strange, fleet lightness. They are the dancing reminder that death is woven into life, and that even death can be met with ease.

**Guru Rinpoche — Padmasambhava (quality: the living lineage)**
Sometimes present in Cham as the teacher who brought the Dharma to Tibet. He appears in his recognisable form: richly robed, holding a khaṭvāṅga staff and a phurba dagger. His presence in the dance invokes the lineage — the unbroken thread of transmission from teacher to student across centuries. Students first encountered Guru Rinpoche in the prayer set's Seven-Line Prayer; they have been chanting to him since Grade 4.

**Dharma Protectors — various animal-headed figures (quality: boundary and protection)**
Multiple protector figures appear in different Cham traditions — lion-headed, garuda-headed, deer-headed, elephant-headed. They protect the ceremony from harmful influences, both external and internal. Their specific forms vary by lineage. In class context, students choosing a protector figure work with whatever images are available from the school's tradition.

**Vocabulary Moment:** Write on the board: *wrathful compassion*. "The tradition does not express compassion only through gentleness. Some of the most powerful forms of compassion are wrathful — fierce, protective, cutting through confusion without hesitation. Mahakala's ferocity is not anger. It is the energy of a parent who would step between their child and any threat, without a second thought. Knowing this changes how you look at the figure. A frightening mask can be an act of love."

**Body Moment:**
"Choose one figure from what I've just described. Stand up."

Students stand. "Take sixty seconds — hold that figure in your body. Not a costume — just posture, face, presence. If you are Yama: what is your posture? What is in your face — not rage, but inescapable truth? If you are Citipati: how do you hold a body that knows it has died? If you are Mahakala: where does your fierce energy gather?"

Sixty seconds. Teacher watches — does not correct or prompt during the hold.

"Normal posture, normal face. Sit down. What changed in the body when you held that figure?"

Take two or three responses. Receive them.

**Emotional Hook:** The strangeness and — for many students — the unexpected ease of briefly inhabiting a figure from the tradition. The body knows something that the mind is still catching up with. Receive whatever responses come to this moment without analysis.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 4 minutes
*"Tell your partner: which figure are you planning to make a mask of? What does that figure represent — and what does it mean to wear it? What quality will you be invoking when you put on that mask?"*

Pairs work. Some students will have changed their minds from C1; that is fine. What matters is specificity: can they say what the figure represents, not just what it looks like?

Brief whole-group share: three or four students name their figure and its quality. No lengthy explanation needed — name and one phrase.

---

#### Activity — 10 minutes

**Part 1 — Confirm mask figure and write meaning profile:**
Students confirm their mask choice and write a meaning profile in their portfolios:
- Name of figure
- Physical appearance (enough to guide the mask-making)
- What the figure represents in Cham
- Connection to this year's study (where have they encountered this figure or this quality before?)

Teacher circulates: "Does this description give you enough to make the mask from? What would you need to add?"

**Part 2 — Preliminary mask sketch (if time allows):**
Students begin a preliminary sketch of their mask design based on the meaning profile. This is a planning sketch, not a final design. Focus on: what is the most recognisable feature of this figure's face? (Yama's bull head; Mahakala's dark colour and wide-open eyes; Citipati's skeletal structure.) Start there.

---

#### Debrief — 4 minutes
Teacher: *"In Cham, the dancer does not disappear into the figure — they hold the figure while remaining aware that they are the dancer. That double awareness — I am here, and I am also this — is a form of mindfulness. The mask does not take over. You wear it. The same quality we practise in meditation: present to what is arising, not consumed by it. You practise that double awareness now."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full prayer set including Dolma. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer at close.

Teacher's closing line: "Next class: the movements go into the body. Come with your figure in mind."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: Students should have their mask figure confirmed by the end of C2. Read the confirmed figures before C3 — knowing which figures the class is working with allows the teacher to connect specific movement qualities to specific figures during C3 (Yama's grave weight, Mahakala's fierce wide stance, Citipati's skeletal lightness). Gather mask-making materials before C4: card stock or thick paper, paint or markers, elastic for tying, any decorative materials available. Simple is the right standard — the mask's meaning matters more than its elaborateness. Ensure physical space is arranged before students arrive for C3 — the movement class needs the room fully cleared. If the school has Cham masks or images on site, consider bringing them into the space before C1 or C2 so students encounter them in the environment before the teaching. Spiral connections: Yama has been encountered in G6.U2 (the Bardo unit) and in Grade 5 (the Wheel of Life). Mahakala is a Dharma protector encountered in various contexts across the curriculum. Guru Rinpoche's Seven-Line Prayer has been in the prayer set since Grade 4.
