# G6.U2.C5 — Concept Mapping the Bardo
## Grade 6 · Unit 2 · Class 5 of 6 · Concept Mapping the Bardo
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A student holding their own map — looking at it whole — and asking "is this complete?" → the work of synthesis: combining five classes of learning into a coherent, legible representation that could teach the Bardo to someone who wasn't there

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Synthesis as a visible act — the concept map makes the student's understanding visible as an object they can examine and evaluate. The class gives students the criteria for a complete map (structure, connections, personal understanding) and asks them to assess their own work against those criteria before refining it. This metacognitive loop — making, evaluating, revising — is a higher-order move than simply adding more content.

**Bloom's Level:** Analyze — the mini lesson frames what it means for a concept map to be analytically complete (not just informationally dense). The synthesis move — connecting the three Bardos to each other and to the Unit 1 rebirth teaching — is the core analytic demand of the class. The personal annotation layer requires students to take a position on what they find compelling and what resists, which is evaluative reasoning grounded in the evidence of five classes.

**Prior Class Summary:** C4 addressed the third Bardo — becoming, karmic momentum, what continues and what shapes it. Students completed the third section of their concept map. They carry the phrase "karmic momentum" and the question they held quietly: what tendency would I most want to carry forward, and what would I most want to leave behind?

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** the three criteria for a complete Bardo concept map: structural clarity (the three Bardos in sequence), connection (how the Bardos relate to each other and to the Unit 1 rebirth teaching), and personal understanding (the student's own synthesis, not a transcription of lesson content).

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that synthesis — combining elements into a coherent whole — requires more than accumulation: it requires the student to take a position on how the parts relate, what matters most, and what their own understanding is.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** produce a concept map that is legible to a reader who was not present in the unit, with at least one personal annotation that names what the student finds compelling and what resists.

Students will **FEEL** the satisfaction — and the slight vertigo — of holding one's own work as a whole and assessing it honestly: not "is this done?" but "is this true to what I actually understand?"

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set (Units 1–2 set):
Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

*Note: The Food Offering Prayer appears in closing only. The Dolma (Tara Prayer) is not yet introduced.*

Student leader leads. After the prayers, teacher distributes concept maps face-down. "Don't look yet."

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "You've mapped all three Bardos. Before we refine — what is the most important thing you've understood about the Bardo teaching, from the whole unit? Not the most interesting — the most important. The thing that would be lost if it were left out."

Take 3–4 responses. Receive each carefully. The range of answers is genuinely useful data — it shows what has landed for different students. Do not adjudicate which is most correct. After the last response: "All of those are things that are in the teaching. Notice which ones you named — and whether they're visible in your map. Turn it over."

Students turn their maps face up. Sixty seconds of quiet looking.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 5 minutes

Brief. This is the only class where the mini lesson should be genuinely short — the time is needed for the work.

Teacher: "What makes a concept map complete? Not long — complete. Here are three things to check."

Write on the board:

**1. Structure** — Can someone who wasn't here read your map and understand the sequence? Is it clear what the three Bardos are, in order, and what happens in each?

**2. Connections** — Does your map show how the Bardos relate to each other? How the teaching connects to what we studied in Unit 1 — the consciousness stream, the flame analogy, karmic momentum? These connections are the hardest part of the map — and the most important.

**3. Your understanding** — Is there something in the map that is yours? Not a transcription of the lesson — your synthesis. What you find compelling. What still resists. What the teaching means when you apply it to your own experience. This is the personal annotation layer.

"A map that has all three of these is a complete map. A map that has only the first one is a well-organised set of notes. The difference is the second and third — the connections and the personal layer. That's what we're building today."

**Vocabulary Moment:** "*Synthesis* — from the Greek, meaning 'to put together.' Not the same as summary (which recounts what was said) and not the same as analysis (which takes apart). Synthesis combines elements into something new — a coherent whole that shows how the parts relate. Your concept map is a synthesis of five classes of work. It should be possible for someone who wasn't here to pick it up and learn the Bardo teaching from it."

**Body Moment:** Students hold their concept maps with both hands — not reading, just holding the whole thing. "Look at it as a complete object. Is it complete? Where is it thin? Where does the connection break?" Sixty seconds of honest assessment. "Write one word somewhere in the margin: what is the most underdeveloped part?"

**Emotional Hook:** The satisfaction of having mapped a complex, ancient system from scratch over five classes — and the productive pride of holding that map as one's own work. Teacher names it plainly: "You built this. From a blank piece of paper in the first class. That is not a small thing. And it's not done yet — but it will be."

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: what is one thing about the Bardo teaching that you still find genuinely difficult to accept or understand? Not something you've resolved — something that still resists. Name it as specifically as you can."

Structure: each partner speaks for about ninety seconds. Teacher circulates — listening for the specific shape of resistance. What resists reveals what is being genuinely engaged with: discomfort with the lack of empirical proof, difficulty with the idea that visions arise from the mind's own nature, uncertainty about what karmic momentum means in a context without a fixed self.

---

#### Activity — 20 minutes

Students work. The task has three parts — teacher writes them on the board alongside the three criteria from the mini lesson:

**Part 1 — Fill gaps (8 min):** Review the three Bardo sections. Is each one complete? Does each one include: what the Bardo is, what arises in it, the teaching's instruction, and the connection to living experience? Add what is missing. Clarify what is unclear.

**Part 2 — Draw connections (7 min):** Using a different colour, draw connecting lines and annotations between:
- The three Bardos (how does one lead to the next? what are the threshold points?)
- The Bardo teaching and the Unit 1 teaching on rebirth (where does the consciousness-stream concept appear? where does karmic momentum connect to the flame analogy?)
- The Bardo teaching and the concept of rigpa (how does rigpa appear in each of the three Bardos — or only in some?)

**Part 3 — Personal annotation layer (5 min):** In a distinct section of the map — or as a border annotation — write: "What I find most compelling about this teaching. What I find hardest to accept. What I would need to believe — or what kind of evidence I would need — for the teaching to be fully convincing to me."

Teacher circulates throughout with high-quality engagement: "Tell me about this connection you've drawn — what made you put it there?" "What did you write in the personal layer? Why this particular resistance?" "If someone who knew nothing about Buddhism picked up your map, what's the first thing they'd need to understand before the rest would make sense?"

**Scaffold for students who are struggling with Part 2:** "Start with one connection — the most obvious one. Can you draw an arrow from the first Bardo to the second? Label the arrow: what determines whether a being moves from one to the other? What is the threshold?" Then: "Can you draw a line from 'karmic momentum' on your map to the flame analogy from Unit 1? What is the connection in words?" One connection at a time.

**Extension for students who finish all three parts:** "Write a title for your map — not 'Bardo Concept Map.' A title that captures what the teaching is fundamentally about, in your own words. Then write three sentences explaining why you chose that title."

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

Opening question: "Next class is the final class of Unit 2. The question for that class is: do you believe it? Not a rhetorical question — a real one. Come ready to actually answer it, with reasons. What would it mean to believe the Bardo teaching? What would it mean to partially believe it? What would it mean to find it compelling but unprovable?"

Take 2–3 brief responses — not full arguments, just initial positions. "Good. Hold those. You'll need them."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full closing prayer set:
Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Teacher collects concept maps. They will be returned for C6 so students can refer to them during the discussion.

Teacher's closing line: "Next class: the question. Come with an answer — and come with the reason for it."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: Completed concept maps are the portfolio artefact for Unit 2 — collect them at the end of C5. Return them at the start of C6 so students can use them during the discussion; after C6, file them in the student portfolio. The personal annotation layer is the most important part of the map for assessment purposes — it shows the quality of genuine engagement. A student whose personal layer is thin ("I find it interesting") has not done the work; a student whose personal layer names a specific epistemological question or a specific moment from their experience that the teaching connects to has done excellent work. C6 is a structured philosophical discussion — prepare by thinking about the range of positions students might take and the arguments they might make. The question "do you believe it?" has four genuinely valid responses: belief, partial belief (plausible as a working hypothesis), suspension of judgment (insufficient evidence either way), and disbelief. All are philosophically honest if argued with reasons. The teacher's own position should remain present in the room — not hidden, but also not authoritative. If students ask directly: "What do you believe?" — the teacher should answer honestly and briefly, then redirect: "What matters more right now is what you've concluded. Why?"
