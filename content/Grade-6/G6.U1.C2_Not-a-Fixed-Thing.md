# G6.U1.C2 — Not a Fixed Thing
## Grade 6 · Unit 1 · Class 2 of 4 · Not a Fixed Thing
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A river with a name and a recognisable character, but not a single fixed drop of water → the Buddhist teaching of anattā — no fixed, permanent self — as the prerequisite for understanding what rebirth actually means

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Conceptual bridging — students already hold a common-sense notion of personal identity ("I am the same person I was five years ago"). The lesson places that assumption under pressure by building toward a more accurate description of what "I" actually is: a process, not a thing. The river analogy bridges the familiar and the philosophically demanding.

**Bloom's Level:** Analyze — students are asked to examine the claim "there is no fixed self" rigorously: what does it mean, what does it not mean, what is the difference between nihilism and the Buddhist position, and how does this teaching complicate the question "what survives death?"

**Prior Class Summary:** C1 introduced the Buddhist view of rebirth — the consciousness stream (*vijñāna-santāna*), the flame-to-flame analogy, and the distinction from a fixed soul traveling unchanged. Students wrote their starting-position paragraph on what happens after death. They carry the question of what "continues" if not a fixed self.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** the Buddhist teaching of *anattā* (Pali) / *anātman* (Sanskrit) — commonly translated "no-self" or "non-self" — as one of the three marks of existence, alongside impermanence and suffering.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that anattā is not nihilism ("you don't exist at all") but a more precise description of what "you" actually is: a collection of constantly changing, interdependent processes — body, sensation, perception, mental formations, consciousness — none of which is a fixed, standalone "thing."

Students will **BE ABLE TO** connect the anattā teaching to the rebirth teaching: if there is no fixed self even now, the question "what survives death?" becomes more subtle — not "does the self survive?" but "what is the self in the first place?"

Students will **FEEL** the productive discomfort of finding that the "I" which seemed solid and obvious is, on closer inspection, more fluid and process-like than expected — not destabilising, but genuinely surprising.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set (Units 1–2 set):
Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

*Note: The Food Offering Prayer appears in closing only. The Dolma (Tara Prayer) is not yet introduced.*

Student leader leads. Teacher waits for the room to settle after the final prayer, then speaks — no rush.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "Last class I gave the analogy of a flame passing from candle to candle — something transmitted without a fixed thing traveling. Today I want to start with a simpler question, right here. Are you the same person you were five years ago? In what ways yes — and in what ways no?"

Take 3–4 responses. Listen carefully. Students will typically offer both: "Yes, I'm still me — same family, same memories." And: "No — I'm taller, I think differently, my interests changed." Both are right, and the tension between them is exactly the entry point.

Then: "Here's what the Buddhist teaching says about that tension: you are more of a *process* than a *thing*. And that changes everything about how we understand what 'you' are — and what it means for 'you' to continue after death. That's where we're going today."

**Fallback if students are reluctant:** "Write down three ways you're the same person you were at age six, and three ways you're different. Then share one from each column." This externalises the thinking and gives less confident speakers something to hold.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

**The teaching — anattā:**

Teacher writes *anattā* on the board (Pali spelling), with *anātman* beside it (Sanskrit).

"This is one of the most important teachings in the entire Buddhist curriculum — and one of the hardest. I'll be as precise as I can. *Anattā* means: there is no unchanging, permanent self at the core of experience. What we call 'I' is a collection of constantly changing processes."

Write the five on the board:
1. Body (form) — *rūpa*
2. Sensation (feeling tone) — *vedanā*
3. Perception — *saññā*
4. Mental formations (thoughts, intentions, emotions) — *saṅkhāra*
5. Consciousness — *viññāna*

"These five — the 'five aggregates' — are what we are. All five are always changing. All five are interdependent. None of them, on its own, is a fixed 'you.' And there is nothing hiding behind all five that is the real, permanent you. What we call the self is what these five look like from the inside — in motion, in relationship, moment by moment."

**What anattā is not:**
"This is not nihilism. The teaching does not say you don't exist — it says you don't exist the way you think you do. You exist as a process — the way a flame exists, or a river. Real, recognisable, continuous — but not made of any fixed substance."

**The river analogy:**
"A river has a name. The Ganges is the Ganges. It has a character — its speed, its colour, the shape of its bed, the history of its course. But not one drop of water in the Ganges today was there ten years ago. All of it has moved on. What makes it the same river? The shape of its bed. The momentum of its flow. The continuity of its process. Consciousness is like this. 'You' are like this."

**The relevance to rebirth:**
"If there is no fixed self even now — if 'you' are already a stream of changing processes — then the question 'does the self survive death?' becomes more subtle. What the Buddhist teaching says survives is the stream — the momentum — not a 'you' as a fixed thing. What passes from one life to the next is more like the river's momentum than the river's water."

**Body Moment:** "Look at your hand." Students look at their own hands, genuinely. "Is this the same hand you had when you were five? Biologically — most of the cells are different. Completely replaced. Your bones are heavier. Your skin is new. And yet you recognise it as yours. What makes it yours?" Allow ten seconds for the genuine question to land. "The answer is the continuity of the process — not the permanence of the substance. The same logic applies to everything you call 'yourself.'"

**Vocabulary Moment:** Written on the board alongside *anattā*:

*"Anattā (Pali) / anātman (Sanskrit) — 'no-self' or 'non-self.' One of the three marks of existence in Buddhist teaching, alongside impermanence (anicca) and suffering (dukkha). It does not mean you don't exist — it means you don't exist the way you think you do: as a fixed, permanent, independent thing. You exist processually — as the river exists."*

**Emotional Hook:** The mild existential discomfort of discovering that the self is less solid than it felt. Teacher names it directly: "This idea tends to land differently for different people. Some find it fascinating — even freeing. If there's no fixed 'me' to defend and protect, that's a kind of relief. Others find it unsettling — if there's no fixed self, what am I? Both responses are philosophically appropriate. Neither one is wrong. Hold whichever one you're having."

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: if you are more of a process than a fixed thing — what does that say about what it means to 'be yourself'? Does it change anything?"

Structure: partners speak for about ninety seconds each. Teacher circulates.

What to listen for: Students who collapse anattā into nihilism ("so nothing matters?") — gently correct: "The river still has banks, a direction, a history. It still matters where it flows." Students who grasp it quickly and find it liberating — note them. Students who are clearly unsettled — receive this as philosophically appropriate, not as a problem.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

Journal entry 2. Teacher reads the prompt aloud:

*"What I understand by 'no fixed self' — and how it connects to the rebirth teaching."*

"Write what you understand — and write what you don't understand. If something resists, name it. 'I understand this part but not this part' is an excellent journal entry."

Teacher circulates with genuine questions: "What's the hardest part of this idea for you? What is it that resists?" Not asking to fix the confusion — asking to make it legible. "Write that down — the resistance itself."

**Scaffold for students who are stuck:** "Start with the river analogy. Write: 'The river is like...' and explain what makes it the same river even though all the water changes. Then say: 'consciousness is like this because...'"

**Extension for students who finish early:** "Write a short dialogue between someone who believes in a fixed soul and someone who holds the anattā teaching. What is the strongest argument each side makes? What does each side find hardest to answer?"

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

Opening question: "If there is no fixed self — even now — then the question 'what survives death?' is more interesting than it first appears. Not 'does the self survive?' but something prior to that: 'what is the self in the first place?' We've been trying to answer that today. Who has an answer? Who has a better question?"

Take 2–3 responses. Do not resolve.

"We'll come back to this — directly — in Unit 2, when we walk into the Bardo. The Bardo teaching will ask: if the self is a process, what happens to the process when the body stops? That question is waiting for us. Today: just hold the idea that 'you' are more river than rock."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full closing prayer set:
Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Teacher's closing line: "You looked at your hand today and found a question. That is exactly the right thing to have found."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The anattā teaching is one of the most philosophically significant and cognitively demanding concepts in the entire Dharma curriculum. Some students will grasp it quickly and find it liberating — the release from a defended, fixed sense of self can feel like an opening. Others will find it genuinely threatening: "if there's no fixed self, what am I? Am I nothing?" Both responses are philosophically appropriate and should be named as such in the class — not managed away. The teaching is not meant to destabilise students; it is meant to liberate from the over-attachment to a fixed, defended sense of self that causes much of the suffering described in the three poisons. It is also the prerequisite for genuine compassion: if the boundary between "me" and "not me" is less solid than it appears, then the suffering of others is not entirely foreign. Spiral connections: anattā revisits — though does not name — the concept of interdependence that students encountered in Grade 4 (the web of causes and conditions). It will be revisited philosophically in Grade 8 in the context of the Twelve Links of Dependent Origination. For now: plant the seed clearly and precisely, without over-elaborating. Students do not need to master this concept today — they need to have genuinely met it.
