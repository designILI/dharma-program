# G6.U2.C6 — Do You Believe?
## Grade 6 · Unit 2 · Class 6 of 6 · Do You Believe?
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Standing in a room with others who disagree — defending a philosophical position with reasons → the epistemological question at the heart of the Bardo teaching: on what grounds do we hold a belief, what kind of evidence is relevant, and what does it mean to hold a teaching as a working hypothesis rather than a proven fact?

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Structured controversy as a learning structure — physically taking positions in the room makes the discussion visible, embodied, and dynamic. Position-changing is explicitly permitted and honoured, so students learn that intellectual movement is a sign of engagement rather than inconsistency. The teacher facilitates rather than adjudicates: asking for evidence, introducing challenges, holding space for genuine disagreement without resolving it prematurely.

**Bloom's Level:** Analyze — the discussion requires students to evaluate the Bardo teaching as a whole, distinguish between kinds of evidence (empirical, experiential, philosophical consistency), construct and defend an argument, and respond to counter-arguments. This is the highest-demand activity of the unit, and the one most directly in the Analyze register.

**Prior Class Summary:** C5 completed the Bardo concept maps — structure, connections, and personal annotation layer. Students came with one thing they still find hard to accept or understand. They were asked to come to C6 with a position and a reason. They carry the question: "Do you believe it?"

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** the distinction between belief, agnosticism, and disbelief as philosophical positions, and the concept of *epistemology* — the study of how we know what we know, and what makes knowledge justified.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that in the Buddhist tradition, the Bardo teaching is primarily practical rather than primarily metaphysical: the invitation is to practise now — to cultivate the quality of awareness that will be available in the Bardo — whether or not the literal teaching is held as true.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** defend a philosophical position on the Bardo teaching with reasons, respond to at least one counter-argument, and articulate what kind of evidence would change their view.

Students will **FEEL** the specific combination of intellectual engagement and personal exposure that comes from genuinely defending a philosophical position in a room of peers — the aliveness of a real discussion about something that actually matters.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set (Units 1–2 set):
Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

*Note: The Food Offering Prayer appears in closing only. The Dolma (Tara Prayer) is not yet introduced.*

Student leader leads. Teacher: "Today's class is a philosophical discussion. The question is real — it is not rhetorical, it is not a test with a correct answer. Having studied the Bardo teaching carefully over five classes, do you believe it? I want to hear your reasoning — not just your conclusion. The reasoning is what matters."

Return concept maps to students. "You can refer to these during the discussion."

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "Before we begin: what is 'belief,' actually? Is it the same as knowing? Is it different from 'thinking it might be true'? Is it a choice — can you decide to believe something? Or does belief just happen?" Take 4–5 responses. This is the philosophical framing for the discussion, and it is important: if students don't have a clear sense of what "belief" means, the question "do you believe it?" cannot be answered precisely.

Likely responses will include: "Belief is when you think something is true without being certain." "Knowing is when you have proof, belief is when you don't." "I think you can choose to believe something." The teacher does not resolve these — they sit as open distinctions that the discussion will navigate.

Then: "Good. Keep all of that in mind. Because the quality of your answer to today's question depends on what you mean by belief."

---

#### Mini Lesson — 5 minutes

Brief. Teacher writes three positions on the board:

**1. Belief** — "I find the teaching compelling. It is consistent with what I observe about the mind, about causality, about the nature of practice. I hold it as likely true."

**2. Agnosticism** — "I find it plausible but not provable. I hold it as a working hypothesis — it shapes how I practise without requiring certainty. I suspend judgment on whether it is literally true."

**3. Disbelief** — "I find it implausible — inconsistent with what I understand about consciousness and causality. The evidence available to me does not support it."

"All three are valid philosophical positions if argued with reasons. None of them is the Buddhist answer, the correct answer, or the expected answer. What makes a position philosophically strong is not the conclusion but the reasoning. Tell me why — not just what."

**Vocabulary Moment:** "*Epistemology* — from the Greek *episteme* (knowledge) and *logos* (study). The branch of philosophy concerned with how we know what we know, and what justifies a belief. An epistemological question is: on what grounds? What makes this claim credible? What would count as evidence for or against it? The question 'do you believe the Bardo teaching?' is an epistemological question — it asks: on what basis? What kind of evidence is relevant? What would change your mind?"

**Body Moment:** Students stand. Teacher: "If you currently lean toward belief — move to the left side of the room. Agnostic — the middle. Leaning toward disbelief — the right. You can move during the discussion. Moving is encouraged. If an argument genuinely changes your position, move. Position-changing is a sign that you're listening and reasoning — not a sign of weakness."

Students move. Teacher observes the distribution without commenting on it. This makes the discussion visible and embodied — and makes position-changing a natural, respected part of the class.

**Emotional Hook:** The specific quality of genuine philosophical exposure — being asked to stand somewhere in the room based on what one actually thinks, and to defend it to peers. Not performative debate. Real inquiry with real stakes.

---

#### Activity — Structured Philosophical Discussion, 20–25 minutes

The teacher facilitates — does not lecture, does not adjudicate. The teacher asks, challenges, connects, and holds the space.

**Opening move:** "Who wants to start? State your position and your best reason for it." Take the first volunteer. Then: "Who holds a different position? What's your strongest argument?"

**Facilitation moves the teacher uses throughout:**

- **Pressing for evidence:** "What makes you say that? Is that an empirical observation, a philosophical argument, or an appeal to what the tradition says? Those are three different kinds of evidence."
- **Introducing counter-arguments:** "What would someone who disagrees with you say? Can you make that argument yourself — and then respond to it?"
- **Connecting to the teaching:** "Where in the Bardo teaching does that argument appear? Is there something in what we studied that addresses your objection — or doesn't?"
- **Honouring position changes:** If a student moves across the room: "You moved. What changed? What argument did you find you couldn't answer?"
- **Pushing the agnostic position:** "Agnosticism is intellectually honest — but what would you need in order to move off it? Is there any evidence that could shift you toward belief or disbelief? If not, why not?"
- **Pushing the belief position:** "What is the strongest argument against your position? Have you genuinely grappled with it?"
- **Pushing the disbelief position:** "Does the materialist account of consciousness — that it is entirely brain-dependent — fully account for everything the Bardo teaching is describing? What does it leave out?"

Teacher holds the room without resolving the question. The goal is not consensus — it is the quality of the reasoning.

Near the end (last five minutes of the discussion): "Before we close — what would change your mind? Each position: what evidence, what argument, what kind of experience would move you? If nothing could move you, that is worth examining."

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

Opening question: "In the Buddhist tradition, the teaching on the Bardo is not primarily about whether you believe in it. It is primarily about how you practise now — because the quality of your awareness now is what will be available in the Bardo, whenever that comes. Belief or disbelief: practice is the point. What does that say about the relationship between belief and practice? Can you practise well without believing the teaching literally?"

Take 2–3 responses. Do not resolve.

"That question — whether belief is necessary for practice to be genuine — is one of the most important questions in Buddhist philosophy. You don't have to answer it today. But carry it."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full closing prayer set:
Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Teacher collects concept maps from students — into portfolio folders.

Teacher's closing line: "Six classes. The Bardo. You walked through it — and you came out the other side with your own thinking. That is what this unit asked of you."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The Bardo concept maps are the portfolio artefact for Unit 2 — collect them at the end of C6 and file them. Unit 3 begins next: White Tara and Green Tara. The transition is significant. Students have been in the territory of death and the Bardo for six classes — the emotional register has been serious, sometimes heavy, and the philosophical demands have been high. Tara is the figure who stands at the threshold of difficult transitions — who, according to the teaching, appears when called and does not abandon those in extremity. The transition from Unit 2 to Unit 3 should feel like emerging from a deep dive into more open air. Tara is, among other things, relief. The Dolma (Tara Prayer) is introduced in Unit 3 — its first appearance should feel like an arrival, not an administrative addition to the prayer set. Brief students on this: "Next unit, you'll add a new prayer to the set. It is a prayer to Tara. After what we've been through — the dying process, the Bardo, the question of what comes after — Tara is the figure who stands at the threshold of all of that and says: I will not leave you there alone." The discussion in C6 may surface students who are processing difficult personal material — grief, fear of death, anxiety about what happens to people they love. If this arises, receive it with care. The school's pastoral process should be used for anything that warrants it. Spiral connection: the epistemological frame introduced in C6 (belief, agnosticism, disbelief; what counts as evidence; what would change your mind) is the philosophical frame that the curriculum will use across Grades 7 and 8 whenever a teaching is examined critically. Students who engage genuinely in this discussion are learning a philosophical skill they will use for the rest of the program.
