# G6.U2.C3 — The Mountains of Tibet
## Grade 6 · Unit 2 · Class 3 of 6 · The Mountains of Tibet
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Sitting quietly with eyes closed, a moment of awareness without content → the Bardo of dharmata — the ground luminosity, the visionary appearances, the instruction to move toward light — and the recognition that what the ancient text describes is not foreign but has a living analogue in the student's own experience of awareness

**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Recognition transfer — the class is designed so that students encounter the ground luminosity teaching and then, through the 90-second awareness practice, have a micro-experience that the teacher explicitly connects to what the teaching describes. The cognitive move is: "this ancient text is describing something you have already, in small form, touched." This makes the teaching not merely information but a recognition — which is precisely what the word *rigpa* means.

**Bloom's Level:** Analyze — students examine the Bardo of dharmata as a structured system (sequence, phenomenology, instruction, metaphorical extension), annotate it on their concept map with enough precision to represent it to a reader who wasn't present, and articulate the connection between the visionary appearances and the mind's own nature. The highest demand: "what in living experience resembles this Bardo?" — this is metaphorical analysis requiring conceptual transfer.

**Prior Class Summary:** C2 examined the dying process in detail — the dissolution of the four elements, awareness becoming fully exposed — and introduced grief as a living person's Bardo. The Pajapati grief thread appeared briefly. Students carry the concept of dissolution (what falls apart when what has been held together dissolves), the phrase "stay present," and the weight of Pajapati's name having finally become real.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** the Tibetan teaching of *gzhi'i 'od gsal* — ground luminosity — as the fundamental radiance of awareness, fully exposed at the moment of death; and the sequence of the Bardo of dharmata, including the arising of peaceful and wrathful visions and the instruction to move toward the gentle lights.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the visions of the Bardo of dharmata are not external events but arise from the mind's own nature — its accumulated love, fear, and habitual patterns — and that the practitioner's task is recognition: seeing what arises as mind, not as external threat.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** annotate the Bardo of dharmata section of their concept map with structural detail, phenomenological description, the teaching's instruction, and a personal note on the metaphorical reading — what in living experience has the quality of this Bardo.

Students will **FEEL** the vertiginous recognition that a state described in an ancient Tibetan death manual has a living analogue in the student's own experience of sitting quietly — that the tradition is not describing something alien, but something familiar at an extreme.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 6 prayer set (Units 1–2 set) — chanted with particular care today:
Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

*Note: The Food Offering Prayer appears in closing only. The Dolma (Tara Prayer) is not yet introduced.*

Student leader leads. Teacher: "Chant slowly today. Let each prayer actually land before moving to the next." After the prayers settle, a longer pause than usual before speaking. Then:

"Today we go into the second Bardo — the Bardo of dharmata. This is the most extraordinary and the most difficult territory in the teaching. Listen carefully."

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes

Teacher: "In the Bardo of dying — which we studied last class — the elements dissolve and awareness becomes fully exposed. Naked. Unfiltered. What comes next?" Take responses from students drawing on C1 and C2 knowledge: the Bardo of dharmata — the nature of reality. Then:

"What do you imagine that looks like? If awareness is fully exposed — no body, no ordinary mind, no habits of thought to filter what arises — what do you imagine might appear?" Take 3–4 responses. Accept all. Do not correct or affirm — this is genuine inquiry, and the range of answers (peace, terror, brightness, nothing) is all appropriate and will find its place in the teaching.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

Teacher moves through the Bardo of dharmata teaching with care and precision. This is the densest content of the unit — pace accordingly.

**The ground luminosity:**

"After the dying process is complete — after all elements have dissolved and ordinary consciousness has fallen away — there is a moment described in the Tibetan teaching as the ground luminosity. The Tibetan is གཞིའི་འོད་གསལ — *gzhi'i 'od gsal*. *Gzhi* means ground — the fundamental basis. *'Od gsal* means clear light, luminosity. Together: the fundamental radiance of awareness, unclouded by any thought, habit, or filter, directly exposed."

"The teaching says: for a practitioner who has spent years in meditation — who has, in sitting practice, touched this quality of bare awareness repeatedly — this moment is recognisable. It is the same quality they have touched countless times in meditation, now fully revealed without any covering. Recognition of this quality — *rigpa*, the recognition we studied in C1 — equals liberation. Not later. At that moment."

"Why? Because what is recognised is the true nature of mind — not foreign, not threatening, but the practitioner's own nature, always present beneath all the movement of thought and habit. Recognising it is like suddenly seeing the sky that has always been there behind the clouds. The clouds were never the sky. They were just what was in the way."

**If recognition doesn't happen — the arising of the visions:**

"If the ground luminosity is not recognised — if the practitioner is swept past it by disorientation or fear — the Bardo of dharmata continues. What arises next is a sequence of vivid appearances."

"The teaching describes peaceful deities arising first — figures of extraordinary colour, light, and beauty, emanating a brilliance that the ordinary mind cannot quite face. These peaceful figures represent the natural qualities of awareness: compassion, wisdom, openness. Alongside them — not in opposition but in contrast — appear harsher lights: more ordinary colours, more familiar, less intense."

"Here is the crucial point: the teaching instructs the being in the Bardo to move toward the bright, intense, beautiful light of the peaceful deities — not away from it. The impulse is always to move toward what is familiar and manageable. The teaching says: resist that impulse. The gentle lights are the natural radiance of your own awareness. The familiar lights are the ordinary mind's habitual tendencies — what you default to out of comfort, not out of wisdom."

"Later, wrathful figures arise — fierce, loud, overwhelming. The same instruction holds: these too are your own nature, not external threats. They are the same compassion and wisdom that appeared as peaceful figures, now in a form that shocks through the obscurations. Don't run. Recognise."

**The crucial insight — mind's own projection:**

"These visions are not external. They arise from the mind itself — from the person's own patterns, their own unresolved states, their accumulated love and fear. The mountains of Tibet are a metaphor here: they have witnessed countless beings navigating this territory, each bringing their own mind into the brightness. The mountains do not judge what arises. They have simply been there, through all of it."

**The metaphorical reading:**

"The Bardo of dharmata is also a description of something that happens in living experience — in any period of extreme disorientation, when the ordinary structures of meaning collapse and the raw ground becomes exposed. Illness. Grief. Trauma. A period of complete uncertainty, when the familiar ways of being in the world are no longer available. These have a dharmata quality: what arises is intense, visionary in its way, difficult to face. The teaching that applies is the same: stay present. Move toward what is real rather than what is comfortable. Don't run from what arises. Recognise."

**Vocabulary Moment:** Written clearly on the board:

*"Ground luminosity — Tibetan: གཞིའི་འོད་གསལ (gzhi'i 'od gsal). The fundamental radiance of awareness, unclouded by thought or habit, exposed at the moment of death. The 'ground' is what is always there beneath ordinary experience. Meditation practice is partly the practice of touching this ground while alive — so that it is recognisable when encountered in the Bardo. Every moment of stable, clear awareness is a small rehearsal."*

**Body Moment:** "Sit with your eyes closed. Let your breath settle completely." Wait until the room is genuinely still. "Now: without thinking anything particular — without following any thought — just be aware. Not of any object. Not of any thought. Just of the quality of awareness itself. The thing that is aware. What is that quality?" Hold for ninety full seconds. Do not speak during this time. Then, softly: "Open your eyes. What is the closest word you have for what you just touched? Don't explain it — just name it."

Take three or four one-word or short-phrase responses. Receive them without comment. Then:

"That quality — whatever you named it — is the closest we come in ordinary experience to what the Bardo of dharmata describes. A hint of it. A small instance. The ground luminosity is that quality, but without any veiling whatsoever. The clouds gone. Just the sky."

**Emotional Hook:** The vertiginous recognition that a state described in an ancient Tibetan death manual is something that has a living analogue in the student's own experience of sitting quietly. Teacher names it directly: "The tradition is not describing something that belongs to another world, another time, another kind of person. It is describing something that every one of you has access to — in small form, right now, in this room. That is not a small claim."

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes

"Tell your partner: have you ever had an experience — in meditation, in a dream, in a moment of illness or great stillness — that felt like what the ground luminosity is pointing at? A moment of awareness that felt qualitatively different from ordinary consciousness — clearer, somehow, or more open?" Each partner speaks for about ninety seconds.

What to listen for: Students who have a genuine experiential reference to offer — even a partial, tentative one. These are worth hearing and worth noting. Students who say "no, nothing like that" — receive this honestly: "That's a real answer. The tradition says this quality is always present beneath ordinary experience — that it's just covered. Meditation practice is partly about learning to notice the covering."

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes

Teacher returns concept maps. Students annotate the Bardo of dharmata section in detail.

The annotation should include four things — teacher writes these on the board:

1. **What it is** — the ground luminosity and when it arises
2. **What arises** — the sequence of visions (peaceful, wrathful) and the teaching's instruction (move toward the gentle lights; recognise what arises as mind)
3. **The crucial point** — the visions are not external but projections of the mind's own nature
4. **The metaphorical reading** — what in living experience has the quality of this Bardo (a personal note in the student's own words)

Teacher circulates — deep, specific engagement. Ask: "Tell me what you wrote here — in your own words." "Why did you put it that way?" "What is the most important thing to understand about this Bardo?" Do not let students copy the board verbatim — push for paraphrase and personal rendering.

**Scaffold for students who are struggling:** "Start with number one — just describe what happens first. 'After the dying process, there is a moment of...'" Once they have that, move to number two.

**Extension for students who finish early:** "Write a short paragraph: if you were advising someone entering the Bardo of dharmata — what would you tell them? What is the single most important instruction?" This requires students to synthesise the teaching into its essential action-guidance.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes

Opening question: "The mountains of Tibet have been here through all of it — through all the meditators and monks and ordinary people who have died in their shadow, navigating this territory. The teachings exist because someone, somewhere, recognised the ground luminosity and came back to describe it. Why does the tradition preserve the description? What is it for?"

Take 2–3 responses.

"The tradition is built on people telling each other the truth about what they found — in meditation, at death, in the difficult territories of the mind. These teachings are preserved because they are useful — because the territory they describe is real, whether encountered at death or in the difficulty of a life. That is what makes them worth studying."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Student leader leads the full closing prayer set:
Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam.

Teacher collects concept maps.

Teacher's closing line: "You sat for ninety seconds with nothing but awareness. The mountains of Tibet have been waiting for exactly that."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: This is the exemplar class for Unit 2 — it should feel like the densest, richest, most personally encountering class of the unit. After C3, students should carry the weight of what the Bardo teaching is attempting: not cosmological description but practical instruction for the mind's most exposed moment. The 90-second awareness practice is the class's experiential heart — do not abbreviate it. The silence must be held fully. Students who have a genuine experience during the practice (however small) and name it honestly afterward are engaging at the highest level the curriculum asks for. Note these students — they are tracking something important. The concept map should now have substantial, precise content in the first two Bardo sections after C3. C4 addresses the third Bardo and the question of what continues; C5 is synthesis and refinement; C6 is the philosophical climax. The ground luminosity teaching connects forward to the curriculum's treatment of the nature of mind in Grade 7 (Lojong — mind training) and to the rigpa teaching in Grade 8's Dzogchen introduction. Plant the seed precisely. The "mountains of Tibet" image first used in U1.C4 appears here as its full metaphorical weight: witness, continuity, the tradition's unbroken testimony to what practitioners have found. This image should feel cumulative, not repetitive.
