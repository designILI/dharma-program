# G6.U3.C3 — Learning Her Prayer
## Grade 6 · Unit 3 · Class 3 of 4 · Learning Her Prayer
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Syllables of the Dolma prayer → the act of invoking a quality into presence through sound and repetition
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Meaning before sound — when students understand what a prayer means before they hear it chanted, the chanting encodes the meaning in the body rather than remaining as unconnected sound. The meaning is the scaffolding; the sound is what remains.
**Bloom's Level:** Analyze — students examine each structural element of the prayer (homage, invocation, petition, aspiration), understanding how the prayer's architecture reflects the teachings they have received. Chanting with understanding is different from chanting by rote.
**Prior Class Summary:** C2 paired Tara-as-archetype with Pajapati-as-history — students received Pajapati's story at real depth, walked the body moment, and wrote the archetype/history journal entry. They carry the weight of Pajapati's name finally arriving with full historical significance, and the question of what makes a person keep going when everything says stop.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the Dolma (Tara Prayer) — its meaning, structure, and key petition content — and will have chanted it at least twice.
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the prayer is an act of invocation — calling into presence the quality of swift, active compassion — not merely a request for external help.
Students will **BE ABLE TO** copy the key lines of the Dolma prayer with their plain-language meanings and chant along with recognisable syllables.
Students will **FEEL** the arrival of the prayer in the body after three classes of preparation — a sense that the sound was already waiting.

---

### Materials Checklist
- Written-out key lines of the Dolma prayer in Tibetan (with transliteration) — one copy per student to keep in portfolio
- Plain-language meaning of each key section — written clearly alongside the Tibetan (prepared by teacher in advance; use the school's authorised version)
- Pencils
- The teacher's own copy of the full prayer for reference during chanting
- Resident teacher consultation complete before this class — the authorised version of the Dolma used at this school confirmed in advance
- **Plan B:** If the teacher is uncertain of the chant tune, invite the resident teacher to lead the first chanting together. This is the preferred option if any uncertainty exists — the prayer's first landing should be as accurate as possible. A recorded version played while the teacher chants along is also acceptable if live guidance is unavailable, provided the recording is of the school's own tradition.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
**This is the class in which the Dolma joins the prayer set for the first time.**

Before the prayer set begins, the teacher speaks: "Today we add a new prayer to our set. It is called the Dolma — Drolma, in Tibetan, meaning 'she who liberates.' It is Tara's prayer. We have spent two classes learning who Tara is and who Pajapati is — the archetype and the history. Now: the prayer arrives. We will hear it and chant it once today. But first: meaning. Always meaning first."

Full Grade 6 prayer set, now including Dolma for the first time:
Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · **Dolma / Tara Prayer (NEW)**

The Dolma is chanted slowly. The teacher leads. Students listen fully on the first pass.

Student leader presides over the full set as usual.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes
Teacher: *"Before I explain the Dolma prayer: I want to ask you something simple. What would you want to say to someone in difficulty? Not advice — a wish. If you could send a single sentence to someone who is in the middle of the Bardo, or grief, or real fear — what would you say?"*

Take three or four responses. Receive them without evaluation — they will range from tender to humorous to serious. All of it is welcome.

Then: "That quality — the wish, the reaching toward someone in difficulty — is what the Dolma prayer is. It is an organised form of exactly what you just said. Let me show you how."

---

#### Mini Lesson — 15 minutes

**The structure of the Dolma prayer:**
The full Dolma prayer is twenty-one verses in its traditional form — a complete invocation, each verse honouring a different quality of Tara. In the classroom context, the teacher works with the key structural sections rather than all twenty-one verses. What matters is that students understand the architecture — so that when they chant, they know where they are.

The teacher distributes the written copies of the key lines (with transliteration and plain-language meanings). Explain each section before moving to the next:

**1. Homage (opening):**
Tara's qualities are named — she is recognised as swift, as fearless, as active, as present. This is not flattery; it is acknowledgment. By naming her qualities, we are calling them into focus. "When we say: homage to Tara who is swift — we are putting our attention on swiftness. We are saying: yes, swiftness is real. Yes, compassion acts immediately. This is recognition before request."

**2. Invocation:**
Tara is called to be present. The invocation is the moment of calling — not asking for something specific, but opening the space. "Think of it like turning on a lamp. The room was not dark because the lamp was gone — it was dark because the lamp was not on. Invocation turns the lamp on."

**3. Petition — protection from the eight great fears:**
The prayer asks for protection from specific dangers. The teacher writes on the board:

*The eight great fears:* lions (pride), elephants (ignorance), fire (hatred), snakes (envy), bandits (wrong views), water (attachment), illness (doubt), evil spirits (greed).

"Notice: most of these are internal states. Lions, elephants, fire, snakes — in the traditional context, these were real dangers. But they have always also been understood as metaphors for the states of mind that cause beings the most suffering. When Tara protects from the elephant of ignorance, she is helping beings see clearly. When she protects from the fire of hatred, she is cooling what burns. Tara protects from the dangers of the mind."

**4. Aspiration:**
The prayer closes with an aspiration — the wish that all beings, not just oneself, be free from suffering and reach liberation. "This is the bodhisattva dimension of the prayer. We do not chant it for ourselves alone. We chant it and then send it outward — as we do with Ngowa. The prayer is not a personal request; it is a universal wish."

**Vocabulary Moment:** Write on the board: *jigpa gye (འཇིགས་པ་བརྒྱད)* — "the eight great fears." "This phrase is important in the Tara tradition. When you see Tara depicted with eight fear-postures or eight figures around her, this is what they refer to. The eight fears are not only historical dangers — they are a complete map of what causes suffering in the mind. Tara is the antidote to all of them."

**Body Moment:**
"Close your eyes. Hands in the lap. I am going to chant the Dolma prayer. Do not try to follow — just listen. Feel where the sound lands in your body."

Teacher chants the key sections of the Dolma prayer — slowly, with full attention, as accurately as possible.

Silence for a few seconds.

"Open your eyes. Now: join in any syllable you recognise. We go again."

Second chanting — students begin to find the sounds. Some will enter confidently; some will watch and listen; some will find one or two syllables to hold. All of this is correct.

**Emotional Hook:** The prayer landing in the body after three classes of preparation — Tara introduced, Pajapati received, and now the sound arrives as the culmination of both. The students have not been given the prayer prematurely; they have earned it. That sequencing creates a specific quality of reception that cannot be manufactured any other way.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3 minutes
*"Tell your partner: what did you feel when the prayer was chanted — the first time or the second? Which part of what we've studied this unit does it connect to most strongly?"*

Brief sharing — two or three pairs. What to listen for: students who name the connection between the prayer's petition content and the Bardo material; students who feel the prayer in an unexpected place in the body; students who connect the aspiration section to the bodhisattva vow.

---

#### Activity — 10 minutes
Students copy the key lines of the Dolma prayer into their portfolios — Tibetan transliteration on one side, plain-language meaning on the other.

The teacher circulates: "What does this line mean, in your own words — not mine?"

Students who have copied the lines then practise chanting in pairs: one chants, one listens, then switch. The listener's role is to receive, not correct. Pairs find their own pace. Teacher circulates, chanting quietly alongside pairs who are struggling.

These copied lines are a permanent portfolio piece — they will be the student's reference copy of the prayer for the rest of Grade 6 and beyond.

---

#### Debrief — 4 minutes
Teacher: *"The Dolma prayer joins our set from today. Every class from this point forward in Grade 6 — and in the grades that come — we will chant this prayer. It will become part of your body. You will know it in your sleep. That is how prayer works: repeated, until it is inseparable from how you move through the world. Today was the first time. It will sound different in a year. It will sound different again when you are older. The prayer does not change — you do."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full prayer set — including Dolma for the first time in the closing. Student leader. The Dolma is chanted with full care — this is its second chanting of the class, and the first time it closes the class.
Prayers in order: **Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer.**

Teacher's closing line: "She who liberates. That is her name, in Tibetan. And now you know how to call on her."

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The Dolma prayer is now part of the standing prayer set — it joins at both opening and closing from C3 of Unit 3 onwards and is permanent for Grade 6 and beyond. Confirm the authorised version with the resident teacher before this class — this is not optional. Different schools and lineages use slightly different versions; the version taught here should be the one the school uses liturgically so that students' practice aligns with the community's practice. The eight great fears as internal states (pride, ignorance, hatred, envy, wrong views, attachment, doubt, greed) is a significant teaching moment — connect it to the Wheel of Life's six realms and three poisons from Grade 5; students may notice the overlap immediately. C4 is the "Our Taras" drawing and presentation — the culminating assessment for Unit 3. Students should come to C4 having reflected on the question of who embodies swift compassion in their own lives. Spiral connections: the prayer's aspiration section connects directly to Ngowa (dedication of merit), which students have been chanting since Grade 3. The eight great fears as a map of mind-states connects to the three poisons from Grade 5's Wheel of Life unit. The prayer's structure (homage → invocation → petition → aspiration) mirrors the structural logic of the Guru Rinpoche Seven-Line Prayer already in their set.
