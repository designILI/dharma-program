# G7.U2.C2 — Choosing a Topic
## Grade 7 · Unit 2 · Class 2 of 7 · Choosing a Topic
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A confirmed teaching topic and a one-sentence explanation for a nine-year-old → the criteria that make a topic genuinely teachable in twenty minutes to a younger audience
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Audience awareness as a cognitive constraint — framing the topic for a specific, younger audience (eight-to-ten-year-olds) forces the student to simplify and concretise in ways that reveal whether the understanding is solid or approximate. The nine-year-old test is a precision instrument.
**Bloom's Level:** Create (planning phase) — students move from the evaluative work of C1 (assessing which topics they understand) into early creation: selecting a teaching topic, constructing a one-sentence explanation for a younger audience, and beginning the lesson design worksheet that scaffolds the first draft in C3.
**Prior Class Summary:** C1 introduced the Feynman Principle and the sixty-second Om Mani Padme Hung fumble. Students made a brainstorm list of potential teaching topics from Grades 1–6, starred their most confident topics, and carried the question: "What does the younger student need — not what do I want to show them I know?"

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** four criteria for a teachable near-peer topic: concrete before abstract, story-friendly, one main idea, teachable depth
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that a lesson plan is a form of translation — the teacher's understanding rendered in terms a younger mind can receive — and that this translation reveals the limits of one's own understanding as much as its strengths
Students will **BE ABLE TO** confirm a teaching topic, write a one-sentence explanation for a nine-year-old, and complete the lesson design worksheet through the concrete anchor and story/analogy fields
Students will **FEEL** the specific creative energy of a topic becoming a teaching — the moment when the abstract possibility of "I could teach this" becomes the concrete intention of "here is what I will do with it"

---

### Materials Checklist
- C1 brainstorm lists (students bring theirs)
- Lesson design worksheet, one per student — printed or written on board to copy (fields: Topic; Audience: grade and approximate age; One main idea; One learning objective written as "Students will be able to..."; Concrete anchor — what the younger student can see, touch, or do; Story or analogy)
- Pens or pencils, one per student
- Board and chalk or marker for vocabulary, criteria, and shared examples
- Grade 7 prayer set visible
- **Plan B (for undecided students):** Any student who cannot confirm a topic by the end of C2 is assigned one by the teacher before C3 begins. The teacher has a short list ready: Om Mani Padme Hung and the six syllables; the three realms of the six (hungry ghost, human, animal) with one Jataka tale; Tara and the vow not to abandon; Tsethar as a practice. These are well-bounded, story-rich, and within Grade 7 students' understanding.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full Grade 7 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer at closing only.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
*"Last class I asked you to think about your audience — younger students, probably eight to ten years old. Before you choose your topic today: think back to when you were eight or nine. What do you remember about Dharma class then? What worked — what actually stuck? And what bored you, or went over your head?"*

Take 3–4 responses. Write the qualities that emerge on the board — these become the informal criteria for topic selection:
- Stories that had characters I cared about
- Things I could touch or do
- When I understood the point quickly and it mattered
- Prayers whose meaning I knew
- Analogies that made it click
- Long explanations with no activity

*"That list — that is your criteria for a strong teaching topic. You are designing for the eight-year-old you were."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–12 minutes

**What makes a topic teachable for near-peer teaching.**

Four criteria. Write each on the board:

**1. Concrete before abstract.**
Every strong teaching topic for younger students needs a concrete anchor — something the student can see, touch, draw, or physically do. Teaching "impermanence" without a concrete anchor (a leaf, a candle, a melting ice cube) produces an abstract lesson that younger students cannot hold. Teaching "Tsethar" with a physical practice — even a symbolic one — grounds the abstraction in something real. The concrete anchor is not decoration: it is the cognitive entry point.

**2. Story-friendly.**
Topics with a story attached — a Jataka tale, a biographical moment from the Buddha's or Milarepa's life, a narrative analogy — are significantly easier to teach to younger students than abstract frameworks. Stories are what the mind of an eight-year-old uses to organise understanding. A lesson without a story asks younger students to hold concepts they cannot yet organise. A lesson with a story gives them a structure.

**3. One main idea.**
Twenty minutes holds one strong idea well, with a body moment and an activity. It does not hold two. Trying to cover multiple ideas in twenty minutes produces shallow engagement with all of them. Before choosing a topic, the student should be able to state: "The one main idea I want the younger students to leave with is: ___." If they cannot state one main idea, the topic is too large or too diffuse.

**4. Teachable depth.**
The student needs to understand the topic well enough to handle questions they didn't anticipate. This is the Feynman test again: not "can I recite this" but "can I respond to a nine-year-old who asks 'but why?'" three times in a row and still have an answer. If the answer runs out after one "why," the understanding is not yet deep enough to teach.

Walk through the brainstorm lists as a class — name two or three topics and assess them against the four criteria. This gives students a shared model before working independently.

---

**Meta-awareness moment:** *"You may have noticed that the lesson design framework you're learning — concrete anchor, story, one main idea, audience-focused objective — is exactly the structure this curriculum uses. You are learning curriculum design by doing it. That is itself an example of the Feynman Principle: the best way to understand how lessons work is to have to build one."*

---

**Vocabulary Moment:**
Write on the board: **learning objective** — a specific statement of what the lesson is designed to help students understand or do, written from the student's perspective.

*"A strong learning objective is specific enough to be useful. Not: 'students will understand compassion.' That is too broad — you cannot tell whether it has been met. Instead: 'students will be able to give one example of a compassionate act they could do today.' That is specific, achievable in twenty minutes, and verifiable — you can ask the younger students to give that example, and you will know immediately whether the lesson worked."*

---

**Body Moment:**
*"Hold your top topic in mind. Now: explain it in one sentence, for a nine-year-old. Not a paragraph — one sentence. You have thirty seconds."*

Thirty seconds of private composing.

*"Share it with your partner. Partner: does that sentence make you want to know more? Does it make the topic feel important? If not — what's missing?"*

Partners give one word of honest feedback: "clear," "confusing," "too long," "interesting."

*"If your sentence didn't land — that is information. It means either the topic isn't clear enough in your own mind yet, or you haven't found the right entry point for the nine-year-old. Both are fixable. We fix them now."*

---

**Emotional Hook:** The creative energy of a topic becoming a teaching — the moment when an abstract possibility (I could teach this) becomes a concrete intention (I know what the main idea is, I have the sentence). This shift is the beginning of making.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
*"Tell your partner: your chosen topic, your one-sentence explanation, and your one main idea. Partner: does the explanation make you want to know more? Is the main idea clear — or is it still several ideas pretending to be one?"*

Two minutes. Then take 2–3 examples from the group — topic, sentence, main idea. Hold each one up against the four criteria briefly. This is a model, not a judgment.

---

#### Activity — 12–15 minutes

**Topic confirmation and lesson design worksheet.**

*"Your topic is now confirmed. If you're still uncertain between two, I will help you choose before you leave today."*

Begin the lesson design worksheet. Students fill in each field:

- **Topic:** (confirmed)
- **Audience:** Grade __ (age approximately __ years)
- **One main idea:** The single understanding I want the younger students to leave with
- **One learning objective:** "Students will be able to..."
- **Concrete anchor:** What the younger student can see, touch, draw, or physically do with the teaching
- **Story or analogy:** The narrative element that will make the concept accessible

The teacher circulates: *"What's the concrete anchor? If you don't have one yet, we find one before next class — because a lesson without a concrete anchor will not hold for an eight-year-old. What can they do with their hands? What can they see? What is the physical version of this concept?"*

Common concrete anchors by topic (have these ready to suggest):
- Om Mani Padme Hung: the six syllables written on six fingers of both hands, or six stones
- The three poisons: three coloured ropes tied around the wrist (or drawn), and the act of loosening them
- Tsethar: releasing something — a piece of paper carrying the name of something to be freed
- The six realms: a wheel drawn together, with students populating each realm with drawings
- Tara: the story told with gesture — the vow, the teaching position, the compassion in action

---

#### Debrief — 3–5 minutes
*"Next class: first draft. Come with your topic locked and your concrete anchor clear. The lesson design worksheet is your foundation — if it's incomplete, the draft cannot stand up. If you are still uncertain about your topic or your concrete anchor, speak to me before next class."*

Note aloud: any student who does not have a confirmed topic by C3 will be assigned one. This is said without judgment — as information, not pressure.

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: Every student must leave C2 with a confirmed topic. The teacher should track this actively — circulate during the worksheet activity with a mental register of which students are confirmed and which are not. For any student still undecided at the close of class, make a direct, brief decision: *"I'd like you to teach [topic]. Here's why: it's concrete, it has a good story, and I think your understanding of it is solid. Does that work?"* Do not leave it open — C3's drafting session requires a locked topic.

Review all worksheet drafts quickly between C2 and C3 — not to assess them, but to know which students have a genuine concrete anchor and which have something abstract masquerading as one ("I'll explain it to them" is not a concrete anchor; "I'll have them draw the six realms on a wheel" is). Come to C3 with two or three specific interventions ready for students whose concrete anchors are still too abstract.

Spiral connection: the lesson design framework (concrete anchor, story, one main idea, learning objective) mirrors the Dharma curriculum's own structure. Students are implicitly learning curriculum design by doing it. This meta-awareness is worth naming briefly — the curriculum in which they are learning to design lessons uses the same principles they are now being asked to apply. This is not coincidental.

Logistics check: Teaching Day (C6) must be confirmed with the receiving class teacher before C3 begins. Students should know before they start writing their lessons who their audience will be: which grade, what the younger students already know from their own Dharma curriculum, what the receiving teacher expects.
