# G7.U3.C3 — An Aspiration for Someone
## Grade 7 · Unit 3 · Class 3 of 4 · An Aspiration for Someone
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Drafting a formal healing aspiration → the particular intimacy and discipline of composing a genuine, specific wish for another person
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Precision as practice — the teacher's coaching throughout the drafting session pushes students from vague compassion toward accurate attention; the formal structure of the aspiration is a tool for precision, not a constraint on feeling
**Bloom's Level:** Create / Evaluate — students create a formal healing aspiration for a real person, evaluating at each stage whether the suffering they name is what the person actually carries, or what the student imagines they carry
**Prior Class Summary:** C2 introduced the Medicine Buddha mantra (*TAYATHA OM BEKANDZE BEKANDZE MAHA BEKANDZE RADZA SAMUDGATE SOHA*) and the Buddhist teaching that illness and suffering share a root in the three poisons. Students chanted the mantra for their chosen person and have been practising it between classes.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** what a formal Buddhist aspiration (*paṇidhāna* / *smon lam*) is — a vow of intention directed toward a specific being or outcome, distinct from a request or a prayer for help.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the four-part structure of the aspiration (name and affection / specific suffering named / the wish for liberation / dedication of merit) is a form designed to prevent vagueness and hold the practitioner to genuine attention.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** produce a substantive draft healing aspiration for their chosen person — at minimum three paragraphs following the four-part structure — with the suffering named specifically enough to be recognisable to someone who knows that person.

Students will **FEEL** the particular intimacy of composing something that is entirely for another person — the quality of writing that no one but the recipient is the subject of.

---

### Materials Checklist

- Student portfolios (students will need their C1 entry — the chosen person and the specific suffering they identified in C1)
- Writing instruments
- Whiteboard or visible surface for the four-part aspiration structure and the example aspiration
- Extra paper for students who prefer to draft separately and transfer to the portfolio
- **Plan B:** If a student has not completed their C1 entry or has lost their portfolio, the teacher has a brief re-entry: the student re-identifies their chosen person and spends the first three minutes of drafting time re-establishing the specific suffering before joining the rest of the class in the formal draft. Do not delay the whole class for one student's re-entry.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 7 prayer set, student leader.

Prayer sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod

After the full prayer set, the Medicine Buddha mantra is chanted three additional times together, without pause, before moving on. The student leader may lead these extra chantings or the teacher may hold them — either is appropriate. These three chantings are the bridge from the prayer set into the class's work.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

Teacher: *"Did anyone chant the mantra between C2 and today? What was the experience?"*

Take 3–4 responses. Receive them without evaluation — a student who found it powerful and a student who forgot entirely on Tuesday are both welcome. "Thank you. The practice is in you whether you chanted it or not — you learned it in this room, and the body retains that. Today: we write the aspiration. The formal wish."

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10 minutes

**What a healing aspiration is.**

"In the Buddhist tradition, aspirations — in Pali *paṇidhāna*, in Dharma usage *smon lam* — are formal wishes. Not requests. Not prayers to someone outside yourself asking them to act. Aspirations are statements of intention — this is what I am putting my will, my attention, my practice toward. The Medicine Buddha's twelve vows are aspirations in exactly this sense. He did not ask the universe to heal beings from suffering. He vowed that his own cultivation of the healing quality would be the mechanism."

"Your aspiration is the same structure, smaller scale. One person. One set of specific sufferings. One genuine wish for their liberation. One dedication of the merit of your practice to them."

**The four-part structure.** Write on the board:

*1. Name and affection — address the person specifically and with genuine care*
*2. Specific suffering — name what they are carrying, precisely*
*3. The wish for liberation — not only comfort, but freedom from the root*
*4. Dedication — all the merit of this practice goes to them*

"Notice: the second part says specific suffering, not general suffering. Not 'may they be well' — that is too wide to be practice. The question is: what are they actually carrying? What do you actually know — from watching them, from being near them — about what is pressing on them this season?"

**Read an example.** The teacher reads aloud an aspiration they have composed:

*"May you be free from the pain that is pressing on your heart this season. May the fear that wakes you at night grow quieter — not because the situation has changed, but because you find, in your own chest, a steadiness you didn't know was there. May you know — in your body — that you are not alone in this. May the goodness of whatever practice I have done, however small, go entirely to you. May it reach you."*

"Notice: specific — the fear that wakes them at night. Not generic well-being, but a real, named quality of suffering. The aspiration is specific enough that the person, if they read it, would know it was written for them."

---

**Vocabulary Moment:** *Dedicate* — to give something specifically to someone or something. The Ngowa prayer, which closes every class, is a dedication of merit: the goodness of our practice is not kept for ourselves but offered outward to all beings. The healing aspiration ends with this same move — all the merit of the practice goes to the person being held. This is not a small thing. It is a formal relinquishment of the benefit of one's own effort.

---

**Body Moment:** Students close their eyes. "Hold your chosen person in your mind. See them clearly — not a general image, but this specific person. Notice what you know about what they are carrying right now. What is sitting on their shoulders? What are they afraid of? What are they tired from?" Two full minutes of silence. Then: "Open your eyes. Write."

---

**Emotional Hook:** The particular intimacy of composing something that is entirely for another person — writing that has no audience but one, no purpose but one person's good.

---

#### Activity — 20 minutes

Full drafting session. Students write their healing aspirations — at minimum three substantive paragraphs, following the four-part structure on the board. Extra paper is available for students who prefer to draft separately.

Teacher circulates throughout, offering specific coaching — not rewriting, but questioning:

- "Is the suffering specific? Read paragraph 2 again — is that what they're actually carrying, or is it what you imagine they might be carrying? What do you actually know?"
- "The dedication in paragraph 4 — have you actually given the merit? Or does it still feel like it belongs to you? Try rewriting it as a direct address to the person."
- "Paragraph 3: the wish for liberation, not only comfort. What is the difference? Comfort means the feeling gets better. Liberation means the cause of the feeling changes. Which are you wishing for them?"

These questions are not corrections. They are the practice — pushing the student further into genuine attention rather than allowing them to stay in the territory of good-sounding sentiment.

Students who finish early may revise any section that feels vague. There is no upper limit on length. The aspiration should be complete enough to read aloud in C4.

---

#### Debrief — 3 minutes

"Next class: you will read your aspiration aloud and dedicate it. Between now and then, chant the mantra for your person at least once. And if you can: spend five minutes actually thinking about what they're carrying. Not about your aspiration — about them. See if anything you learn between now and C4 wants to go into the aspiration before you read it."

"The aspiration should be finished — or very close — before C4. You do not need to memorise it. You will read from your portfolio. But it should be a completed piece, not a draft."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set, student leader. Food Offering Prayer included.

Teacher's closing line: "You have made something for someone. That already matters — before it's read, before they know. The making itself is part of the practice."

---

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*

Carry forward: Aspirations should be read by the teacher between C3 and C4 — not edited or corrected, but read with care. If an aspiration is generic or abstract in its naming of the suffering (paragraph 2), the teacher speaks briefly with the student before C4: "What specifically do you want them to be free from? Is there a more precise way to name it?" This is not criticism — it is the same coaching that happened during the activity. The goal is that every student enters C4 with a piece specific enough to be real.

Students should have their aspirations written and nominally complete before C4 — the reading in C4 requires a finished piece. If a student has not finished, they finish in the first five minutes of C4 while others are settling. C4 should feel ceremonial — plan accordingly. Arrange seating in a circle or arc if possible, so students can read to the group rather than to the back of a head.

The four-part aspiration structure in this class mirrors the three-part reflection format from Unit 2 (what I imagined / what happened / what I understand now). Both are formal structures designed to prevent vagueness. Students who carry the Unit 2 reflection format will recognise the move: the structure is a tool, not a cage.

Spiral connections: Unit 2 three-part reflection format (Grade 7 — the discipline of structured writing as practice); Unit 1 position paper on cruelty (Grade 7 — non-abstract, specific, committed writing); Ngowa dedication prayer as the closing of every class (embedded throughout the curriculum since Grade 1).
