# G7.U2.C1 — Learning by Teaching
## Grade 7 · Unit 2 · Class 1 of 7 · Learning by Teaching
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Explaining Om Mani Padme Hung to someone who has never heard it → the gap between feeling like you know something and knowing it well enough to transmit it
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Desirable difficulty — the sixty-second transmission exercise deliberately creates productive struggle, making visible the exact boundary of the student's solid understanding. The fumble is not failure; it is data.
**Bloom's Level:** Create (entry point) — students begin moving toward the highest level of Bloom's: not evaluating or analysing others' knowledge, but attempting to produce and transmit their own. The brainstorm activity initiates the creative project.
**Prior Class Summary:** C5 of Unit 1 was the position paper share — students completed and read their papers on cruelty, empathy, and bystander responsibility. The teacher closed with: "You cannot teach empathy without having sat in three chairs." Unit 2 was announced. Students arrive carrying the bridge.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the Feynman Principle — if you cannot explain something simply, you do not understand it well enough — and its implication: teaching is the deepest test of understanding
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the gap between "knowing" and "knowing well enough to teach" is not a gap between success and failure; it is the productive space in which real learning happens
Students will **BE ABLE TO** produce a brainstorm list of potential teaching topics from Grades 1–6, with an honest note on which they feel confident about and which they are less sure of
Students will **FEEL** the specifically humbling — and generative — recognition that what felt like solid understanding has edges; and the creative energy of a large project whose beginning is visible from here

---

### Materials Checklist
- Brainstorm paper or journals, one per student
- Pens or pencils, one per student
- Board and chalk or marker for vocabulary and shared notes
- Dharma curriculum overview or topic list for Grades 1–6 (summary posted on board or printed, one per student — to support brainstorming; see note below)
- Grade 7 prayer set visible
- **Note on curriculum overview:** The teacher prepares a simple one-page list of topics from Grades 1–6 before this class: key concepts, prayers, stories, and practices studied each year. This is not an exhaustive document — a prompt list that jogs memory. Examples: Grade 1 (taking refuge, Om Mani Padme Hung, the story of the Buddha's birth), Grade 2 (Tsethar, the hungry ghosts, the story of Milarepa's teacher), Grade 3 (the six realms, the three poisons, the Wheel of Life), Grade 4 (Jataka tales, the story of Angulimala, the meaning of refuge), Grade 5 (the Wheel of Life in full, karma, the Bardo introduction), Grade 6 (the Bardo, Tara, Cham Dance, Pajapati). The teacher adapts this list to what was actually taught.
- **Plan B (for brainstorm):** If students are struggling to recall earlier years of the curriculum, the prompt list is the primary support. If it is still thin, open a brief whole-class recollection round: *"Grade 3 — what do you remember? Grade 4?"* The brainstorm list does not need to be long; three or four genuine candidates per student is sufficient to support C2's topic selection.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full Grade 7 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer at closing only.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
*"Has anyone here ever had to explain something to a younger sibling, a cousin, or a friend — and realised mid-explanation that you didn't actually understand it as well as you thought? Not that you forgot it — that you realised you hadn't really understood it deeply."*

Take responses. Give them room — these stories are usually vivid and specific. A student might describe explaining how multiplication works, or why a religious practice exists, or how a rule in a game functions, and finding that the explanation collapsed.

*"That experience — right there — is the Feynman Principle. That is what today is about."*

**Fallback:** If no student offers a story: *"Let me give you one. I once thought I understood [name a concept from the curriculum — e.g., the three poisons] quite well. Then I tried to explain it to someone who asked 'but why would anyone do harmful things if they knew they were harmful?' I fumbled. I found I was reciting rather than understanding. Has anything like that happened to you?"*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–12 minutes

**The Feynman Principle.**

Richard Feynman was a physicist — one of the twentieth century's most brilliant scientists and most passionate teachers. He said, in different ways at different times, one essential thing: *if you cannot explain something simply, you do not understand it well enough.*

Write on the board: **If you cannot explain it simply, you do not understand it well enough.**

*"The test of real understanding is the ability to transmit it — clearly, accurately, in terms that another person can actually receive. When you try to teach something and find you're fumbling — reaching for words that don't arrive, or realising your explanation doesn't actually answer the question — that fumbling is information. It shows you the exact boundary of your solid understanding."*

This is not discouraging. It is useful. It means: every fumble is a map of where to go next.

*"The implication of the Feynman Principle is counterintuitive: the best way to deepen your own understanding of something is to teach it. Not to study it more. To teach it. Because teaching reveals what studying can hide."*

**Context for the project:** In Unit 2, each student will choose a topic from the Dharma curriculum — something studied in Grades 1–6 — and design and teach a twenty-minute lesson to a younger class. Not a presentation. Not a performance. A real lesson, to real younger students, who will learn from them.

*"You are not demonstrating what you know. You are responsible for what they understand. That is a different relationship to knowledge entirely."*

---

**Vocabulary Moment:**
Write on the board: **pedagogy** — from the Greek *paidagogos*: the person (originally a slave in ancient Athens) who accompanied a child to school, guiding and supporting the learning journey. Now: the art and science of teaching.

*"In Unit 2, you become pedagogues. Not because you have mastered the curriculum — you haven't, and neither has any teacher. But because you will take responsibility for transmitting something you understand well enough to help someone else understand it. That act of responsibility is pedagogy."*

---

**Body Moment:**
*"Stand up. You have sixty seconds. Turn to the person beside you and explain Om Mani Padme Hung — what it means, why we chant it, what it does — as if they have never heard it and have no context. Go."*

Sixty seconds. The teacher observes — does not intervene or assist.

Sit.

*"Where did you fumble? Not where you went quiet — where did you find that the explanation you reached for wasn't quite right? Where did you realise you were chanting rather than understanding? That is where your understanding stops being solid. That is where the project begins."*

Take 3–4 responses — students naming specific moments of fumble. Write key ones on the board.

---

**Emotional Hook:** The specifically humbling recognition that what felt like knowing and what is "knowing well enough to teach" are different. This should not be resolved too quickly. The productive discomfort is the entry point to the project.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
*"Tell your partner: what is one topic from your six years of Dharma study that you think you understand well enough to teach — not perfectly, but solidly? And what is one topic you would be afraid to teach, because you're genuinely not sure you understand it well enough?"*

Two minutes of genuine conversation.

Then: *"For anyone willing to share — what was the topic you'd be afraid to teach? Why that one?"*

Take 2–3 responses to the group. These are often the most interesting topics — the ones students sense they only half-understand. The teacher notes them: they may be better teaching topics than the ones students feel comfortable with, depending on how they're addressed in C2.

---

#### Activity — 12–15 minutes

**Topic brainstorm.**

*"Using the topic list in front of you and your own memory — make a list of all the topics from Grades 1 through 6 that you could potentially teach. Don't edit yet. List everything that comes to mind."*

Five minutes of independent listing.

Then: *"Now: put a star beside the three you feel most confident about. Put a question mark beside any you'd be afraid to teach. And circle the one you think would be most important for a younger student — eight or nine years old — to understand."*

The teacher circulates: *"Which one would push you the most? Which is most important for younger students? If you had to teach one tomorrow and couldn't change it — which would you actually be ready for?"*

Students who finish early: *"Add a line next to each starred topic: what is the one main idea you'd teach? If you can't write one main idea, the topic may be too large for twenty minutes."*

---

#### Debrief — 3–5 minutes
*"In C2 you choose your topic and begin designing your lesson. Between now and then: think about your audience. The younger students you'll be teaching are probably eight to ten years old. Think not about what you want to show them you know — but about what they need. What does an eight-year-old in Dharma class actually need from you? That question should guide your topic choice."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader.

**Teacher's closing line:** *"The Sang offering carries our prayers outward. Today you found the edge of your understanding. Next class you choose a topic and begin building toward the centre of it. The fumble was the beginning."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: Topic selection in C2 should be guided, not a free-for-all. Review the brainstorm lists from C1 before C2 and have suggestions ready. Strong teaching topics (concrete, story-friendly, one main idea, teachable depth at Grade 7 level): Om Mani Padme Hung and the mani prayer (clear, concrete, the six syllables have specific meaning); the six realms (highly visual, engaging for younger students, good for drawing activity); Tsethar (actionable practice, emotionally resonant, accessible to eight-year-olds); the three poisons and the Wheel of Life (concrete with physical analogy — the pig, snake, and bird); Tara (strong story, clear emotional resonance, the vow not to abandon beings in difficulty); a specific Jataka tale (story-driven, built-in moral).

Steer students away from: abstract topics where their own understanding is still thin (the Bardo teachings, karma's mechanism, interdependence as a philosophical concept); topics that are too large for twenty minutes (the whole Wheel of Life, all six realms in depth, the full Bardo sequence); topics that require vocabulary the younger students don't have.

The sixty-second Om Mani Padme Hung exercise is deliberately slightly uncomfortable — it exposes the gap between chanting and understanding. The teacher should be ready for students who handle the exposure with humour (acceptable and healthy) and students who find it more destabilising (rare, but worth watching for). The goal is productive discomfort, not shame.

Logistics check: the receiving class (Grade 3, 4, or 5) must be confirmed before U2.C2. Teaching Day is C6 — the lesson plan design process in C2–C5 should be calibrated to that date. If C6 scheduling is not yet arranged, arrange it now.
