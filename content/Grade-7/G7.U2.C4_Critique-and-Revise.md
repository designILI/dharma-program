# G7.U2.C4 — Critique and Revise
## Grade 7 · Unit 2 · Class 4 of 7 · Critique and Revise
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A first-draft lesson plan held in someone else's hands → the specific improvements that come from genuinely useful feedback
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Peer critique as metacognitive tool — reading a peer's lesson plan through three specific questions requires the critiquer to apply the criteria they've studied; in doing so, they deepen their own understanding of what makes a lesson work. The feedback process teaches the feedback-giver as much as the recipient.
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluate and Create — students evaluate each other's lesson plans against specific criteria (Evaluate), then revise their own (Create). The revision is not cosmetic — it addresses structural weaknesses identified through specific, honest critique.
**Prior Class Summary:** C3 was the first full drafting session — students wrote lesson plans in the five-section structure (Opening, Activation, Main Teaching, Activity, Closing). The teacher has reviewed all first drafts between sessions. Students carry their drafts and the instruction: "The critique is not about whether it's good — whether it will work."

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the three-question critique protocol: (1) Is the main idea clear? (2) Is the concrete anchor actually concrete? (3) Is there a real activity — something the younger student does with the teaching?
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that iteration — repeating a design process to move toward a better result — is the normal method of lesson design; professional curriculum writers never work from a first draft, and the revision process is not a sign of weakness in the first draft
Students will **BE ABLE TO** give specific, written feedback on a peer's lesson plan using the three-question protocol, and revise their own draft in response to feedback received, keeping changes that improve the lesson and discarding those that don't
Students will **FEEL** the specific vulnerability of sharing unfinished work — and the specific relief when feedback is honest, targeted, and genuinely aimed at improvement rather than evaluation of the person

---

### Materials Checklist
- First-draft lesson plans (students bring theirs — teacher has any left from C3)
- Fresh paper for written feedback, one sheet per student
- Pens or pencils, one per student
- Board with three critique questions written and visible:
  1. Is the main idea clear?
  2. Is the concrete anchor actually concrete?
  3. Is there a real activity?
- Board with shared criteria for useful feedback (generated at lesson start):
  - Specific (not "it's good" or "it's confusing" — what specifically?)
  - Actionable (the person can do something with it)
  - About the work, not the person
  - Aimed at making it work better
- Grade 7 prayer set visible
- **Plan B (for peer critique):** If a particular pairing generates defensiveness rather than productive engagement, the teacher joins the pair briefly and models one specific piece of feedback using the three questions on the draft. This demonstrates the tone and specificity required without assigning blame. If the defensiveness is significant, the student may critique their own draft using the three questions before receiving peer feedback — self-critique first can reduce the vulnerability response.

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes
*"Before we begin critique: what is the difference between useful feedback and feedback that doesn't help? Think of a time someone gave you feedback — on schoolwork, on something you made, on how you did something — that actually helped. What made it useful?"*

Take responses. Build a shared criteria list on the board as students speak: specific, actionable, about the work not the person, aimed at making it better. Leave this list on the board for the duration of the session.

Then: *"And feedback that didn't help — what was wrong with it? What made it useless or discouraging?"*

Take 2–3 responses: too vague ("it was good"), too personal ("you're not good at this"), contradictory, or just absent.

*"The criteria on the board are the criteria for the feedback you give today. Your partner is trusting you with an unfinished piece of work. Use the criteria."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 5 minutes

**The critique protocol.**

*"You are reading your partner's lesson plan with three specific questions. Write your answers to these three questions on a separate sheet — not on their draft. Give it to them in writing, then discuss it."*

Point to the board:
1. **Is the main idea clear?** Can you name it in one sentence? If not, where does the draft lose it?
2. **Is the concrete anchor actually concrete?** Does the younger student see, touch, draw, or do something physical? Or is the "anchor" still abstract — more explanation, more language?
3. **Is there a real activity?** Does the younger student do something with the teaching — make, create, produce, gesture, move? Not listen to more teaching.

*"Those three questions are the critique. Not 'is the writing good.' Not 'do I like the topic.' Whether it will work for a real nine-year-old, on Teaching Day, in twenty minutes."*

---

**Vocabulary Moment:**
Write on the board: **iterate** — to repeat a design process, with adjustments, in order to move toward a better result.

*"Lesson design is iterative. This is not a weakness of the design — it is the design. You write a draft, someone reads it with specific eyes, you revise. Then you rehearse, you find what's missing, you revise again. Professional curriculum designers iterate many times before a lesson is taught. You are on iteration two. There will be at least one more before Teaching Day."*

---

**Body Moment:**
*"Take your partner's draft. Hold it — both hands. Read it as if you are a nine-year-old hearing this lesson for the first time. What would confuse you? What would make you want to lean in? What would lose you? What would you find most interesting about the activity? Read it from inside that nine-year-old."*

Sixty seconds of silent reading from the nine-year-old perspective before the formal critique begins.

---

**Emotional Hook:** The vulnerability of sharing work that isn't finished — and the specific relief when feedback is specific and useful rather than vague or evaluative of the person. Both sides of this are present in a well-run peer critique session.

---

#### Activity — 35 minutes

**Phase 1 — Written feedback (10 min).**

Students work in pairs. Each person reads the partner's draft silently and writes answers to the three critique questions on their feedback sheet. Ten minutes. No talking yet — written first.

The feedback is addressed to the work, not the person. The teacher circulates and checks: *"Is your feedback specific? 'The main idea is unclear' is not specific. 'The main idea is unclear in the teaching section — I couldn't tell whether the lesson is about Tsethar as a practice or about why we do Tsethar' is specific."*

**Phase 2 — Discussion (10 min).**

Partners exchange written feedback and discuss. The recipient's job: listen first, ask questions, then decide what to incorporate and what to keep as is. Not every piece of feedback is right — some feedback reflects the reviewer's own preferences rather than genuine structural problems. The recipient is responsible for the revision, not the reviewer.

The teacher circulates during discussion: watching for feedback that is too vague to act on, or revision discussions that turn defensive. Intervene quietly: *"What specific change would address that note? Can you say it in one sentence?"*

**Phase 3 — Revision (15 min).**

Solo revision. Each student incorporates the feedback that is right, discards what isn't, and decides independently what to keep. The teacher circulates with specific questions:

- *"What did you change? Why that — and not the other feedback you received?"*
- *"The concrete anchor — is it more concrete now? What does the younger student touch or do?"*
- *"Your activity — what specifically does the nine-year-old make or produce? Can you name it in a sentence?"*

Students who incorporated all feedback without selecting: *"Not all feedback is correct. What do you actually believe about your lesson — does that change work better, or worse? Trust your judgment about your own material."*

Students who rejected all feedback: *"Is there anything in the critique that might be right — even partially? What would the lesson look like if you tried that one change?"*

---

#### Debrief — 3 minutes
*"The teacher's job is not to be right. It is to help the student understand something. If your lesson plan is built around showing what you know rather than helping the younger student understand — it won't work. That is the most important revision to make: from 'look at what I know' to 'here is what you will understand.' Those are completely different lessons."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: Revised lesson plans should be reviewed by the teacher between C4 and C5. Read all revised drafts and make brief written notes: what is now working, what still needs attention, and whether the student is ready to rehearse. The decision about readiness for rehearsal is important — rehearsing a flawed lesson plan embeds the flaw rather than revealing it. Students who have significant structural problems remaining after C4 need direct teacher guidance at the start of C5, before rehearsal begins.

The most common residual problem after C4 revision: the activity is still really "more teaching with movement" rather than a genuine making or doing. Watch for this specifically. If a student's revised activity still describes the teacher explaining something while the younger student listens or responds verbally — push one more time: "What does the younger student make or produce? What exists after the activity that did not exist before?"

Coordinate with the receiving class teacher now if not already done. Teaching Day is C6. Students need to know before rehearsal (C5): the grade, the room, the arrival time, the receiving teacher's name. This information grounds the rehearsal in reality — students who know they are teaching Grade 3 on Thursday afternoon rehearse differently than students rehearsing in the abstract.
