# G7.U2.C5 — Rehearsal
## Grade 7 · Unit 2 · Class 5 of 7 · Rehearsal
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Standing up and beginning to teach → the gap between what a lesson plan says and what teaching it actually requires
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Enactment as diagnosis — rehearsing a lesson reveals problems that reading and writing about it cannot: timing miscalculations, transitions that feel smooth on paper but break down in delivery, concrete anchors that are harder to handle than expected, activation questions that produce no response. Rehearsal is not performance; it is a diagnostic tool.
**Bloom's Level:** Create (testing phase) — students move the created lesson plan from paper to enactment. The feedback from rehearsal informs final adjustments before the real lesson. This is the last iteration before Teaching Day.
**Prior Class Summary:** C4 was peer critique and revision — students gave and received specific feedback using three questions, then revised. The teacher has reviewed revised drafts between C4 and C5. Students carry their revised lesson plans and the distinction: "Am I showing what I know, or helping the younger student understand?"

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** that rehearsal serves a diagnostic function — it reveals the specific places where the lesson plan is not yet adequate for the reality of a live classroom, so that those places can be fixed before Teaching Day
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the lesson plan is a guide, not a script — the teacher who is locked to their plan cannot respond to what actually happens in the room; the teacher who has rehearsed knows the plan well enough to depart from it when needed
Students will **BE ABLE TO** deliver a full twenty-minute rehearsal of their lesson to a partner in the younger-student role, and make at least one specific, concrete adjustment to the lesson plan as a result
Students will **FEEL** the gap between planning and doing — the specific experience of standing up and beginning to teach, discovering what that feels like in the body, and finding that it is both more manageable and more demanding than expected

---

### Materials Checklist
- Revised lesson plans (students bring theirs — teacher has any that were left)
- All concrete anchors and materials each student plans to use in their lesson (students bring these — teacher checks before rehearsal begins that everything is present)
- Space to stand and deliver — desks rearranged so students can stand with a small "audience" space in front of them; pairs work in different parts of the room simultaneously
- Timer or clock visible to all students
- Board with one reminder sentence: **The plan is a guide. Stay present.**
- Grade 7 prayer set visible
- **Plan B (for students significantly underprepared):** If a student arrives at rehearsal without a lesson plan in workable shape, do not allow rehearsal until a quick triage with the teacher (5 minutes) identifies what is missing and produces a minimal workable structure. Rehearsing an unworkable lesson does not help; it wastes Teaching Day preparation time. The triage should be brief and direct: "What is your main idea? What is the activity? What is the concrete anchor?" These three elements, working, are enough to rehearse from.
- **Plan B (for timing):** If rehearsals consistently run long (students are teaching 30-minute lessons without realising), use a visible signal at the 20-minute mark — a raised hand or a gentle sound. Partners call time. The lesson plan must then be cut, live, to fit. This cut is itself valuable preparation: Teaching Day will have a fixed time.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full Grade 7 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader. Food Offering Prayer at closing only.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 3 minutes
*"Today you rehearse. Before you begin: what is the one part of your lesson you are most uncertain about? Not the part you think might be weak — the part you genuinely don't know will work. Name it to yourself. Don't say it aloud yet."*

Thirty seconds of silence.

*"Good. That part — whatever you named — is what to watch most carefully in the rehearsal. Not to protect it. To find out what's actually there."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 3 minutes

**What rehearsal is for.**

*"Rehearsal is not to make the lesson perfect. It is to find what is missing — in time to fix it. Things that seem clear in writing often become unclear when spoken aloud. Activities that seem fast in planning often take twice as long in a real room. The concrete anchor that seemed simple may be harder to handle than expected. Transitions that read smoothly on paper may stall when you're actually standing in a room."*

*"Rehearsal reveals these. That is its entire purpose. A rehearsal in which nothing needs adjusting is a wasted rehearsal — it means you weren't paying attention."*

---

**Vocabulary Moment:** No new vocabulary today — the session is practical. The teacher may briefly revisit: *"Iterate — we have one more iteration. This rehearsal is the last before the real thing."*

---

**Body Moment:** The rehearsal itself. Students stand and deliver their full lesson — full body engagement, not sitting at a desk with the plan open and reading from it. The plan may be nearby but should not be a script.

---

**Emotional Hook:** The moment of actually standing up and beginning to teach — the shift from planning to doing, and the discovery of what that feels like. This is not a comfortable experience for most students. It should not be made comfortable prematurely.

---

#### Activity — 30 minutes

**Full rehearsal pairs.**

Students pair up. Each partner teaches their full lesson (approximately 20 minutes) while the other plays the role of a younger student — asking the kinds of questions a younger student might ask (genuine curiosity, "why?", "what does that mean?", a moment of distraction that requires the teacher to redirect), doing the activity as a younger student would, and giving honest feedback after. Then roles swap.

The teacher circulates during both rounds, making specific adjustments in real time — not interrupting the lesson-in-progress, but watching and noting, then giving feedback at a natural pause or at the transition between roles:

- *"Slow down in the Main Teaching section — you moved past the concrete anchor before the younger student had time to hold it."*
- *"Your activation question produced silence. What do you do if no one answers? Have a fallback — a simpler version of the same question, or a more direct connection to what they already know."*
- *"The activity ran seven minutes instead of five. What can you cut? Decide now, not on Teaching Day."*
- *"You were looking at your plan the whole time you were explaining. You know this material. Put the plan down for the Main Teaching section and speak from what you know."*
- *"What do you do if a younger student asks a question you don't know the answer to? Say it aloud to me — practice saying 'that's a really good question, I don't know the answer to that one, but here's what I do know.'"*

After both rounds: each pair takes two minutes to identify the one thing each person is going to change before Teaching Day.

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
*"Teaching Day is next class. One thing to carry: the lesson plan is a guide, not a script. If something unexpected happens — a younger student asks a question you didn't anticipate, the activity takes longer than planned, the room is noisier than expected — the lesson plan is what you fall back to, not what you're locked to. The plan is a structure that lets you stay present. Stay present. That is the single most important thing you bring into that room."*

Brief final check: does every student know — before they leave today — the grade, the room, the arrival time, and the receiving teacher's name? If anyone does not have this information, provide it now.

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader.

**Teacher's closing line:** *"The Sang offering carries our practice outward. Tomorrow — or whenever Teaching Day comes — you carry it further: into a room of younger students who will learn something from you. You are ready. Go make the one change you identified today, and then: you are ready."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: After rehearsal, the teacher confirms each student's readiness in the teacher's own log — not formally, not shared with students, but as an internal assessment. Note: which students are genuinely ready to teach; which have a minor issue they can correct before Teaching Day; which have a significant problem that requires additional guided time before C6. Any student who is significantly underprepared must have a brief one-on-one session with the teacher between C5 and C6 — not to rescue them from difficulty, but to ensure the younger students receive a genuine lesson rather than a confused performance.

The receiving class teacher should be re-confirmed now. Students should know:
- Which class they are teaching (grade, approximate age)
- What the younger students already know from their Dharma curriculum (so activation questions are calibrated correctly)
- The room or space where they will teach
- The arrival time and duration
- Whether they will teach sequentially (one student at a time while others observe) or in parallel groups

Remind students clearly: they are teaching real younger students. This is not a rehearsal for the younger students — it is the real thing for them. The younger students will learn from this lesson. That responsibility is real.

Spiral connection forward: the Teaching Day experience will generate the raw material for C7's three-part reflection (What I imagined / What happened / What I understand now). The teacher begins taking observational notes on each student's delivery during C6 — these notes support the C7 reflection conversation. Begin keeping the observation framework in mind from the rehearsal stage.

**My reflection** *(complete after class):*
Timing: ___
Energy & engagement: ___
Students to follow up: ___
What to change: ___
