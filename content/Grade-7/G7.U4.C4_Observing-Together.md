# G7.U4.C4 — Observing Together
## Grade 7 · Unit 4 · Class 4 of 4 · Observing Together
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** Reading the culminating reflection aloud — "What I honour, and what I owe" → the experience of declaring gratitude and intention in a community of practitioners, and the particular weight of a vow spoken in the presence of others
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Declaration as consolidation — when a student reads their reflection aloud in a ritual context, the act of public articulation consolidates understanding into a form that persists; this is not performance but a cognitive and affective event that the listener and the speaker both undergo
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluate / Create — students evaluate what they have received and what it means; they create a declaration of gratitude and intention that is both a summation of Grade 7 and a turning toward Grade 8
**Prior Class Summary:** C3 deepened understanding of the full Panglhabsol ceremony and students began their culminating reflection. They carried the Cham walk back into their bodies and the recognition: the arc of Grade 7 is the arc of practice. Sections 3 and 4 of the reflection were to be completed before today.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** that the word *vow* — a solemn intention — appears throughout the Himalayan Buddhist tradition (the Medicine Buddha's twelve vows, Tara's vow to remain in female form, the Bodhisattva vow of the Kyap Dro) and that section 4 of the reflection ("what I carry forward") is a vow in the same sense: a declared intention.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the sharing of this reflection in a ceremonial context is not a presentation but an act of declaration — a statement made in front of the lineage and the community of practitioners about what one has received and what one intends to carry.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** read their completed reflection aloud to the class with presence, holding the awareness that they are speaking both as an individual and as a practitioner in a lineage.

Students will **FEEL** the particular seriousness of a vow spoken aloud — however small, however provisional — and the recognition that Grade 7 is complete.

---

### Materials Checklist

- Student portfolios with completed four-section reflections ("What I honour, and what I owe")
- Writing instruments (for any last-minute additions)
- Seating in a circle or arc, arranged before students arrive
- Juniper or incense + fire-safe vessel (for a second small Sang offering at the opening, if possible — same materials as C2)
- Matches or lighter
- Open window or door access
- **Plan B (Sang offering):** If the offering cannot be performed for safety or logistical reasons, the candle version (as in C2 Plan B) is appropriate. If even a candle is not available, open the window before the prayer set and let the morning air or the sound of the outside world serve as the opening gesture — then proceed directly.
- **Plan B (incomplete reflections):** If a student has not finished sections 3 or 4, they use the first five minutes of class to complete them while others are settling. No student is excused from reading. A short, honest declaration is preferable to a long, vague one.

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 8 minutes

Full Grade 7 prayer set, student leader. The Rewo Sang Chod is chanted with full attention.

If the Sang offering is being performed today: while the prayer set is in progress, the teacher has already placed the vessel and materials. At the moment the Rewo Sang Chod is chanted, the teacher (or student leader, if comfortable) lights the offering — so the smoke begins as the prayer is spoken. The window is open. The smoke travels.

After the full prayer set: teacher speaks: "We open with the offering — because what we are about to do is an offering too."

One breath together. Silence.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — none

The offering is the preparation. There is no separate PKA. The transition into the sharing is direct.

---

#### Mini Lesson — 3 minutes

Teacher frames the sharing only. Brief.

"Your reflection is called 'What I honour, and what I owe.' Reading it aloud is an act of declaration — you are saying, in front of the lineage, what you have received and what you intend to carry forward. We listen as practitioners: full attention, no commentary. After each reading, thirty seconds of silence — then the Ngowa, one chanting together. Then the next person reads."

---

**Vocabulary Moment:** *Vow* — a solemn intention. The Medicine Buddha made twelve vows. Tara made a vow to remain in female form until samsara is emptied. The Bodhisattva vow in the Kyap Dro and Sem kye is a vow to work for the liberation of all beings. In section 4 of your reflection — what you carry forward into Grade 8 — you are making a vow. However small. However uncertain. It is real. The tradition does not require that a vow be complete before it is spoken. It requires that it be genuine.

---

**Body Moment:** Before any reading begins: everyone sits in meditation posture — spine upright, hands in lap, feet flat. One breath together, in and out. Teacher: "Whatever happens in this room in the next thirty minutes — receive it with full attention. That is the practice."

---

**Emotional Hook:** The particular seriousness of a vow spoken aloud in a community of practitioners. This is named here so the teacher can hold the tone — not solemnly performed, but genuinely present.

---

#### Activity — 25 minutes

**The sharing.**

Each student reads their complete reflection aloud — all four sections. The order is volunteered or determined by the student leader before class begins. No pressure on order; what matters is that every student reads.

**Structure for each reading:**
- Student reads all four sections of the reflection
- Thirty seconds of shared silence
- The whole class chants the Ngowa together once — one clean chanting, unhurried
- Next student reads

The teacher holds the space. No commentary between readings. The Ngowa is the response — not a reaction, but a continuation.

If a student becomes emotional, the teacher holds steady. The student continues when ready. If they cannot continue, the teacher reads the remaining lines quietly, without explanation.

**After all have read:**

Teacher: "We close by directing what is in this room — all of it — outward."

One final chanting of the Rewo Sang Chod together. If the smoke offering is still present (candle or remaining incense), students direct their attention toward it as they chant. If it has ended, they direct their attention toward the open window.

Then: one full minute of silence.

---

#### Debrief — 3 minutes

"Grade 7 is complete. You began this year studying suffering — what causes it, what we are responsible for when we see it in others. You ended it by honouring the lineage that taught you how to meet suffering with practice. That is the arc: from empathy to teaching to healing to honouring. It is also the arc of the Buddhist path."

"Grade 8 is the final year. You will study the nature of mind, the life of Milarepa, the stupas, and — for the first time in full — the story of Pajapati. You will come to it differently than you came to any other grade. Because you have done what you did this year. You have been a teacher. The things you vowed to carry today — carry them."

---

#### Closing — 2 minutes

Full prayer set, student leader. Food Offering Prayer included.

Teacher's closing line: one sentence for the whole year. The teacher writes this line before class — it is not improvised. It should hold the arc: from cruelty to empathy to teaching to healing to honouring. One sentence.

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*

Carry forward: Reflections are the Unit 4 portfolio artefact. Collect them after the sharing — together with the Unit 1 position paper, the Unit 2 lesson plan and reflection, and the Unit 3 healing aspiration, these form the Grade 7 portfolio. This is the most substantial body of student-generated work in the curriculum to date. Four pieces, four modes: analytical argument, pedagogical design, healing aspiration, and personal declaration. Each was composed under the discipline of a formal structure. Collect all four and keep them in the class portfolio record.

The debrief names Grade 8 directly and specifically: the nature of mind, Milarepa, the stupas, and Pajapati's full story. This is intentional. Grade 7 students should know what is coming so that the transition is not a break but a continuation. The teacher who receives this class into Grade 8 should know the full Pajapati thread — carried since Grade 2, partially told in Grade 6, fully told in Grade 8 for the first time — and understand why the Grade 8 telling lands so differently than it would have in an earlier year. The thread has been held for six years.

The teacher's closing line is the most important single sentence of Grade 7. Write it before class. It should hold the year's arc honestly: not a summary, not a moral, but a sentence that is true and particular to what this class has done this year.

Sensitive content note: this class is the closing ceremony of Grade 7. For some students, it may be the last year they are in the same class configuration. For others, it is simply a good year ending well. The teacher does not need to mark the emotional weight verbally — the ceremony does it. Hold the space and let it be what it is.

GRADE 8 NOTE: Grade 8 is "The Nature of Mind" — the final year of the program. It includes the full Pajapati novel study (Units 1–4, specifically Unit 4 all seven classes). Students who have carried Pajapati's name since Grade 2 — who heard a fragment of her story in Grade 6, who have never known her full arc — now receive it. The teacher who opens Grade 8 should understand: these students have been waiting for this story, even if they do not know they have been waiting. The thread has been carried for six years. Receive it carefully.

Spiral connections: Kyap Dro and Bodhisattva vow (throughout the curriculum — explicitly named here as a vow-form parallel to the student's vow in section 4); Ngowa dedication prayer (since Grade 1 — here it closes each reading as a formal dedication of what was offered); Pajapati thread (held since Grade 2, not told at Grade 7 — the thread is named in the debrief only in terms of what is coming, not what is still withheld).
