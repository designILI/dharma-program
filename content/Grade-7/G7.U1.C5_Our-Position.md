# G7.U1.C5 — Our Position
## Grade 7 · Unit 1 · Class 5 of 5 · Our Position
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A piece of writing held in two hands → a genuinely-formed position that can be defended and has cost something to build
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Productive struggle followed by consolidation — the long arc of the unit (disruption in C1, deepening in C2, embodied perspective-taking in C3, evaluative analysis in C4) culminates in C5's making moment. The writing is not a test of memory; it is the act of synthesis through which the student's position becomes genuinely theirs.
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluate and Create — students complete a position paper (Evaluate: building a defensible claim, acknowledging counter-argument, marshalling evidence), then share it orally (Create: presenting original thinking to an audience and holding it under the encounter with other positions).
**Prior Class Summary:** C4 introduced three bystander response frameworks and students began drafting their position papers. They carry the recognition that there is no clean formula — only the principle of reducing suffering without creating new harm — and the specific claim they began constructing in C4.

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the four structural features of a strong Bloom's Evaluate-level position paper: clear defensible claim, evidence from what has been studied, acknowledgment of the strongest counter-argument, and a reason the position holds despite the counter-argument
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that completing and sharing a position is a practice act — not a performance of the correct view, but an honest articulation of what they have actually worked out
Students will **BE ABLE TO** complete a position paper that holds a defensible claim and share its central claim and one supporting argument with the group
Students will **FEEL** the particular satisfaction — specific and quiet — of having finished a piece of writing that articulates something genuinely worked out and genuinely held

---

### Materials Checklist
- C4 draft position papers (students bring theirs; teacher has any that were left)
- Fresh paper for students who need to start over or expand significantly
- Pens or pencils, one per student
- Board and chalk or marker for the four structural features (written at lesson start)
- Grade 7 prayer set visible
- **Plan B (for sharing):** If any student is not ready to share aloud (paper incomplete, genuine reluctance), allow that student to share in a pair rather than the full group. Pair sharing is not a lesser option — it is appropriate when the writing is genuinely private or unfinished. The goal is that every student hears their own position spoken aloud, not that every position is spoken publicly.

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes
*"Before you finish your paper — has your position shifted since C1? Not necessarily the conclusion. But what you understand about why you hold it — has that changed?"*

Take 3–4 responses. Do not evaluate them — simply receive. The purpose of this question is to help students locate where they are before the final writing push.

*"Good. Whatever shifted — or didn't — belongs in your paper. The paper is a record of where you actually are, not where you started."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 5 minutes

**What makes a position paper strong at Bloom's Evaluate level.**

Write the four features on the board and leave them visible for the writing phase:

1. **A clear, defensible claim.** Not: *"I think everyone should be kind."* But: *"Lateral support — moving toward the person being harmed without confronting the bully directly — is the most consistently available form of bystander action, because it requires less courage than direct confrontation while delivering direct benefit to the person who most needs it."* That is a claim. It can be argued against. It can be defended.

2. **Evidence from what we've studied.** Not just assertion. Use the Three Chairs exercise, the bystander effect, the psychological roots of bullying, the Buddhist framing — the unit has given you material. Use it.

3. **The strongest counter-argument.** Acknowledge it honestly. If your claim is that lateral support is best, the counter-argument is: *"But it doesn't address the bully's behaviour — the harm continues."* Write that. Don't hide it.

4. **Why you hold the position despite the counter-argument.** This is the most important part. Anyone can acknowledge a counter-argument. Holding a position despite one requires genuine reasoning: *"Lateral support still leaves the behaviour unaddressed — but in situations where direct intervention risks escalation or makes the target a further focus, the reduction of isolation is the most immediate need. The imperfect response that can actually be made is more valuable than the ideal response that cannot."*

*"The paper does not need to be long. It needs to be honest and specific."*

---

**Vocabulary Moment:**
Write on the board: **burden of proof** — the responsibility resting on the person making a claim to support it with evidence sufficient to justify belief.

*"Your position paper carries the burden of proof for the position you take. If you claim that direct intervention is always most effective, the burden is on you to address the clear situations where it isn't. If you claim that bystanders have no real responsibility because the social cost is too high, the burden is on you to address what that means for the person being harmed. The burden of proof is not an accusation — it is the intellectual standard that makes a position worth holding."*

---

**Body Moment:**
*"Take your draft paper. Hold it with both hands — actually hold it. Read it silently, from the beginning. Read it as if someone else wrote it."*

Sixty seconds of silence.

*"Is this what you actually believe? Is there a sentence in there you wrote because it sounded right — but you don't yet own it? Underline anything that still feels borrowed. Those sentences need revision before you share."*

---

**Emotional Hook:** The specific experience of reading one's own writing and finding that some of it is genuine and some is performance — and the clarity that comes from being able to tell the difference. The satisfaction of finishing is real. So is the discomfort of revision.

---

#### Activity — 35 minutes

**Phase 1 — Complete the position paper (20 min).**

Students write. The teacher circulates with specific, honest feedback — not generic encouragement:

- *"Your claim is clear. What's the strongest argument against it? Have you addressed it?"*
- *"You've acknowledged the counter-argument — but what is your response to it? Why do you hold your position despite it?"*
- *"This paragraph is assertion without evidence. What from our unit supports it?"*
- *"This sentence reads like what you think you're supposed to think. What do you actually think?"*

Students who are confident and finished early: *"Read it again. Find the weakest sentence — the one you're least sure of. Strengthen it or cut it."*

Students who are significantly behind or struggling: Sit with them briefly. *"What is your claim — say it aloud to me in one sentence."* Once the claim is spoken, help them write it down. The paper follows from the claim.

---

**Phase 2 — Sharing (15 min).**

Each student reads their paper's central claim and one supporting argument to the group. Not the whole paper — just these two elements. This keeps the sharing round tight and ensures every student speaks.

The group listens without comment while each student shares. No immediate critique.

After all have shared, the teacher asks: *"Did anyone shift their position slightly as a result of what you heard? Not a full reversal — a small adjustment. Why?"*

Take 3–4 responses. These responses are the living evidence of why the sharing round matters: encountering other people's genuinely formed positions is itself a form of evidence.

---

#### Debrief — 3 minutes
*"Grade 7 is the year you become teachers. Unit 2 begins next class — you will design and teach actual lessons to younger students. The unit we just completed matters for that: you cannot teach empathy without having sat in three chairs. You cannot teach about cruelty without having formed and defended your own position on it. You carry something real into Unit 2. That matters."*

Collect position papers. These are the Unit 1 portfolio artefact.

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: Position papers are the Unit 1 portfolio artefact — collect them at the close of C5. Review them before C7 of Unit 2 (the Reflection class), where students will be asked to connect their empathy understanding to their teaching experience. Note which students produced genuinely formed positions and which produced more surface-level responses — this information is useful for guiding the quality of Unit 2 reflections.

The bridge to Unit 2 at the end of the debrief is critical. Students who genuinely engaged with Unit 1 arrive at Unit 2 with earned seriousness — they have done the interior work. Students who went through the motions of Unit 1 will find Unit 2 superficial as a result. The bridge sentence (*"you cannot teach empathy without having sat in three chairs"*) is not rhetorical — it is a functional truth about what Unit 1 built.

Unit 2 is the most logistically complex unit in the curriculum. Read the full Unit 2 blueprint before teaching C5 so the transition is clear and students understand what is coming. Coordinate with the receiving class teacher before C5 is taught — Teaching Day (U2.C6) requires advance scheduling with a Grade 3, 4, or 5 class. If that coordination has not happened, it must happen immediately.

Spiral connection: the position paper format (claim, evidence, counter-argument, response) is a skill that appears again in Grade 8's philosophical synthesis unit. Students are building a writing practice, not just completing an assignment. Note the quality of their argumentative structure — students who struggle with the four-part format will need support in Grade 8.
