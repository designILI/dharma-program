# G7.U3.C1 — The Healer
## Grade 7 · Unit 3 · Class 1 of 4 · The Healer
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** The Medicine Buddha and his twelve vows → what it means to hold a genuine healing intention for a specific person
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Emotional grounding before conceptual content — students locate a real person and a real wish before they encounter the doctrine; the concept lands inside an experience already in the room
**Bloom's Level:** Understand / early Evaluate — students encounter the Medicine Buddha's framework, begin to evaluate what healing means in the Buddhist sense, and make a real choice (their chosen person) that carries the unit
**Prior Class Summary:** The final class of Unit 2 (C7) was the three-part reflection on Teaching Day. Students reflected on what they imagined, what happened, and what they understand now. They carry the Feynman Principle's final lesson: the teaching came back to them; they learned twice.

---

### Learning Objectives

Students will **KNOW** who Sangye Menla (the Medicine Buddha) is — his appearance, his twelve vows, and the purpose of his practice in the Himalayan Buddhist tradition.

Students will **UNDERSTAND** that the Buddhist understanding of healing extends beyond the physical — that healing intention, when genuine and sustained, is itself a form of compassionate practice.

Students will **BE ABLE TO** identify one real person in their life to hold through this unit, and articulate — in writing — what they want that person to be free from.

Students will **FEEL** the quiet recognition that the capacity for genuine healing intention is already present in them — that it requires direction and form, not a new quality they do not yet possess.

---

### Materials Checklist

- Student portfolios (one per student)
- Writing instruments
- Image of Sangye Menla if available (printed or displayed — lapis-lazuli blue, myrobalan fruit in right hand, medicine bowl in left, seated in meditation posture); if no image, the teacher describes him in sufficient detail that students can form an internal picture
- Whiteboard or large paper for noting responses during PKA
- **Plan B (image):** If no image is available, teacher draws a simple silhouette on the board and narrates the visual details — the practice does not depend on the physical image

---

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes

Full Grade 7 prayer set, student leader.

Prayer sequence: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod

Student leader sets the pace and holds the space. The teacher follows the student leader's lead, only correcting if the sequence is lost.

After prayers, the teacher speaks: "Units 1 and 2 turned you outward — toward cruelty and empathy, toward teaching. Now we turn inward again, but with what you've earned. Unit 3 is about healing."

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5 minutes

Teacher: *"When you think of healing — not the Buddhist version, just the word itself — what do you imagine? What does healing look like?"*

Take 3–4 responses. Students will likely offer physical healing (illness, injury), but may also offer emotional healing, relational repair, or spiritual renewal. Write each category on the board as it arises — physical, emotional, relational, spiritual — without ranking them.

"All of those are real. All of those are forms of healing. Today we add one more: the Buddhist understanding of what healing is at its root. And it turns out to be connected to everything you just named."

---

#### Mini Lesson — 12 minutes

**Introducing the Medicine Buddha.**

If an image is available, place it where the whole class can see it and allow a moment of simple looking before speaking.

"This is Sangye Menla — the Medicine Buddha. Notice: he is lapis-lazuli blue, the colour of a deep sky. In his right hand, he holds a myrobalan plant — a medicinal herb. In his left hand, a bowl of medicine. He is seated in the full meditation posture. He is completely still. And he has that quality you can sometimes feel from a figure like this: total warmth, total stillness, at the same time. Hold that image."

**His twelve vows.**

"Sangye Menla made twelve great vows. Each vow is a commitment to heal a specific form of suffering: illness, poverty, being born in circumstances that make practice impossible, ignorance, suffering caused by gender discrimination, sensory impairments, fear, hunger. The vow-form is total — not 'I will try to help those who are suffering from illness' but 'I vow that those who even hear my name will be freed from this suffering.' That is the scale of the vow. That completeness — that is what makes him the Medicine Buddha. Not the lapis skin, not the myrobalan. The vow."

**What his practice is for.**

"The Medicine Buddha practice is not a claim that chanting will cure illness. It is the practice of genuinely directing one's attention and care toward the suffering of a specific being. The healing intention, when practised with full attention, is itself a form of compassion in action. The tradition says it changes the practitioner as well as — in some dimension we cannot measure — the person being held."

**Connection to Unit 1.**

"Last unit we studied empathy. Remember: empathy is not sympathy. Sympathy is 'I feel sorry for you.' Empathy is: I actually allow myself to feel what you are feeling, from the inside. Healing intention is empathy in action — directed, sustained, specific. It is a decision to put one's practice in service of another person's liberation from suffering."

---

**Vocabulary Moment:** *Metta* (Pali) / *maitrī* (Sanskrit) — loving-kindness. Often translated as "loving friendliness." It is the wish that all beings be happy and free from suffering, directed first toward oneself, then outward in expanding circles. The Medicine Buddha practice is a *metta* practice with a specific form — visual, mantra, dedicated to a particular person.

---

**Body Moment:** "Think of one person in your life who is suffering — physically, emotionally, or in any way. Hold their face in your mind. Hold their name. Now — let yourself actually feel that you want them to be well. Not as an idea. As a genuine wish. Let it be real, for thirty seconds."

Teacher holds silence for thirty seconds. Then: "That quality — right there, what you just felt — is the beginning of the Medicine Buddha practice. You do not need to learn a new capacity. You already have it. What the practice gives you is a form and a direction."

---

**Emotional Hook:** The recognition that the quality of genuine healing intention is already present — students have it now, as they sit here. The practice does not install something foreign. It focuses something real.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 4 minutes

*"Tell your partner: who is the person you held in your mind just now? You don't have to say their name if you don't want to. But tell me this: what is the quality of your wish for them? And what, specifically, do you want them to be free from?"*

After 3 minutes of partner talk, take one or two brief responses from the room — receiving them with full attention, no evaluation. "Thank you. What you just named — hold that."

---

#### Activity — 12 minutes

Students write in their portfolios. Teacher prompt, written on the board:

*Write:*
*1. Who the Medicine Buddha is and what his practice is for — in your own words, enough that you could explain it to someone who has never heard of him.*
*2. The one person you are going to hold through this unit. (You do not need to write their name — describe them if you prefer. But be specific about the person. This practice is not abstract.)*
*3. What, specifically, you want that person to be free from.*

Teacher circulates. The main coaching note at this stage: "Make sure the person is real. Not a concept of a person — a specific human being you actually know." If a student has chosen a public figure or someone they have never met, gently ask: "Is there someone closer? Someone whose suffering you actually know from the inside?"

Students should be writing for most of this time. The teacher reads over shoulders quietly, checking that the third point — the specific suffering — is not abstract.

---

#### Debrief — 3 minutes

"The Medicine Buddha made twelve vows — complete, total, irreversible commitments to heal specific forms of suffering. He didn't write a general wish. He was specific about what each vow was for. In C4, you will make one aspiration, for one person. It will not be complete or perfect. But it will be real — specific about the person, specific about the suffering. That specificity is what makes it practice rather than sentiment."

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes

Full prayer set, student leader. Food Offering Prayer included.

Teacher's closing line: "The person you chose today — carry them. Between now and next class, whenever you think of them, let yourself feel that quality again: the genuine wish that they be free. That's enough for now."

---

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*

Carry forward: The person chosen in C1 is the anchor of the entire unit. Students hold this person through C2 (the mantra), C3 (the aspiration draft), and C4 (reading the aspiration aloud and dedicating it). The choice made in this class matters — it shapes everything that follows. Some students will choose a grandparent or parent who is ill. Some will choose a classmate they are quietly worried about. Some — and this is valid and should be received with complete openness — will choose someone they are in conflict with. All of these are legitimate Medicine Buddha practice contexts; the tradition explicitly includes difficult relationships. If a student chooses someone in serious or acute suffering (a dying parent, a sibling in crisis), receive this with particular steadiness. The teacher's presence and composure are the most important support available in that moment — not a referral, not a resource, just genuine human acknowledgment.

The connection between this unit and Unit 1 (empathy, bystander responsibility, the position paper on cruelty) is explicit: genuine healing intention requires the same quality of attention as empathy. The Unit 1 position papers asked students to take a real ethical position; the Unit 3 aspiration asks them to direct real practice toward a real person. The same quality of non-abstraction applies. Hold students to it.

Spiral connections: Unit 1 empathy framework (Grade 7); Unit 2 Feynman Principle and the experience of caring for a younger student's understanding (Grade 7 — carry the continuity explicitly); metta practice as first introduced in Grade 4 Unit 1.
