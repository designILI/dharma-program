# G7.U1.C1 — What Bullying Really Is
## Grade 7 · Unit 1 · Class 1 of 5 · What Bullying Really Is
*Duration: 40 minutes*

**Topic (Concrete and Invisible):** A familiar word — "bullying" → the psychological roots of power, fear, and suffering that word conceals
**Cognitive Principle Featured:** Conceptual disruption — the lesson begins by surfacing the student's existing mental model, then introduces information that reveals that model to be incomplete. The discomfort of revision is itself the learning.
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluate — students assess the adequacy of the simple story ("bullying = being mean") against a more accurate psychological and Buddhist account, and begin forming a judgment about where cruelty comes from.
**Prior Class Summary:** This is the first class of Grade 7. Students carry Grade 6's serious engagement with impermanence, the Bardo, and the body as a vehicle for the Dharma (Cham Dance). They arrive knowing the year's theme: "Teaching as Practice — what does it mean to truly understand something? What changes when you have to teach it?"

---

### Learning Objectives
Students will **KNOW** the distinction between bullying and conflict (power imbalance, repetition, inability to stop it) and the three main psychological roots of bullying behaviour
Students will **UNDERSTAND** that harmful action arises from suffering and confusion — not from a fundamentally bad nature — and that understanding this changes the response without removing responsibility
Students will **BE ABLE TO** write a first-draft journal entry that honestly describes what has shifted in their thinking about bullying by the end of this class
Students will **FEEL** the specific discomfort of recognising that the capacity for cruelty is not alien — that it arises from the same fear and powerlessness they have themselves experienced

---

### Materials Checklist
- Journals or paper, one per student
- Pens or pencils, one per student
- Board and chalk or marker for vocabulary
- Grade 7 prayer set printed or displayed (including Rewo Sang Chod text and meaning — this is first use)
- Rewo Sang Chod pronunciation guide for the teacher, prepared in advance
- **Plan B (for journal entry):** If the room is too activated after the body moment and turn-and-talk to settle into writing, move the journal entry to the first ten minutes of C2, and use the remaining time for a spoken sharing round instead. Students describe in one sentence what has shifted — no writing required.

### Lesson Body

#### Opening — 5 minutes
Full Grade 7 prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod.

**Rewo Sang Chod — first introduction.** Before chanting, the teacher explains:

*"This prayer is new to Grade 7 — it is called the Rewo Sang Chod, the Smoke Offering prayer. We follow the rule we always follow here: meaning first, then chanting. The Sang offering purifies the environment, invites positive qualities, and dedicates our practice outward — to all beings, without exception. The smoke carries the prayers. When we chant it today, we are sending our intention outward, beyond this room. This matters especially in a unit about cruelty and empathy — we are dedicating our understanding to beings who are suffering."*

Then chant together for the first time, slowly. Student leader leads the remainder of the prayer set. Food Offering Prayer is closing only.

---

#### Prior Knowledge Activation — 5–8 minutes
Teacher: *"When you were younger — six, seven, eight years old — what did you think bullying was? Not the definition from a book. Your actual idea of it then."*

Take 3–4 responses. Receive them fully — write key words on the board.

Then: *"Now: do you think that's the whole picture? What's missing from those descriptions?"*

Take 2–3 more responses.

**Strong response:** Students identify that bullying involves some element of power, or that the simple idea doesn't explain why someone would do it. Build on whatever they offer.

**Fallback** (if responses are thin or students are quiet): *"Most people when they're young think bullying means someone being mean on purpose. Is 'mean' the same as 'bullying'? Can you be mean to someone without bullying them?"* This distinction usually opens the conversation.

Close the PKA: *"Today we look at what's actually going on — not just what to call it."*

---

#### Mini Lesson — 10–15 minutes

**Part 1: Bullying vs. conflict.**

Most people use the word loosely. The important distinction: conflict is two people in genuine disagreement with roughly equal power — it can be fierce, but both people have real standing in it. Bullying is different in three ways: (1) there is a power imbalance — one person or group uses social, physical, or verbal power that the other cannot easily match; (2) it is repeated — not a single incident but a pattern; (3) the person being harmed cannot easily stop it. These three features together are what distinguish bullying from ordinary conflict.

Write on the board: **CONFLICT** (equal power, genuine disagreement) · **BULLYING** (power imbalance + repetition + inability to stop it).

*"That's useful — but it's still the surface. The more important question is: why does someone bully? Because if you don't understand the why, you can't respond wisely."*

**Part 2: The psychological roots.**

The standard answer — "they're just mean" or "they have no empathy" — is insufficient. Research and observation both point to a more accurate answer: people who bully are typically experiencing significant suffering themselves. Specifically: **fear of powerlessness** (they feel unsafe, out of control, or humiliated, and the only strategy they have found is to establish dominance over someone else); **disconnection from belonging** (they feel on the outside of a group they want to be inside, and aggression is the strategy they're using to manage that); **shame** (deep, often hidden shame about some aspect of themselves, and cruelty toward others deflects attention outward).

*"Bullying is a mistaken strategy the suffering self uses to manage its own pain. That is the Buddhist insight: all harmful action arises from suffering and confusion — not from a fundamentally bad nature."*

Pause. Let that land.

*"Critically: understanding this does not excuse the behaviour. It does not reduce the responsibility to stop it. What it changes is how we respond — from 'punish the bad person' to 'address the suffering that's driving the behaviour.' Those lead to very different places."*

**Preview of C2:** Next class uses the Three Chairs exercise — three perspectives: Chair 1 (the person being bullied), Chair 2 (the person doing the bullying), Chair 3 (the bystander). Today's content is the foundation for that.

---

**Vocabulary Moment:**
Write on the board: **empathy** — from the Greek *em* (in) + *pathos* (feeling). To feel *into* another's experience.

*"Empathy is not the same as sympathy. Sympathy: I feel sorry for you — from outside your experience. Empathy: I understand what it's like from inside your experience. Sympathy looks at suffering from across the room. Empathy sits down inside it. We will work on both this unit — but empathy is the harder and more useful one."*

---

**Body Moment:**
*"Stand up, or sit very still — whichever keeps you most present. I want you to think of a time you felt genuinely powerless. A specific situation where you had no good options — nothing you could do would fix it. Hold that feeling. Don't explain it, don't narrate it — just feel it. Ten seconds."*

Silence. Ten seconds.

*"Now: that feeling — the one you just held — is the engine of most bullying. The person doing the bullying is usually trying to escape exactly what you just felt. They've found a strategy. It's the wrong strategy. But it starts in the same place you just visited."*

---

**Emotional Hook:** The discomfort of recognising that the capacity for cruelty is not a foreign thing — it lives in the same interior space as one's own fear and powerlessness. This recognition is not comfortable. It should not be made comfortable too quickly. Let it sit.

---

#### Turn and Talk — 3–5 minutes
*"Tell your partner: have you ever done something unkind — not necessarily bullying, just unkind — because you were afraid? Or because you were trying to feel safe or in control? You don't have to share the detail with me or the group. But tell your partner honestly: has it happened?"*

Pause for 90 seconds of genuine partner conversation.

Then: *"What does that tell you about where cruelty comes from?"*

Take 2–3 responses to the whole group — not to interrogate but to name: this recognition is real, it is shared, and it is the beginning of understanding rather than the end.

**What to listen for:** Students who make the connection between their own experience of fear/powerlessness and the roots of cruelty. Also: students who resist — who say "I'd never bully someone." That resistance is worth holding gently: the unit is not accusing anyone. It is examining something that lives in all of us.

---

#### Activity — 15–20 minutes
Journal entry 1: *"What I used to think bullying was — and what I think now."*

This is not a polished essay. It is a genuine first-draft encounter with the complexity this class has opened. Instructions:

*"Write for ten minutes. Begin with what you thought bullying was before today. Then: what has shifted? What do you understand now that you didn't? And — if something still doesn't sit right with you, write that too. The resistance is as important as the acceptance."*

The teacher circulates — not correcting, but asking questions quietly: *"What changed for you? What still doesn't sit right? Is there something you're not sure you believe yet?"*

Students who finish early: *"Add a paragraph about the body moment — what you felt when you held that powerlessness. What did it tell you?"*

Students who are struggling to write: *"Start with one sentence: 'Before today I thought bullying was...' That's your first line. The next line will follow."*

---

#### Debrief — 5 minutes
*"Buddhism teaches that all harmful action arises from suffering and confusion. Not from a bad nature — from suffering and confusion. That's not a comfortable idea, because it makes cruelty understandable. Understanding cruelty doesn't mean accepting it. It means we know what we're actually looking at when we see it — and that changes what we can do about it."*

Brief: does anyone want to share one sentence from their journal? Voluntary. Do not press.

*"Next class we go deeper into the inner life of the person who bullies — and we begin to ask what practice asks of us in response."*

---

#### Closing — 3 minutes
Full prayer set: Kyap Dro · Om Mani Padme Hung (7×) · Manjushri Prayer · Sem kye · Ngowa · Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer · Food Offering Prayer · Sevenline extended form · Dusum Sangay · Dechen Monlam · Dolma / Tara Prayer · Rewo Sang Chod. Student leader.

**Teacher's closing line:** *"The Sang offering carries our prayers outward — to all beings. Today's understanding belongs not just to you. It belongs to everyone whose suffering we were just trying to see."*

### Teacher Notes

**Curriculum guidance** *(read before class — written by curriculum author):*
Carry forward: The Rewo Sang Chod introduction requires unhurried care — probably two to three minutes before the first chanting. Prepare the pronunciation in advance; students will follow the teacher's lead on a new prayer, and hesitancy in delivery signals that the prayer itself is unfamiliar rather than new-to-them. The meaning-first protocol (established for Dolma in Grade 6) applies in full: no chanting before the meaning is given.

This unit will be personally encountering for twelve-year-olds. Many students will carry direct experience of bullying — as target, as perpetrator, or as bystander. All three positions must be held with equal care throughout the unit. This class in particular: do not allow the classroom dynamic to implicitly position some students as "the bullies" and others as "the victims." The psychological root framework (cruelty arises from suffering) is specifically designed to dissolve that binary. Return to it whenever the conversation starts to sort into categories.

The body moment (holding powerlessness for ten seconds) is the emotional core of C1. Give it real time and real silence. If students giggle or deflect, wait. The instruction is clear and the silence is part of it.

C2 preview: the Three Chairs exercise requires understanding before C2. Read C3 in full before teaching C2. The chairs are: Chair 1 (the person being bullied), Chair 2 (the person doing the bullying), Chair 3 (the bystander). Students take all three perspectives.

Spiral connection: Grade 6 — the Bardo unit established serious engagement with the interior life, including the "Do you believe it?" discussion about difficult teachings. Students at Grade 7 arrive having been in serious territory; they can hold complexity. Grade 5 — the three poisons (aversion, ignorance, desire) via the Wheel of Life. Name these in C2; they have the language.
