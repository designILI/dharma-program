The Year in One Sentence

Grade 7 is the year students become teachers — and then discover, through that experience, what it means to be a practitioner who heals, holds, and honours.

Central question: What does it mean to truly understand something? What changes when you have to teach it?

The Year's Central Reversal

Grade 7 is structured around a single movement that deepens across four units.

Units 1 and 2 face outward. In Unit 1, students analyse suffering they have witnessed — the suffering of cruelty, bystander dynamics, the ethics of empathy. They write a personal position paper: a committed, non-abstract argument. In Unit 2, they take the Feynman Principle ("you only truly understand something when you can teach it") and apply it. They design a lesson for a younger class, teach it, watch what happens, and reflect in three parts on the gap between what they imagined and what occurred. By the end of Unit 2, students have been transmitters. They have stood in front of younger children and given them the Dharma. That changes them.

Units 3 and 4 face inward — but inward with everything Unit 2 earned. Unit 3 asks: what does it mean to hold the healing intention for someone else, the way you once held a younger student's learning? The Medicine Buddha practice is the structure. The healing aspiration is the artefact. The thirty-second body moment of C1 — the genuine wish for a specific person to be well — is the foundation the whole unit builds on. Unit 4 asks: what does it mean to honour what was given to you? Panglhabsol is the ceremony. The lineage map is the scaffold. The culminating reflection is the declaration.

The arc of the year: from receiving the dharma (Grades 1–6) to transmitting it (Unit 2) to practising it for others (Unit 3) to honouring what transmitted it (Unit 4). This arc is the practitioner's path in miniature. Students do not need to be told this — the structure enacts it.

Bloom's Level: Evaluate and Create

Grade 7 is the first year operating at the full top two levels of Bloom's taxonomy — Evaluate and Create — throughout all four units. What this means in practice:

Evaluate requires students to make judgments with criteria — not simply to describe or apply but to assess. In Unit 1, they evaluate the ethical claims around bystander responsibility and take a position. In Unit 3, they evaluate whether the suffering they have named in the healing aspiration is what the person actually carries (not what the student imagines they carry). In Unit 4, they evaluate the meaning of lineage and what they specifically owe to the transmission they have received. All of these require actual judgment, not paraphrase.

Create asks students to produce something that did not exist before, using what they know. The position paper, the lesson plan, the healing aspiration, and the culminating reflection are all original compositions with formal structures. The structures are not constraints on originality — they are the discipline that prevents vagueness. The teacher's coaching throughout the year holds students to the same standard: specificity over sentiment, precision over goodwill. This is what Evaluate and Create require at this age.

In practice: expect significant range. Some students will produce work of genuine depth; others will stay in the comfortable territory of general statements. The teacher's role is to see the difference and name it — not critically, but precisely. "Is that specific? What do you actually know?" is the year's coaching question.

The Prayer Set

Grade 7 uses the full twelve-prayer set at every opening and closing:

  1. Kyap Dro
  2. Om Mani Padme Hung (7×)
  3. Manjushri Prayer
  4. Sem kye
  5. Ngowa
  6. Seven-Line Guru Rinpoche Prayer
  7. Sevenline extended form
  8. Dusum Sangay
  9. Dechen Monlam
  10. Dolma / Tara Prayer
  11. Rewo Sang Chod — NEW this grade
  12. Food Offering Prayer (closing only)

The Rewo Sang Chod is introduced in Unit 1, Class 1. Before the first chanting, the teacher gives the meaning: this is the Mountain Smoke Offering, the prayer addressed to the Dharma Protectors and the mountain protectors of the Himalayan tradition, particularly Kangchenjunga. It is the practice that becomes the focus of Unit 4. Students learn the meaning before they chant — this is the curriculum's meaning-first protocol, established in Grade 1 and held throughout.

The Rewo Sang Chod receives additional attention in Unit 4: it is led with particular deliberateness, chanted extra times in U4 classes, and performed as a real smoke offering in U4 C2 and C4 (conditions permitting).

Student Leader Protocol

The student leader protocol is inherited from Grade 6. One student leads the prayer set — both opening and closing — at each class. The rotation is managed by the class (alphabetical, volunteer, or whatever system they have established). The teacher does not determine the rotation but may need to hold it in the first week if the class has not yet found its own system.

The student leader sets the pace of the prayer set. The teacher follows the student leader's pace and corrects only if the sequence is lost. By Grade 7, most students have been leading prayers for six years; the protocol should run without significant teacher management. If a student leads for the first time or leads with unusual uncertainty, the teacher holds quietly alongside — not correcting, but present.

The Making Thread — Four Artefacts

Grade 7 produces the curriculum's most substantial student portfolio. Four artefacts, four modes of composition, four disciplines of honesty:

Unit 1 — Personal Position Paper on cruelty and bystander responsibility. Argument-form writing. Students must take a position and defend it with specificity about what they believe and why. Not a report — a commitment.

Unit 2 — Lesson Plan + Three-Part Teaching Reflection. The lesson plan is a working document — not an ideal, but a real plan used to teach a real class. The three-part reflection (what I imagined / what happened / what I understand now) is the Feynman Principle in reflective form. The gap between the imagined and the actual is the learning.

Unit 3 — Healing Aspiration. Formal written wish for a specific person — four parts: name and affection, specific suffering named, the wish for liberation, dedication of merit. Composed over two classes, chanted for in between, read aloud and dedicated in C4. The aspiration is the most intimate piece of writing in the curriculum to this point.

Unit 4 — Lineage-and-Gratitude Reflection. "What I honour, and what I owe." Four sections: the lineage I am part of (names, not categories); what this year taught me about transmission; what I am grateful for (specifically); what I carry forward into Grade 8 (a vow). Read aloud as a declaration. The closing piece of the year.

All four artefacts should be collected and kept in a class portfolio record. They are the evidence of the year and the foundation for the Grade 8 teacher's knowledge of who enters their room.

Logistics Notes

Grade 7 is the most logistically complex grade in the curriculum. Three units have external components that require advance planning. Do not leave any of these to the last moment.

Unit 2 — Near-Peer Teaching Project: Students teach a lesson to a younger class. This requires scheduling in advance with the receiving class's teacher — confirming the date, the space, the younger students' availability, and the topic fit. This coordination should happen before Unit 2 begins. A Teaching Day that falls apart because of scheduling failures is not recoverable in forty minutes. The project is the spine of Unit 2; its logistical success is the teacher's responsibility.

Unit 4 — Sang Offering: The smoke offering in U4 C2 and C4 requires juniper branches or appropriate fragrant incense, a fire-safe vessel, matches or lighter, and reliable access to an open window or outdoor space. Confirm with school administration whether open flame is permitted in the classroom. Source materials before Unit 4 begins — not between C1 and C2. The Plan B (candle) is adequate but significantly less powerful as a sensory anchor for the practice.

Unit 4 — Panglhabsol Ceremony: If the school's actual Panglhabsol ceremony falls during or near Unit 4, coordinate with school administration to bring Grade 7 students to observe or participate. Check the school calendar at the start of the year and note the date. A Grade 7 student watching the actual Cham Dance, smelling the actual Sang, and seeing the whole ceremony held by a community of practitioners is a more powerful experience than any classroom version can replicate. Plan for it if it is available.

Pajapati Thread — No Touchpoint This Grade

Pajapati's story is not told in Grade 7. The thread is held.

Students have encountered her name and her question in earlier grades. The full story — her walking barefoot, her community of women, her request to the Buddha, Ananda's advocacy, the Buddha's eventual assent, the founding of the first bhikkhuni sangha — belongs to Grade 8. It lands there differently than it would have landed in any earlier grade, because of everything students carry from Grades 1 through 7. Do not introduce it now. Hold the thread.

What Grade 7 Plants for Grade 8

Grade 8 is "The Nature of Mind" — the final year. It is the most philosophically demanding year in the curriculum, and it works because of what Grade 7 has established.

What Grade 7 establishedHow it opens Grade 8
The Feynman Principle as a framework for genuine understandingScaffold for philosophical analysis of the nature of mind — "can you teach it?" becomes "can you hold it without concepts?"
The practice of sustained healing intention (Unit 3)Preparation for the compassion practices in Grade 8's Milarepa unit — Milarepa's capacity for sustained compassion is earned, not given
The concept of lineage — the chain of transmission (Unit 4)Scaffold for the Milarepa narrative (teacher-student relationship as the spine of the story) and for the Stupa unit (the stupa as physical embodiment of the lineage)
Vow-making in U4 C4 — "what I carry forward"Preparation for Grade 8's final year declarations, including the full Bodhisattva vow ceremony that closes the program

The Grade 8 teacher should read the Grade 7 portfolio artefacts — all four — before the year begins. Knowing what each student wrote in their healing aspiration and their lineage-and-gratitude reflection is knowing who walks into the room.

Sensitive Content Notes

Unit 1 — Personal Exposure: The position paper on cruelty and bystander responsibility invites students to draw on real experience. Some students will have been targets of bullying; some will have been perpetrators; some will have been bystanders. All three positions carry their own weight. The teacher does not probe — the structure does the inviting, and students decide what to bring. If a student discloses something acute (ongoing harassment, involvement in a recent incident), follow the school's pastoral care protocol outside class time.

Unit 3 — Chosen Person: Some students will choose a person in serious or acute suffering as the subject of their healing aspiration. A dying grandparent, a parent with serious illness, a sibling in crisis. These are legitimate and valuable choices for the practice — the Medicine Buddha's vows cover exactly these situations. Receive the choice with steadiness. If the content of an aspiration suggests the student themselves is in distress — not only worried for another, but frightened or overwhelmed — act after class, not in the sharing circle.

Unit 4 — Gratitude and Grief: The lineage-and-gratitude reflection may surface grief for teachers, lineage holders, or family members who have died. This is not a problem — it is the tradition's honest acknowledgment that transmission includes loss. The teacher holds this without explanation or management. A student who names a deceased teacher in their lineage map or their reflection is doing the practice correctly.

Practical Notes

The teacher who takes Grade 7 should read Units 1 and 2 before the year begins — not only as a planning exercise but as orientation to the year's shape. The Near-Peer Teaching Project in Unit 2 is the central logistical and pedagogical challenge of the grade. Specifically: the lesson planning process requires coaching across multiple drafts; the Teaching Day requires a younger class and a confirmed date; the three-part reflection requires that students actually remember the Teaching Day clearly enough to reflect on the gap. All three require the teacher's active management. The template lessons in Unit 2 provide the structure; the teacher provides the logistical follow-through.

The Year Theme — "Teaching as Practice: What does it mean to truly understand something? What changes when you have to teach it?" — should be visible in the classroom throughout the year. Not as a motivational poster, but as the question the teacher returns to at moments of transition: between units, in debrief moments, when a student asks "why are we doing this?" This question is always the answer.

Grade 7 is the year the program's investment in the spiral design becomes most visible. Students who have been in the program since Grade 1 arrive in Grade 7 with six years of embodied practice — the Cham walk in their legs, the Ngowa in their mouths, Pajapati's name in some corner of their memory. The teacher's job is not to install the tradition in these students. It is to help them see that they have been inside it all along.
